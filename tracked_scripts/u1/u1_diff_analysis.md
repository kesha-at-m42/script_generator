## 2. Generic Medium Remediations — Method, Not Answer

### What changed

Pre-revision mediums frequently gave the specific counts or named the correct values while pretending to scaffold. The student was told the answer in medium-level clothing.

**Before:**
> "Let's think about this together. Count the rows: that's 4. Count the dots in one row: that's 2. First number is rows, second is items per row."

> "Let's count together. I see 3 columns going up and down. Each column has 4 dots in it."

**After:**
> "Not there yet. The number of horizontal rows goes into the first slot. Count the rows across and place that number in the slot. Then count the number of dots in each row. Place that number in the second slot."

> "Count carefully. How many rows go across? How many dots are in each row? Then multiply to find the product."

### Pattern

Good generic mediums teach the *procedure* the student should follow, without supplying the values that come out of it. The student still has to look at the array and count. The medium tells them *what to count and in what order* — not *what they'll find*.

### Rule
**Medium = method, not numbers.** Name the slots, name the steps, name what to look at. Leave the counting to the student. **End with a call to action** — the student has another attempt coming, so close by directing them back to the problem.

Call to action forms:
- "Try again." / "Place those in the slots."
- A direct question: "How many rows do you see?" / "Does it change?"
- An imperative pointing at the array: "Count those and place the numbers."

### More examples (post-revision)

> "Take another look. Count each line going across horizontally. There's one on top, one in the middle, one on the bottom."
*(Guides the eye without stating the count — the enumeration itself is the CTA)*

> "Here's a hint: the factors in the turn-around fact are the same, just switched. And what do we know about the product for turn-around facts? Does it change?"
*(Restates the rule, ends with a question the student must answer to re-attempt)*

> "Let's try again. The first number is how many rows go across. The second number is how many dots in each row. Place those numbers into the multiplication expression."
*(Pure procedural redirect — ends with the explicit action "Place those numbers")*

## 3. Specific Condition (Per-Distractor) Mediums — The Biggest Shift

### What changed

This is the highest-signal change in the diff. Pre-revision specific condition mediums over-explained: they identified the error, gave the correct method, AND named the correct counts. Post-revision versions do one thing well — they make the student's specific error concrete, redirect to the right concept, and give exactly one actionable step.

**Before:**
> "You counted Fish but missed Lizards. We need both categories combined. Count Fish symbols: 4. Count Lizards symbols: 1. Add them together."

> "That's just Fish. The question asks for Fish OR Lizards combined. Count both categories together: 4 Fish plus 1 Lizard."

**After:**
> "You counted Fish. The question asks for Fish OR Lizards. That means add them together."

> "The question asks for Fish OR Lizards. Count the symbols in those two columns, then add."

The revision removes the specific numbers (4, 1) and shrinks the explanation. The student now has to do the counting themselves.

### Two gold-standard patterns

**Pattern A — Credit + narrow**

When the student got *part* of the answer right, acknowledge it, then focus on the one thing that was off.

> "You're right that there are 4 columns. But count how many dots are in each column. Are there 4?"

> "You're right that each column has 5 dots. But count the columns. Are there 5 columns?"

> "That's the Fish and Lizards total. The question asks how many MORE have Cats. You need to compare Cats to that total."

Structure: `[What was right]. [But / Now] [what to re-examine]. [Focused question or single step]`

**Pattern B — Name + redirect + point**

When the student made a specific identifiable error, name what they did, redirect to the correct concept, and point to the relevant evidence.

> "You counted the Dogs. When we ask how many fewer, we need to find the difference. Count how far apart Dogs and Fish are."

> "You counted the columns. Rows go across horizontally. How many lines go across?"

> "That's the rows description. Look at columns now. How many columns are there? Each column has how many dots?"

Structure: `[What the student did — short]. [What the question actually needs]. [One action or question]`

### What specific condition mediums do NOT do

- Give the correct counts ("Count Fish: 4. Count Lizards: 1.")
- Explain the full procedure top-to-bottom
- Mention what the final answer should be

### Rule
**Specific condition mediums name the error precisely, redirect to the concept, and close with a call to action.** The CTA is the single step that sends the student back to attempt again — a pointed question, an imperative, or a "try again" attached to the redirect. They do not solve the problem. The student still has to execute.

## 4. Heavy Remediations — Principle Ending

### What changed

Pre-revision heavies narrated the mechanics: count the things, write the expression. Post-revision heavies narrate the thinking and end with the structural insight — the *why* that makes the answer make sense.

**Before:**
> "Let me show you how this works. First, I count the rows: 1, 2. That's 2 rows. Then I count the dots in one row: 7 dots. So the expression is 2 times 7."

**After:**
> "Let me show you the two ways to think about this array. If I want rows, I count the horizontal rows: 6 rows. Then I count the dots in one row: 3 dots. 6 rows of 3 is 18 total dots. 6 times 3 equals 18. If I want columns, I count the vertical columns: 3 columns. Then I count the dots in one column: 6 dots. 3 columns of 6 is 18 total dots. 3 times 6 equals 18. Two ways to describe this array and equations that match. Both are correct."

### Pattern

Good heavies:
1. Name the structure being demonstrated ("horizontal rows", "vertical columns")
2. Connect each counting step to what it *means* ("6 rows of 3 is 18 total dots")
3. Show the full picture when the concept has multiple valid paths (rows AND columns)
4. End with the principle, not just the answer

More examples of principle endings:

> "The answer is: same dots, you just read them a different way."
*(Lesson s1_4 — commutativity)*

> "Rotation switches the numbers in the multiplication expression."
*(Exitcheck s1_3 — rotation)*

> "The words match the expression."
*(Lesson s2_1 — description-to-expression)*

> "Same dots, same total. That's why it's called a property. It's always true."
*(Synthesis s2_1 — generalization)*

### Rule
**Heavy ends with the principle, not the answer.** Name the structural insight that makes this particular answer correct. The student should leave knowing not just what the answer is, but why it has to be.

## 5. On-Correct Beats — Same Closing Philosophy as Heavy

### The shared rule

Heavy remediations and on-correct beats share the same closing philosophy: **don't just state the answer, state what the answer demonstrates.** The closing sentence should name the structural insight, not just confirm the result.

The difference in register:
- **Heavy**: principle explains *why* — the student was just shown the answer and needs to understand why it has to be that way
- **On-correct**: principle names *what* — the student just demonstrated something and the beat names what they proved

Both end with a sentence that a student could carry forward to the next problem.

### Examples from the diff

**On-correct with principle ending:**
> "Right. Same dots. Whether you read them by rows or columns, whether you turn the array, the dots don't change. They are just organized differently. That's why the product can't change."
*(Synthesis s2_4 — names the invariant behind commutativity)*

> "How many more and how many fewer both require subtraction. That's called COMPARING. You find the difference to compare. And remember: in all means COMBINE, so you add. Compare and combine, those are your two problem types."
*(Synthesis s3_2 — ends by naming both categories the student now owns)*

> "Right. 8 rows of 3. That's 3 rows of 8, just turned. Same dots, same product. Now look at the others: 4 × 6, 6 × 4, 2 × 12. They all have 24 dots too, but they're different shapes. The same product doesn't always mean the same array."
*(Lesson s3_3 — closes with a discrimination the student should now hold)*

**What a principle-less closing looks like (avoid):**
> "Right. 4 times 5 equals 20. You counted 6 rows with 3 in each row."
*(Just restates the answer and what the student did — no transferable insight)*

- Removed emphasis capitalization inconsistencies
