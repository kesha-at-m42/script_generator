## s2_1_comparison_question_single_step
# Section Summary: s2_1_comparison_question_single_step

**VISUAL STATE:** A horizontal bar graph titled "After-School Activities" is displayed in reading mode with scale 2. The graph contains four categories with exact values: Soccer (12), Basketball (7), Tennis (6), and Swimming (11). A multiple-choice prompt with options [18, 12, 6, 3] is active on screen. Upon correct selection of 6, the Soccer and Tennis bars highlight to confirm the answer.

**CONTENT:** Students practiced comparison reasoning using subtraction to find the difference between two data values. The vocabulary "how many more" was reinforced as a comparison operation requiring subtraction (larger value minus smaller value).

**STUDENT ACTION:** The student answered a multiple-choice question by selecting 6 as the correct answer to "How many more students chose soccer than tennis?" (12 − 6 = 6), demonstrating understanding of data comparison in a bar graph context.

## s4_1_bridge_lesson
# Section Summary: s4_1_bridge_lesson

**VISUAL STATE:** A vertical bar graph titled "Favorite Lunch" displays four categories (Pizza, Tacos, Salad, Burgers) with values 45, 25, 10, and 15 respectively, using a scale of 5. Below it, a word problem area displays the question: "How many more students chose pizza than tacos and salad together?" Both elements remain on screen throughout the section in reading mode.

**CONTENT:** This transition section bridges prior graph-reading and single-step comparison skills to multi-step problem-solving. The lesson introduces the concept that certain word problems—particularly those containing the word "together"—require multiple steps to solve, moving beyond simple pairwise comparisons.

**STUDENT ACTION:** The student passively observed the bar graph and word problem while the instructor highlighted the complexity of the multi-step question. No interactive response or calculation was required; this section serves as a preview and motivation for upcoming content.
