## s2_1_scale_preview_exploration
# Section Summary: s2_1_scale_preview_exploration

**VISUAL STATE:** Two tangibles are on screen at section end: (1) a data_table displaying student-collected block data with four categories—Red blocks, Blue blocks, Green blocks, Yellow blocks—with values that are multiples of 5 between 10 and 45; (2) a vertical bar_graph in reading mode with the Scale Preview System visible, showing four clickable scale buttons (Scale of 1, Scale of 2, Scale of 5, Scale of 10) that allow interactive exploration of how different scales represent the same dataset.

**CONTENT:** This section introduced the concept of scale selection and flexibility—specifically, that multiple scales can work appropriately for a single dataset. Students learned that choosing a scale involves evaluating which option best displays their data, rather than there being a single "correct" answer. The vocabulary "scale" was reinforced in context (Scale of 1, Scale of 2, Scale of 5, Scale of 10).

**STUDENT ACTION:** The student clicked through all four available scale options sequentially to preview how each scale would render their block-counting data on the vertical bar graph, confirming that all four scales (1, 2, 5, and 10) successfully display the data without issues.

## s3_1_efficiency_comparison
# Section Summary: s3_1_efficiency_comparison

**VISUAL STATE:** Three tangibles are displayed at section end: (1) a data table containing student-collected block counts for Red blocks, Blue blocks, Green blocks, and Yellow blocks (values are multiples of 5 between 10–45); (2) a vertical bar graph in reading mode with scale 5, categories Red blocks/Blue blocks/Green blocks/Yellow blocks, positioned on the left; (3) a vertical bar graph in reading mode with scale 10, same categories, positioned on the right.

**CONTENT:** This section introduced the concept of **scale efficiency**—that multiple scales can validly represent the same data, but different scales offer different advantages (scale 5 provides more detail; scale 10 is cleaner/simpler). The key principle taught is that when multiple scales work, the student has the freedom to choose based on readability preference.

**STUDENT ACTION:** The student compared two side-by-side bar graphs and answered a multiple-choice question ("Which graph is easier to read?") with three options: Scale of 5, Scale of 10, or They're the same. All three responses were marked correct with affirming feedback reflecting the student's choice.

## s4_1_bridge_lesson
# Section Summary: s4_1_bridge_lesson

**VISUAL STATE:** Two tangibles are displayed: (1) a data table containing student-collected block counts for four categories—Red blocks, Blue blocks, Green blocks, Yellow blocks—with values that are multiples of 5 between 10 and 45; (2) a vertical bar graph in reading mode displaying the same four categories (Red blocks, Blue blocks, Green blocks, Yellow blocks) with the Scale Preview System visible, offering four scale button options (Scale of 1, Scale of 2, Scale of 5, Scale of 10).

**CONTENT:** This transition section bridges from scale exploration to scale selection strategy. Students are introduced to the concept that while multiple scales can work for a given dataset, some scales are "better" than others, and that certain scales may not work at all depending on data values. The vocabulary reinforced includes "scale" and the specific scale options (1, 2, 5, 10).

**STUDENT ACTION:** Students observed the bar graph displaying their data under different scales and recognized that all four scales functioned for their dataset. No direct interaction was required; this section sets up the next learning objective of determining which scale is optimal and identifying when a scale fails.
