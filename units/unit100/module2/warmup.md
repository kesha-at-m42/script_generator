## 1.6 WARMUP (3–5 minutes)

### Core Purpose

The Warmup orients the student's attention to equation orientation as a dimension of variation before the lesson formally introduces both-orientation equivalence. It re-encounters the canonical 4 × 5 = 20 rectangle from M01 (Concrete prelude) so that the lesson's Mode C first reveal at L.1 lands on already-familiar ground.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Duration | 3–5 minutes (W.1 itself runs 60–90 seconds; remainder absorbed by Purpose Frame transition) |
| Toy(s) Used | §2.11 Multiple Choice / Checkbox |
| Problem Count | 1 (W.1) |
| Stage | Concrete prelude (re-encounter, not Concrete production) |
| Rectangle | Canonical 4 × 5 = 20 only (per SME 2026-04-28; OQ-M02-RECT-1 RESOLVED) |

### Constraints

| Constraint | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Single canonical fact | All four W.1 candidates involve the canonical 4 × 5 = 20 fact only; no other multiplication facts appear in W.1. |
| No new build | W.1 references the rectangle the student built in M01; no Mode B build affordances are activated. |
| Vocabulary fence | "multiple" / "is a multiple of" forbidden in W.1 student-facing copy (Beats 1–4 gate). "factor," "factor pair," "prime," "composite," "height," "handle," bare "squares," and WODB language all forbidden. |
| Discrimination routine name | "Which one breaks the pattern?" — inherited from M01 (Vocabulary Guidance §4 / correction #3). |
| Non-evaluative post-Check | Required bridge statement (ISF-M02-5) must accompany the post-Check teacher move; the warmup must not signal that any orientation is aberrant. |

---

### Warmup Type Rationale

**Type:** [EXPOSURE] — re-encounter + dimension-of-variation orientation.

The student already trusts the 4 × 5 = 20 rectangle from M01. Re-encountering it in W.1 capitalizes on established prior knowledge and frees cognitive bandwidth for the relational-equal-sign insight that Mode C will deliver at L.1. Using equation-orientation as the discriminative dimension (three candidates with the product on the right; one with the product on the left) primes the student to attend to where the product sits — the precise dimension the lesson's One Thing will reframe as "two equivalent forms of the same fact." The post-Check bridge statement ("That form is today's topic — and you'll see that it is just as correct as the others") immediately neutralizes the risk that "breaks the pattern" reads as "is wrong."

---

### Interaction W.1: Re-encounter the 4 × 5 = 20 Rectangle [REVIEW]

**Setup:**
Four equation candidates appear via §2.11 Multiple Choice. All four candidates involve the canonical 4 × 5 = 20 fact. Three share a pattern: they are written with the product on the right side (`a × b = c`). One breaks the pattern: it is written with the product on the left side (`c = a × b`). Hook: "Look at the rectangle you built last lesson. Four equations all describe it. Three share a pattern. One breaks it. Which one?"

Candidate set (authoring constraint, single canonical fact only): `4 × 5 = 20` ✓-pattern, `5 × 4 = 20` ✓-pattern, `4 × 5 = 20` ✓-pattern (presented with neutral variation in display order so visual placement is not the discriminator), `20 = 4 × 5` ✗-pattern-breaker (breaks the "product on right" pattern).

*(Authoring note 1: To preserve four distinct candidates while keeping the single canonical fact discipline, content authors may use the four equivalent canonical-fact equations: `4 × 5 = 20`, `5 × 4 = 20`, `20 = 4 × 5`, `20 = 5 × 4`. Three should display with the product on the right; one — and only one — with the product on the left as the pattern-breaker. The pattern-breaker MUST be a reversed-orientation form of the canonical 4 × 5 = 20 fact. Per SME 2026-04-28, no non-canonical facts (e.g., `3 × 6 = 18`, `2 × 7 = 14`) appear in W.1.)*

*(Authoring note 2: The purpose of W.1 is not to teach that the reversed orientation is "wrong" — the tutor framing after Check will immediately note that BOTH forms are used in today's lesson. The warmup serves only to orient the student's attention to equation orientation as a dimension of variation.)*

**Student Action:**
Student taps the equation that breaks the pattern, then taps Check.

**Teacher Move:**
Post-Check (observable only): "You tapped that one — it looks different from the others. **That form is today's topic — and you'll see that it is just as correct as the others.** Let's go."

*(Bridge statement is REQUIRED verbatim per ISF-M02-5: prevents the warmup from inadvertently signalling that `c = a × b` is aberrant. The post-Check teacher move must explicitly name the reversed-orientation form as "just as correct" before the lesson proceeds.)*

**Key Observation:**
Student notices orientation as a dimension of variation — the product position is different. Any correct or incorrect tap is acceptable in the warmup; the teacher move is non-evaluative and bridges forward.

**Common Detour:**
Student taps a standard-orientation equation as "the pattern-breaker" (incorrect selection). The post-Check teacher move is the same either way — non-evaluative, bridges to the lesson. Do not correct.

**Intervention:**
If student appears disengaged or taps randomly, acknowledge the selection in observable terms: "You tapped that one. Let's look at what makes these equations different." Proceed to Purpose Frame.

**Next Move:**
Proceed to Purpose Frame, then to Interaction L.1.

**Duration:** 60–90 seconds.
