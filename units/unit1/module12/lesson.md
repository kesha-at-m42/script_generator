## **1.7 LESSON (~10-14 minutes)**

**Core Purpose**

Students prove that two expressions describing the same array always give the same product — through two visual methods (highlighting and rotation) — then name this discovery ("you can multiply in any order" / commutative property) and apply it as a strategy. By the end of the Lesson, students can explain WHY order doesn't change the product using arrays, predict the result of rotating an array, distinguish rotated arrays from genuinely different arrays, and apply the property to derive unknown facts from known ones.

### **Pedagogical Flow**

Students arrive from Warmup having re-activated dual interpretation (M11 skill) and observed that 2 × 7 and 7 × 2 both equal 14 — without explanation. The Lesson delivers the explanation through three sections.

Section 1 consolidates M11's dual interpretation and extends it: students don't just write two expressions for the same array, they explicitly verify both expressions give the same product. This is grounding — students see the equivalence pattern across multiple arrays before any explanation of WHY.

Section 2 introduces the new element: rotation animation. The array physically turns 90°, making it visually undeniable that rows become columns, columns become rows, and no dots appear or disappear. Rotation is the "proof" — it explains WHY both expressions give the same product (same dots, just reorganized). Students predict rotation outcomes before seeing them, building understanding rather than passive observation.

Section 3 names what students have discovered and applies it strategically. Vocabulary ("you can multiply in any order" / "commutative property" / "strategy") comes AFTER the visual proof creates mental hooks. Students apply the property to derive facts, and discriminate between rotated arrays (same product because same dots) and genuinely different arrays (same product but different dimensions).

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Highlighting Review + Product Equivalence**

**Purpose:** Consolidate M11's dual interpretation AND extend it by explicitly verifying both expressions give the same product. Students see equivalence happen across multiple arrays before Section 2 explains WHY. This section produces the observation; Section 2 produces the proof.

---

### **Interaction 1.1: Worked Example — Dual Interpretation with Product Verification (3×5 array)**

* **Visual:** Arrays with Pictures (dot mode): 3×5 dot array displayed centrally, neutral emphasis.
* **Guide:** "Let's figure out whether those two expressions really do always give the same product. Watch how I test this idea."
* **Visual:** System applies row emphasis (3 horizontal bands highlighted sequentially).
* **Guide:** "I see the rows. 3 rows of 5. That's 3 × 5. And 3 times 5 is 15."
* **Visual:** Row emphasis clears. System applies column emphasis (5 vertical bands highlighted sequentially).
* **Guide:** "Now the columns. 5 columns of 3. That's 5 × 3. And 5 times 3 is... 15. Same product! Same array, same dots. I didn't add anything or take anything away. Just read it a different way."
* **Visual:** Both expressions displayed below array: 3 × 5 = 15 | 5 × 3 = 15.
* **No student action** (worked example — Guide think-aloud).
---

### **Interaction 1.2: Student Tries Similar — Row and Column Expressions (5×4 array)**

* **Visual:** Arrays with Pictures (dot mode): 5×4 dot array. System applies row emphasis.
* **Guide:** "Here's a new array. I highlighted the rows — see them going across? How many rows, and how many in each? Build the expression and find the product."
* **Prompt:** "Build the equation for the rows."
* **Student Action:** Equation Builder Mode 2 (equation mode — 3 slots: `[___] × [___] = [___]`), Methods C/D. Tiles: 3, 4, 5, 6, 9, 10, 16, 20, 24, 25
* **Correct Answer:** 5 × 4 = 20
* **Guide (on correct):** "5 times 4 equals 20. 5 rows of 4."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
*(Immediately followed by:)*

* **Visual:** Row emphasis clears. System applies column emphasis to same array.
* **Guide:** "Now I switched to columns. Build the column expression and find the product."
* **Prompt:** "Build the equation for the columns."
* **Student Action:** Equation Builder Mode 2 (equation mode), Methods C/D. Tiles: 3, 4, 5, 6, 9, 10, 16, 20, 24, 25
* **Correct Answer:** 4 × 5  = 20
* **Guide (on correct):** "4 columns of 5. 4 times 5 equals 20. Same product again."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
---

### **Interaction 1.3: Second Array — Product Comparison Explicit (**7 × 3** array)**

* **Visual:** Arrays with Pictures (dot mode): 7 × 3 dot array. System applies row emphasis.
* **Guide:** "Another array. Rows are highlighted. Build the row expression and find the product."
* **Prompt:** "Build the equation for the rows."
* **Student Action:** Equation Builder Mode 2 (equation mode), Methods C/D. Tiles: 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 10, 18, 20, 21, 24
* **Correct Answer:** 7 × 3 = 21
* **Guide (on correct):** "7 times 3 equals 21."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
*(Immediately followed by:)*

* **Visual:** Column emphasis applied.
* **Guide:** "Now columns. Build it."
* **Prompt:** "Build the equation for the columns."
* **Student Action:** Equation Builder Mode 2, Methods C/D. Tiles: 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 10, 18, 20, 21, 24
* **Correct Answer:** 3 × 7 = 21
* **Guide (on correct):** "3 times 7 equals 21. Same product — again."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
* **Guide (explicit pattern-stating):** "That's three arrays now. Every time — two expressions, same product. 15 and 15. 20 and 20. 21 and 21."
---

### *Interaction 1.4: Pattern Observation — "Is This Always True?" **

* **Visual:** All three array results displayed in a summary:
  * 3 × 5 = 15 | 5 × 3 = 15
  * 5 × 4 = 20 | 4 × 5 = 20
  * 7 × 3 = 21 | 3 × 7 = 21
* **Guide:** "Same product every time. But here's what we need to figure out: is this a coincidence, or does this ALWAYS happen? And if it always happens... why?"
* **Guide:** "That's three arrays now. Every time there were two expressions, same product. 15 and 15. 20 and 20. 21 and 21. Why do you think both expressions give the same product?"
* **Prompt:** "Why do both expressions give the same product?"
* **Options (MC):**
A) Same dots — you just read them a different way
B) The numbers are small enough to match
C) Rows and columns always have the same number
D) It only works for some arrays
* **Correct Answer: **Same dots — you just read them a different way
* **Rationale:**
  * A = Correct. Articulates the structural proof — same items, different organization.
  * B = Numerosity misconception. Student thinks equivalence is coincidental or size-dependent. Diagnostically useful — flags students who won't generalize.
  * C = Structural confusion. Student conflates "same product" with "same count per row and column." Only true for square arrays.
  * D = Skepticism. Student hasn't been convinced by three examples. Diagnostically useful heading into Section 2 where rotation provides the stronger proof.
* **Guide (on correct):** "No dots changed. You just looked at the same dots a different way. Same dots has to mean same total. That's why the products match."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
* **Guide:** "But can we prove it even more clearly? Watch this."
---

### **1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Rotation Discovery**

**Purpose:** Introduce rotation animation as a visual proof of WHY both expressions give the same product. Highlighting showed "two ways to read the same array." Rotation shows "the same array physically turns — rows become columns, columns become rows, nothing changes." Students predict rotation outcomes before seeing them, building causal understanding rather than passive observation.

---

### **Interaction 2.1: Worked Example — Rotation Animation (3×6 array)**

* **Visual:** Arrays with Pictures (dot mode): 3×6 dot array, neutral emphasis.
* **Guide:** "Here's an array showing 3 rows of 6."
* **Visual:** System briefly highlights rows. Expression displayed: 3 × 6 = 18.
* **Guide:** "3 times 6 equals 18. Now watch what happens when I turn this array."
* **Visual:** Rotation animation plays. Array physically rotates 90° clockwise. The 3×6 array becomes a 6×3 array. Animation is smooth and visible (~2 seconds).
* **Guide:** "I turned it. Look, what WERE rows are now columns. What WERE columns are now rows."
* **Visual:** System highlights rows of the new orientation. Expression displayed: 6 × 3 = 18.
* **Guide:** "Now it's 6 rows of 3. 6 times 3 equals 18. Same product."
* **Guide:** "And here's the important part, did any dots disappear when I turned it? Did new dots show up?"
* **Guide:** "No. Every single dot is still there. I just turned the array. Same dots, same total. That's why the product can't change."
* **No student action** (worked example — rotation introduction).
---

### **Interaction 2.2: Student Predicts Rotation — Before Animation (5×3 array)**

* **Visual:** Arrays with Pictures (dot mode): 5×3 dot array. System highlights rows.
* **Guide:** "5 rows of 3. That's 5 × 3, and 5 times 3 is 15. I'm going to rotate this array. Before I do, let's make a prediction. What expression will describe the rows AFTER I turn it?"
* **Prompt:** "After rotation, what will the row expression be?"
* **Options (MC):** A) 3 × 5 | B) 5 × 3 | C) 15 × 1  | D) 3 + 5
* **Correct Answer:** A) 3 × 5
* **Visual:** Rotation animation plays. Array becomes 3×5. System highlights rows.
* **Guide (on correct):** "3 times 5. The 5 rows of 3 turned into 3 rows of 5. And it has the same total of 15 still. You predicted it."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A) 3 × 5 = Correct. Rotation swaps row and column counts.
  * B) 5 × 3 = Misconception. Student believes rotation doesn't change the expression. May indicate #13 confusion about what rotation does.
  * C) 15 × 1 = Misconception. Student conflates product with expression structure.
  * D) 3 + 5 = Misconception. Student adds factors instead of multiplying.
---

### **Interaction 2.3: Predict Rotation + Product Confirmation (**8 × 5** array)**

* **Visual:** Arrays with Pictures (dot mode): 8 × 5 dot array. System highlights rows.
* **Guide:** "8 rows of 5. 8 × 5 = 40. If I turn this array, what will the new row expression be?"
* **Prompt:** "After rotation, what will the row expression be?"
* **Options (MC):** A) 8 × 5 | B) 5 × 8 | C) 5 × 40 | D) 8 + 5
* **Correct Answer:** B) 5 × 8
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A) 8 × 5 = Misconception. Student believes expression stays unchanged after rotation. May indicate #13 — not connecting physical rotation to expression change.
  * B) 5 × 8 = Correct. Student understands rotation swaps row and column counts.
  * C) 5 × 40 = Misconception. Student confuses product (40) with factor count. Conflating total with group size.
  * D) 8 + 5 = Misconception. Student adds factors instead of multiplying.
*(Immediately followed by:)*

* **Guide:** "And will the product change?"
* **Prompt:** "After rotation, will the product be the same or different?"
* **Options (MC):** A) Same. Still 40 | B) Different. It lost rows. | C) Different. It lost columns. | D) Different. The factors changed.
* **Correct Answer:** A) Same. Still 40
* **Visual:** Rotation animation plays. Array becomes 8×5. Expression displayed: 8 × 5 = 40.
* **Guide (on correct):** "8 times 5. Still 40. Same dots, just turned."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A) Same. Still 40 = Correct. Student has internalized the proof from 2.1-2.2.
  * B, C or D) Different = #13 active. Student believes changing the expression changes the product. Core misconception — Section 2 proof hasn't landed yet. Remediation should return to "same dots" anchor.
---

### **Interaction 2.4: Consolidation — Hybrid Understanding**

* **Visual:** 3×6 array displayed. Guide demonstrates both proofs in sequence:
  1. Highlighting: Row emphasis (3 × 6 = 18) → Column emphasis (6 × 3 = 18)
  1. Rotation: 3×6 rotates to 6×3 (same 18)
* **Guide:** "Two different ways to prove the same thing. Highlighting: you read the same array two ways. Rotation: you turn the array and rows become columns. Either way, same dots, same product."
* **Guide:** "This doesn't just work for these arrays. It works for EVERY array. Any array you can build. The two expressions will always give the same product. Always."
* **Prompt:** "Why do both expressions always give the same product?"
* **Options (MC):** A) Same dots, just organized differently | B) The numbers are close together | C) Arrays are always the same size | D) Multiplication always gives the same answer
* **Correct Answer:** A) Same dots, just organized differently
* **Guide (on correct):** "Same dots. Whether you read them by rows or columns, whether you turn the array, the dots don't change. That's why the product can't change."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A) Same dots, just organized differently = Correct. Student has internalized the structural proof.
  * B) The numbers are close together = Numerosity misconception. Student notices factor similarity, not structural equivalence. Doesn't connect to the visual proof.
  * C) Arrays are always the same size = Overgeneralization. Student may think all arrays are equivalent regardless of dimensions. Conflates "same product" with "same array."
  * D) Multiplication always gives the same answer = student is restating the conclusion as the reason — circular reasoning. Knows the property works but can't explain *why*.

### **1.7.3 LESSON SECTION 3: Property Naming + Strategic Application**

**Purpose:** Name the pattern students have proven, introduce it as a strategy, and apply it in three ways: (1) deriving unknown facts from known ones, (2) discriminating rotated arrays from genuinely different arrays, and (3) mixed application with faded scaffolding. Vocabulary introduction follows the Lesson Phase Playbook's "after visual grounding" principle — students have completed two sections of proof before encountering any formal names.

---

### **Interaction 3.1: Property Naming (Vocabulary Introduction)**

* **Visual:** Summary display showing 2-3 of the proven equivalences:
  * 3 × 5 = 15 | 5 × 3 = 15
  * 3 × 6 = 18 | 6 × 3 = 18
  * 5 × 8 = 40 | 8 × 5 = 40
* **Guide:** "You proved something important. No matter which array you check, the two expressions always give the same product. This shows us that you can multiply the factors in any order and always get the same answer. These are turn-around facts."
* **Guide:** "Mathematicians have a name for this. They call it the **commutative property** of multiplication. But the idea is simple, you can change the order of the factors and get the same answer."
* **Guide:** "And here's why this is more than just interesting. It's a **strategy,** a plan for solving problems. If you know one fact, you already know its turn-around fact without doing any extra work."
* **No student action** (naming moment).
> **Voice Note:** "You can multiply in any order" and "commutative property" introduced AFTER visual proof is complete. Vocabulary attaches to mental hooks, not the reverse.

---

### **Interaction 3.2: Worked Example + Student Application — Strategic Use**

* **Visual:** Display: "If 4 × 6 = 24, what's 6 × 4?"
* **Guide:** "Watch how the strategy works. If I know 4 times 6 is 24, do I need to compute 6 times 4? No, I can multiply in any order. Switch the factors. 6 times 4 is also 24. I didn't compute anything. I used what I already knew."
*(Immediately followed by student attempt:)*

* **Visual:** Display: "If 7 × 8 = 56, what's 8 × 7?"
* **Guide:** "Your turn. You know 7 times 8 is 56. What's 8 times 7? Use the strategy."
* **Prompt:** "If 7 × 8 = 56, what's 8 × 7?"
* **Options (MC):** A) 56 | B) 15 | C) 78 | D) 54
* **Correct Answer:** A) 56
* **Guide (on correct):** "56. You didn't need to compute, you used the turn-around facts. You can multiply the factors in any order and get the same product."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A) 56 = Correct. Property applied.
  * B) 15 = Additive error (7 + 8). Misconception #9 residual.
  * C) 78 = Concatenation error (5 and 8 combined as digits).
  * D) 54 = Incorrect multiplication fact (9 × 6) with close product
---

### **Interaction 3.3: Equivalence Discrimination — Which Array Is the Rotation?**

* **Visual:** Arrays with Pictures: A 3×8 dot array displayed as the reference. Four dot arrays displayed as options: A) 4×6 | B) 8×3 | C) 6×4 | D) 2×12
* **Guide:** "Here's an array — 3 rows of 8. That's 24 dots. Now look at these four arrays. They ALL have 24 dots. But which one is the SAME array as 3 × 8, just rotated?"
* **Prompt:** "Which array is the same as 3 × 8, just rotated?"
* **Options (MC):** A) 4 × 6 | B) 8 × 3 | C) 6 × 4 | D) 2 × 12
* **Correct Answer:** B) 8 × 3
* **Guide (on correct):** "8 rows of 3. That's 3 rows of 8, just turned. Same dots, same product. Now look at the others — 4 × 6, 6 × 4, 2 × 12. They all have 24 dots too, but they're different shapes. You can't rotate 3 rows of 8 and get 4 rows of 6. Same product doesn't mean same array."
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A) 4 × 6 = Different array, same product. Student confuses product equivalence with rotational equivalence.
  * B) 8 × 3 = Correct. Rotation swaps 3 rows of 8 into 8 rows of 3.
  * C) 6 × 4 = Rotation of 4×6, not of 3×8. Student understands rotation involves swapping but doesn't track which specific dimensions are swapping. Diagnostically distinct from A.
  * D) 2 × 12 = Different array, same product. Visually most distinct from reference. If chosen, student is likely attending only to product, not shape.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Design Note:** This interaction directly addresses the equivalence discrimination skill from Exit Check scope (Q5). Students must distinguish "same product because rotated" from "same product, different array."

---

### **Interaction 3.4: Mixed Application — Faded Support**

* **Visual:** Display: "9 × 4 = 36."
* **Guide:** "Given that 9 times 4 is 36,  what turn-around fact do you know?"
* **Prompt:** "If 9 × 4 = 36, what turn-around fact do you know?"
* **Student Action:** Equation Builder Mode 2, Click-Place (D). Tiles: 3, 4, 6, 9, 13, 36, 49, 63. Student builds: `4 × 9 = 36`.
* **Correct Answer:** 4 × 9 = 36
* **Guide (on correct):** "4 × 9 = 36. You can multiply in any order and get the same product."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
---

### **Bridge to Exit Check**

* **Guide:** "You proved it: when you change the order of the factors, the product stays the same. You used highlighting, rotation, and the turn-around strategy. Let's see what you know."
* **No student action** (transition).
> **Voice Note:** Brief. References what student DID (proved, used highlighting, rotation, strategy), not what they should feel. Matches playbook transition formula.
