# BACKBONE
---
## 1.0 THE ONE THING
Students must understand that **multiplying by 2, 5, and 10 produces predictable patterns they can use to find products — and these are the SAME skip-counting families they already mastered when reading graph scales.** M9 is the first module where students COMPUTE: expressions become equations, and the × symbol gets an = sign and a product. The learning is that multiplication has an ANSWER you can find, and for 2s, 5s, and 10s, skip-counting is the reliable strategy to get there.
**Critical Misconception:** Skip Counting Without Meaning (#18) — Students can fluently count "5, 10, 15, 20" but don't connect this to 4 × 5 = 20. They have the procedural tool (skip-counting from Grade 2) without the structural bridge to multiplication. This is the INVERSE of M7's bridge: if M7's "scales were multiplication" didn't fully land, students will skip-count without knowing they're multiplying.
**Success Indicator:** Given the expression 6 × 5, student:
1. Recognizes this means "6 groups of 5" (carried from M7-M8)
2. Skip-counts: "5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30" (strategy)
3. Writes or builds: 6 × 5 = 30 (equation with product)
4. Can explain: "Multiplying by 5 gives you numbers that end in 0 or 5" (pattern recognition)
5. Connects: "This is like reading a graph with scale of 5 — 6 symbols would mean 30" (transfer)
**Biggest Risk:** Students who build fluency through rote memorization without connecting to patterns or meaning. The goal isn't just that students CAN compute 7 × 2 = 14 — it's that they understand WHY 2s products are always even, WHY 5s end in 0 or 5, and WHY these patterns exist (because of the structure of the factor families). Without pattern awareness, fluency is brittle: it works for practiced facts but provides no strategy for unfamiliar ones. The secondary risk is that M9 becomes "drill time" and students lose the conceptual thread connecting M7's equal groups → M8's expressions → M9's equations.

## 1.1 LEARNING GOALS
*Verbatim from OUR Curriculum — Script Must Achieve These*
**L14:** "Find products of 2 and another whole number."
**L15:** "Multiply 10 by another whole number."
**Added (Departure 3):** "Multiply 5 by a whole number."
**Rationale for 5s Addition:** Maintains thematic coherence with Unit 1's scale progression — 2s connects to 1:2 scale, 5s to 1:5 scale, 10s to 1:10 scale/place value. Without 5s, students lose the graph-to-multiplication connection for the scale they used most extensively (M3-M4). Additionally, 5s leverage Grade 2 skip-counting fluency and the hand reference (5 fingers), making them a natural bridge between 2s and 10s.
**Module Goal (Student-Facing):** "You know how to BUILD multiplication — 3 times 5, 4 times 2. But what does 3 times 5 EQUAL? Today you'll find out. You'll discover patterns that make multiplying by 2, 5, and 10 fast — and you'll see that the graph scales you've been using were these EXACT multiplication families all along."
**Exit Check Tests:**
- Can student compute a product for a × 2 expression using skip-counting? (e.g., 7 × 2 = 14)
- Can student compute a product for a × 5 expression using skip-counting? (e.g., 6 × 5 = 30)
- Can student compute a product for a × 10 expression using skip-counting or pattern? (e.g., 4 × 10 = 40)
- Can student identify a pattern characteristic of each factor family? (2s are even, 5s end in 0/5, 10s end in 0) *(Assessed via Synthesis S.2/S.4 and Practice, not Exit Check — EC focuses on computation)*

### 1.1.2 Module Bridges
**From Module 8:** Students can BUILD multiplication expressions using the Equation Builder. They know "X groups of Y" = "X × Y" and can work bidirectionally (visual → expression, expression → visual). They use Methods A (MC) and C/D (tiles) to construct expressions. Factor combinations are broad (2-5 groups, 2-6 per group) — M8 taught STRUCTURAL understanding, not factor-specific fluency. M8's Synthesis closure explicitly previewed M9: "Next time, you'll find out what 4 times 3 EQUALS. You'll learn to figure out the total. You're ready because you already know what the expression means."
**This Module:** Students cross from expression to equation — the × symbol gets an = sign and a product. M9 narrows from M8's broad factor ranges to three specific families (2s, 5s, 10s) and teaches computation through skip-counting strategies. Key learning:
- Expressions become equations: "4 × 5" → "4 × 5 = 20" (= sign and product introduced)
- Skip-counting IS the computation strategy: "Count by 5s four times: 5, 10, 15, 20"
- Repeated addition as alternative strategy: "5 + 5 + 5 + 5 = 20" (not primary, but validated)
- Pattern discovery: 2s are always even; 5s end in 0 or 5; 10s end in 0
- Graph scale callback: "4 symbols at scale 5 = 4 × 5 = 20 — you've been computing this since Module 3!"
- Vocabulary formalized: **factor** (the numbers being multiplied), **product** (reinforced from M7 — now applied through computation), **equation** (expression with "= product"), **multiples** (products when multiplying by a number), **pattern**
- Factor order constraint: Second factor is ALWAYS 2, 5, or 10 (the skip-counting unit). "7 × 2" not "2 × 7" — preserves the "groups OF skip-counting-unit" structure that matches graph scale reading.
**To Module 10:** M9 built FLUENCY with 2s, 5s, and 10s. Students can compute products automatically for these families. M10 introduces multiplication EQUATIONS with unknowns (☐ × 5 = 20, 3 × ☐ = 12) — students now solve FOR missing numbers rather than just finding products. M9's fluency reduces cognitive load so M10 can focus on the new demand: understanding what the unknown represents and using equation structure to find it. M10 also introduces context variety (rows, stacks, teams — moving beyond bags/boxes/circles) and the = sign flexibility (12 = 3 × 4 AND 3 × 4 = 12).

- Reinforce from M7-M8: expression, multiplication, equal groups, times, skip counting

### ⚠️ Scope Warnings
> ⚠️ **Scope Warning:** EQUATION ≠ UNKNOWN: M9 introduces equations (4 × 5 = 20) — but the product is ALWAYS the target. Students compute the product from two known factors. They do NOT solve for unknown factors (☐ × 5 = 20). That's M10. The Equation Builder template is `[___] × [___] = [___]` where the first two slots are given and the third is the answer.
> ⚠️ **Scope Warning:** FLUENCY ≠ MEMORIZATION: M9 builds fluency through UNDERSTANDING (pattern discovery, skip-counting strategy, graph connections), not through rote drill. Students who "just know" 5 × 10 = 50 are celebrated — but students who need to skip-count are equally valid. The goal is reliable, strategic computation, not flashcard speed. Automaticity is the end-of-Grade-3 goal (3.OA.C.7), not the M9 goal.
> ⚠️ **Scope Warning:** FACTOR ORDER IS CONSTRAINED: In M9, the second factor is ALWAYS 2, 5, or 10. This means "7 × 2" not "2 × 7." The rationale: (1) preserves "groups OF skip-counting-unit" structure from M7-M8, (2) matches graph scale reading ("4 symbols at scale 5" = 4 × 5), (3) makes skip-counting strategy transparent ("7 groups of 2 → count by 2s seven times"). Late M9 activities may preview turn-around facts (7 × 2 AND 2 × 7) but this is OBSERVATION only, not formal commutativity teaching.
> ⚠️ **Scope Warning:** CONTEXT CONTINUITY: M9 maintains bags/boxes/circles from M7-M8 for any visual representations. Context variety (rows, stacks, teams) is M10's territory. This was a scope decision during the M9↔M10 reordering — when factor fluency moved before equations, context variety moved with equations.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS
### 1.4.1 #18: Skip Counting Without Meaning (PRIMARY)
**Trigger Behavior:** Student can count "5, 10, 15, 20" fluently when asked to "count by 5s" but doesn't recognize this as 4 × 5 = 20. When shown 4 groups of 5 and asked to multiply, they start over from scratch rather than recognizing they already have the tool. When shown the equation 4 × 5 = ?, they may compute correctly via skip-counting BUT can't explain WHY skip-counting works for multiplication.
**Why It Happens:** Skip-counting was taught as a COUNTING pattern in Grades 1-2, not as a multiplication concept. Modules 1-6 used skip-counting to read graph scales without explicitly naming it as multiplication. Even M7's bridge ("scales were multiplication") may not have fully connected for all students. The procedural skill (counting by Xs) and the conceptual structure (X groups of Y = multiplication) live in separate mental compartments.
**Why Critical in M9:** M9's PRIMARY computation strategy IS skip-counting. If students don't connect "count by 5s four times" to "4 × 5," then M9 becomes a rote exercise — students skip-count because they're told to, not because they understand it as a multiplication strategy. This creates brittle fluency that collapses when factor families change in later modules.
**Prevention Strategy:**
1. **Explicit bridge in every computation:** Guide narrates the connection: "7 groups of 2. Count by 2s seven times: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 14. So 7 × 2 = 14. Skip-counting BY 2s is how we MULTIPLY by 2."
2. **Graph callback threading:** "4 symbols at scale 5. You counted 5, 10, 15, 20. That WAS 4 × 5 = 20."
3. **Pattern discovery BEFORE computation:** Students see the full set of multiples (2, 4, 6, 8...) and discover patterns BEFORE being asked to skip-count to find specific products. This grounds skip-counting in the pattern, not just in procedure.
4. **Verbal mediation:** "How many groups? 7. What do we count by? 2s. Count 2s seven times." This mirrors M7-M8's two-question routine but adds the third step (strategy execution).
---
### 1.4.2 #20: Memorization Without Relationships (SECONDARY)
**Trigger Behavior:** Student can recite "3 × 4 = 12" but cannot use this fact flexibly — doesn't recognize that 12 ÷ 3 = 4, can't connect 3 × 4 to 4 × 3, can't use 3 × 4 = 12 to reason about 3 × 5. Facts are stored as isolated strings rather than as part of a connected network.
**Why It Happens:** Multiplication is often taught as memorization ("times tables") without structural understanding. Students who learn "3 × 4 = 12" as a fact-to-be-memorized don't build the conceptual web that connects it to equal groups, skip-counting patterns, and related facts.
**Why Relevant to M9:** M9 is the first module where students build toward automaticity. The risk is that fluency practice becomes pure memorization drill, disconnecting from the conceptual foundation built in M7-M8. If M9 doesn't explicitly maintain the pattern → strategy → fact connection, students build the wrong KIND of fluency.
**Prevention Strategy:**
1. **Pattern discovery precedes computation:** Students see and articulate WHY 2s are even, WHY 5s end in 0/5, WHY 10s end in 0 — BEFORE doing computation practice.
2. **Strategy narration in every worked example:** Guide always names the strategy alongside the answer: "6 × 5 = 30. I know because I counted by 5s six times. OR I know because 5s always end in 0 or 5, and 6 × 5 is the sixth multiple of 5."
3. **Multiple pathways validated:** Skip-counting, repeated addition, and "just knowing" are all presented as valid. Guide: "Smart mathematicians choose what works best for them."
4. **Connection questions:** Periodically ask students to relate facts: "You know 4 × 5 = 20. What about 5 × 5?" (builds from known facts, not isolated recall)
---
### 1.4.3 #21: Computation Replaces Structure (M9-specific)
**Trigger Behavior:** Student focuses entirely on FINDING THE ANSWER and stops attending to equal groups structure. When shown 4 × 5, they race to "20" without pausing to consider what 4 × 5 MEANS. When asked "what does 4 × 5 represent?", they say "20" rather than "4 groups of 5."
**Why It Happens:** The introduction of computation (finding products) can overwhelm the structural understanding built in M7-M8. Students shift from "what does this MEAN?" (M7-M8 focus) to "what does this EQUAL?" (M9 focus) and lose the meaning in the process.
**Why Critical in M9:** If structure understanding is lost, students can't reason about multiplication flexibly. They'll struggle in M10 when unknown factor problems REQUIRE structural understanding ("☐ groups of 5 = 20" only makes sense if you know multiplication MEANS groups of).
**Prevention Strategy:**
1. **Structure check before computation:** In worked examples, Guide asks "What does this mean?" before "What does this equal?" — "4 × 5. That means 4 groups of 5. Now, how do we find the total?"
2. **Graph callbacks maintain meaning:** "4 symbols at scale 5 means 4 groups of 5 items. That's 4 × 5 = 20." The context keeps the meaning alive.
3. **Occasional reverse tasks:** Even in M9, include 1-2 moments where student sees an equation and identifies what it REPRESENTS (not just the numerical answer).

## 1.7 LESSON (\~10-12 minutes)
### Core Purpose
Students learn that skip-counting IS multiplication — that counting by 2s, 5s, or 10s is the strategy for COMPUTING products for these factor families. They discover predictable patterns in products (2s are always even, 5s end in 0 or 5, 10s end in 0), then use skip-counting to build complete EQUATIONS (factor × factor = product) with the Equation Builder. By the end of the lesson, students can independently compute products for 2s, 5s, and 10s using skip-counting and build equations using tiles.
M9 does NOT teach new conceptual structure — equal groups and expressions are M7-M8 territory. M9 teaches COMPUTATION: expressions become equations, and skip-counting is the strategy that gets you from one to the other.
---
### Pedagogical Flow
M9 is a COMPUTATION module that builds on two prior modules of structural and notational work. Students arrive from the Warmup having built an expression (W.1) and watched 5 circles accumulate with synced skip-counting (W.2). The Lesson extends that accumulation, names the pattern, bridges skip-counting to multiplication, then teaches equation building. "You know how to write 5 × 2. Now let's figure out what 5 × 2 EQUALS."

### Interaction 1.2: Pattern Discovery — 2s Are Always Even \[CONCRETE — EXPLICIT NAMING\]

### Misconception Prevention
**#18 Skip Counting Without Meaning (PRIMARY):**
- Animated accumulation (W.2 + 1.1) makes skip-counting VISIBLE as group-building — each circle adds a group, each count adds to the product.
- Bridge explicitly named in 1.3: "That skip-counting WAS multiplication."

- Graph callback (3.2): "Reading graph scales WAS multiplication." (Uses ×10 — scale of 10 callback.)

**#20 Memorization Without Relationships (SECONDARY):**

### 1.7.5 Success Criteria
**The One Thing:** Students understand that skip-counting IS multiplication — counting by 2s, 5s, or 10s is how you COMPUTE products for these factor families. They can use patterns (2s are even, 5s end in 0/5, 10s end in 0) to CHECK their work, and they can build complete equations using the Equation Builder.
**Ready for Module 10:** Students who complete M9 can:
- Compute products for × 2, × 5, and × 10 reliably using skip-counting
- Build complete equations: `factor × factor = product`
- Identify patterns in products (2s even, 5s end in 0/5, 10s end in 0)
- Use "equation," "product," "factor," and "multiples" with precision
- Connect multiplication to graph scale reading from M1-M6
- Read equations in standard form: "5 times 2 equals 10"
**What M10 adds:** Equations with UNKNOWNS (☐ × 5 = 20), = sign flexibility (12 = 3 × 4) with conceptual explanation ("= means same value as"), and context variety (rows, stacks, teams). M9's fluency reduces cognitive load so M10 can focus on these new demands.

- **Guide:** "Look what happened. Same graph. Same number of symbols. But when we changed the scale, the multiplication changed — the product got bigger. That's because we were multiplying by bigger numbers."

**Task S.2: Pattern Summary as Checking Toolkit (Type A — Pattern Discovery / Generalization)**

The three strips displayed together let students see the patterns as a unified toolkit.

**Task S.3: Strategy Comparison (Type B — Progressive / Metacognitive)**
- **Purpose:** Students see three representations of the same problem and reflect on which approach works for them. This is connection-making between representations, not preference polling.
- **Visual:** Three-panel display showing 6 × 5 = 30 solved three ways:
	- Left: Context Visualization — 6 groups of 5 dots
	- Center: Skip-counting display: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30 (six counts highlighted)

- **Guide:** "Which way made more sense to you today?"
- **Prompt:** "Which strategy helped you more?"
- **Method:** MC (three options: Looking at the groups / Skip-counting / Repeated addition)

- **On "Skip-counting":** "Skip-counting is fast when you know the counts. That'll keep being useful."
- **On "Repeated addition":** "Adding lets you see each group. As you add, you're also counting by 5s. That's a solid strategy too."
**Design Note:** This is a Type 3 metacognitive reflection (Tool/Approach Preference) per the playbook. All three responses are validated — no "right" answer. The three-panel display shows strategies as parallel representations progressing from concrete (visual groups) to efficient (skip-counting). Per SME feedback, the visual option completes the representational chain from M7–M8 and the Guide response for that option explicitly bridges it to skip-counting: "counting those groups by 5s IS skip-counting." This reinforces M9's core message that the strategies are connected, not competing. No identity labels ("you're a skip-counter"). Observable only: "which helped you" not "which do you understand better."
**Voice compliance:** No assumed internal states. All responses reference what the strategy DOES, not what the student thinks or feels.

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS
### KDD 1: 5s Addition to Curriculum
**Rationale:** Module 9's original learning goals (L14, L15) covered only 2s and 10s. The curriculum addition of 5s (Departure #3, documented in §1.1 Learning Goals) maintains Unit 1's thematic coherence: 2s ↔ 1:2 scale, 5s ↔ 1:5 scale, 10s ↔ 1:10 scale/place value. Without 5s, students lose the graph-to-multiplication connection for the scale they used most extensively (M3-M4). Additionally, 5s leverage Grade 2 skip-counting fluency and the hand reference (5 fingers), making them a natural bridge between 2s and 10s. **Impact on Module Design:** Lesson Section 2 expanded to include 5s discovery and computation (Interaction 2.3-2.4). Exit Check includes ×5 problem (EC.1). Vocabulary introduced in same section as 2s (allows compression). This addition was made without extending module time — compression achieved via animated accumulation compression (2s full 10-circle animation vs. 5s 6-circle animation) and removing a secondary 2s computation task.
---
### KDD 2: Products Strip as Display Element (Not Interactive Toy)
**Rationale:** Pattern discovery requires students to see products visually grouped. Earlier versions used Multiplication Tables toy or individual MC tasks. Products Strip (a lightweight display row of products with optional glow/highlight) serves the same pedagogical function with lower cognitive overhead: students observe the set, notice patterns, then move to Equation Builder for computation. This separates "What's the pattern?" (Products Strip observation) from "What's the product?" (Equation Builder computation). **Impact on Module Design:** Warmup W.2 and Lesson 1.1 use animated accumulation WITH synced Products Strip growth. Lesson 2.3 (5s) and 3.1 (10s) use Products Strip directly. Students see the full set before being asked to compute from it. This removes the cognitive load of MC-for-every-product and reserves MC for the Equation Builder's expanded templates.
---
### KDD 3: Context Visualization Role Reduction
**Rationale:** M8 used Context Visualizations (equal groups visuals) as the primary teaching tool — students built expressions using the visual. M9 reduces the role: Context Visualizations appear for Warmup bridge (M8 callback), early worked examples (2.1), and remediation, but the primary interactive tool shifts to Equation Builder. This is intentional scaffolding removal: early interactions pair visual + Equation Builder (2.1-2.2), mid interactions compress the visual (2.3 Part A only, then tile computation), late interactions drop the visual entirely (2.4, 3.4). **Impact on Module Design:** Students don't build equal groups visuals in M9 — that's M8's work. By Exit Check, students compute without visual support. This prepares them for M10's unknowns and context variety (which require less reliance on visual ground and more on equation structure understanding).
---
### KDD 4: Factor Order Constraint (Second Factor Always 2, 5, or 10)
**Rationale:** M9 constrains second factor to \{2, 5, 10\} to preserve "groups OF skip-counting-unit" language from M7-M8 and to match graph scale reading ("4 symbols at scale 5" = 4 × 5). This means 7 × 2 (not 2 × 7). This avoids commutativity confusion that would muddy factor-order understanding still being built. M12 teaches commutativity formally. **Impact on Module Design:** Equation Builder templates always show second factor as the skip-counting unit. Prompts use "X groups of Y" language. Exit Check validates responses strictly: 10 × 4 is marked incorrect (not as an error, but as pedagogically out-of-scope). Late M9 activities may show turn-around facts observationally (7 × 2 AND 2 × 7 both equal 14) but this is preview only, not instruction.
---
### KDD 5: Remediation → Pipeline (No Authored Dialogue)
**Rationale:** M9's remediation guidance is brief and structural (e.g., "Skip-count by 2s X times" or "First factor is groups, second is in-group size"). All authored Light/Medium/Heavy remediation dialogue has been replaced with "Pipeline" to allow the Remediation Pipeline tool to infer context-specific hints based on the student's error pattern. This prevents over-scaffolding and ensures hints scale with intervention intensity. **Impact on Module Design:** All "Remediation (Light):" blocks in interactions show: \* **Remediation:** Pipeline. The Interaction block itself contains no conditional logic ("If student chose B, then...") — pipeline infers this. Guide dialogue remains complete; only the remediation system is automated.
---
### KDD 6: Discovery Compression (Animated Accumulation → Tile Computation → Products Strip Only)
**Rationale:** M9 teaches pattern discovery three times (2s, 5s, 10s). To avoid fatigue and respect time constraints, discovery gets progressively compressed: 2s get full animation (Warmup 5 circles + Lesson 5 more = 10 total), 5s get brief animation (6 circles), 10s get Products Strip only (no animation). This mirrors students' growing familiarity with the format and their readiness for abstraction. **Impact on Module Design:** Warmup and Lesson 1.1 feel more like "introduction," Lesson 2.3A feels like "review," Lesson 3.1 feels like "efficient pattern statement." Students who struggle with compression still see each pattern; the scaffold just reduces over time rather than repeating fully.
---
### KDD 7: Graph Callback Concentration at ×10
**Rationale:** M9 makes the "graph scales = multiplication" bridge explicit in three moments: Lesson 1.3 (general bridge, no specific family), Lesson 3.2 (explicit ×10 callback with Picture Graph), and Synthesis S.1 (all three scales accumulated). The ×10 callback is positioned there because 10s pattern (end in 0) most directly connects to place value and M1-M6's 1:10 scale. **Impact on Module Design:** Lesson 3.2 uses ×10, not ×2 or ×5, for the split-screen graph-equation comparison. This makes the newest family (10s) the focus of the deepest connection. Synthesis then generalizes to all three families. This prevents graph callbacks from feeling like tangents and instead positions them as Unit 1's capstone.
---
### KDD 8: Exit Check Structure — All Three Families, Mixed Difficulty
**Rationale:** EC tests all three families (EC.1 ×5 MC, EC.2 ×2 tile, EC.3 ×10 tile) to ensure students didn't over-rehearse one family during Practice. EC.1 uses MC (simplest method) with ×5 (medium family), EC.2 uses tiles with ×2 (most-practiced family) AND visual extraction, EC.3 uses tiles with ×10 (simplest pattern but no visual support). This balances difficulty across three problems while testing method variety. **Impact on Module Design:** EC does NOT use all MC or all tiles. EC does NOT test only one family or exclude any family. EC values span Lesson values but don't duplicate (Lesson used 4×2, 5×2, 8×2; EC uses 6×2 to test fresh computation).
---
### KDD 9: Vocabulary Staging — One Term per Section
**Rationale:** M9 introduces four formal terms: multiples (Section 1), product + equation + factor (Section 2, together in one worked example). Staggering vocabulary over Sections 1-2 allows students to anchor each term to visual moments: "multiples" to the Products Strip, "equation/product/factor" to the worked example visual. Section 3 uses all terms naturally without re-teaching. **Impact on Module Design:** No "vocabulary list" slide before Lesson. All vocabulary is embedded in interaction narration at the moment of first use. Students encounter each term in natural speech, not as memorization task.
---
### KDD 10: Lesson Section Progression — Concrete → Relational → Abstract → Application
**Rationale:** Lesson follows CRA progression deliberately: Section 1 is Concrete (animated accumulation, Products Strip), Section 1→2 bridge is Relational (repeated addition comparison, structure check), Section 2 is Abstract (vocabulary formalization, compressed 5s animation, tile computation), Section 3 is Application (10s pattern-to-computation progression, graph callback, maximum independence). This CRA architecture ensures conceptual grounding before abstraction. **Impact on Module Design:** Students don't jump directly to "compute the product" — they watch accumulation build products first. They don't just memorize vocabulary — they see it anchored to visuals. They don't immediately build equations independently — they see worked examples and attempt similar problems first.
