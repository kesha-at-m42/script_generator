## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**Students must understand that bar graphs with scale of 10 represent data where each unit on the axis equals 10, and they can use skip-counting by 10s to read and create these graphs, including values that fall between tick marks.**

**Critical Misconception:** Can't Interpolate Bar Heights (\#5) — Students read only marked values on scaled axes. When a bar falls between 30 and 40 on an axis scaled by 10s, they report "30" or "I don't know" instead of reading the halfway point as 35\.

**Success Indicator:** Student correctly creates a bar graph where 35 items \= bar ending between the 30 and 40 lines AND can explain "35 is between 30 and 40—halfway, because half of 10 is 5."

**Biggest Risk:** Students who can't read values between tick marks will struggle with M5's scale selection (comparing efficiency across scales) and M6's multi-step problem-solving (which requires precise value reading).

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

* Scale of 10: Each unit on the axis represents 10 items
* Skip-counting connection: "When scale is 10, count by 10s: 10, 20, 30, 40..."
* **Formal "axis" vocabulary:** "The numbers along the side are called the axis" (plural: axes)
* Bar graph CREATION with scale of 10 from data tables
* **Interpolation:** Reading/creating values BETWEEN axis lines (multiples of 5: 15, 25, 35, etc.)
* Half-of-10 principle: "Half of 10 \= 5\. Halfway between 30 and 40 is 35."
* **Visual-to-numeric bridge:** Explicit connection showing picture graph (1:10) and bar graph (1:10) represent same data (implementation TBD—may be animation, side-by-side, or other)
* "How many more/fewer" and "in all" problem-solving (reduced emphasis—same skill, bigger scale)
* Both horizontal and vertical orientations

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* Picture graph CREATION with scale of 10 (students observe picture graphs for comparison but don't create them)
* Scale of 2 or 5 (M2-M3; avoid mixing scales within M4)
* Scale selection/choosing between scales (Module 5\)
* Two-step problems as single submissions (M6 deep practice)
* Multiplication notation (Module 7+)
* Values that require reading beyond halfway points (e.g., 37 would be "between 35 and 40"—too complex)

⚠️ **BAR GRAPH PRIMARY PRINCIPLE:** M4 establishes bar graphs as the primary format for scaled data. Picture graphs appear ONLY for comparison purposes to show the visual-to-numeric bridge. Students do NOT create picture graphs in M4.

⚠️ **INTERPOLATION INTRODUCTION PRINCIPLE:** M4 introduces reading values between tick marks for the first time. Keep interpolation to HALFWAY points only (multiples of 5 between multiples of 10). Do not require students to estimate values like "about 37" or "between 35 and 40."

⚠️ **HALF-SYMBOL BRIDGE:** Connect interpolation to M2's half-symbol concept functionally: "Half of 10 \= 5." This is clean math, like "half of 2 \= 1" was in M2. Brief connection, not heavy reflection.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Can't Interpolate Bar Heights (PRIMARY) — Misconception \#5**

**Trigger Behavior:** When a bar falls between 30 and 40 on an axis scaled by 10s, student reports "30" or "I don't know" instead of reading 35\. Student reads only values at the lines, unable to determine values between them.

**Why It Happens:**

* M2-M3 used values only at axis marks (no interpolation required)
* Natural tendency to read only labeled numbers
* "Halfway" concept not yet connected to graph reading
* May not realize bars can represent values not shown on axis

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Explicit halfway teaching:** First interpolation task has full explanation: "This bar ends BETWEEN THE LINES—between 30 and 40\. What's halfway? Half of 10 is 5\. So 30 \+ 5 \= 35."
2. **Half-symbol bridge:** Brief callback: "Remember half-symbols? Same idea—half of 10 is 5."
3. **Visual gridlines:** Ghost gridlines at 5s (lighter) help students see halfway points
4. **Multiple practice:** Several tasks require reading/creating halfway values before Exit Check

**Remediation Approach:**

- **Light:** "Look where the bar ends. Is it at a line, or between two lines? What's halfway between those?"
- **Medium:** Highlight the two axis marks. "30... 40\. What's in the middle? Half of 10 is 5, so 30 \+ 5 \= ?"
- **Heavy:** Count up from lower mark: "Start at 30\. Count by 5s: 30... 35... 40\. Where does the bar stop?"

---

### **1.4.2 Counting Symbols Instead of Scaling (SECONDARY) — Misconception \#1**

**Trigger Behavior:** During transformation animation or when reading picture graphs, student counts symbols directly instead of using scale. Less common in M4 because bar graphs are primary, but may appear during transformation observation.

**Why It Happens:**

- Habit from earlier graph experiences
- Discrete symbols feel "countable" in a way bars don't
- May not attend to key/scale during transformation

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Transformation narration:** Guide explicitly counts by 10s during animation: "Watch—5 symbols, each worth 10\. That's 10, 20, 30, 40, 50."
2. **Immediate bar connection:** "The bar shows the same thing—50—without counting symbols."
3. **Limited picture graph exposure:** Students don't CREATE picture graphs, reducing symbol-counting practice

**Remediation Approach:**

* **Light:** "Check the key. Each symbol \= 10\. Count by 10s, not by 1s."

---

### **1.4.3 Graph as Picture (TERTIARY) — Misconception \#16**

**Trigger Behavior:** Student treats graph as literal drawing rather than data representation. May expect bar heights to visually "look like" what they represent.

**Why It Happens:**

* Transition from picture graphs (which DO "look like" the data) to bars (abstract)
* Continuous bars feel less connected to discrete items
* Young students may expect visual realism

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Transformation animation:** Explicitly shows picture → bar, reinforcing that bars REPRESENT the same data
2. **Guide language:** "The bar shows 50 votes. You can't SEE 50 things, but the HEIGHT tells you the amount."
3. **Consistent abstraction:** All M4 work uses bars, building comfort with abstract representation

**Remediation Approach:**

* **Light:** "The bar's HEIGHT shows the amount. Where does it end on the axis?"

## **1.5 TOY SPECIFICATIONS**

### **1.5.1 Data Collection Game (Warmup)**

**Purpose:** Generate personalized data that students will see transformed into a bar graph, creating ownership and engagement. M4 uses scale of 10 values (not M3's scale of 5).

#### **Core Specifications**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Interaction Type** | Animated counting activity (Minis appear, student counts) |
| **Categories** | 3-4 categories |
| **Target Value Ranges** | 20-60 items per category, ALL MULTIPLES OF 10 |
| **Context** | Counting Minis by type (colors, accessories, etc.) |
| **Student Action** | Count Minis in each category; input or confirm counts |
| **Validation** | Student's count checked by Guide reveal with correct values |
| **Duration** | \~60 seconds |

#### **M4-Specific Constraints**

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Generate ONLY multiples of 10 (20, 30, 40, 50, 60\) | Generate non-multiples (save interpolation for Lesson) |
| Use 3-4 categories | Use fewer than 3 or more than 4 |
| Keep totals in 20-60 range per category | Use values too small (would make bars hard to distinguish) |
| Use values appropriate for scale of 10 | Use M3's range (10-40 with multiples of 5\) |

**Warmup Flow:** Collected data → Visual comparison showing picture graph (1:10) and bar graph (1:10) represent same data → Bridge to Lesson. Student sees THEIR data in bar graph format.

### **Bar Selection Variety Rule**

When asking questions about specific bars:

1. **Vary position** — Don't always ask about the 1st or last category
2. **Vary value type** — Mix "at line" and "between line" bars across problems
3. **Avoid patterns** — If EC.1 asks about position 1, EC.2 should ask about position 2, 3, or 4

For Exit Check specifically:

- Each problem should target a DIFFERENT category position
- The bar's value type (at-line vs between) should match what's being tested

**Interaction W.1: Data Collection Game**

- **Purpose:** Generate personalized data with scale-10 values. Create investment through Minis context.
- **Hook (first 15-20 seconds):**
  - **Visual:** Minis characters appear in animated counting scenario.
  - **Guide:** "The Minis are collecting items\! Help count what they found."
- **Task:**
  - Student counts Minis/items across 3-4 categories
  - Results generated in multiples of 10 (20, 30, 40, 50, 60 range)
  - Values should show clear differences for later comparison
- **On Complete:**
  - **Guide:** "You counted \[results\]. That's a lot\! Let's see how to show this data."
- **Remediation (Light):** If count is off, Guide reveals correct count: "Let's check—that's \[X\]."
- **Duration:** \~60 seconds

---

**Interaction W.2: Scale Comparison (1:5 vs 1:10)**

- **Purpose:** Activate M3 knowledge while showing scale of 10 is even more efficient. Creates "aha" moment about efficiency progression.
- **Visual:** Picture Graphs (side-by-side display):
  - LEFT: Student's data as 1:5 vertical picture graph (each symbol \= 5), vertical
  - RIGHT: Same data as 1:10 vertical picture graph (each symbol \= 10), vertical
  - Keys visible on both: "Each ⭐ \= 5" and "Each ⭐ \= 10"
- **Guide:** "Here's your data two ways. Look at both graphs. Which graph uses FEWER symbols to show the same data?"
- **Prompt:** "Which graph uses FEWER symbols to show the same data?"
- **Student Action:** Select one of the two graphs
- **Correct Answer:** The 1:10 graph (right)
- **On Correct:** "Scale of 10 uses fewer symbols\! Each symbol shows more, so you need fewer of them."
- **On Incorrect:** "Look at the keys. Scale of 5 means each symbol shows 5\. Scale of 10 means each symbol shows 10\. Which needs fewer symbols for the same amount?"
- **Guide (efficiency reinforcement):** ~~"Scale of 2... scale of 5... now scale of 10\. Each time, fewer symbols for bigger numbers\!"~~ "With scale of 10, each symbol shows more than scale of 5\. That's why you need fewer symbols for the same data\!"
- **Duration:** 45-60 seconds

---

**Interaction W.3: Picture-to-Bar Bridge (1:10)**

- **Purpose:** Show that bar graphs display the same data as picture graphs. This is student's ONLY exposure to 1:10 picture graphs—they won't create them.
- **Visual:** Picture Graphs (Side-by-side display or brief visual comparison):
  - LEFT: 1:10 picture graph (from W.2)
  - RIGHT: 1:10 bar graph showing SAME data
  - Both vertical orientation
  - Bar graph shows axis with scale of 10 (0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60\)
- **Guide:** "Now watch this. Here's the SAME data as a bar graph."
- **Guide:** "This category has \[X\]. The picture graph shows \[X÷10\] symbols. The bar graph shows a bar up to \[X\].Both graphs show \[Y\] for \[second category\]. Can you find where?"
- **Prompt:** "Both graphs show \[Y\] for \[second category\]. Can you find where?"
- **Student Action:** Click on the bar OR the symbols for that category
- **Correct Answer:** Either correct location accepted
- **On Correct:** "Same data\! Picture graphs use symbols. Bar graphs use bars. Both show \[Y\]."
- **Guide:** "Bar graphs are really useful with scale of 10\. You read the HEIGHT instead of counting symbols."
- **Duration:** 45-60 seconds

**Note:** This is student's ONLY interaction with 1:10 picture graphs. They observe but do not create.

---

**Interaction W.4: Bridge to Lesson**

- **Purpose:** Create anticipation for Lesson without teaching. Frame the shift to bar graph CREATION.
- **Visual:** Bar graph from W.3 remains visible. Picture graph removed.
- **Guide:** "You've seen scale of 10 with picture graphs and bar graphs. Both show the same data."
- **Guide:** "Bar graphs are powerful—you can read exact amounts from the numbers on the side."
- **Bridge Statement:** "Ready to learn how to CREATE bar graphs with scale of 10? Let's go\!"
- **No student prompt** (transition to Lesson)
- **Duration:** 15-20 seconds

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Scale of 10 \+ Reading Bar Heights**

**Purpose:** Establish scale of 10 through skip-counting by 10s. Students read and create bar graphs with scale of 10 where all values are multiples of 10\. Build on Warmup's data collection. Guide uses "axis" naturally but it is not a teaching focus.

**Orientation:** All vertical

---

**Interaction 1.1: Reading Bar Heights \[WORKED EXAMPLE\]**

- **Vocabulary Introduced:** "axis," "height of the bar"
- **Visual: Bar Graphs (Mode 1: Reading).** Vertical. Bar graph from Warmup (student's collected data). 4 categories (colors: Red=40, Blue=30, Green=50, Yellow=20). Axis labeled 0–60 in 10s. All 4 bars pre-filled. Red bar highlighted with axis line at 40 glowing.
- **Guide:** "You saw this bar graph in Warmup. Now let's learn how to read it exactly."
- **Guide:** "See these numbers along the side? They show the
- scale—10, 20, 30, 40... That's counting by 10s\!"
- **Guide:** "Red has 40\. The HEIGHT OF THE BAR
- ends at the 40 line."
- **Guide:** "I can count by 10s to check: 10, 20, 30, 40\. You already know how to count by 10s\!"
- **No student prompt** (observation—skill introduction)
- **Vocabulary Introduced:** "height of the bar," "scale of 10"

---

**Interaction 1.2: Reading Bar Height**

- **Visual: Bar Graphs (Mode 1: Reading).** Vertical. Same bar graph as 1.1. Blue bar highlighted (height \= 30, at 30 axis line). Axis available for reference.
- **Guide:** "Look at Blue. The bar's HEIGHT ends at a line on the axis. What value does this show?"
- **Prompt:** "What value does this show?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: 3, 20, 30, 35\]
- **Correct Answer:** 30
- **Answer Rationale:**
  - 30 \= Correct (bar height at 30 line)
  - 3 \= Counted symbols from picture graph (Misconception \#1)
  - 20 \= Misread axis
  - 35 \= Between-the-lines error (not applicable here—bar IS at line)
- **On Correct:** "30. The bar ends at the 30 line. Same as 3 symbols on the picture graph—each symbol shows 10."
- **Remediation (Light):** "Find where the top of the bar lines up with the numbers."

---

**Interaction 1.3: Skip-Counting Connection**

- **Visual: Bar Graphs (Mode 1: Reading).** Vertical. Same bar graph. Green bar highlighted (height \= 50). Axis lines glow sequentially: 10, 20, 30, 40, 50\.

### **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**

- ❌ "Each symbol represents 2/5" (wrong scales—M4 is scale 10 only)
- ❌ "Multiply by 10" / "times 10" / "×" notation (multiplication is M7+)
- ❌ "Estimate the value" / "about \[X\]" (we teach exact halfway, not estimation)
- ❌ "Tick marks" (conflicts with Fractions unit—use "the lines" or "marks on the axis")
- ❌ "Scale of 2" / "Scale of 5" (avoid mixing; M4 uses scale of 10 only)
- ❌ "You're so smart\!" / "Perfect\!" / "Amazing\!" (identity labels, overpraise)
- ❌ "You thought carefully about that" (assumed internal state)

**\#5 Can't Interpolate Bar Heights (PRIMARY):**

- Ghost gridlines at 5s make halfway points visible
- Explicit "half of 10 \= 5" teaching in 2.1

- Remediation explicitly walks through the halfway calculation

**\#1 Counting Symbols Instead of Scaling (SECONDARY):**

- Picture graphs appear only for comparison (not creation)
- Guide explicitly narrates symbol-to-bar connection in 1.1
- All student creation is bar graphs (not picture graphs)

**\#16 Graph as Picture (TERTIARY):**

- Axis vocabulary makes abstract representation explicit

### **Task S.1: Scale Progression Pattern (Type A \- Pattern Discovery)**

- **Purpose:** Students recognize that scales follow a pattern (1 → 2 → 5 → 10\) and understand WHY larger scales are useful.
- **Visual: Bar Graphs (in a 2x2 grid orientation).** Four bar graphs showing SAME data (value of 20). All bars reach same physical height. Axis divisions differ:
  - Graph 1: Scale of 1 (axis: 0, 1, 2, 3... 20\) — 20 lines
  - Graph 2: Scale of 2 (axis: 0, 2, 4, 6... 20\) — 10 lines
  - Graph 3: Scale of 5 (axis: 0, 5, 10, 15, 20\) — 4 lines
  - Graph 4: Scale of 10 (axis: 0, 10, 20\) — 2 lines
- **Visual:** Four bar graphs side by side showing SAME data (value of 20):
  - All bars reach the SAME physical height (same amount shown)
  - Axis divisions differ (closer together for smaller scales, further apart for larger scales)
  - Graph 1: Scale of 1 (axis: 0, 1, 2, 3... 20\) — 20 lines
  - Graph 2: Scale of 2 (axis: 0, 2, 4, 6... 20\) — 10 lines
  - Graph 3: Scale of 5 (axis: 0, 5, 10, 15, 20\) — 4 lines
  - Graph 4: Scale of 10 (axis: 0, 10, 20\) — 2 lines
- **Guide:** "Look at all four graphs. They show the SAME amount—20. Which scale uses the fewest lines to show the same amount?"
- **Prompt:** "Which scale uses the fewest lines to show the same amount?"
- **Student Action:** Click on scale of 10 graph (rightmost)
- **On Correct:** "Scale of 10 uses the fewest lines. Each line shows more, so you need fewer of them."
- **Remediation (Light):** "Count the lines on each axis. Which has the fewest?"
- **Connection (Guide states after task):** "You've learned four scales now: 1, 2, 5, and 10\. Each one is useful for different sized numbers. Bigger numbers work better with bigger scales."

**Task S.2: Orientation Transfer (Type D \- Representation Transfer)**

- **Purpose:** Students recognize that vertical and horizontal graphs show the same data—the orientation changes but the math doesn't.
- **Visual: Bar Graphs (side-by-side comparison).** Same data shown two ways:
  - Left: Vertical bar graph (scale of 10, values include at least one between-lines value at 35\)
  - Right: Horizontal bar graph (same data, same scale)
  - One category with value 35 highlighted in both graphs
- **Guide:** "Here's the same data shown two ways—vertical and horizontal.Find the category with 35 in BOTH graphs."
- **Prompt:** "What category is 35 in BOTH graphs."
- **Student Action:** Click on the 35 bar in vertical graph, then in horizontal graph
- **On Correct:** "Same value, same position between the lines—just different directions."
- **Remediation (Light):** "Find 35 on the axis in each graph. Which bar ends there?"
- **Connection (Guide states after task):** "The axis works the same way whether bars go up or sideways. The math doesn't change—only the direction."

**Task S.3: Real-World Connection (Type C \- Real-World Bridge)**

- **Purpose:** Students connect scale of 10 to familiar contexts where counting by 10s is natural.
- **Visual:** Three real-world scenario images displayed:
  - A: Counting money (stacks of 10 dollar bills)
  - B: Measuring height (ruler marked in 10s of centimeters)
  - C: Counting items one by one
  - Multi-select interaction (A and B correct)
- **Guide:** "Scale of 10 is useful when numbers are bigger. Where might you see counting by 10s outside of math class?"
- **Prompt:** "Which situations would work well with a scale of 10?"
- **Student Action:** Select A and B (both involve counting by 10s)
- **On Correct:** "Money and measuring often use 10s. That's why scale of 10 feels natural for bigger amounts."
- **Remediation (Light):** "Which ones involve counting by 10s?"
- **Connection (Guide states after task):** "You already know how to count by 10s.. Bar graphs use the same skill you use with money and measuring."

**Task S.4: Metacognitive Reflection (Strategy Identification)**

- **Purpose:** Help students recognize the strategy they used for reading between-the-lines values.
- **Visual: Bar Graphs (Mode 1: Reading).** One bar graph with a single bar ending at 45 (between 40–50 lines). Bar highlighted. Ghost gridlines at 5s visible.
- **Guide:** "This bar shows 45—between the lines. How did you figure that out during the lesson?"
- **Prompt:** "What strategy helped you find values between the lines?”
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice:
  - "I picked the line closest to the top of the bar"
  - "I added the two numbers on the lines"
  - "I found halfway—that's always 5 more"
  - "I guessed"
- **Correct Answer:** "I used 'half of 10 is 5'" (or accept "I counted the spaces")
- **On Correct:** "That's the pattern\! Half of 10 is 5, so halfway between any two lines is 5 more than the lower line."
- **Connection (Guide states after task):** "Knowing that pattern means you can read ANY bar graph with scale of 10—even values between the lines. That's a powerful strategy."

- **Guide:** "That pattern-finding will help you when we look at ALL the scales together next time—you'll be able to pick the best one for any data."

**Key Design Decisions Summary:**

1. **Scale of 10 only** — No mixing with 2 or 5 within M4; scale comparison is M5
2. **Bar graph as primary format** — Picture graphs appear only for transformation animation
3. **"Axis" vocabulary formally introduced** — M1-M3 avoided this; M4 teaches it explicitly
4. **Interpolation introduced** — Multiples of 5 between multiples of 10 (halfway points only)
5. **Half-of-10 principle** — Clean math connection: "Half of 10 \= 5, so halfway between 30 and 40 is 35"
6. **Visual-to-numeric bridge is critical** — Research-backed (effect size 0.50-0.77); implementation TBD (animation, side-by-side, or other)
7. **Data Collection Game uses scale of 10 values** — Warmup is about M4 content, not M3 callback
8. **Values range 10-100** — Multiples of 10 (at axis lines) AND multiples of 5 (between lines)
9. **Orientation progression** — Vertical for Sections 1-2, Horizontal for Section 3
10. **Problem-solving reduced emphasis** — Same skill as M3, bigger scale
11. **"Between the lines" vocabulary** — Avoid "tick marks" (conflicts with Fractions unit number lines); use "the lines" or "marks on the axis"
12. **Question/test language included** — "height of the bar," "between the lines," "what value does this show?"
