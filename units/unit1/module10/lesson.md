## 1.7 LESSON (10-14 minutes)
---
### **Pedagogical Flow**
M10 is an EQUATION module that extends M8's notation and M9's computation into complete mathematical statements with unknowns. Students arrive from the Warmup having built an equation (callback to M9) and heard a preview of what's coming. The Lesson teaches three layered concepts in deliberate sequence: equation structure with \\= sign meaning, context variety with product unknowns, and unknown factor positions with language-first scaffolding.
Unlike M9 (which introduced one new demand: computation), M10 introduces three new demands. Each layers onto the prior — the sequence cannot be compressed or reordered without creating cognitive overload. M9's fluency foundation is the load-reduction mechanism: students don't have to think about computing 4 × 5 when they're learning what ☐ × 5 \\= 20 means.
**CRA Sequence for M10:**
| CRA Phase | M10 Application |
| --- | --- |
| **Concrete** | Section 1: Bags/boxes (LAST TIME). Quick equation build (prior skill activation) → \\= sign meaning taught explicitly via worked example (1.2) → both orientations shown → guided and independent practice. Familiar context keeps cognitive load on \\= sign concept, not context or equation construction. |
| **Relational** | Section 2: Context variety (rows, stacks, groups). Explicit announcement → worked example with new context → product unknowns introduced → structure language connects new contexts to familiar pattern. |
| **Application** | Section 3: Unknown factor positions. Language-first scaffolding ("\\*\\*\\_ groups of 4 equals 12 total") → symbolic equation (☐ × 4 \\= 12). Three activity types (Visual → Equation, Specification → Equation, Expression → Construction). Increasing independence. |
**Key Progression:**
- Toy usage: Equal Groups with Pictures (bags → variety) \\+ Equation Builder (full equation tiles → pre-filled with unknowns)
- Context progression: Bags/boxes (1.1-1.5) → Rows/stacks/groups (2.1-2.4) → Full variety (3.1-3.4)
- Unknown progression: None/all given (1.1-1.5) → Product only (2.2-2.4) → Factor positions (3.1-3.4)
- Orientation: Standard introduced (1.1) → Both shown (1.2) → Both practiced (1.3-1.4) → Mixed throughout (2.1+)
- Scaffolding: Full worked example → Partial → Guided → Independent
- Method progression: Observation → Methods C/D (tiles) → MC for specification tasks

### **Required Phrases (Must Appear in Script)**
- "Same value as" (= sign meaning — must appear multiple times across sections)
- "equation" (formal vocabulary — deepened from M9's functional use)
- "equal sign" or "equals sign" (named explicitly)
- "unknown" or "missing number" (named in Section 3\\)
- "\\*\\*\\_ groups of \\*\\*\\_" (structural anchor language — every context, every section. Context-specific terms like "rows" or "stacks" are always bridged back to "groups of")
- "Same equal groups pattern" (variety connection — Section 2+)
- "What number makes this equation true?" (unknown framing — Section 3\\)
### **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**
- ❌ "The answer is..." when referring to the product position (reinforces \\= as "answer comes next" — \\#19)
- ❌ "Solve for..." (algebraic framing — students use known facts, not algebraic reasoning)
- ❌ "It doesn't matter which order" for factors (commutativity is M12)
- ❌ "3 × 4 is the same as 4 × 3" (commutativity is M12)
- ❌ "expression" when referring to a complete equation (3 × 4 \\= 12 is an equation, not an expression)
- ❌ "array" or "rows and columns" (M11 vocabulary)
- ❌ "divide" or "division" (later unit)
---
### **Misconception Prevention**
**\\#19 Equal Sign as "Put Answer Here" (PRIMARY):**
- \\= sign meaning taught explicitly in 1.2 with concrete demonstration
- Both orientations shown from 1.2 onward (prevents standard-form-only exposure)
- Guide consistently says "same value as" — never "the answer is 12"
- Reversed orientation (12 \\= 3 × 4\\) used in every section
**\\#14 Unknown Must Be Last (PRIMARY):**
- Product unknown introduced first (2.2-2.4) — familiar position, low risk
- Factor unknowns introduced with language scaffolding (3.1-3.3)
- Both orientations maintained for unknown tasks (☐ × 4 \\= 12 AND 12 \\= ☐ × 4\\)
- Exit Check tests unknowns in all positions
**\\#10 Groups vs. Size Confusion (SECONDARY):**
- Structure language maintained across ALL contexts ("\\*\\*\\_ groups/rows/stacks of \\*\\*\\_")
- Connection lines: organizational units → first factor, items per unit → second factor
- Context transition explicitly narrated: "Rows work just like bags. Each row IS a group."
- Reversal distractor included in MC options where applicable

## 1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Equation Structure \\+ \\= Sign Meaning\*\*
**Purpose:** Establish the complete equation format (factor × factor \\= product) with explicit teaching of what \\= means. Students learn that \\= means "same value as" — not "the answer comes next" — and that equations can be written in both orientations (3 × 4 \\= 12 AND 12 \\= 3 × 4). Uses bags/boxes contexts (LAST TIME) for continuity so cognitive load falls on equation structure, not new contexts.
**Toys:** Equal Groups with Pictures (bags/boxes — concrete level) \\+ Equation Builder (Mode 4: full equation with \\= tile)
### Interaction 1.1: Quick Equation Build — Setup for \\= Sign Teaching
**Type B — Activate Prior Skill**
- **Purpose:** Quick equation-building task using a prior skill (M9) to generate the equation that 1.2 will flip and teach from. NOT a worked example — students own this skill. The Guide's language signals that something new is coming ("Let's look more closely at that equals sign").
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 3 bags, each containing 4 apples. Bags clearly separated. Equation Builder with template `[___] × [___] = [___]`. Tile palette: `2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 10, 12, 14`.
- **Guide:** "You know how to do this. Build the equation shown by the picture."
- **Prompt:** "Build the equation."
- **Student Action:** Equation Builder Methods C/D (all three slots)
- **Correct Answer:** `3 × 4 = 12`
- **On Correct:** "3 groups of 4\\. 3 times 4 equals 12\\. You've got equation building down. But now — let's look more closely at that equals sign."
- **Visual on Correct:** Equation remains on screen. The \\= sign subtly highlights, priming 1.2.
**Design Note:** This is NOT a worked example — it's a quick activation of a prior skill. Students have built equations in M9 and just built one in the Warmup. The Guide's on-correct dialogue deliberately pivots forward: "But now — let's look more closely at that equals sign." This frames the \\= sign teaching in 1.2 as the module's real new content, not equation construction. The heavy remediation note flags a potential readiness concern: if a student can't build a basic equation here, Section 2-3 demands will compound.
---
### Interaction 1.2: \\= Sign Teaching \\+ Reversed Orientation \\\[WORKED EXAMPLE continuation\\\]
- **Purpose:** Explicitly teach what \\= means ("same value as") and immediately show the reversed orientation. This is the critical \\#19 prevention moment.
- **Visual:** Same equation on screen: `3 × 4 = 12`. Guide highlights the \\= sign.
- **Guide:** "See this sign right here? The equals sign. Here's something important about it."
- **Visual:** \\= sign glows/pulses.
- **Guide:** "It means SAME VALUE AS. 3 times 4 has the same value as 12\\. Not 'the answer is.Equals means same value."
- **Guide:** "And since both sides have the same value... we can flip them around."
- **Visual:** Animation — equation rearranges to `12 = 3 × 4`. Both equations visible briefly (original fades, reversed stays).
- **Guide:** "12 equals 3 times 4\\. Same equation. Same value on both sides. Just written differently."
- **Visual:** Side-by-side display: `3 × 4 = 12` AND `12 = 3 × 4`. Both remain visible.
- **Guide:** "Both of these are correct. The equals sign works both ways — because it means same value as."
- Visual: New Equal Groups with Pictures appears: 6 boxes of 7 crayons. Equation Builder resets with template `[___] × [___] = [___]`. Tile palette: `5, 6, 7, 8, 13, 36, 40, 42, 48, 49`.
- Guide: "Your turn. Build the equation for these boxes."
- Prompt: "Build the equation for these boxes."
- Correct: `6 × 7 = 42`
- On Correct: "6 times 7 equals 42\\. Six boxes, 7 in each, same value as 42."
- Visual on Correct: After confirmation, reversed version appears below: `42 = 6 × 7`. Guide: "And we can also write it this way. Same value, both sides."
**Design Note:** This is the pivotal teaching moment of the entire module. The \\= sign concept is stated three times in different ways ("same value as," "not the answer comes next," "works both ways"). The reversed orientation is shown IMMEDIATELY — not in a separate interaction later. Then the student's first action includes seeing the reversed form of their own equation. Every subsequent interaction reinforces this.
- Visual: Equal Groups with Pictures: 5 bags, 9 stars in each. Equation Builder with template `[___] × [___] = [___]`. Tile palette: `3, 5, 9, 14, 36, 40, 45, 50, 54, 63`.
- Guide: "Same idea. Count the bags, count what's inside, figure out the product. Build the equation."
- Prompt: "Build the equation: \\*\\*\\_ × \\*\\*\\_ \\= \\*\\*\\_"
- Correct: `5 × 9 = 45`
- On Correct: "5 times 9 equals 45\\. Five bags, 9 in each, same value as 45."
- Visual on Correct: Reversed version appears: `45 = 5 × 9`. Guide: "Both ways work."
**Design Note:** Fading begins. Guide gives brief instruction ("Same idea... Build the equation") rather than full narration. Student has seen two full models (1.1 worked example \\+ 1.2 first attempt). Structure language compressed to interaction-level teaching.
---
### Interaction 1.4: Guided Practice — Reversed Form
- **Purpose:** Student builds an equation in REVERSED orientation for the first time. This tests whether they internalized "same value as" — not just "number × number \\= number."
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 3 boxes, 8 cookies in each. Equation Builder with REVERSED template: `[___] = [___] × [___]`. Tile palette: 3, 4, `6`, `7`, 8, 11, 16, 20, 21, 24\\.
- **Guide:** "Here's something different. The template is flipped — product comes FIRST this time. Remember, equals means same value as, so the product can go on either side. Build this equation with the product first."
- **Prompt:** "Build the equation: \\*\\*\\_ \\= \\*\\*\\_ × \\*\\*\\_"
- **Correct Answer:** `24 = 3 × 8`
- **On Correct:** "24 equals 3 times 8\\. Product on the left, multiplication expression on the right. Same value on both sides."
**Remediation:**
- **Light:** "Find the product by skip counting and put it on the left side of equals where there is only one slot."
- **Medium:** Connection lines: product→ left slot, boxes→ right side first slot, items → right side second slot,. "Boxes are groups. Items are per group. Count or skip-count for the total."
- **Heavy:** Guide models with highlighting: "I see 3 boxes. That's 3 groups. Each box has 8 cookies. So 3 groups of 8 is 3 times 8\\. The total? Skip-count by 8s: 8, 16, 24\\. 24 equals 3 times 8."
**Design Note:** First time student constructs in reversed form. The template itself scaffolds — `[___] = [___] × [___]` makes the reversed structure explicit. Guide explicitly connects to \\= sign teaching: "equals means same value as, so the product can go on either side." This prevents the reversed form from feeling like a "trick" or exception.
---
### Interaction 1.5: Independent Practice — Standard or Reversed
- **Purpose:** Student builds an equation independently with minimal support.
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 4 bags, 6 marbles in each. Equation Builder with template `[___] × [___] = [___]`. Tile palette: `3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 10, 16, 18, 24, 28`.
- **Guide:** "One more with bags. Build the equation."
- **Prompt:** "Build the equation that matches the picture."
- **Correct Answer:** `4 × 6 = 24`
- **On Correct:** "4 times 6 equals 24\\. Four bags, 6 in each, same value as 24."
- **Visual on Correct:** Reversed version appears: `24 = 4 × 6`. Guide: "And we can write it this way too — same value."
**Design Note:** Last bags/boxes interaction in the entire curriculum. Standard form assigned (reversed was just practiced in 1.4). Guide shows reversed version on correct to maintain dual-orientation exposure without requiring a template toggle. This also serves as a readiness check: if the student struggles here, Section 2's added complexity (variety \\+ unknowns) will compound the difficulty.

## 1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Context Variety \\+ Product Unknowns\*\*
**Purpose:** Explicitly introduce non-bag/box equal groups contexts (rows, stacks, groups) and first unknown position (product). Students learn that equal groups appear in many real-world forms. "\\*\\*\\_ groups of \\*\\*\\_" remains the structural anchor; context-specific terms (rows, stacks, etc.) are used descriptively, then bridged back: "\\\[context term\\\] → that's \\\[X\\\] groups of \\\[Y\\\]." Product unknowns (3 × 5 \\= ☐) are the simplest unknown type — students count or skip-count to find the total.
**Transition from Section 1:**
- **Guide:** "You built equations with bags and boxes — and you know that the equals sign means same value as. Now, check this out."
---
### Interaction 2.1: Context Variety Announcement \\+ Worked Example \\\[WORKED EXAMPLE\\\]
- **Purpose:** Explicitly announce the shift to new contexts AND model building an equation from a non-bag/box visual. This is the critical variety transition — it must be named, not slipped in.
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 3 rows of 9 books on shelves. Books arranged in clear horizontal rows. Equation Builder with template `[___] × [___] = [___]`. Tile palette: `3`, `4, 8, 9, 10, 12, 24, 27, 30, 36`.
- **Guide:** "Equal groups don't just come in bags and boxes. They're everywhere. Rows of books. Stacks of plates. Groups of animals. The math works the same way."
- **Guide:** "Look at these shelves. I see rows of books. Each row is a group — just like each bag was a group."
- **Visual:** Rows highlight sequentially.
- **Guide:** "1, 2, 3\\. Three rows. That's 3 groups. And in each row?"
- **Visual:** Items in first row highlight.
- **Guide:** "1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9\\. Nine books. So I've got 3 rows of 9\\. Same pattern: groups of items."
- **Guide:** "3 times 9\\. And the product? Skip-count by 9s: 9, 18, 27."
- **Visual:** Guide demonstration — tiles build: `3 × 9 = 27`
- **Guide:** "3 times 9 equals 27\\. Same equation-building, different picture."
- **Visual:** Reversed form appears: `27 = 3 × 9`. Guide: "Both ways — same value."
- **No student action** (observation — worked example)
**Design Note:** The variety announcement is explicit and warm: "Equal groups don't just come in bags and boxes. They're everywhere." Guide narrates the connection between rows and groups ("Each row is a group — just like each bag was a group"). Structure language maintained: "3 rows of 9." The worked example uses the same process students just practiced (count groups, count items, build equation), showing that nothing changed except the picture.
---
### Interaction 2.2: Equal vs. Unequal Groups — Identification \\\[CONCEPTUAL CHECK\\\]
- **Purpose:** Teach that not every grouping of objects represents equal groups — and only equal groups can be described with multiplication. Students evaluate four visual arrangements and select which ones show equal groups. This surfaces the "equal" criterion explicitly, preventing students from writing multiplication equations for any collection of items that happen to be clustered.
- **Visual:** Four images displayed in a 2×2 grid, labeled A-D:
	- A: 3 shelves with 6 jars on each shelf (equal — 3 × 6\\)
	- B: 4 plates with different numbers of cookies: 5, 6, 5, 2 (unequal — same containers, different amounts)
	- C: 2 equal stacks of 9 books (equal — 2 × 9\\)
	- D: 3 groups of animals: 7 ducks, 4 ducks, 7 ducks (unequal — similar visual pattern but middle group differs)
- **Guide:** "Equal groups are everywhere — but not EVERY group of things is an equal group. Look at these four pictures. Each picture has 18 items in total. Which ones show equal groups — the same number in every group?"
- **Prompt:** "Select ALL the pictures that show equal groups."
- **Student Action:** Multi-select (select all that apply: A, B, C, D)
- **Correct Answer:** A and C
- **On Correct (both A and C selected):**
	- **Guide:** "A has 3 shelves with 6 on each — equal groups. C has 2 stacks of 9 — equal groups. We can write multiplication equations for these. B and D? The groups aren't equal — different amounts in different groups. We can't write a multiplication equation for those."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Partial correct (selected A/C plus B or D): highlight the incorrectly added image group by group with counts — ask if every group has the same number. Missed A or C: direct student to count items in every group in each picture; equal groups means every group has the same count. Distractor B uses identical containers with unequal contents; Distractor D uses a near-miss 7/4/7 pattern.
**Design Note:** This is the explicit instructional moment for equal vs. unequal groups. Placed after the variety announcement (2.1) and before the first product-unknown task (2.3), it establishes the evaluation criterion students need before building equations from new contexts. Distractor B uses identical containers with unequal contents — testing whether students check the amounts, not just the containers. Distractor D uses a near-miss pattern (7, 4, 7\\) that could be mistaken for equal at a glance. Multi-select rather than binary avoids the lab-testing issue flagged for W.2. This is a recognition/judgment task, not a construction task — students evaluate representations rather than building them.
---
### Interaction 2.3: Product Unknown — Standard Form
- **Purpose:** First unknown encounter. Product position is the simplest — students already know how to find the total. The new element is the ☐ in the equation.
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 8 stacks of 4 plates. Plates clearly stacked. Equation Builder with pre-filled equation: `8 × 4 = ☐`. The ☐ is visible in the product slot. Palette with answer tiles: `28, 30, 32, 36`.
- **Guide:** "8 stacks of 4 plates. These are equal groups so we can write a multiplication equation to represent the picture. The expression part is done — 8 times 4\\. But see that box where the product goes? That's the unknown — the number we need to figure out. Find the total and place it."
- **Prompt:** "8 × 4 \\= ☐. Find the product. Place the number."
- **Correct Answer:** `32`
- **On Correct:** "8 times 4 equals 32\\. You found the unknown — 32 is the product. 8 stacks, 4 in each, same value as 32."
**Design Note:** First unknown is highly scaffolded — the expression is pre-filled, student only places the product. This isolates the "unknown" concept from the equation-building skill. Guide explicitly names the ☐: "That's the unknown. The number we need to figure out." This is the formal introduction of "unknown" vocabulary per §1.3 (after visual experience with the concept).
### Interaction 2.4: Product Unknown — Reversed Form
- Visual: Equal Groups with Pictures: 5 clusters of 7 sheep in a field — sheep grouped by natural spatial proximity, NOT circled or contained. Clear spatial gaps between clusters define the groups. Equation Builder with REVERSED template, pre-filled: `☐ = 5 × 7`. Palette: `12, 30, 35, 42`.
- Guide: "Look at this field. See the sheep? They're grouped together equally — 5 groups, 7 in each. The equation is flipped — unknown first this time. But equals still means same value as. Figure out the product and place it."
- Prompt: "☐ \\= 5 × 7\\. What is the product?"
- Correct: `35`
- On Correct: "35 equals 5 times 7\\. The unknown was 35 — same value on both sides."
**Design Note:** First context using natural spatial clustering WITH equal groups. Students just evaluated spatial clustering in 2.2 (distractor D: unequal animal clusters) and correctly identified it as NOT equal groups. Now they encounter spatial clustering that IS equal — reinforcing that clustering alone doesn't determine equal groups; you have to check the counts. Sheep in a field group by proximity — no circles, no bags, no shelves. Click-to-highlight-group interaction is especially valuable here — tapping a cluster confirms the grouping the student perceived.

### Interaction 2.5: Independent — New Context, Product Unknown
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 2 shelves with 8 jars each. Equation Builder with pre-filled equation: `2 × 8 = ☐`. Palette: `10, 14, 16, 18`.
- **Guide:** "New context, same pattern of equal groups. 2 shelves of 8 jars. Find the unknown — the product."
- **Prompt:** "2 × 8 \\= ☐. Find the product."
- **Correct Answer:** `16`
- **On Correct:** "2 times 8 equals 16\\. Two shelves, 8 in each, 16 total."
- **Visual on Correct:** Reversed version appears: `16 = 2 × 8`. Guide: "Same value — both ways."
**Design Note:** Non-fluency-family factors (×8) — Section 2 is a natural place to build comfort with broader multiples since the factors are pre-filled and the visual provides counting support. Fluency families (2s, 5s, 10s) are reserved for Section 3's unknown-factor tasks where recall is required. Minimal Guide narration; student is expected to succeed independently.

## 1.7.3 LESSON SECTION 3: Unknown Factor Positions\*\*
**Purpose:** Unknowns move from the product position to FACTOR positions. This is the module's most challenging demand — students must reason BACKWARD from a known product to find a missing factor. The two factor positions require different strategies: first-factor unknown (☐ × 5 \\= 20\\) can be solved by skip-counting by the known second factor and tracking counts — a direct M9 extension. Second-factor unknown (2 × ☐ \\= 10\\) requires recalling which fact from the known factor's family produces the target product. First-factor is taught first because the strategy is more intuitive. Language-based prompts ("\\*\\*\\_ groups of 5 equals 20 total") scaffold both positions. Three activity types from §1.5.1 are used: Type 1 (Visual → Equation), Type 2 (Construction → Equation), and Type 3 (Expression → Construction).
**Transition from Section 2:**
- **Guide:** "You've been finding the total — the product. But here's the thing. Sometimes you KNOW the product, and you need to figure out one of the other numbers."
### Interaction 3.1: First Factor Unknown — Skip-Counting Strategy \\\[WORKED EXAMPLE\\\]
- **Purpose:** Model backward reasoning for unknown first factor (number of groups). This position is taught FIRST because it has a clean, modelable strategy: skip-count by the known second factor and track how many counts it takes to reach the total. This directly extends M9 skip-counting — students count by a familiar number, with the added step of tracking how many jumps.
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: ☐ clusters of 5 birds — clusters are faded. Total shown: "20 birds total." Equation Builder with pre-filled template: `☐ × 5 = 20`. Language prompt displayed above Equation Builder: "\\*\\*\\_ groups of 5 equals 20 total."
- **Guide:** "Here's the new unknown. I know there are 5 birds in each group. And I know the total is 20 birds. But I don't know how many groups — that's the unknown."
- **Guide:** "Let me read the sentence: HOW MANY groups of 5 equals 20 total? I need to figure out how many groups of 5 make 20."
- **Guide:** "I can skip-count by 5s — I know how to do that. Watch: 5..."
- **Visual:** First cluster of 5 birds reveals/highlights. Running count: "5"
- **Guide:** "...10..."
- **Visual:** Second cluster reveals. Running count: "10"
- **Guide:** "...15..."
- **Visual:** Third cluster reveals. Running count: "15"
- **Guide:** "...20. I'm at 20\\!"
- **Visual:** Fourth cluster reveals. Running count: "20"
- **Guide:** "I counted 4 times. That means 4 groups. 4 groups of 5 equals 20."
- **Visual:** Equation updates: `4 × 5 = 20`. All 4 clusters fully visible.
- **Guide:** "The unknown was 4\\. I used skip-counting — something I already know — to figure out how many groups."
- **No student action** (observation — worked example)
**Design Note:** First-factor unknown is taught before second-factor because skip-counting by the known second factor is a direct M9 extension and produces the cleanest think-aloud. The Guide models: skip-count by the known factor (5s), stop when you hit the total (20), count your jumps (4). The visual reveals clusters one at a time, synchronized with each skip-count — making the strategy concrete, not abstract. This avoids division language entirely. Second-factor unknown (3.3) follows after students have the backward-reasoning framework established.
### Interaction 3.2: First Factor Unknown — Student Constructs (Construction → Equation)
- **Purpose:** Student uses Construction Mode to apply the skip-counting strategy from 3.1. First-factor unknown — student builds groups of a known size until the running total hits the target.
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures in Construction Mode. Equation Builder with pre-filled template: `☐ × 10 = 40`. Language prompt: "\\*\\*\\_ groups of 10 equals 40 total." Construction area starts empty. \\+/- group control available (each new group pre-set to 10 items per the equation's known factor). Running total display: "Total: 0 / 40."
- **Guide:** "Now you try. Groups of 10\\. Total is 40\\. How many groups do you need? Build groups until you hit 40."
- **Prompt:** "☐ × 10 \\= 40\\. Build groups of 10 until the total reaches 40."
- **Student action:** Uses \\+ to add groups of 10\\. Running total updates: 10, 20, 30, 40\\.
- **On Correct (4 groups of 10, total \\= 40):**
	- **Guide:** "4 groups of 10 equals 40\\. You skip-counted — 10, 20, 30, 40 — and it took 4 groups."
	- **Visual:** Equation completes: `4 × 10 = 40`. Reversed shown: `40 = 4 × 10`.
- **On Overshoot (e.g., 5 groups of 10 \\= 50):**
	- **Guide:** "That's 50 — too many. Remove a group and check again."
**Design Note:** Uses ×10 family for the student's first attempt — simplest skip-counting pattern, lowest computation load, so cognitive effort goes to the new backward-reasoning process. Construction Mode makes the skip-counting strategy physical: each group added IS a skip-count. The running total makes "how many jumps to reach the target" visible. **Fallback if Construction Mode unavailable:** MC specification: `[2] [3] [4] [5]` → system builds visual for selected answer.

**New Interaction 3.3: Second Factor Unknown — Construction Strategy**
- **Purpose:** Unknown moves to the second factor position (items per group). This is harder than first-factor because you can't directly skip-count by an unknown number. Strategy: builds groups to match with Construction Equation Builder, demonstrating structural understanding.
- **Visual:** Equation Builder with pre-filled template: `2 × ☐ = 10`. Language prompt: "2 groups of \\*\\*\\_ equals 10 total." Equal Groups with Pictures in Construction Mode: 2 empty groups displayed (group count locked at 2 per equation) — 2 circled groups visible, items inside covered/hidden. Per-group \\+/- controls. Running total: “Total: 0."
- **Guide:** "Here's the equation. 2 groups of how many cats equals 10\\. We need to build the groups. Let’s add equal items to the 2 groups until you reach 10\\. Add 5 to each group so the total reaches 10."
- **Prompt:** "2 × ☐ \\= 10\\. Add 5 cats to each of the 2 groups to make 10 total."
- **Student action:** Uses \\+/- to add 5 items to both individual groups. Running total updates live.
- **On Correct (2 equal groups of 5, total \\= 10):**
	- **Guide:** "2 times 5 equals 10\\. You started with the equation and BUILT the answer. Now we can skip count by 5s two times: 5, 10\\. That’s the total of 10\\. Each side of the equation has the same value."
- **On Correct Total, Unequal Groups:**
	- **Guide:** "You hit 10\\. But check — are the groups equal? Multiplication needs equal groups. Adjust so each group has the same number."
- **On Equal Groups, Wrong Total:**
	- **Guide:** "Equal groups — great\\! But the total isn't 10\\. Add more to each group (or remove some) and watch the total."
- **Guide:** "Your turn with a different one. 2 groups, 14 total. 2 times what equals 14?"
- **Prompt:** "2 × ☐ \\= 14\\. How many in each group?"
- **Student action:** Uses \\+/- to add items to individual groups. Running total updates live.
- **Correct Answer:** `7`
- **On Correct (2 equal groups of 7, total \\= 14):**
	- **Visual:** 7 cats appear in each group. Equation completes: `2 × 7 = 10`.
	- **Guide:** "2 times 7 equals 14\\. 2 groups of 7 is 14\\. You built the answer."
	- **Visual:** Reversed form: `14 = 2 × 7`.
- **On Correct Total, Unequal Groups:**
	- **Guide:** "You hit 14\\. But check — are the groups equal? Multiplication needs equal groups. Adjust so each group has the same number."
- **On Equal Groups, Wrong Total:**
	- **Guide:** "Equal groups — great\\! But the total isn't 14\\. Add more to each group (or remove some) and watch the total."
**Design Note:** The Guide explicitly names why second-factor is harder: "I can't skip-count by a number I don't know yet." This validates the difference students may feel. The strategy — recalling facts from the known factor's family — is less mechanical than skip-counting but leverages M9 fluency directly. Uses ×2 family (most automatic from M9) to keep computation trivial while students internalize the new reasoning direction. MC scaffolds the answer space. Placed third in the sequence so students already have the backward-reasoning mindset from 3.1-3.2.
### Interaction 3.4: Mixed Positions — Expression → Construction
- **Purpose:** Student encounters an equation with unknown factor and builds groups to match, demonstrating structural understanding. 2nd factor position to show Equation Builder with unknown 2nd factort.
- **Visual:** Equation Builder displays: `5 × ☐ = 30`. Language prompt: "5 groups of \\*\\*\\_ equals 30 total." Equal Groups with Pictures in Construction Mode. 5 empty groups displayed (group count locked at 5 per equation). Per-group \\+/- controls. Running total: "Total: 0."
- **Guide:** "Here's the equation. 5 groups of something equals 30\\. Build the groups — add items until you hit 30\\. Keep them equal\\!"
- **Prompt:** "5 × ☐ \\= 30\\. Build 5 equal groups that make 30 total."
- **Student action:** Uses \\+/- to add items to individual groups. Running total updates live.
- **On Correct (5 equal groups of 6, total \\= 30):**
	- **Guide:** "5 times 6 equals 30\\. You started with the equation and BUILT the answer. That's exactly what equations are for — they tell you how the numbers connect."
	- **Visual:** Reversed form: `30 = 5 × 6`.
- **On Correct Total, Unequal Groups:**
	- **Guide:** "You hit 30\\! But check — are the groups equal? Multiplication needs equal groups. Adjust so each group has the same number."
- **On Equal Groups, Wrong Total:**
	- **Guide:** "Equal groups — great\\! But the total isn't 30 yet. Add more to each group (or remove some) and watch the total."
**Design Note:** Type 3 activity — equation comes FIRST, student constructs groups, construction confirms understanding. This reverses the typical flow (students have been going Visual → Equation). Construction Mode makes this the richest interaction in the sequence: students must coordinate group count (locked), items per group (their control), equality constraint, AND target product — all simultaneously. The running total and unequal-groups feedback provide scaffolding without reducing the task to MC selection. If students can go Equation → Construction, they demonstrate genuine understanding of what the equation represents. **Fallback if Construction Mode unavailable:** MC specification: `[3] [5] [6] [8]` → system builds visual for selected answer.

### **Bridge to Exit Check**
- **Guide:** "You built equations with bags, rows, stacks, and groups. You found unknown numbers in different positions. Let's see what you know."
- **No student action** (transition)
**Voice Note:** Brief. References what student DID (built equations, found unknowns), not what they should feel. Matches playbook transition formula: "You've got the idea. Let's see what you know."

### 1.7.4 Incomplete Script Flags
**If ANY of these are true, STOP and complete the script:**
- [ ] \\= sign meaning ("same value as") not explicitly taught before student attempts
- [ ] Reversed orientation not shown by end of Section 1
- [ ] Context variety not explicitly announced at start of Section 2
- [ ] Language prompts missing from Section 3 unknown-factor tasks
- [ ] Any unknown-factor task uses non-fluency-family values without MC scaffolding
- [ ] Total interaction count below 13
- [ ] Missing any required phrase
- [ ] Contains any forbidden phrase
- [ ] Student attempts before worked example in any section
- [ ] Type 2 or Type 3 activity missing from Section 3
### 1.7.5 Success Criteria
**The One Thing:** An equation uses the equal sign to show that two sides have the **same value** — and an unknown can go anywhere because we solve based on what we know, not where the blank is.
**Ready for Practice:** Students can build multiplication equations from any equal groups context, handle unknowns in all positions, and understand \\= as "same value as." M11 introduces arrays — a new way to organize equal groups into rows AND columns. M10's equation flexibility carries forward; arrays just provide a new context for the same equation-building skills.
