# BACKBONE
## 1.0 THE ONE THING
An equation uses the equal sign to show that two sides have the **same value** — and an unknown can go anywhere because we solve based on what we know, not where the blank is.
**Critical Misconception:** Equal Sign as "Put Answer Here" (\\#19) — students who believe \\= means "the answer comes next" cannot make sense of reversed equations (12 \\= 3 × 4\\) or unknowns in non-product positions (☐ × 4 \\= 12). This misconception, if uncorrected, blocks access to algebraic reasoning across all future math.
**Success Indicator:** Student correctly completes equations with unknowns in all three positions (☐ × 4 \\= 12, 3 × ☐ \\= 12, 3 × 4 \\= ☐) AND in both orientations (standard and reversed) across varied contexts — without hesitation or position-dependent errors.
**Biggest Risk:** Teaching \\= sign flexibility and unknown positions and context variety simultaneously without sufficient scaffolding. M9's fluency foundation reduces arithmetic load, but the three new demands must layer deliberately: equation structure first (Activities 1-3), then context variety (Activities 4-5), then unknown factor positions (Activities 6-8). Collapsing these layers invites cognitive overload and surface-level pattern-matching rather than structural understanding.

### 1.1.2 Module Bridges
**From Module 9:** Students have computational fluency with 2s, 5s, and 10s — they can produce products automatically (e.g., 4 × 5 \\= 20 without counting). They know "product" from M7's formal introduction (Lesson 2.2) and M9's reinforcement through computation, and "factor" from M9's formal introduction. They have used "equation" as a functional label (M9 KDD \\#10) — they've seen 4 × 5 \\= 20 as a complete statement — but without the conceptual emphasis on what \\= means. M9 used standard form only (expression \\= product). M9 Synthesis explicitly bridged to M10: "Next, you'll see equations where one of the FACTORS is missing — like something times 5 equals 30."
Students also have extensive prior knowledge of unknowns from Grades 1-2 addition and subtraction (☐ \\+ 3 \\= 7, 7 \\- ☐ \\= 4, etc.). This is NOT new territory conceptually — it's the same idea applied to a new operation.
**This Module:** Students cross three thresholds simultaneously, scaffolded in sequence:
1. **Equation structure with \\= sign meaning (Activities 1-3):** The expression 3 × 4 becomes the equation 3 × 4 \\= 12\\. The \\= sign is explicitly taught as "same value as" — NOT "the answer comes next." Both orientations introduced from the start: 3 × 4 \\= 12 AND 12 \\= 3 × 4\\. Still bags/boxes contexts (LAST TIME for these).
2. **Context variety (Activities 4-5):** Explicit Guide announcement mid-lesson: "We've been seeing bags and boxes. Now OTHER ways to show equal groups: rows, stacks, groups. The same idea applies: \\*\\*\\_ groups/rows/stacks of \\*\\*\\_." Unknown as product only (3 × 4 \\= ☐ and ☐ \\= 3 × 4).
3. **Unknown factor positions (Activities 6-8):** Unknowns move to factor positions: ☐ × 4 \\= 12 (How many groups?) and 3 × ☐ \\= 12 (How many in each group?). Language-based prompts scaffold: "\\*\\*\\_ groups of 4 equals 12 total" → equation. Full context variety. Both orientations maintained.
Key learning:
- \\= means "same value as" — both sides of an equation represent the same amount
- Equations can be written in both orientations (3 × 4 \\= 12 and 12 \\= 3 × 4\\)
- Unknowns can appear in any position — we solve based on what we KNOW
- Structure language ("\\*\\*\\_ groups/rows/stacks of \\*\\*\\_") connects varied contexts to the same mathematical pattern
- M9 fluency makes unknown-factor equations accessible: ☐ × 5 \\= 20 is solvable because students know 4 × 5 \\= 20
**To Module 11:** M10 established that multiplication equations can represent any equal groups situation, with unknowns in any position. M11 introduces a new way to ORGANIZE equal groups: arrays (rows and columns). Where M10 used diverse unstructured contexts (bags, rows, stacks, groups), M11 shows that arranging items into rectangular arrays creates a spatial structure that can be described two ways — by rows OR by columns. The array is the visual bridge to the commutative property (M12). M10's equation flexibility (= sign meaning, unknown positions) carries forward — arrays just provide a new context for the same equation-building skills.

### ⚠️ Scope Warnings
⚠️ **EQUATION ≠ EXPRESSION:** M8-M9 students built expressions (3 × 4\\) — NO equal sign. M10 completes the mathematical sentence: 3 × 4 \\= 12\\. The Equation Builder template expands from \[***\] × \[***\] to \[***\] × \[***\] = \[***\] (and reversed: \[***\] = \[***\] × \[***\]). This is a structural upgrade, not just "adding a number."
⚠️ **\\= SIGN FLEXIBILITY FROM THE START:** Both orientations (3 × 4 \\= 12 AND 12 \\= 3 × 4\\) are introduced in Activities 1-3. This is deliberate — teaching standard form first then "correcting" later creates the exact misconception (\\#19) we're trying to prevent. Dual exposure from the start prevents rigid thinking.
⚠️ **THREE LAYERS, THREE STAGES:** The three new demands (= sign meaning, context variety, unknown positions) MUST layer in sequence per Tool/Visual Plan. Activities 1-3 \\= equation structure with familiar contexts. Activities 4-5 \\= context variety with product-only unknowns. Activities 6-8 \\= unknown factors with full variety. Collapsing these stages invites cognitive overload.
⚠️ **UNKNOWN ≠ ALGEBRA:** Students are NOT doing algebra. They are using known multiplication facts (from M9 fluency) to determine what value makes the equation true. ☐ × 5 \\= 20 is solved by "I know 4 × 5 \\= 20, so ☐ \\= 4." This is fact application, not algebraic reasoning. Language should reflect this: "What number makes this true?" not "Solve for x."
⚠️ **FACTOR RANGE FOR UNKNOWNS:** When the unknown IS a factor (☐ × 4 \\= 12 or 3 × ☐ \\= 12), use M9 fluency families (2s, 5s, 10s) so students can reason from automatic recall. When the unknown is the product (3 × 4 \\= ☐), broader ranges are acceptable because students can count the visual. This asymmetry is intentional — it keeps unknown-factor tasks accessible.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS
### 1.4.1 Equal Sign as "Put Answer Here" (\\#19) (PRIMARY)
**Trigger Behavior:** Student hesitates, protests, or errors when shown 12 \\= 3 × 4 or ☐ \\= 3 × 4 formats. May say or indicate "that's backwards" or "the answer goes at the end." Can complete 3 × 4 \\= ☐ but freezes on ☐ \\= 3 × 4\\. When building equations with tiles, consistently places \\= at the end with product after it, even when asked to create reversed form.
**Why It Happens:** Years of arithmetic experience reinforce a left-to-right "problem → answer" pattern: 3 \\+ 4 \\= 7, 5 \\- 2 \\= 3, 3 × 4 \\= 12\\. Students internalize \\= as "and the answer is..." rather than as a relational symbol meaning "same value as." This is one of the most well-documented misconceptions in mathematics education research, and it directly impedes algebraic thinking in later grades.
### Path A Intervention
**Visual Cue:** Balance/scale metaphor embedded in Guide language: "Think of equals as a balance. 3 × 4 on one side, 12 on the other. Both sides have the same value. It doesn't matter which side goes first." Equation Builder displays both orientations side-by-side after student completes standard form.
**Prevention Strategy:**
- Introduce BOTH orientations from Activities 1-3 — never establish standard form as "the" way
- Guide shows reversed form immediately after each standard completion: "We can also write: 12 \\= 3 × 4\\. Same equation, different order."
- Explicit language: "= means SAME VALUE AS, not 'the answer is'"
- Mix orientations throughout all subsequent activities so neither feels "special"
- Tie to prior knowledge: "Remember from addition? You saw 7 \\= 3 \\+ 4\\. Same idea here."
**Remediation Approach:**
- If student errors on reversed form: Guide pauses, redisplays standard form alongside reversed. "Look — 3 × 4 \\= 12\\. Now look — 12 \\= 3 × 4\\. Same numbers, same values. The equals sign connects them."
- Do NOT tell student they're wrong — reframe: "Both ways are correct. Let's look at why."
---
### 1.4.2 Unknown Must Be Last (\\#14) (PRIMARY)
**Trigger Behavior:** Student completes 3 × 4 \\= ☐ easily but becomes confused or errors on ☐ × 4 \\= 12 or 3 × ☐ \\= 12\\. May try to rearrange the equation to put ☐ at the end. May confuse which number is unknown — e.g., for ☐ × 4 \\= 12, enters 12 as the unknown (treating ☐ as the product position despite its actual location).
**Why It Happens:** Reinforced by \\#19 — if \\= means "answer comes next," then the unknown should logically be the "answer" at the end. Students who haven't internalized \\= as relational will default to positional reasoning: "the blank goes where the answer goes." Also compounded by limited prior exposure: most K-2 unknown work uses ☐ in the result position (3 \\+ 4 \\= ☐ much more common than ☐ \\+ 4 \\= 7).
### Path A Intervention
**Visual Cue:** Structure language prompts displayed alongside equation: "\\*\\*\\_ groups of 4 equals 12 total" paired with ☐ × 4 \\= 12\\. The language makes clear WHAT the unknown represents — it's not "the answer," it's "how many groups." Connection lines from Equal Groups with Pictures to equation components reinforce meaning.
**Prevention Strategy:**
- Language-based prompts BEFORE symbolic equations: "\\*\\*\\_ groups of 4 equals 12 total. How many groups?" THEN build ☐ × 4 \\= 12
- Guide emphasizes WHAT the unknown represents, not WHERE it sits: "The unknown is HOW MANY GROUPS. That's the first number."
- Progress through positions deliberately: unknown product (Activities 4-5) → unknown second factor (early Activity 6-7) → unknown first factor (Activity 7-8)
- Use M9 fluency as solving strategy: "You know your fours. What times 4 equals 12?"
**Remediation Approach:**
- Return to language prompt: "Let's read it as a sentence. \\*\\*\\_ groups of 4 equals 12\\. How many groups of 4 make 12?"
- Show Equal Groups with Pictures: "Look — there are some groups, each with 4 items. There are 12 items total. How many groups do you see?"
- Connect to known fact: "You know 3 × 4 \\= 12\\. So ☐ must be 3."

### 1.4.3 Groups vs. Size Confusion (\\#10) (SECONDARY — Carried from M7-M9)
**Trigger Behavior:** In varied contexts (rows, stacks, groups), student reverses factor positions: sees 3 rows of 4 but writes 4 × 3 \\= 12 or ☐ × 3 \\= 12 when the unknown should be the number of rows. More likely to emerge with NEW contexts (rows, stacks) where the group/size distinction is less visually obvious than with bags/boxes.
**Why It Happens:** New contexts (rows, stacks, kitchen groups) lack the clear container structure of bags/boxes/circles that made group counting intuitive in M7-M9. "3 rows of 4" requires abstracting "row" as "group" — and without container boundaries, students may focus on items first (seeing "4 things") rather than organizational units first (seeing "3 rows"). Context variety introduction increases this risk.
### Path A Intervention
**Visual Cue:** Maintain "\\*\\*\\_ groups of \\*\\*\\_" as the structural anchor across all contexts. Context-specific terms (rows, stacks) are named first, then bridged: "3 rows — that's 3 groups of 4." Guide highlights organizational units first (rows, stacks, groups), then items within each. Connection lines map organizational units → first factor, items per unit → second factor. Structural labels ("groups" / "in each group" / "total") appear alongside connection lines during remediation, reinforcing what each factor position represents.
**Prevention Strategy:**
- Explicit Guide transition language uses two-step bridging: "I see 3 rows. Each row IS a group. That's 3 groups of 4\\. Count the rows first — that's your first number."
- Verbal mediation routine carried from M7-M8: "How many groups/rows/stacks? How many in each?"
- Equal Groups with Pictures highlights organizational units (rows, stacks) sequentially during worked examples
- Connect back to familiar: "Same equal groups pattern — just organized differently\\!"
**Remediation Approach:**
- Return to verbal mediation: "How many rows? That's the groups. How many in each row? That's the items per group."
- Show familiar context alongside new: "3 bags of 4 \\= 3 × 4\\. 3 rows of 4 \\= 3 × 4\\. Same math, different picture."
**Correct Product, Wrong Structure (Special Case):**
When a student sees 3 rows of 4 and writes **4 × 3 \\= 12**, the product is mathematically correct but the factor positions are reversed. This requires careful handling:
- **DO acknowledge the correct product first:** "The total IS 12 — nice work on that."
- **DO redirect to structure:** "But let's check the equation. How many rows? 3\\. How many in each row? 4\\. So it's 3 groups of 4 — that's 3 × 4, not 4 × 3."
- **DO use Equal Groups with Pictures:** Highlight the 3 rows, then the 4 items within one row. Connection lines: rows → first factor, items per row → second factor.
- **DO NOT mark as fully incorrect** — the student demonstrates computation understanding; the gap is structural.
- **DO NOT say "it doesn't matter"** — commutativity (3 × 4 \\= 4 × 3\\) is M12. Prematurely validating reversed order undermines the group/size distinction that M10's unknown-factor tasks depend on ("☐ × 4 \\= 12" only makes sense if the student knows the first factor is groups, not items).
- **System behavior:** Light error state (not harsh red). Prompt retry with structure reminder. Track as \\#10 occurrence for remediation pipeline.

**Type 2 & 3 Implementation:** These use the Equal Groups with Pictures/Dots toy in **Construction Mode** — student uses \\+/- controls to add/remove groups AND add/remove items within individual groups. Per-group item controls allow unequal group states, creating natural equal-groups reinforcement. A running total displays live, updating as the student adds or removes items. System detects three states: (a) correct total \\+ equal groups \\= success, (b) correct total \\+ unequal groups \\= "You reached \\\[total\\\], but are the groups equal? Multiplication needs equal groups," (c) equal groups \\+ wrong total \\= "Equal groups\\! But check your total." The construction interface is richer than MC specification because students iterate toward a solution, generating real-time feedback about the relationship between group structure and product.
**Pedagogical rationale:** Type 1 alone only tests whether students can READ a visual and WRITE an equation. Types 2 and 3 test whether students understand what the unknown REPRESENTS — can they reason FROM a constraint TO a structure? This is precisely the unknown-factor reasoning M10 is supposed to build. A student who can look at 4 stacks of 5 and write ☐ × 5 \\= 20 may be pattern-matching position. A student who can take "20 items, 4 stacks" and determine "5 in each" demonstrates structural understanding.

⚠️ **Engineering dependency — Construction Mode is pedagogically essential, not just preferred UX.**
Construction Mode IS the instructional strategy for unknown-factor reasoning. It solves a fundamental design problem: how do students find unknown factors without division and without relying on fact recall as the primary approach? Answer: they build. For 1st-factor unknown, students add groups of a known size until the running total hits the target — skip-counting emerges from the construction naturally. For 2nd-factor unknown, students adjust items per group across a fixed number of groups until the total matches — iterative reasoning replaces fact recall. The tool makes the abstract strategy physical.
**Required capabilities:** Per-group \\+/- controls (add/remove items to individual groups independently), \\+/- group controls (add/remove entire groups), running total display (updates live), equal/unequal group state detection, feedback branching for three states (correct+equal, correct+unequal, wrong total). Students can build unequal groups — this is pedagogically intentional, not a bug. The "correct total but unequal groups" state reinforces the M7-M8 foundational concept that multiplication requires EQUAL groups.

### 1.5.3 Data Constraints
**Why M9 Fluency Families for Unknown-Factor Tasks:** When the unknown IS a factor (☐ × 4 \\= 12 or 3 × ☐ \\= 12), the student must reason backward from the product. This requires automatic recall — if a student has to count to figure out what × 4 \\= 12, they're managing two cognitive demands simultaneously (counting AND equation structure). Using 2s, 5s, and 10s — the families students have automaticity with from M9 — isolates the equation-reasoning demand.
**Why Broader Ranges for Unknown-Product Tasks:** When the unknown IS the product (3 × 4 \\= ☐), students can count the Equal Groups with Pictures to find the answer. The equation-building is the cognitive demand, not the computation. Broader factor ranges (up to 10 × 10\\) are acceptable because the visual provides the answer pathway.
**Why Both Orientations From the Start:** Research consistently shows that students who learn \\= as "answer comes next" (through exclusive standard-form exposure) develop \\#19 misconceptions that are resistant to correction. Introducing both orientations simultaneously prevents the misconception from forming, rather than trying to fix it after establishment.

## 1.6 WARMUP (\\\~3-4 minutes)\*\*
---
### **Core Purpose**
Activate equation-building knowledge from M9 and prime the \\= sign flexibility that Section 1 of the Lesson will teach explicitly. Students build a complete equation (M9 skill) and then encounter the reversed orientation (12 \\= 3 × 4\\) as a curiosity gap — they SEE it and judge whether it's valid, but the Lesson is where they'll HEAR "equals means same value as."
**Key Function:** This Warmup positions M10 as the natural next step from M9. Students arrive knowing how to BUILD equations with tiles (M9 Exit skill). The Warmup activates that skill (W.1), then presents a visually surprising rearrangement of their own equation (W.2) — priming the \\= sign meaning the Lesson will teach. The bridge previews today's three new demands (= sign flexibility, new contexts, unknowns) without teaching any of them.
**Warmup Type:** Quick Activation Sequence \\+ Binary Choice (W.2)
**Why this serves the concept:**
- M10's new learning is equation structure, \\= sign meaning, and unknowns — all conceptual, not procedural
- Students need confident activation of equation building (M9 skill) before three new demands layer on
- The reversed equation in W.2 is a genuine curiosity gap — students haven't seen this orientation before
- Unlike M7-M8, we don't need context variety here (that's a Lesson Section 2 demand, deliberately held back)
- Quick, successful equation building in W.1 confirms M9 fluency is intact, reducing cognitive load for the Lesson
**Test:** If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson? YES — they would enter Lesson 1.1's worked example without activating the Equation Builder interface or confirming equation fluency. And Lesson 1.2's \\= sign teaching would lack the curiosity priming that makes "same value as" feel like an answer to a real question rather than an arbitrary rule.

### Interaction 2.2: Equal vs. Unequal Groups — Identification \\\[CONCEPTUAL CHECK\\\]

- **Correct Answer:** A and C
- **On Correct (both A and C selected):**

- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Partial correct (selected A/C plus B or D): highlight the incorrectly added image group by group with counts — ask if every group has the same number. ### Interaction 2.3: Product Unknown — Standard Form
- **Purpose:** First unknown encounter. Product position is the simplest — students already know how to find the total. The new element is the ☐ in the equation.
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 8 stacks of 4 plates. Plates clearly stacked. Equation Builder with pre-filled equation: `8 × 4 = ☐`. The ☐ is visible in the product slot. Palette with answer tiles: `28, 30, 32, 36`.
- **Guide:** "8 stacks of 4 plates. These are equal groups so we can write a multiplication equation to represent the picture. The expression part is done — 8 times 4\\. But see that box where the product goes? That's the unknown — the number we need to figure out. Find the total and place it."
- **Prompt:** "8 × 4 \\= ☐. Find the product. Place the number."
- **Correct Answer:** `32`
- **On Correct:** "8 times 4 equals 32\\. You found the unknown — 32 is the product. 8 stacks, 4 in each, same value as 32."
**Design Note:** First unknown is highly scaffolded — the expression is pre-filled, student only places the product. This isolates the "unknown" concept from the equation-building skill. Guide explicitly names the ☐: "That's the unknown. The number we need to figure out." This is the formal introduction of "unknown" vocabulary per §1.3 (after visual experience with the concept).
### Interaction 2.4: Product Unknown — Reversed Form
- Visual: Equal Groups with Pictures: 5 clusters of 7 sheep in a field — sheep grouped by natural spatial proximity, NOT circled or contained. Clear spatial gaps between clusters define the groups. Equation Builder with REVERSED template, pre-filled: `☐ = 5 × 7`. Palette: `12, 30, 35, 42`.
- Guide: "Look at this field. See the sheep? They're grouped together equally — 5 groups, 7 in each. The equation is flipped — unknown first this time. But equals still means same value as. Figure out the product and place it."
- Prompt: "☐ \\= 5 × 7\\. What is the product?"
- Correct: `35`
- On Correct: "35 equals 5 times 7\\. The unknown was 35 — same value on both sides."
**Design Note:** First context using natural spatial clustering WITH equal groups. Students just evaluated spatial clustering in 2.2 (distractor D: unequal animal clusters) and correctly identified it as NOT equal groups. Now they encounter spatial clustering that IS equal — reinforcing that clustering alone doesn't determine equal groups; you have to check the counts. Sheep in a field group by proximity — no circles, no bags, no shelves. Click-to-highlight-group interaction is especially valuable here — tapping a cluster confirms the grouping the student perceived.

- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 2 shelves with 8 jars each. Equation Builder with pre-filled equation: `2 × 8 = ☐`. Palette: `10, 14, 16, 18`.
- **Guide:** "New context, same pattern of equal groups. 2 shelves of 8 jars. Find the unknown — the product."

- **Correct Answer:** `16`

## 1.7.3 LESSON SECTION 3: Unknown Factor Positions\*\*
**Purpose:** Unknowns move from the product position to FACTOR positions. This is the module's most challenging demand — students must reason BACKWARD from a known product to find a missing factor. The two factor positions require different strategies: first-factor unknown (☐ × 5 \\= 20\\) can be solved by skip-counting by the known second factor and tracking counts — a direct M9 extension. Second-factor unknown (2 × ☐ \\= 10\\) requires recalling which fact from the known factor's family produces the target product. First-factor is taught first because the strategy is more intuitive. Language-based prompts ("\\*\\*\\_ groups of 5 equals 20 total") scaffold both positions. Three activity types from §1.5.1 are used: Type 1 (Visual → Equation), Type 2 (Construction → Equation), and Type 3 (Expression → Construction).
**Transition from Section 2:**
- **Guide:** "You've been finding the total — the product. But here's the thing. Sometimes you KNOW the product, and you need to figure out one of the other numbers."
### Interaction 3.1: First Factor Unknown — Skip-Counting Strategy \\\[WORKED EXAMPLE\\\]
- **Purpose:** Model backward reasoning for unknown first factor (number of groups). This position is taught FIRST because it has a clean, modelable strategy: skip-count by the known second factor and track how many counts it takes to reach the total. This directly extends M9 skip-counting — students count by a familiar number, with the added step of tracking how many jumps.
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: ☐ clusters of 5 birds — clusters are faded. Total shown: "20 birds total." Equation Builder with pre-filled template: `☐ × 5 = 20`. Language prompt displayed above Equation Builder: "\\*\\*\\_ groups of 5 equals 20 total."
- **Guide:** "Here's the new unknown. I know there are 5 birds in each group. And I know the total is 20 birds. But I don't know how many groups — that's the unknown."
- **Guide:** "Let me read the sentence: HOW MANY groups of 5 equals 20 total? I need to figure out how many groups of 5 make 20."
- **Guide:** "I can skip-count by 5s — I know how to do that. Watch: 5..."
- **Visual:** First cluster of 5 birds reveals/highlights. Running count: "5"
- **Guide:** "...10..."
- **Visual:** Second cluster reveals. Running count: "10"
- **Guide:** "...15..."
- **Visual:** Third cluster reveals. Running count: "15"
- **Guide:** "...20. I'm at 20\\!"
- **Visual:** Fourth cluster reveals. Running count: "20"
- **Guide:** "I counted 4 times. That means 4 groups. 4 groups of 5 equals 20."
- **Visual:** Equation updates: `4 × 5 = 20`. All 4 clusters fully visible.
- **Guide:** "The unknown was 4\\. I used skip-counting — something I already know — to figure out how many groups."
- **No student action** (observation — worked example)
**Design Note:** First-factor unknown is taught before second-factor because skip-counting by the known second factor is a direct M9 extension and produces the cleanest think-aloud. The Guide models: skip-count by the known factor (5s), stop when you hit the total (20), count your jumps (4). The visual reveals clusters one at a time, synchronized with each skip-count — making the strategy concrete, not abstract. This avoids division language entirely. Second-factor unknown (3.3) follows after students have the backward-reasoning framework established.
### Interaction 3.2: First Factor Unknown — Student Constructs (Construction → Equation)
- **Purpose:** Student uses Construction Mode to apply the skip-counting strategy from 3.1. First-factor unknown — student builds groups of a known size until the running total hits the target.
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures in Construction Mode. Equation Builder with pre-filled template: `☐ × 10 = 40`. Language prompt: "\\*\\*\\_ groups of 10 equals 40 total." Construction area starts empty. \\+/- group control available (each new group pre-set to 10 items per the equation's known factor). Running total display: "Total: 0 / 40."
- **Guide:** "Now you try. Groups of 10\\. Total is 40\\. How many groups do you need? Build groups until you hit 40."
- **Prompt:** "☐ × 10 \\= 40\\. Build groups of 10 until the total reaches 40."
- **Student action:** Uses \\+ to add groups of 10\\. Running total updates: 10, 20, 30, 40\\.
- **On Correct (4 groups of 10, total \\= 40):**
	- **Guide:** "4 groups of 10 equals 40\\. You skip-counted — 10, 20, 30, 40 — and it took 4 groups."
	- **Visual:** Equation completes: `4 × 10 = 40`. Reversed shown: `40 = 4 × 10`.
- **On Overshoot (e.g., 5 groups of 10 \\= 50):**
	- **Guide:** "That's 50 — too many. Remove a group and check again."
**Design Note:** Uses ×10 family for the student's first attempt — simplest skip-counting pattern, lowest computation load, so cognitive effort goes to the new backward-reasoning process. Construction Mode makes the skip-counting strategy physical: each group added IS a skip-count. The running total makes "how many jumps to reach the target" visible. **Fallback if Construction Mode unavailable:** MC specification: `[2] [3] [4] [5]` → system builds visual for selected answer.

**New Interaction 3.3: Second Factor Unknown — Construction Strategy**
- **Purpose:** Unknown moves to the second factor position (items per group). This is harder than first-factor because you can't directly skip-count by an unknown number. Strategy: builds groups to match with Construction Equation Builder, demonstrating structural understanding.
- **Visual:** Equation Builder with pre-filled template: `2 × ☐ = 10`. Language prompt: "2 groups of \\*\\*\\_ equals 10 total." Equal Groups with Pictures in Construction Mode: 2 empty groups displayed (group count locked at 2 per equation) — 2 circled groups visible, items inside covered/hidden. Per-group \\+/- controls. Running total: “Total: 0."
- **Guide:** "Here's the equation. 2 groups of how many cats equals 10\\. We need to build the groups. Let’s add equal items to the 2 groups until you reach 10\\. Add 5 to each group so the total reaches 10."
- **Prompt:** "2 × ☐ \\= 10\\. Add 5 cats to each of the 2 groups to make 10 total."
- **Student action:** Uses \\+/- to add 5 items to both individual groups. Running total updates live.
- **On Correct (2 equal groups of 5, total \\= 10):**
	- **Guide:** "2 times 5 equals 10\\. You started with the equation and BUILT the answer. Now we can skip count by 5s two times: 5, 10\\. That’s the total of 10\\. Each side of the equation has the same value."
- **On Correct Total, Unequal Groups:**
	- **Guide:** "You hit 10\\. But check — are the groups equal? Multiplication needs equal groups. Adjust so each group has the same number."
- **On Equal Groups, Wrong Total:**
	- **Guide:** "Equal groups — great\\! But the total isn't 10\\. Add more to each group (or remove some) and watch the total."
- **Guide:** "Your turn with a different one. 2 groups, 14 total. 2 times what equals 14?"
- **Prompt:** "2 × ☐ \\= 14\\. How many in each group?"
- **Student action:** Uses \\+/- to add items to individual groups. Running total updates live.
- **Correct Answer:** `7`
- **On Correct (2 equal groups of 7, total \\= 14):**
	- **Visual:** 7 cats appear in each group. Equation completes: `2 × 7 = 10`.
	- **Guide:** "2 times 7 equals 14\\. 2 groups of 7 is 14\\. You built the answer."
	- **Visual:** Reversed form: `14 = 2 × 7`.
- **On Correct Total, Unequal Groups:**
	- **Guide:** "You hit 14\\. But check — are the groups equal? Multiplication needs equal groups. Adjust so each group has the same number."
- **On Equal Groups, Wrong Total:**
	- **Guide:** "Equal groups — great\\! But the total isn't 14\\. Add more to each group (or remove some) and watch the total."
**Design Note:** The Guide explicitly names why second-factor is harder: "I can't skip-count by a number I don't know yet." This validates the difference students may feel. The strategy — recalling facts from the known factor's family — is less mechanical than skip-counting but leverages M9 fluency directly. Uses ×2 family (most automatic from M9) to keep computation trivial while students internalize the new reasoning direction. MC scaffolds the answer space. Placed third in the sequence so students already have the backward-reasoning mindset from 3.1-3.2.
### Interaction 3.4: Mixed Positions — Expression → Construction
- **Purpose:** Student encounters an equation with unknown factor and builds groups to match, demonstrating structural understanding. 2nd factor position to show Equation Builder with unknown 2nd factort.
- **Visual:** Equation Builder displays: `5 × ☐ = 30`. Language prompt: "5 groups of \\*\\*\\_ equals 30 total." Equal Groups with Pictures in Construction Mode. 5 empty groups displayed (group count locked at 5 per equation). Per-group \\+/- controls. Running total: "Total: 0."
- **Guide:** "Here's the equation. 5 groups of something equals 30\\. Build the groups — add items until you hit 30\\. Keep them equal\\!"
- **Prompt:** "5 × ☐ \\= 30\\. Build 5 equal groups that make 30 total."
- **Student action:** Uses \\+/- to add items to individual groups. Running total updates live.
- **On Correct (5 equal groups of 6, total \\= 30):**
	- **Guide:** "5 times 6 equals 30\\. You started with the equation and BUILT the answer. That's exactly what equations are for — they tell you how the numbers connect."
	- **Visual:** Reversed form: `30 = 5 × 6`.
- **On Correct Total, Unequal Groups:**
	- **Guide:** "You hit 30\\! But check — are the groups equal? Multiplication needs equal groups. Adjust so each group has the same number."
- **On Equal Groups, Wrong Total:**
	- **Guide:** "Equal groups — great\\! But the total isn't 30 yet. Add more to each group (or remove some) and watch the total."
**Design Note:** Type 3 activity — equation comes FIRST, student constructs groups, construction confirms understanding. This reverses the typical flow (students have been going Visual → Equation). Construction Mode makes this the richest interaction in the sequence: students must coordinate group count (locked), items per group (their control), equality constraint, AND target product — all simultaneously. The running total and unequal-groups feedback provide scaffolding without reducing the task to MC selection. If students can go Equation → Construction, they demonstrate genuine understanding of what the equation represents. **Fallback if Construction Mode unavailable:** MC specification: `[3] [5] [6] [8]` → system builds visual for selected answer.

### Problem EC.2: Solve Product Unknown — Reversed Form
- **Purpose:** Test whether student can solve a product unknown AND handle the reversed orientation. Two Lesson demands combined: product unknown (Section 2\\) \\+ \\= sign flexibility (Section 1).
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 4 groups of 7 flowers (flowers in circled clusters). Equation Builder with reversed pre-filled equation: `☐ = 4 × 7`. Palette with answer tiles: `11, 21, 24, 28` .
- **Guide:** "4 groups of 7 flowers. The equation is flipped — total goes first. Find the total and place it."
- **Prompt:** "☐ \\= 4 × 7\\. Find the total."
- **Student Action:** Equation Builder — place product tile in ☐ slot.
- **Correct Answer:** `28`
- **Answer Rationale:**
	- 28 \\= Correct (4 x 7 \\= 28; skip count by 7s: 7, 14, 21, 28\\)
	- 11 \\= Addition error (4 \\+ 7 \\= 11 — Misconception \\#10 echo: confusing groups/items with addition)
	- 21 or 24 \\= Skip-count error (stopped at 3 counts of 7 instead of 4 or counted 4 groups of 6 instead of 7\\)
- **On Correct:** "28 equals 4 times 7. Same value, both sides."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Have student skip-count by 7s four times to compute the product (7, 14, 21, 28). Distractor 11 = addition error (4+7); 21/24 = skip-count stopping too early.
**Design Note:** Reversed form is the critical \\#19 check — students who believe \\= means "answer comes next" will be confused by ☐ on the left. The Guide's "equation is flipped — total goes first" mirrors Lesson 2.3 language. The 11 distractor catches students who ADD groups \\+ items rather than multiplying — surfacing a potential structural misconception. Values not used in Lesson.

- **Key principle:** The visual basis of building/seeing groups MUST be present for CONFIDENCE, SUPPORT, and the majority of BASELINE. Construction Mode is the taught strategy — removing it is a difficulty increase, not a neutral formatting choice.
### Tier Differentiation for M10
Tiers differentiate across three dimensions: factor families, interaction method, and scaffolding. Construction Mode removal is itself a tier increase.
- **CONFIDENCE:** Fluency families only, Construction Mode, language prompts visible, familiar contexts
- **SUPPORT:** Fluency families only, Construction Mode, language prompts visible, mixed contexts
- **BASELINE:** Fluency families primary, Construction Mode for 50%+ of unknown-factor tasks (remainder: MC with visual), both equation orientations, language prompts for factor unknowns
- **STRETCH:** Broader factor families acceptable, MC with visual (no Construction Mode), reduced language scaffolding, less common contexts, tighter distractors
- **CHALLENGE:** Broader factor families acceptable, MC without visual (equation-first, visual confirms after answer), no language prompts, all three unknown positions mixed
### Context Variety in Practice
Practice should use full variety contexts (NOT bags/boxes). Bags/boxes are retired after Lesson Section 1\\. Practice reinforces the generalization that equal groups appear everywhere.
### Equal vs. Unequal Groups Identification (1-2 templates)
At least one practice template should target equal vs. unequal group identification — extending Lesson Interaction 2.2. Template types:
- **"Which shows equal groups?"** — Multi-select from 3-4 visuals. Mix of equal and unequal groupings using variety contexts. Student selects all that could be written as a multiplication equation. BASELINE tier.
- **"What's wrong with this picture?"** (metacognitive variant) — Show an unequal grouping alongside a proposed equation (e.g., image shows groups of 5, 3, 5 with equation `3 × 5 = 15`). Student identifies whether the equation matches. MC options: "Yes, it matches" / "No — the groups aren't equal" / "No — the total is wrong" / "No — the number of groups is wrong." STRETCH tier.

### Task S.1: \\= Sign Truth Check
- **Purpose:** Consolidate the \\= sign as a "same value" check, not a signal for "answer comes next." Surfaces any lingering \\#19 by including a reversed equation alongside a false equation. This is TVP Synthesis Thread 1 (= sign meaning consolidation).
- **Visual:** Four equations displayed in a column:
	- A: `3 × 5 = 15`
	- B: `15 = 3 × 5`
	- C: `3 × 5 = 20`
	- `D: 15 = 5 x 3`
- **Guide:** "Four equations. Three are true — both sides have the same value. One is false. Which one is false?"
- **Prompt:** "Which equation is false?"
- **Student Action:** MC (select one: A, B, C or D)
- **Correct Answer:** C
**On Correct (selected C):**
- **Guide:** "3 times 5 is 15, not 20\\. The sides don't match." *(pause)* "And notice — the other equations are all true. The total is on the right in A and on the left in B and D, but both sides have the same value either way. That's what the equals sign checks."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Selected A (3×5=15 — true): redirect to finding the equation where the sides don't match, not a true equation. Selected B or D (#19 indicator — reversed true equation): name what the student observed ("total is on the left") and clarify that same value on both sides means true regardless of orientation. Selected wrong equation: prompt student to check if both sides equal the same number.
**Design Note:** This task is explicitly diagnostic for residual \\#19. Students who select B or D reveal they still believe \\= requires the product on the right. The guide's response addresses this without re-teaching — it names what the student observed ("the total is on the left") and redirects to the principle ("same value"). Students who select C have consolidated the \\= sign meaning. The guide's follow-up explicitly names the A/B comparison, reinforcing that both orientations are valid. Using the SAME factors (3 × 5\\) across all three equations isolates the \\= sign check from computation — student already knows 3 × 5 \\= 15, so the only question is whether each equation is TRUE.
### Task S.2: Context Variety — Same Structure
- **Purpose:** Consolidate that different real-world situations can share the same mathematical structure. Students see three visually distinct contexts that all represent the same equation, and identify the structural invariant. This is TVP Synthesis Thread 2 (context variety review).
- **Visual:** Three context images displayed side by side:
	- Image 1: 4 rows of 2 books on shelves
	- Image 2: 4 stacks of 2 cups
	- Image 3: 4 groups of 2 butterflies (circled clusters)
	- Below all three: One equation: `4 × 2 = 8`
- **Guide:** "Three pictures. One equation fits all of them. What makes them the same?"
- **Prompt:** "What do all three pictures have in common?"
- **Student Action:** Multi-select
- **Options:**
	- A: "All have 4 groups of 2"
	- B: "All have 8 things"
	- C: "All look the same"
	- D: "All use books"
- **Correct Answer: A and B**
**On Fully Correct (selected A and B only):**
- **Guide:** "Both true\\! They all have 8 things — and the reason is the same every time: 4 groups of 2\\. The structure underneath is what the equation captures."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Selected A only (missed B — total): affirm the structure and connect it to the total (4×2=8 means all have 8 things; both A and B are true). Selected B only (missed A — structure): affirm the total then redirect to WHY — count groups in each picture to surface the 4 groups of 2 structure. Selected C or D (surface feature distractor): redirect from visual appearance to the group structure — how many groups, how many in each.

### Task S.3: Unknown Position Flexibility
- **Purpose:** Consolidate that the unknown can appear in any position without changing the underlying relationship. Students see three equations representing the same multiplication fact with the unknown in different positions, and identify what stays constant. This is TVP Synthesis Thread 3 (unknown position flexibility).
- **Visual:** Three equations displayed vertically, all representing 4 × 5 \\= 20:
	- `4 × 5 = ☐` → *(product unknown)*
	- `4 × ☐ = 20` → *(2nd factor unknown)*
	- `☐ × 5 = 20` → *(1st factor unknown)*
	- Small labels appear after display: "product unknown," "second factor unknown," "first factor unknown"
- **Guide:** "Three equations. Same numbers — 4, 5, and 20\\. But the unknown moved to a different spot each time. What stays the same?"
- **Prompt:** "What stays the same in all three equations?"
- **Student Action:** MC
- **Options:**
	- A: "4 groups of 5 equals 20"
	- B: "The unknown is always the hardest number"
	-
	- C: "The numbers are different each time"
	- D: "The equation changes meaning each time"
- **Correct Answer:** A
**On Correct (selected A):**
- **Guide:** "The unknown moves, but the relationship stays — 4 groups of 5 is always 20\\. You figure out whichever piece is missing."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Selected B ("unknown is hardest number"): clarify the unknown can be any of the three numbers — redirect to what relationship stays constant. Selected C ("numbers are different"): point out that 4, 5, and 20 appear in all three equations; the unknown box hides one each time. Selected D ("equation changes meaning"): have student read all three and ask if it's still 4 groups of 5 equals 20.
**Design Note:** The key insight is STRUCTURAL INVARIANCE — the relationship (4 groups of 5 \\= 20\\) persists regardless of which number is unknown. Students aren't SOLVING any of these equations — they're observing that the underlying fact doesn't change when the unknown moves. Option B ("hardest number") is a common student belief that the unknown must be the answer/product. Option C (skip-counting) describes a strategy, not the invariant structure. Option D is the opposite of the correct answer — students who select it haven't yet generalized.
### Task S.4: M9 → M10 Growth
- **Purpose:** Explicit side-by-side comparison of M9 and M10 equation work. Students identify what's NEW in M10 — consolidating all three layered demands into one observation. This is the Consolidation Moment (Playbook §3H) and serves as TVP Synthesis Thread 4 setup (bridge follows in closure).
- **Visual:** Two equations displayed side by side:
	- LEFT (labeled "Last time"): `6 × 5 = 30` with bags context image (M9 style — standard form, no unknown, bags)
	- RIGHT (labeled "Today"): `☐ = 3 × 10` with stacks context image (M10 style — reversed, unknown, variety context)
- **Guide:** "Here's an equation you have seen before. And here's one from today. What changed?"
- **Prompt:** "What's different about today's equation?"
- **Student Action:** MC
- **Options:**
	- A: "The total moved to the other side"
	- B: "There's an unknown number"
	- C: "The picture uses stacks, not bags"
	- D: "All of these are different today"
- **Correct Answer:** D
**On Correct (selected D):**
- **Guide:** "Three new things, all in one equation. The equals sign works both ways. Unknowns can go anywhere. And equations work for stacks, rows, groups — not just bags."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Each of A, B, C names one real M10 change but misses the others. Redirect student to compare both equations again and count how many things changed — the goal is to recognize all three changes together (= sign flexibility, unknown position, context variety).
**Design Note:** This task is the consolidation moment. Each individual option (A, B, C) names one M10 demand; option D names all three. Students who select A, B, or C aren't wrong — they noticed a real change — but haven't yet seen the FULL scope of what M10 added. The guide redirects them to compare more carefully, and the correct answer ("All of these are new") provides the consolidation: M10 was a three-layered upgrade to their equation toolkit.

**Design Note:** Each option maps to one M10 demand. No wrong answers — all are valid takeaways. ## **1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS SUMMARY**
### KDD 1: = Sign Flexibility From Day One
Both equation orientations introduced simultaneously in Section 1.
### KDD 2: Three Layers, Three Stages
= sign meaning → context variety → unknown positions, layered in sequence.
### KDD 3: Language Before Symbols
Language prompts precede or accompany symbolic equations for unknown-factor tasks.
### KDD 4: M9 Fluency Enables Unknowns — Across All Phases
Unknown-factor tasks use 2s, 5s, 10s across Lesson, Exit Check, AND Practice so students reason from automatic recall. This is a solvability constraint, not a difficulty dial. Broader factor ranges are reserved for unknown-product tasks where students count the visual. Per SME feedback, Sections 1-2 deliberately use non-fluency-family factors to build comfort with broader multiples while visuals provide counting support — fluency families appear only when backward reasoning requires them (Section 3+).
### KDD 5: Context Variety via Explicit Announcement
Mid-lesson Guide announcement names the shift from bags to variety.
### KDD 6: Bags/Boxes Last Stand
Section 1 uses bags/boxes for continuity; they never appear again.
### KDD 7: Unknown Prior Knowledge Activated
Connects to Grades 1-2 addition/subtraction unknowns.
### KDD 8: Factor Order Still Matters
3 × 4 ≠ 4 × 3 until M12 proves commutativity.
### KDD 9: "Groups of" as Structural Anchor
"___ groups of ___" is the structural anchor across all contexts and unknown positions. Context-specific terms (rows, stacks, etc.) are used descriptively, then bridged back via two-step pattern: name the arrangement first, then restate as "groups of." Per SME feedback, the slash notation ("groups/rows/stacks") was replaced to prevent ambiguity about what student-facing language should sound like.
### KDD 10: M11 Bridge: Spatial Organization Preview
Synthesis previews arrays visually without naming them.
### KDD 11: Why M10 Is Separate from M8
Three cognitive demands layered across M8→M9→M10, not collapsed into one module.
### KDD 12: Backbone Warmup Departure
= sign teaching relocated from Warmup to Lesson 1.2 per Warmup Playbook constraints. Warmup primes with reversed equation observation (curiosity gap), Lesson provides the answer. Improves design: "same value as" feels earned, not arbitrary.
### KDD 13: 1st-Factor Unknown Taught First
Per SME feedback, Section 3 was resequenced: 1st-factor unknown (3.1-3.2) before 2nd-factor unknown (3.3). Rationale: 1st-factor unknown has a clean, modelable strategy (skip-count by known 2nd factor, track counts) that directly extends M9. 2nd-factor unknown requires fact recall, which is less intuitive to model in a think-aloud. Teaching the more accessible strategy first establishes the backward-reasoning framework before the harder variant.
### KDD 14: No Template Toggle
Reversed orientation maintained via Guide-shown reversed form on correct, NOT by student clicking a toggle button (not a supported interaction).
### KDD 15: Construction Mode IS the Unknown-Factor Strategy
Per SME insight, Construction Mode isn't just the preferred interaction method — it IS the instructional strategy for unknown-factor reasoning. It solves the fundamental design problem of teaching unknown factors without division: students build groups physically and watch the running total, making backward reasoning concrete. For 1st-factor unknown, students add groups (skip-counting emerges from building). For 2nd-factor unknown, students adjust items per group (iterative reasoning replaces fact recall). Same tool, different locked parameters, unified approach. UX mockups in progress. MC-based specification is the fallback but significantly weakens the pedagogy.
### KDD 16: Equal vs. Unequal Groups — Two-Module Sequence
Equal groups identification is first taught in M8 (structural level — "can you write an expression for this?") and reinforced in M10 Section 2 (equation level — equal groups AND correct total). Per SME feedback, M10 is the last natural window for this concept before M11's array structure enforces equality by definition.
### KDD 17: Structural Labels on Equation Components
Per SME feedback, labels ("groups" / "in each group" / "total") appear above/below equation slots during worked examples and remediation, reinforcing what each factor position represents. Labels are NOT always-on — they fade after teaching/remediation moments to promote internalization.
### KDD 18: No Binary MC in Student-Facing Interactions
Per SME lab testing feedback, binary yes/no questions don't work well with young students (wrong answer → skip ahead without listening). All MC interactions use 3-4 options minimum. Warmup W.2 uses 4 reasoning-based options instead of Yes/No to surface diagnostic data about = sign understanding.
### KDD 19: Section 1-2 Factor Variety, Section 3 Fluency Families
Per SME feedback, Sections 1-2 deliberately avoid 2s, 5s, and 10s as second factors. Students build comfort with broader multiples while visuals provide counting support. Section 3 switches to fluency families because unknown-factor tasks require backward reasoning from known facts. The contrast is pedagogically productive — the ease students feel in Section 3 IS M9's fluency paying off.
