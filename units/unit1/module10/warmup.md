## 1.6 WARMUP (\\\~3-4 minutes)\*\*
---
### **Core Purpose**
Activate equation-building knowledge from M9 and prime the \\= sign flexibility that Section 1 of the Lesson will teach explicitly. Students build a complete equation (M9 skill) and then encounter the reversed orientation (12 \\= 3 × 4\\) as a curiosity gap — they SEE it and judge whether it's valid, but the Lesson is where they'll HEAR "equals means same value as."
**Key Function:** This Warmup positions M10 as the natural next step from M9. Students arrive knowing how to BUILD equations with tiles (M9 Exit skill). The Warmup activates that skill (W.1), then presents a visually surprising rearrangement of their own equation (W.2) — priming the \\= sign meaning the Lesson will teach. The bridge previews today's three new demands (= sign flexibility, new contexts, unknowns) without teaching any of them.
**Warmup Type:** Quick Activation Sequence \\+ Binary Choice (W.2)
**Why this serves the concept:**
- M10's new learning is equation structure, \\= sign meaning, and unknowns — all conceptual, not procedural
- Students need confident activation of equation building (M9 skill) before three new demands layer on
- The reversed equation in W.2 is a genuine curiosity gap — students haven't seen this orientation before
- Unlike M7-M8, we don't need context variety here (that's a Lesson Section 2 demand, deliberately held back)
- Quick, successful equation building in W.1 confirms M9 fluency is intact, reducing cognitive load for the Lesson
**Test:** If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson? YES — they would enter Lesson 1.1's worked example without activating the Equation Builder interface or confirming equation fluency. And Lesson 1.2's \\= sign teaching would lack the curiosity priming that makes "same value as" feel like an answer to a real question rather than an arbitrary rule.

### **Warmup Interactions**
### Interaction W.1: Equation Building Callback \\\[ACTIVATION\\\]
- **Purpose:** Activate equation-building skill from M9. Confirm students can build a complete equation from an equal groups visual. Quick success with familiar skill.
- **Hook (first 15-20 seconds):**
	- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 5 bags with 2 items each. Equation Builder appears alongside with full equation template: `[___] × [___] = [___]`. Tile palette: `1 through 10`.
	- **Guide:** "Let's warm up. You've been building equations — let's make sure you've still got it. Build the equation for this picture."
- **Prompt:** "Build the equation for this picture."
- **Student Action:** Equation Builder Methods C/D (tiles — M9 owned skill).
- **Correct Answer:** Equation must be `5 × 2 = 10`
- **On Correct:** "5 groups of 2\\. 5 times 2 equals 10\\. You know how to build equations."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Guide factor identification from visual: count bags (first factor), count items per bag (second factor). Then skip-count by 2s five times to find the product (10).
**Engagement Anchor 1:** Quick Success (familiar M9 skill, high confidence entry)
**Design Note:** Pure M9 callback. Uses bags (concrete, primes Lesson Section 1 which continues with bags). Values deliberately use ×2 family (easiest M9 fluency) for maximum accessibility. Full equation template `[___] × [___] = [___]` is the M9 format students know. The Equation Builder interface activation matters — Lesson 1.1 uses the same tool immediately.
---
### Interaction W.2: Reversed Equation — Judgment Task \\\[CURIOSITY GAP\\\]
- **Purpose:** Present the reversed orientation of the student's own equation as a genuine judgment task. Creates the curiosity gap that Lesson 1.2 will satisfy ("equals means same value as"). All responses are valid Warmup responses — none are wrong, because the student's reasoning reveals their current understanding of \\=. The Lesson teaches the concept regardless.
- **Visual:** The equation from W.1 (`5 × 2 = 10`) remains on screen. A second equation appears alongside or below it, reversed: `10 = 5 × 2`.
- **Guide**: "Now look at this equation. What do you think?"
- **Prompt**: "What do you think about 10 \\= 5 × 2?"
- **Method**: MC (4 options — reasoning-based)
- **Options**:
	- A: "It's true — both sides are 10"
	- B: "It's true — it's just written differently"
	- C: "It's wrong — the answer should come last"
	- D: "I'm not sure"
	On A: "You checked both sides. Smart thinking. Hold that thought."
	On B: "You noticed it's the same equation, just flipped. Hold that thought."
	On C: "Interesting — something looks different about it. Hold that thought."
	On D: "That's honest — it does look different. Hold that thought."
**Engagement Anchor 2:** Curiosity Gap ("Is this still true?" — genuine question that creates anticipation for the Lesson's answer)
**Design Note:** This is the Warmup's key priming moment. Students who say "Yes" may already have flexible \\= sign understanding — Lesson 1.2 will deepen and formalize it. Students who say "No" hold the common misconception (\\#19: equals means "answer comes next") — Lesson 1.2 directly addresses this. Either way, the student has now THOUGHT about what \\= means before the Lesson teaches it. This is the difference between teaching and priming: the Warmup raises the question, the Lesson provides the answer. Guide says "Hold that thought" for both responses — maintaining mystery without validating either answer as correct. No "You're right" or "Not quite" — that would constitute teaching.
---
**Bridge to Lesson:**
- **Visual:** Both equations remain visible (`5 × 2 = 10` and `10 = 5 × 2`).
- **Guide:** "You can build equations. But today, equations have some surprises. You'll see them in new ways and figure out missing numbers."
- **No student action**
**Design Note:** Bridge previews all three Lesson demands in compressed form: "new ways" (= sign flexibility \\+ context variety) and "missing numbers" (unknowns). Session-relative language avoided — no "today" in the Guide line, replaced with "but today" which functions as a natural conjunction. The preview matches the backbone's specified language: *"You'll write equations, see them in new ways, and figure out missing numbers."* Both equations staying visible through transition creates seamless visual continuity into Lesson 1.1.
