## 1.8 EXIT CHECK (\\\~3-4 minutes)
---
### Purpose
Verify Lesson understanding before Practice. Tests whether student can build a complete equation from a variety-context visual, solve product unknowns in reversed orientation, and solve 1st-factor unknowns using the Equation Builder — all skills explicitly taught in Lesson Sections 1-3.

### Problem EC.1: Build Complete Equation from Variety Visual
- **Purpose:** Test whether student can build a complete equation from a variety-context visual using tiles. Tests full equation construction (Section 1 skill) with variety context (Section 2 skill) — combining two Lesson demands.
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 3 rows of 6 pencils on a desk. Pencils arranged in clear horizontal rows. Equation Builder with template `[___] × [___] = [___]`. Tile palette: `2`, `3`, 4, `5`, `6`, 9, 12, 15, 18, 20 .
- **Guide:** "Rows of pencils. Build the equation."
- **Prompt:** "Build the equation."
- **Student Action:** Equation Builder tiles (Methods C/D — same as Lesson Section 1).
- **Correct Answer:** Equation must be `3 × 6 = 18`
- **On Correct:** "3 times 6 equals 18."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Direct student to count rows first (that's the number of groups = first factor), then count pencils in one row (items per group = second factor). Then skip-count by 6s three times.
**Design Note:** Simplest problem — full equation with all numbers visible. Rows context confirms variety transfer — students saw rows of pencils in the equal vs. unequal groups task (2.2) but did not write an equation, so this is a natural extension. Uses non-fluency-family second factor (×6), consistent with Sections 1-2 principle: broader factors are appropriate when all numbers are given and the visual provides counting support. Values 3 × 6 \\= 18 not used in Lesson. Distractor tile `5` tests whether student counts rows accurately.
---
### Problem EC.2: Solve Product Unknown — Reversed Form
- **Purpose:** Test whether student can solve a product unknown AND handle the reversed orientation. Two Lesson demands combined: product unknown (Section 2\\) \\+ \\= sign flexibility (Section 1).
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures: 4 groups of 7 flowers (flowers in circled clusters). Equation Builder with reversed pre-filled equation: `☐ = 4 × 7`. Palette with answer tiles: `11, 21, 24, 28` .
- **Guide:** "4 groups of 7 flowers. The equation is flipped — total goes first. Find the total and place it."
- **Prompt:** "☐ \\= 4 × 7\\. Find the total."
- **Student Action:** Equation Builder — place product tile in ☐ slot.
- **Correct Answer:** `28`
- **Answer Rationale:**
	- 28 \\= Correct (4 x 7 \\= 28; skip count by 7s: 7, 14, 21, 28\\)
	- 11 \\= Addition error (4 \\+ 7 \\= 11 — Misconception \\#10 echo: confusing groups/items with addition)
	- 21 or 24 \\= Skip-count error (stopped at 3 counts of 7 instead of 4 or counted 4 groups of 6 instead of 7\\)
- **On Correct:** "28 equals 4 times 7. Same value, both sides."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Have student skip-count by 7s four times to compute the product (7, 14, 21, 28). Distractor 11 = addition error (4+7); 21/24 = skip-count stopping too early.
**Design Note:** Reversed form is the critical \\#19 check — students who believe \\= means "answer comes next" will be confused by ☐ on the left. The Guide's "equation is flipped — total goes first" mirrors Lesson 2.3 language. The 11 distractor catches students who ADD groups \\+ items rather than multiplying — surfacing a potential structural misconception. Values not used in Lesson.

### Problem EC.3: Solve 1st-Factor Unknown
- **Purpose:** Test whether student can solve a 1st-factor unknown — the easier unknown position ("how many groups?") allowing skip counting by the 2nd factor. This is the CRITICAL coverage problem per the Lesson Phase 1st-Factor Coverage Note. Language prompt included alongside symbolic equation, matching Lesson 3.3 scaffolding method.
- **Visual:** Equal Groups with Pictures in Construction Mode. Equation Builder with pre-filled template: `☐ × 5 = 15`. Language prompt: "\\*\\*\\_ stacks of 5 equals 15 total." Construction area starts empty. \\+/- group control available (each new group pre-set to 5 items per the equation's known factor). Running total display: “Total: 0” MC options: `[2] [3] [4] [5]`.
- **Guide:** "How many stacks of 5 make 15? Pick the number."
- **Prompt:** "☐ × 5 \\= 15\\. How many stacks?"
- **Student Action:** MC selection (same as Lesson 3.3).
- **Correct Answer:** `3`
- **Answer Rationale:**
	- 3 \\= Correct (3 × 5 \\= 15; skip-count by 5s: 5, 10, 15 — three counts)
	- 2 \\= Skip-count error (stopped at 10, thought 2 was enough)
	- 4 \\= Skip-count error (counted one too many)
	- 5 \\= Confused unknown with the visible factor (sees "5" in the equation, places "5")
- **On Correct:** "3 stacks of 5 equals 15."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** Have student skip-count by 5s and count how many times they count to reach 15 (5, 10, 15 = 3 times). Distractor 2 = stopped at 10; Distractor 4 = counted one too many; Distractor 5 = confused unknown with the visible factor.
- **Visual on Correct:** 3 stacks of 5 items appear, building one at a time (same confirmation pattern as Lesson 3.3).
**Design Note:** Language prompt ("\\*\\*\\_ stacks of 5 equals 15 total") is essential scaffolding — it was taught this way in Lesson 3.3 and must appear the same way in Exit Check. MC keeps computation accessible. The value 3 × 5 \\= 15 appeared in Lesson 1.5 as a full equation build (standard form, all numbers given, bags context). The Exit Check tests a fundamentally different skill: identifying a missing FACTOR from a pre-filled equation in a variety context. Different cognitive type (IDENTIFY vs CREATE), different unknown position (1st factor vs none), different context (stacks vs bags). This is not recall — it's transfer.

## **PRACTICE PHASE NOTES FOR TEMPLATE GENERATION**
- Factor Range Constraint (Revised per Construction Mode)
- The factor range constraint for unknown-factor tasks is now tied to the INTERACTION METHOD, not a blanket restriction. Construction Mode allows students to iterate toward any unknown factor — the solvability constraint only applies when the construction tool is removed.
- **Unknown-factor tasks by tier:**
| **Tier** | **Factor Families** | **Interaction Method** | **Rationale** |
| **CONFIDENCE / SUPPORT** | 2s, 5s, 10s only | Construction Mode always | Maximum scaffolding: familiar facts + physical iteration |
| **BASELINE** | 2s, 5s, 10s primary | Construction Mode for 50%+ of problems; some MC with visual | Fluency families keep backward reasoning accessible; construction provides visual iteration support |
| **STRETCH** | Broader families acceptable | MC with visual present (no Construction Mode) | Students reason from visual but don't iterate; broader factors test developing fluency beyond M9 families |
| **CHALLENGE** | Broader families acceptable | MC without visual (equation-first; visual confirms after answer) | True fact-based reasoning without visual or construction support |
- **Unknown-product tasks** (3 × 4 \\= ☐): Broader factor ranges acceptable at all tiers. Students can count the visual or skip-count to find the total. No change from original constraint.
- **Key principle:** The visual basis of building/seeing groups MUST be present for CONFIDENCE, SUPPORT, and the majority of BASELINE. Construction Mode is the taught strategy — removing it is a difficulty increase, not a neutral formatting choice.
### Tier Differentiation for M10
Tiers differentiate across three dimensions: factor families, interaction method, and scaffolding. Construction Mode removal is itself a tier increase.
- **CONFIDENCE:** Fluency families only, Construction Mode, language prompts visible, familiar contexts
- **SUPPORT:** Fluency families only, Construction Mode, language prompts visible, mixed contexts
- **BASELINE:** Fluency families primary, Construction Mode for 50%+ of unknown-factor tasks (remainder: MC with visual), both equation orientations, language prompts for factor unknowns
- **STRETCH:** Broader factor families acceptable, MC with visual (no Construction Mode), reduced language scaffolding, less common contexts, tighter distractors
- **CHALLENGE:** Broader factor families acceptable, MC without visual (equation-first, visual confirms after answer), no language prompts, all three unknown positions mixed
### Context Variety in Practice
Practice should use full variety contexts (NOT bags/boxes). Bags/boxes are retired after Lesson Section 1\\. Practice reinforces the generalization that equal groups appear everywhere.
### Equal vs. Unequal Groups Identification (1-2 templates)
At least one practice template should target equal vs. unequal group identification — extending Lesson Interaction 2.2. Template types:
- **"Which shows equal groups?"** — Multi-select from 3-4 visuals. Mix of equal and unequal groupings using variety contexts. Student selects all that could be written as a multiplication equation. BASELINE tier.
- **"What's wrong with this picture?"** (metacognitive variant) — Show an unequal grouping alongside a proposed equation (e.g., image shows groups of 5, 3, 5 with equation `3 × 5 = 15`). Student identifies whether the equation matches. MC options: "Yes, it matches" / "No — the groups aren't equal" / "No — the total is wrong" / "No — the number of groups is wrong." STRETCH tier.
