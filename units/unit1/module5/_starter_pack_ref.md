## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

Students must understand that scale selection follows a decision sequence: **first, check which scales fit your biggest number; then, choose the most efficient (easiest to read) scale.** For large data ranges, bigger scales (5 or 10\) are more efficient. For small data ranges, scale of 2 (or 1\) may be both practical and precise.

**Critical Misconception:** Wrong Scale Selection (\#4) — Students choose inefficient scales (too small, so data doesn't fit) or impossible scales (can't represent data precisely).

**Success Indicator:** Given a dataset with values like 15, 30, 45, 70, student correctly identifies that scale of 10 works and can create the graph accurately. Given a dataset like 7, 12, 19, 23, student correctly identifies that scale of 2 works well and can create the graph accurately. Given a dataset like 63, 98, 37, 45, student correctly identifies scale of 10 as the best choice and explains: "The biggest number is 98, so I need scale of 10—it's the most efficient even though some numbers won't land exactly on lines."

**Biggest Risk:** Students who don't understand the decision sequence may always default to scale of 2 (thinking it's "safer" for precision) even when the data range makes it impractical, or may ignore fit entirely and choose an inefficient scale. M6 problem-solving requires flexible scale reading—students need to understand that estimation is acceptable for larger data.

*Verbatim from OUR Curriculum — Script Must Achieve These*

**Module Goal (Student-Facing):** "You know how to use scales of 1, 2, 5, and 10\. But which one should you CHOOSE? Today you'll learn to pick the best scale for your data—and explain why it's the smart choice."

* Can student justify their scale choice using data characteristics (range, multiples)?

* Can student apply scale selection to create a bar graph?

### **1.1.2 Module Bridges**

**From Module 4:** Students mastered bar graph creation with scale of 10\. They learned interpolation ("half of 10 \= 5") for reading values between tick marks. They have formal "axis" vocabulary and fluency with both orientations. M4 Synthesis asked: "Bars work great for big numbers. But how do you CHOOSE which scale?" Students are primed for strategic thinking.

**This Module:** Students shift from FOLLOWING a given scale to CHOOSING the appropriate scale. Key learning:

* Data range determines scale feasibility (highest value must fit)
* Data characteristics determine precision requirements (non-multiples of 5 require scale of 2\)
* Efficiency matters (bigger scale \= fewer tick marks \= easier to read)
* Scale Preview System lets students see consequences before committing
* Justification is part of the skill—students explain their reasoning

**To Module 6:** Students will apply scale selection AND graph reading to solve one-step and two-step comparison problems. M5's strategic foundation ensures students can flexibly read any scaled graph they encounter in M6, not just graphs they created.

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

* Scale selection decision sequence: (1) Fit—which scales can accommodate the biggest number? (2) Efficiency—which is easiest to read? (When data is large, efficiency takes priority over precision)
* "Preview before commit" strategy using Scale Preview System
* Pattern recognition: Large range (70+) → scale of 10; Medium range (25-70) with multiples of 5 → scale of 5 or 10; Small range (under 25\) → scale of 2 is often both practical and precise; Very small range → scale of 1 is an option
* Justification language: "I chose scale of \_\_\_ because..."
* "Doesn't fit" concept: what happens when scale is too small (bar goes off graph)
* "Non-multiples recognition: For CREATION tasks, small-range data that doesn't end in 0 or 5 → scale of 2\. For JUSTIFICATION tasks, large-range non-multiples → explain why scale of 10 is most efficient."
* Creating bar graphs with self-selected scale
* Both horizontal and vertical orientations
* Task-to-data alignment: When students CREATE graphs, data must be clean multiples of the appropriate scale. When students JUSTIFY scale choice (without creating), data may include non-multiples to test reasoning about efficiency vs. precision.
* All four scales available: 1, 2, 5, 10

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* Picture graph creation (bar graphs only—reduces cognitive load)
* Scales other than 1, 2, 5, 10 (e.g., scale of 3, scale of 4\)
* "Graphing tasks with imprecise data (if students CREATE a graph, data must be clean multiples of the chosen scale)"
* Two-step problem solving (M6)
* New axis vocabulary (established in M3-M4)
* Multiplication notation (M7+)
* Graphing tasks with imprecise data (creation always uses clean multiples)

⚠️ **TASK-DATA ALIGNMENT:** When students CREATE graphs, data must be clean multiples of the chosen scale (bars land exactly or half values for scale of 2 and 10). When students JUSTIFY scale choice without creating, data may include non-multiples to test reasoning about efficiency. Students never graph imprecisely—but they DO learn that efficiency sometimes matters more than precision.

⚠️ **CUMULATIVE COMPLEXITY:** Early activities use multiples of 5; mid activities introduce non-multiples; late activities feature mixed datasets requiring careful analysis. Complexity increases throughout—never simplifies.

⚠️ **STRATEGIC OVER PROCEDURAL:** M5 is about meta-learning (WHEN to use which tool), not procedural practice (HOW to use the tool). Students already know how to create bar graphs—now they learn when to use which scale.

⚠️ **DATA DESIGN RULE:**

* **Creation tasks:** Data must be multiples of the target scale (students place bars exactly). Half values are permitted for scale of 2 and 10 (students place bars exactly at halfway).
* **Justification tasks:** Data may include non-multiples (students explain why larger scale is most efficient despite imprecision)
* **Never ask students to graph data imprecisely** — if they're creating, the data is clean

## **1.3 VOCABULARY ARCHITECTURE**

**Assessment Vocabulary (appears on STAAR):** scale, scaled bar graph, data, category

**Instructional Vocabulary (used to teach, not assessed):** axis, range, efficient/efficiency (or "easier to read"), appropriate, fit

**Command Words (students will encounter):** explain, justify, choose, compare

### **Vocabulary Staging by Phase**

| Phase | Terms | Introduction Approach |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Warmup** | "range" (informal), "fits," "works," "easier to read" | Use naturally: "Look at your data. What's the biggest number? That tells us what scales might work." |
| **Lesson Section 1** | "doesn't fit," "easier to read" | Introduce during poor-choice demonstration: "The bar goes off the graph—it doesn't fit. The scale is too small." |
| **Lesson Section 2** | "multiples of 5," "end in 0 or 5" | Formalize during non-multiples introduction: "Numbers that end in 0 or 5 are multiples of 5." |
| **Lesson Section 3** | (All terms) | Expect student use in justification tasks |
| **Exit Check** | (All terms) | Use in question contexts |
| **Synthesis** | (All terms) | Consolidate patterns; students articulate decision rules |

### **Terms to Avoid**

* "best" as absolute (multiple scales can be appropriate—we discuss tradeoffs)
* "estimate" or "about" for bar placement (precision required)
* "overflow" (not age-appropriate; use "doesn't fit")
* "messy data" (not student-facing; describe as "not multiples of 5" or "numbers that don't end in 0 or 5")
* Technical terms beyond Grade 3 (e.g., "domain," "interval")

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Wrong Scale Selection (PRIMARY) — Misconception \#4**

**Trigger Behavior:** Student selects scale of 1 for data ranging 0-85 (inefficient, tedious), or scale of 10 for data like 7, 12, 19 (impossible to show precisely).

**Why It Happens:**

* No explicit instruction on HOW to choose scales (M2-M4 always provided the scale)
* Preference for "bigger is better" (scale of 10 seems more impressive)
* Defaulting to most recently learned scale (scale of 10 from M4)
* Not checking if data values can actually be represented precisely on chosen scale

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Preview System:** Students see consequences before committing—"doesn't fit" is visible, imprecision is flagged
2. **Guide demonstrates failure first:** "Let's try scale of 5 for this data... oh no, the bar went off the graph\!"
3. **Explicit decision sequence:** "1. What's my biggest number? 2\. Can ALL my numbers be shown exactly? 3\. Which scale is easiest to read?"
4. **Non-multiples encounters:** Multiple activities where scale of 2 works better because of precision—builds recognition pattern

**Remediation Approach (Light):** "Check the biggest number. Does it fit? Now check—can every number land exactly on a line or halfway between lines?"

---

### **1.4.2 Ignoring Precision Requirements (SECONDARY)**

**Trigger Behavior:** Student chooses scale of 5 or 10 for data containing values like 7, 13, 23—values that can't be shown exactly on those scales.

**Why It Happens:**

* Focus on "efficiency" without checking "precision"
* Assuming all numbers work on all scales
* Not connecting "multiples of 5" concept to scale selection

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Explicit identification:** Guide teaches: "Numbers that end in 0 or 5 are multiples of 5\. Other numbers can be shown more precisely with scale of 2."
2. **Preview shows the problem:** "Where would 7 go? It's between 5 and 10... but WHERE exactly? We can't show it precisely."
3. **Scale of 2 as precision tool:** "Scale of 2 works for ANY number. Half of 2 is 1, so we can always be exact."
4. **Reinforcement across activities:** Non-multiples of 5 appear in mid AND late activities, not just once

## **1.5 TOY SPECIFICATIONS**

### **1.5.1 Scale Preview System (NEW IN M5)**

**Scale Preview (Bar Graph Mode 3\)** — A mode of Bar Graphs (and Picture Graphs) that allows students to see how the same data renders at different scales before committing to a choice. Can be activated by student (button), Guide (demonstration), or system (remediation). In M5, this is the primary teaching mechanism.
---

### **1.5.2 Bar Graphs (Mode 2: Creating)**

**Purpose:** Students create bar graphs with SELF-SELECTED scale (vs. M3-M4 where scale was given).

#### **Core Specifications**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Scales Available** | 1:1, 1:2, 1:5, 1:10 (student chooses after preview) |
| **Orientations** | Both horizontal and vertical |
| **Categories** | 4-5 categories |
| **Axis Display** | Tick marks at scale increments; axis extends to accommodate largest value |
| **Ghost Gridlines** | Available for scaffolding |
| **Snap Behavior** | Snap-to-scale (bars land exactly on tick marks or halfway points) |
| **Interaction** | Click-to-set (hover shows preview, click confirms). NOT drag. Consistent with M3–M4. |
| **Snap Behavior** | Configurable per interaction and per selected scale. Scale of 1: snap-to-1. Scale of 2: snap-to-1. Scale of 5: snap-to-5. Scale of 10: snap-to-5. Ghost gridlines match snap increment. |

#### **M5-Specific Constraints**

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Require scale selection before creation begins | Allow creation without explicit scale choice |
| Use same interaction patterns as M4 (familiarity) | Introduce new creation mechanics |
| Enable both orientations | Restrict to one orientation |
| Show axis labels clearly | Hide scale information after selection |

---

### **1.5.3 Data Tables**

**Purpose:** Standard numeric data display serving as source for scale selection and graph creation.

#### **Core Specifications**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Structure** | 2-column (Category vs Value/Count/Frequency) |
| **Rows** | 4-5 categories |
| **Values** | Varied by activity (see Data Constraints below) |
| **Interaction** | Read-only reference |

#### **M5 Data Constraints by Activity Phase**

| Phase | Data Characteristics | Example Values |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Early** | Clear digit signals; moderate range | 7, 12, 19, 24 (Scale 2\) / 20, 40, 60, 80 (Scale 10)| All four |
| **Mid** | Mixed digits; larger ranges; trade-offs  | 15, 22, 35, 48 | All four |
| **Late** | Ambiguous; justification required | Varied | All four  |

**Cumulative Complexity:** Late activities do NOT simplify. Students encounter the full range of data types and must apply complete decision-making process.

---

### **1.5.4 Data Collection Game**

**Purpose:** Generate personalized data for Warmup scale exploration.

#### **M5-Specific Configuration**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Categories** | 3-4 (e.g., colored blocks, items to sort) |
| **Value Range** | 10-45 (ensures all three scales can work) |
| **Value Types** | **Multiples of 5 only** (10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45\) |
| **Rationale** | Clean data in Warmup; non-multiples reserved for Lesson Section 2 |

---

### **Interaction Constraints (All Toys)**

* NO verbal/spoken student responses — Guide speaks, student acts
* NO keyboard/text input — all responses via click/tap/drag or multiple choice
* NO open-ended typed answers — use selection or action tasks
* Justification via multiple choice with reasoning options (e.g., "I chose scale of 5 because: A) It's the biggest B) All values are multiples of 5 C)The values all fit on my graph")

## **1.6 WARMUP (3-4 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

---

### **Purpose**

Activate prior knowledge of all four scales (1, 2, 5, 10\) from M1-M4 while introducing the key M5 question: "Which scale should you CHOOSE?" Students collect personal data, then use the Scale Preview System to see that multiple scales can work for the same data. The discovery that "you have options" sets up Lesson's teaching of selection criteria.

---

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 3 interactions \+ Bridge |
| Active Participation | 3 of 4 require student response |
| Format | Data Collection Game → Scale Preview Exploration → Efficiency Comparison → Bridge |
| Cognitive Load | 20-30% (exploration and comparison are accessible) |
| Remediation | Light only (or none—low stakes) |
| Tone | Medium-High energy, curious, inviting |
| Engagement Anchors | Narrative Setup (Minis), Personalization (their data), Choice/Agency (which scale?) |

---

### **Constraints**

| USE | AVOID |
| :---- | :---- |
| Informal language ("Check this out," "Let's see") | Formal phrasing ("Observe the following") |
| All four scale names (familiar from M1-M4) | Teaching selection criteria (save for Lesson) |
| Observable acknowledgments ("You picked Scale of 10") | Assumed states ("You noticed the efficiency") |
| 3-4 categories | 2 or 5+ categories |
| **Multiples of 5 only** (10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45\) | Non-multiples (save for Lesson Section 2\) |
| Values where both Scale 5 and Scale 10 work | Values that force only one scale option |
| "works" / "fits" / "easier to read" (informal) | "efficient" / "appropriate" / "precision" (formal—save for Lesson) |

---

### **Warmup Type Rationale**

**Standard Playbook Types:** Binary Choice, WODB, Number Talk, Notice & Wonder, Estimation 180, Visual Patterns, Mystery Reveal

**M5 Approach:** Data Collection Game \+ Scale Preview Exploration

**Why this serves the concept:**

* Unit 1 is about DATA—personal data creates investment
* M5's core question is CHOICE: which scale should you use?
* Scale Preview System is the new tool—Warmup introduces it informally with low stakes
* Students experience "multiple options work" before Lesson teaches decision criteria
* Follows Playbook PRINCIPLES (low cognitive load, engagement anchors, judgment task, bridge) while adapting STRUCTURE for strategic thinking

**Test:** If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for Lesson? YES—they would miss the framing that scale selection involves choices, making Lesson feel like arbitrary rules rather than useful decision-making.

**Interaction W.1: Data Collection Game**

* **Purpose:** Generate personalized data. Create investment through Minis context. Provide concrete numbers for scale comparison.
* **Hook (first 15-20 seconds):**
  * **Visual:** Minis characters appear in animated counting scenario
  * **Guide:** "The Minis are organizing their collections\! Let's help them count."
* **Engagement Anchor:** Narrative Setup (Minis), Personalization (their counts)
* **Game Specifications:**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Style | Counting/sorting game |
| Categories | 4 (e.g., Red blocks, Blue blocks, Green blocks, Yellow blocks) |
| Values | **Multiples of 5 only:** 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45 |
| Duration | \~45-60 seconds |
| Student Interaction | Count items per category; confirm counts |

* **On Complete:** Counts recorded. Guide: "You counted \[results\]. Now let's make a graph with your data."

---

**Interaction W.2: Scale Preview Exploration**

* **Purpose:** Introduce Scale Preview System informally. Students discover that multiple scales can work for the same data.
* **Visual:**
  * Data Table showing student's collected data
  * "Scale Preview System with four clickable buttons: 'Scale of 1' / 'Scale of 2' / 'Scale of 5' / 'Scale of 10'
  * Vertical bar graph displays as the scale button is clicked
* **Guide:** "You know how to use different scales. But which one should you use for YOUR data?"
* **Guide:** "Let's find out. Click each scale to see what the graph would look like."
* **Student Action:** Explore all four scales freely
  * Scale of 1: ✓ All fit (but lots of tick marks\!)
  * Scale of 2: ✓ All fit
  * Scale of 5: ✓ All fit
  * Scale of 10: ✓ All fit
* **Guide (after exploration):** "All four scales work for this data\! You have choices."
* **Engagement Anchor:** Choice/Agency (student controls exploration)

---

**Interaction W.3: Efficiency Comparison**

* **Purpose:** Surface the idea that some scales are easier to read than others—without formally teaching "efficiency."
* **Visual:** Side-by-side display showing student's data at Scale of 5 AND Scale of 10
* **Guide:** "Both of these work. Take a look at them side by side. Which graph is easier to read?"
* **Prompt:** "Which graph is easier to read?"
* **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: "Scale of 5" / "Scale of 10" / "They're the same"\]
* **On Any Selection:** Guide validates without declaring a "right" answer:
  * (Scale of 10): "You picked Scale of 10\. Nice and clean\!"
  * (Scale of 5): "You picked Scale of 5\. More detail\!"
  * (Same): "You think they're both easy to read. Both do work\!"
* **Guide:** "When more than one scale works, you get to choose."
* **Engagement Anchor:** Choice/Agency (genuine preference question)

---

**Interaction W.4: Bridge to Lesson**

* **Purpose:** Create anticipation for Lesson by posing the problem Lesson will solve.
* **Visual:** Scale Preview System remains visible
* **Guide:** "You just saw that all four scales worked for your data. Nice to have options\!"
* **Guide:** "But what if the numbers are bigger? Or what if you need to figure out which scale is BEST?"
* **Bridge Statement:** "Let's learn how to figure out which scale to choose—and what tells us that a scale won’t work for the data we have."
* **No student prompt** (transition)

## **1.7 LESSON (10-12 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

---

### **Core Purpose**

Teach students to strategically select the appropriate scale (1, 2, 5, or 10\) for a given dataset. Students learn a decision sequence: (1) FIT—the scale must accommodate the largest value, (2) EFFICIENCY—choose the scale that's easiest to read, and (3) PRECISION—for small-range data, scale of 2 shows all values exactly. Students see consequences of poor choices and apply learning to create graphs with self-selected scales.
---

### **Pedagogical Flow**

M5 is meta-learning: students already know HOW to use each scale (M1-M4); now they learn WHEN to use each.

| CRA Phase | M5 Application |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Concrete** | Scale Preview System shows visual consequences of choices (doesn't fit, can't be exact) |
| **Relational** | Students connect data characteristics (range, multiples) to scale appropriateness |
| **Abstract** | Formalize selection criteria: "Check fit, choose most efficient, verify precision when practical" |
| **Application** | Independent scale selection \+ graph creation with justification |

---

| Section | Focus | Interactions | Orientation | Data Type |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | Range & Efficiency: Why Scale Matters | 4 | Vertical | Multiples of 5 (large range) |
| Section 2 | Smaller Scales for Smaller Data | 6 | Both | Small range: non-multiples \+ very small |
| Section 3 | Independent Selection & Creation | 3 | Both | Mixed |

### **Required Phrases (Must Appear in Script)**

- "doesn't fit" (introduced in 1.2 when scale is too small)
- "multiples of 5" or "end in 0 or 5" (introduced in 2.3)
- "Scale of 2 works for any numbers" (stated in 2.2)
- "easier to read" or "easiest to read" (introduced in 1.4)
- "Check the biggest number" (stated in 1.4, bridge)

### **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**

- ❌ "overflow" (not age-appropriate; use "doesn't fit")
- ❌ "messy data" (not student-facing; describe the characteristic instead)
- ❌ "best scale" as absolute (multiple scales can be appropriate)
- ❌ "estimate" or "about" for bar placement during CREATION tasks (creation uses clean data)
- ❌ "guess" where to put the bar (students should know exactly where bars go)
-

---

### **Misconception Prevention**

**\#4 Wrong Scale Selection (PRIMARY):**

- Worked example shows "doesn't fit" FIRST (1.2)—students see consequences before attempting
- Explicit "check biggest number" instruction (1.4)
- Preview system gives feedback BEFORE commitment
- Justification prompts require reasoning, not just selection

**\#5 Variant: Can't Recognize Imprecision (TERTIARY):**

- Guide names the problem: "can't be shown exactly"
- Side-by-side comparison of multiples vs. non-multiples of 5 (2.3)
- Multiple exposures to non-multiples (2.1, 2.4, 3.2)

### **1.7.5 Success Criteria**

**The One Thing:** Students understand that scale selection is a strategic choice based on data characteristics—bigger ranges need bigger scales, and non-multiples of 5 require scale of 2\.

**Ready for Exit Check:** Student can:

1. Identify when a scale is too small (biggest number doesn't fit)
2. Recognize when numbers aren't multiples of 5 (need scale of 2\)
3. Select appropriate scale using Preview System
4. Justify choice with reasoning
5. Create bar graph with self-selected scale

**Ready for Module 6:** Student can flexibly select scales for any dataset, enabling them to focus on problem-solving with data rather than graph construction mechanics.

### **Opening Frame**

- **Purpose:** Signal shift from Exit Check to reflection/connection.
- **Visual:** Clean transition screen.
- **Guide:** "You learned to choose scales. You checked if the biggest number fits. You checked the last digits. Now let's connect what you discovered."

**Task S.1: Decision Checklist Consolidation (Type B \- Consolidation)**

- **Purpose:** Explicitly consolidate the two-question decision process students learned across Sections 1 and 2\.
- **Visual (Step 1):** Data Table: "Points Scored" — Ana: 20, Ben: 35, Cara: 55, Dan: 80\. Scale Preview shows Scale of 5 selected → ❌ "80 doesn't fit"
- **Guide:** "Here's the first question you learned: Does the biggest number fit?"
- **Guide:** "Dan scored 80\. That's too big for Scale of 5\. You'd need a bigger scale."
- **Visual (Step 2):** Data Table: "Stickers Collected" — Eli: 7, Fay: 12, Gus: 19, Hal: 23\. Scale Preview shows Scale of 5 selected → ⚠️ "Can't be shown exactly"
- **Guide:** "Here's the second question: Do all the numbers end in 0 or 5?"
- **Guide:** "7, 12, 19, 23—none of them end in 0 or 5\. Scale of 2 is the only one that works exactly. When you look at new data, which question do you check FIRST?"
- **Prompt:** "Which question should you check FIRST?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: "Does the biggest number fit?" / "Do the numbers end in 0 or 5?"\]
- **Correct Answer:** "Does the biggest number fit?"
- **On Correct:** "Right. Check if it fits first—that tells you which scales are even possible. Then check the digits."

### **Task S.2: Scale Strengths (Type A \- Pattern Discovery)**

- **Purpose:** Help students SEE what each scale is "good for" through visual comparison.
- **Visual:** Four bar graphs side-by-side, ALL showing the same data (Apples: 20, Bananas: 40, Cherries: 60):
  - Graph A: Scale of 1 (most tick marks, very detailed)
  - Graph B: Scale of 2 (many tick marks)
  - Graph C: Scale of 5 (medium tick marks)
  - Graph D: Scale of 10 (fewest tick marks, clean)
- **Guide:** "Here's the same data shown three different ways. Look at the differences."
- **Guide:** "Scale of 1 has the most lines. Scale of 10 has the fewest. All four show the same information. Which graph is easiest to read?"
- **Prompt:** "Which graph is easiest to read?"
- **Student Action:** \[Click Graph A / Graph B / Graph C / Graph D\]
- **Correct Answer:** Graph D (Scale of 10\)
- **On Correct:** "Scale of 10 is easiest to read here—fewer lines to count. When all your numbers end in 0 or 5, the bigger scale is usually cleaner."
- **On Incorrect (A, B, or C):** "That one works\! But look at Scale of 10—fewer lines makes it quicker to read. When numbers are multiples of 5, bigger scales are often easier."
- **Guide (connection):** "Scale of 1 and 2 are for smaller data—they show every number exactly. Scale of 5 and 10 are for bigger data—they're cleaner and easier to read."

### **Task S.3: Real-World Connection (Type C \- Real-World Bridge)**

- **Purpose:** Connect scale selection to authentic data by examining actual data tables.
- **Visual:** Three data tables displayed:
  - Table A: "Daily Steps" — Mon: 4,500 / Tue: 3,200 / Wed: 5,100 / Thu: 2,800
  - Table B: "Coins Collected" — Jar 1: 7 / Jar 2: 12 / Jar 3: 19 / Jar 4: 23
  - Table C: "Books Read" — Room A: 25 / Room B: 30 / Room C: 35 / Room D: 40
- **Guide:** "People collect data about all kinds of things. Look at these three tables. Which data would HAVE to use Scale of 2?"
- **Prompt:** "Which data would HAVE to use Scale of 2?"
- **Student Action:** \[Click Table A / Table B / Table C\]
- **Correct Answer:** Table B (Coins: 7, 12, 19, 23\)
- **On Correct:** "The coin counts—7, 12, 19, 23\. None of them end in 0 or 5, so Scale of 2 is the only way to show them exactly."
- **On Incorrect (A):** "Look at the step counts—4,500, 3,200, 5,100, 2,800. These are very large numbers and would need a larger scale. Which has smaller numbers to graph exactly with scale of 2?."
- **On Incorrect (C):** "The book counts all end in 0 or 5 so scales of 5 and 10 both work. Check the coins."
- **Guide (connection):** "Remember this quick check: Do all the numbers end in 0 or 5? If yes, you have options. If no and the numbers are small, Scale of 2 is your go-to."

### **Task S.4: Metacognitive Reflection (Type 1 \- Strategy Identification)**

- **Purpose:** Help students recognize effective strategies and reinforce the two key questions.
- **Visual:** Scale Preview System shown with all three scale buttons visible.
- **Guide:** "What helped you most when deciding which scale to use?"
- **Prompt:** What helped you most when deciding which scale to use?
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice:
  - "Checking the biggest number first"
  - "Looking at the last digits"
  - "Trying the preview to see what happens"
  - "Thinking about which graph would be easiest to read"\]
- **On Selection:** Guide validates, then coaches toward key strategies:
  - (Biggest number): "That's one of the two key questions. Checking the biggest number tells you right away which scales can even work."
  - (Last digits): "That's one of the key checks. Spotting those last digits tells you whether you have multiples of 5 or need a smaller scale like 1 or 2."
  - (Preview): "The preview is helpful\! And here's a shortcut: ask yourself the two key questions first—Does the biggest number fit without too many lines? Are the numbers multiples of 5? Those two checks guide you every time."
  - (Easiest to read): "That matters once you know your options. Start with the two key questions: Does the biggest number fit? Are the numbers multiples of 5? Then pick what's easiest to read without too many lines."

### **Identity-Building Closure**

- **Purpose:** Specific, growth-oriented affirmation connecting to future learning.
- **Visual:** Brief display of a completed bar graph with Scale of 10\.
- **Guide:** "You learned to CHOOSE scales—not just use them. You check if the biggest number fits. You check the last digits to look for multiples of 5\. Then you pick what's easiest to read without too many lines."
- **Guide:** "That's strategic thinking. You're not just following rules—you're making decisions."
- **Bridge to M6:** "Next time, you'll use your graphs to solve problems and answer questions. The scale you choose will help you find answers faster."

**Key Design Decisions:**

- Decision sequence: (1) Does biggest number fit? (2) Which scale is easiest to read? (3) For small data, can we achieve precision? ...
- Section 2 models full decision sequence for small data, introducing Scales 1 and 2 as "smaller scales for smaller data"
- Scale Preview System as central teaching tool—students see consequences before committing
- "Doesn't fit" language replaces "overflow" (more age-appropriate and mathematically accurate)
- "Multiples of 5" / "end in 0 or 5" replaces "messy data" (student-facing, descriptive)
- Decision sequence: (1) Does biggest number fit? (2) What do the last digits tell you? (3) Which scale is easiest to read?
- Efficiency taught as "easier to read" rather than formal "efficient" vocabulary
- Bar graphs only (no picture graphs)—reduces cognitive load, focuses on scale selection
- Section 1 uses only multiples of 5 to isolate range/efficiency concept
- Section 2 introduces non-multiples to teach precision requirement
- Section 3 uses mixed data for full independent application
- Warmup activates "you have options" discovery before Lesson teaches decision criteria
- Synthesis consolidates the two-question process with visual support (not text-heavy)
- M6 bridge emphasizes that scale selection enables problem-solving focus

## SPIRAL REVIEW COMPONENT

### Position

Before M5 Lesson begins (can be end of Warmup or dedicated pre-lesson activity)

### Purpose

Reactivate scales 2 and 5 before students must compare/select between all three scales. M4 focused exclusively on scale 10; students haven't actively used scales 2 and 5 since M2 and M3 respectively.

### Structure (3-4 interactions, \~2-3 minutes)

INTERACTION 1: Scale of 2 Quick Recall

- Show a simple picture graph, scale of 2
- "Each symbol represents 2\. How many total?" \[6 symbols → 12\]
- Light touch, activates memory

INTERACTION 2: Scale of 5 Quick Recall

- Show a simple bar graph, scale of 5
- "The scale is 5\. What value does this bar show?" \[bar at 3rd line → 15\]
- Confirms retrieval

INTERACTION 3: Scale of 10 Quick Recall

- Show a bar graph, scale of 10
- "The scale is 10\. What value?" \[bar between 20-30 → 25, tests interpolation\]
- Bridges to M4 learning

INTERACTION 4 (optional): Side-by-Side Preview

- Show same data displayed in all three scales
- "Here's the same information shown three different ways. Today we'll learn how to choose which scale works best."
- Sets up the M5 learning goal

### Validation

Students who struggle on Interactions 1-3 may need M2/M3/M4 Practice revisit before M5 Lesson. Flag for routing consideration.
