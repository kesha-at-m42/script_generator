## **1.7 LESSON (10-12 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

---

### **Core Purpose**

Teach students to strategically select the appropriate scale (1, 2, 5, or 10) for a given dataset. Students learn a decision sequence: (1) FIT—the scale must accommodate the largest value, (2) EFFICIENCY—choose the scale that's easiest to read, and (3) PRECISION—for small-range data, scale of 2 shows all values exactly. Students see consequences of poor choices and apply learning to create graphs with self-selected scales.

---

### **Pedagogical Flow**

M5 is meta-learning: students already know HOW to use each scale (M1-M4); now they learn WHEN to use each.

| CRA Phase | M5 Application |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Concrete** | Scale Preview System shows visual consequences of choices (doesn't fit, can't be exact) |
| **Relational** | Students connect data characteristics (range, multiples) to scale appropriateness |
| **Abstract** | Formalize selection criteria: "Check fit, choose most efficient, verify precision when practical" |
| **Application** | Independent scale selection + graph creation with justification |

---

### **Lesson Structure**

| Section | Focus | Interactions | Orientation | Data Type |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | Range & Efficiency: Why Scale Matters | 4 | Vertical | Multiples of 5 (large range) |
| Section 2 | Smaller Scales for Smaller Data | 6 | Both | Small range: non-multiples + very small |
| Section 3 | Independent Selection & Creation | 3 | Both | Mixed |

**Total: 13 interactions**

### **Orientation Strategy**

| Section | Orientation | Rationale |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Section 1** | Vertical | Familiar orientation while learning new concepts |
| **Section 2** | Both | Guide acknowledges switch. Build flexibility while discovering precision requirement |
| **Section 3** | Both | Prove flexibility |

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Range & Efficiency — Why Scale Matters**

**Purpose:** Establish that scale selection has consequences. Students see what happens when a scale needs too many tick marks and learn to check if their biggest value fits cleanly. All data is multiples of 5 to focus on range/efficiency before introducing precision.

**Orientation:** All vertical

---

**Interaction 1.1: Transition from Warmup**

- **Visual:** Brief callback—Scale Preview System from Warmup showing student's data with all four scales working (all show ✓).
- **Guide:** "In Warmup, all four scales worked for your data. You had options!"
- **Guide:** "But what happens when the numbers are bigger? Let's find out."
- **No student prompt** (transition framing)

---

**Interaction 1.2: When the Scale Needs Too Many Tick Marks [WORKED EXAMPLE]**

- **Visual:** NEW data context: "Books Read This Month"
  - Data Table: Aisha: 20, Ben: 35, Carlos: 55, Dana: 80
  - Scale Preview System with "Scale of 5" pre-selected
  - Vertical graph currently shows axis 0-50, initially empty
- **Guide:** "Here's data about how many books four students read this month. Dana read the most—80 books!"
- **Guide [Think-Aloud]:** "Let me try Scale of 5 and see what happens."
- **Animation:** Preview generates. Aisha (20), Ben (35), Carlos (55) bars appear normally. Dana's bar (80) extends PAST the current graph boundary. ⚠️ indicator appears.
- **Guide:** "Hmm. Carlos's bar is just above my graph, but Dana's bar goes past the top. The graph only goes up to 50 right now."
- **Guide [Think-Aloud]:** "I COULD make the graph taller to fit 80. Let's see... I'd need 55, 60, 65, 70, 75, 80..."
- **Animation:** Axis extends upward, showing tick marks appearing: 55, 60, 65, 70, 75, 80. The axis now looks crowded with many tick marks.
- **Guide:** "That's a LOT of numbers on the axis. 0, 5, 10, 15, 20... all the way up to 80. That's 17 tick marks!"
- **Visual Highlight:** Axis shown with all 17 tick marks visible (0, 5, 10, 15... 75, 80)
- **Guide:** "It works... but is there a better way?"
- **Key Teaching Point:** "When one scale needs too many tick marks, try a bigger scale."
- **No student prompt** (observation—demonstration teaches)

---

**Interaction 1.3: Trying a Bigger Scale**

- **Visual:** Same "Books Read" data. Scale Preview System now interactive. The crowded Scale of 5 graph still visible.
- **Guide:** "Scale of 5 works, but that's a lot of tick marks. Let's try a bigger scale."
- **Prompt:** "Click 'Scale of 10' to see what happens."
- **Student Action:** Click "Scale of 10" button
- **Preview displays:** All four bars fit cleanly. ✓ indicator. Axis shows 0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80—only 9 tick marks.
- **Guide:** "Look at that—only 9 tick marks now instead of 17. Much cleaner!"
- **Guide:** "All the data fits, and the graph is easier to read."

---

**Interaction 1.4: Range Check + Efficiency**

- **Visual:** Same data with Scale of 10 preview showing. Largest value (80) highlighted in data table.
- **Guide:** "Here's the first thing to check: What's your BIGGEST number? That tells you what scales might work."
- **Prompt:** "What's the biggest number in this data?"
- **Student Action:** [Multiple choice: 20 / 35 / 55 / 80]
- **Correct Answer:** 80
- **On Correct:** "80 is the biggest. Always check the biggest number first—does it fit on the scale without too many lines on the axis?"
- **Remediation (Light):** "Look at all four numbers. Which one is the greatest?"
- **Guide:** "You saw that Scale of 5 needed 17 tick marks. Scale of 10 only needs 9. For big data, bigger scales make cleaner graphs."
- **Key Teaching Point:** "When more than one scale fits, pick the one that's easier to read."

**→ SECTION 1 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 2.**

---

### **[SECTION TRANSITION]**

- **Visual:** Clean transition screen.
- **Guide:** "You learned to check if the biggest number fits. But there's something else to check..."

### **1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Smaller Scales for Smaller Data**

**Purpose:** Teach when smaller scales (1 and 2) are the best choice. Students first confirm that ALL scales FIT small data, then discover that for small numbers, we CAN achieve precision—so we SHOULD. The decision sequence (fit → efficiency → precision) is explicitly modeled for small data contexts.

**Orientation:** Both orientations used

---

**Interaction 2.1: All Scales Fit Small Data [FIT CHECK]**

* **Visual:** NEW data context: "Marbles in Jars"
  * Horizontal Data Table: Jar A: 7, Jar B: 12, Jar C: 19, Jar D: 23
  * Scale Preview System with all four buttons. Horizontal bar graphs. Initially no graph showing.
* **Guide:** "Here's data about marbles. Look at the biggest number—23. That's pretty small!"
* **Guide:** "This graph is horizontal—the bars go sideways. Let's check: which scales fit? Try clicking each scale"
* **Prompt:** "Click each scale to see if 23 fits."
* **Student Action:** Explore all four scales
  * Scale of 1: ✓ fits
  * Scale of 2: ✓ fits
  * Scale of 5: ✓ fits
  * Scale of 10: ✓ fits
* **Guide:** "All four scales fit! Let's look at Scale of 1."
* **Prompt:** "Click 'Scale of 1' to see what it looks like."
* **Student Action:** Click "Scale of 1"
* **Visual:** Preview shows all bars fit with ✓, but axis displays 24 tick marks (0-23).
* **Guide:** "It works — every number lands exactly. But look at all those lines on the side!"
* **Key Teaching Point:** "Small data = all scales fit. But some are cleaner than others."

---

**Interaction 2.2: But Which Is Best? [EFFICIENCY vs. PRECISION SETUP]**

* **Visual:** Same "Marbles" data. Scale of 1 still showing from previous interaction (24 tick marks visible).
* **Guide:** "Scale of 1 works, but that's a lot of lines. You learned bigger scales are usually easier to read."
* **Guide:** "Let's try Scale of 10."
* **Prompt:** "Click 'Scale of 10' to compare."
* **Student Action:** Click "Scale of 10"
* **Preview displays:** Bars appear with ⚠️ indicators. Jar A (7) between 0 and 10. Jar C (19) between 10 and 20.
* **Guide:** "Hmm. Scale of 10 fits... but there are only 3 marks on the axis. Look at those warnings. Can you tell the exact value of where those bars end? Someone looking at our graph without the data table would need to guess how many marbles are shown."
* **Visual Highlight:** ⚠️ on Jar A with message: "7 won't land exactly on a line or in the middle"
* **Guide:** "For big data, we'd pick Scale of 10 anyway—it's the easiest to read because we don't want too many lines on the axis. But these numbers are small and it's hard to find the exact location of the bar on the graph. We can do better."
* **Key Teaching Point:** "When data is small, you have room to find a scale where everything lands exactly."

---

**Interaction 2.3: Scale of 2 Works for Non-Multiples [PRECISION ACHIEVED]**

* **Visual:** Same "Marbles" data. Graph showing Scale of 10. Scale Preview System interactive.
* **Guide:** "Let's try a smaller scale. Try a Scale of 2."
* **Prompt:** "Click 'Scale of 2' to preview."
* **Student Action:** Click "Scale of 2" button
* **Preview displays:** All bars land exactly. ✓ indicator. No warnings.
* **Guide:** "Look—we can read the end of the bars clearly. ALL the numbers land exactly. 7 is right at 7, exactly halfway between 6 and 8. 12 is right at 12."
* **Guide:** "With Scale of 2, half of 2 is 1. So you can show ANY whole number exactly."
* **Key Teaching Point:** "Scale of 2 works to show the exact value for all numbers—and for small data, the graph won't have too many marks on the axis."

---

**Interaction 2.4: Digit Pattern Recognition [SHORTCUT]**

* **Visual:** Side-by-side comparison:
  * Left: Data Set A — 20, 35, 55, 80 (labeled "Ones digits: 0 or 5")
  * Right: Data Set B — 7, 12, 19, 23 (labeled "Ones digits: 7, 2, 9, 3")
* **Guide:** "Here's a quick way to know if you need Scale of 2. Check the last digit of each number in the ones place."
* **Guide:** "Numbers that end in 0 or 5—like 20, 35, 55, 80—those are multiples of 5 and can be shown clearly with Scales of 5 and 10."
* **Guide:** "But look at 7, 12, 19, 23. See the digits in the ones place? 7, 2, 9, 3. None are 0 or 5—that tells you they aren't multiples of 5 so Scale of 2 might be your best choice. Check our groups. Which data set has last digits that are NOT 0 or 5?"
* **Prompt:** "Which data set has last digits that are NOT 0 or 5?"
* **Student Action:** [Click Data Set A / Click Data Set B]
* **Correct Answer:** Data Set B (7, 12, 19, 23)
* **On Correct:** "Right. Look at those last digits: 7, 2, 9, 3. None are 0 or 5—that's your Scale of 2 signal."
* **Remediation (Light):** "Check the last digit of each number. Does 23 end in 0 or 5? Does 12?"

---

**Interaction 2.5: Practice with Non-Multiples**

* **Visual:** NEW data context: "Points Scored"
  * Vertical Data Table: Round 1: 22, Round 2: 15, Round 3: 8, Round 4: 31
* **Guide:** "Here's data from a game. Four rounds of points: 22, 15, 8, 31."
* **Guide:** "Check the ones place digits: 2, 5, 8, 1. Do they all end in 0 or 5?"
* **Guide:** "22... no. 15... yes! 8... no. 31... no. Three of them don't so they aren't multiples of 5. Which scale will show ALL values exactly?"
* **Prompt:** "Which scale will show ALL values exactly?"
* **Student Action:** [Multiple choice: "Scale of 1" / "Scale of 2" / "Scale of 5" / "Scale of 10"]
* **Correct Answer:** "Scale of 2"
* **On Correct:** Visual - Scale of 2 "Points Scored" vertical bar graph shown. "Scale of 2. All of the bars fit well on the graph and their exact values are clear."
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look at the last digits: 2, 5, 8, 1. Most aren't 0 or 5. Which scale works for any number?"
* **Visual (transition):** Side-by-side comparison (same height bars, only axis markings changed):
  * Scale of 2 "Points Scored" vertical bar graph
  * Scale of 1 "Points Scored" vertical bar graph
* **Guide (transition):** "Scale of 1 also shows the exact values, but it would require a lot of marks on the axis to get to the greatest value of 31. Let's consider when we might choose a scale of 1."

---

**Interaction 2.6: Very Small Data — Scale of 1**

* **Visual:** NEW data context: "Stickers Earned Today"
  * Vertical Data Table: Mia: 9, Noah: 5, Olivia: 7, Pete: 3
  * Scale Preview System available. Vertical bar graphs. No bars showing.
* **Guide:** "Look at these numbers: 9, 5, 7, 3. They're really small!"
* **Guide:** "What happens if we try Scale of 10?"
* **Prompt:** "Click 'Scale of 10'"
* **Student Action:** Click "Scale of 10" button.
* **Preview displays:** Only tick marks at 0 and 10. Bars appear with ⚠️ indicators.
* **Guide:** "The bars fit, but we don't know the exact value for any of the bars."
* **Guide:** "Think about the numbers: 9, 5, 7, 3. They don't end in 0 or 5 so we know Scale of 2 is clearer to show any number. Click 'Scale of 2.'"
* **Prompt:** "Click 'Scale of 2'"
* **Student Action:** Click "Scale of 2" button.
* **Preview displays:** All bars show ✓ indicator. Clean graph with 0-10 range.
* **Guide:** "That's better! We can see the bars end at lines or exactly halfway. Scale of 2 could work. But when numbers are this small, you have another option. Try Scale of 1. What do you notice?"
* **Prompt:** "Click 'Scale of 1'"
* **Student Action:** Click "Scale of 1" button
* **Preview displays:** All bars land exactly on tick marks. ✓ indicator. Clean graph with 0-10 range.
* **Guide:** "Every number lands right on a line. No halfway bars needed. We can read the graph accurately without having the data table."
* **Key Teaching Point:** "For very small numbers, Scale of 1 can be a great choice—simple and exact."

**→ SECTION 2 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 3.**

### **1.7.3 LESSON SECTION 3: Independent Selection & Creation**

**Purpose:** Apply learning independently. Students select appropriate scales for mixed datasets, justify their choices (multiple choice), and create bar graphs. Both orientations used.

**Orientation:** Both orientations used

---

**Interaction 3.1: Independent Selection — Multiples of 5**

- **Visual:** NEW data context: "Favorite Fruits" (HORIZONTAL orientation)
  - Vertical Data Table: Dates: 60, Cherries: 45, Bananas: 30, Apples: 15
  - Scale Preview System available
- **Guide:** "Here's data about students' favorite fruits. The most votes went to Dates with 60. Scale of 1 doesn't make sense here because it would take 60 lines on the axis - that's too much!"
- **Guide:** "Your job: pick the right scale. Which scale will you use?"
- **Student Action:** Explore Scale Preview System with 2, 5 and 10 freely (all three scales show ✓)
- **Prompt:** "Which scale will you use?"
- **Student Action:** [Select: Scale of 2 / Scale of 5 / Scale of 10]
- **Correct Answer:** Scale of 10
- **On Correct:** "Good choice."
- **On Incorrect (Scale 2 or 5):** "That scale could work, but check—all values end in 0 or 5. Which scale would be easiest to read?"
- **Prompt (justification):** "Why is Scale of 10 a good choice? Choose all of the reasons."
- **Student Action:** [Multi-Select:
  - A) "All values end in 0 or 5 so they can be graphed precisely."
  - B) "The graph is easy to read."
  - C) "The biggest number is 60 so the bars fit on the graph."
  - D) "Scale of 2 takes too many marks to reach the biggest number."]
- **Correct Answer:** All of them selected
- **On Correct:** "Exactly. All values are multiples of 5, so scales of 5 and 10 both work to read the graph clearly. For data going up to 60, Scale of 10 gives you a cleaner graph."
- **On Incorrect:** "Let's consider the questions we ask when choosing a scale. What is the biggest number? Does the data all fit? Look at all the numbers—do they end in 0 or 5 so we can find the exact location of the bars? What scale can we use for the best looking graph? All of these reasons help us choose the scale of 10."
- **Remediation (Light):** "Think of everything we need to consider when choosing a scale."

---

**Interaction 3.2: Selection + Creation — Non-Multiples**

- **Visual:** NEW data context: "Minutes of Practice" (VERTICAL orientation)
  - Vertical Data Table: Guitar: 27, Piano: 14, Violin: 41, Drums: 33
- **Guide:** "Here's data about how many minutes students practiced their instruments."
- **Guide:** "Check: are these numbers multiples of 5? Which scale is best for this data?"
- **Prompt:** "Which scale is best for this data?"
- **Student Action:** [Select: Scale of 1 / Scale of 2 / Scale of 5 / Scale of 10]
- **Correct Answer:** Scale of 2
- **On Correct:** "Scale of 2. You saw that 14, 27, 33, 41 aren't multiples of 5 so we need scale of 2 to graph them precisely. Scale of 2 can work when the largest value is 41."
- **On Incorrect (5 or 10):** "These numbers aren't multiples of 5 so the values can't be shown exactly with that scale. Try scale of 2."
- **On Incorrect (1):** "Scale of 1 lets us graph exact numbers, but the largest value is 41. That would be a lot of marks on the axis. Which scale would be exact and look cleaner?"
- **Remediation (Light):** "Check those ones digits: 7, 4, 1, 3. None are 0 or 5. Which scale works for any number?"
- **[After correct scale selection]**
- **Visual:** same data context: "Minutes of Practice" (VERTICAL orientation bar graph)
  - Vertical Data Table: Guitar: 27, Piano: 14, Violin: 41, Drums: 33
- **Guide:** "Now create the bar graph."
- **Visual:** Bar Graph creation tool with Scale of 2 active. Gridlines visible at each tick mark (consistent with standard graph display).
- **Student Action:** Set bar heights for all 4 categories (click-to-set)
- **On Complete:** Graph displays with all 4 bars correctly placed.
- **Guide:** "You chose the right scale and created the graph. Every bar lands exactly where it should."

**Interaction 3.3: Final Application**

- **Visual:** NEW data context: "Team Scores" (HORIZONTAL orientation)
  - Data Table: Red Team: 25, Green Team: 55, Yellow Team: 70, Blue Team: 40
  - Scale Preview System available
- **Guide:** "Last one! Here are scores from four teams. Yellow Team won with 70 points."
- **Guide:** "You've done this vertical and horizontal now. Same process every time: check the numbers, choose the scale."

**Step 1: Scale Selection**

- **Prompt:** "Which scale works best for this data?"
- **Student Action:** [Select: Scale of 1 / Scale of 2 / Scale of 5 / Scale of 10]
- **Correct Answer:** Scale of 10 (most efficient; all values end in 0 or 5; range up to 70)
- **On Correct:** "Good choice. All values end in 0 or 5, and the biggest is 70. Scale of 10 gives you a clean graph."
- **On Incorrect (Scale of 1 or 2):** "That would need a lot of lines on the axis. All the values end in 0 or 5—which bigger scale would be easier to read?"
- **On Incorrect (Scale of 5):** "That works! But notice the biggest value is 70. Scale of 10 would give you fewer lines and still show everything exactly."

**Step 2: Graph Creation**

- **Visual:** Bar Graph creation tool (HORIZONTAL) with Scale of 10 active. Empty graph with axis 0-80 showing tick marks at 0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80.
- **Guide:** "Now create the graph. Place each bar at the right length."

- **On Correct:** Bar locks at 25. "Right at 25—halfway between 20 and 30."
- **On Incorrect:** "25 is halfway between 20 and 30. Find that spot."
- **Prompt:** "Click to place the bar for Blue Team (40 points)."
- **Student Action:** Click-to-set at height 40
- **On Correct:** Bar locks at 40. "Right on 40."
- **Prompt:** "Click to place the bar for Green Team (55 points)."
- **Student Action:** Click-to-set at height 55
- **On Correct:** Bar locks at 55. "55—halfway between 50 and 60."
- **Prompt:** "Click to place the bar for Yellow Team (70 points)."
- **Student Action:** Click-to-set at height 70
- **On Correct:** Bar locks at 70. "Right on 70."
- **On Complete:** "That's it. You chose the scale and placed every bar exactly where it should be, some exactly on the lines and some halfway between."

### **Required Phrases (Must Appear in Script)**

- "doesn't fit" (introduced in 1.2 when scale is too small)
- "multiples of 5" or "end in 0 or 5" (introduced in 2.3)
- "Scale of 2 works for any numbers" (stated in 2.2)
- "easier to read" or "easiest to read" (introduced in 1.4)
- "Check the biggest number" (stated in 1.4, bridge)

### **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**

- ❌ "overflow" (not age-appropriate; use "doesn't fit")
- ❌ "messy data" (not student-facing; describe the characteristic instead)
- ❌ "best scale" as absolute (multiple scales can be appropriate)
- ❌ "estimate" or "about" for bar placement during CREATION tasks (creation uses clean data)
- ❌ "guess" where to put the bar (students should know exactly where bars go)

---

### **Misconception Prevention**

**#4 Wrong Scale Selection (PRIMARY):**

- Worked example shows "doesn't fit" FIRST (1.2)—students see consequences before attempting
- Explicit "check biggest number" instruction (1.4)
- Preview system gives feedback BEFORE commitment
- Justification prompts require reasoning, not just selection

**#5 Variant: Can't Recognize Imprecision (TERTIARY):**

- Guide names the problem: "can't be shown exactly"
- Side-by-side comparison of multiples vs. non-multiples of 5 (2.3)
- Multiple exposures to non-multiples (2.1, 2.4, 3.2)

---

### **1.7.4 Incomplete Script Flags**

**If ANY of these are true, STOP and complete the script:**

- [ ] Fewer than 13 interactions total
- [ ] No worked example showing "doesn't fit" (scale too small)
- [ ] No worked example showing imprecision (non-multiples of 5)
- [ ] No explicit "check the biggest number" instruction
- [ ] No explicit "multiples of 5" / "end in 0 or 5" instruction
- [ ] No independent scale selection with justification
- [ ] No bar graph creation with self-selected scale
- [ ] Missing Scale Preview System usage
- [ ] Missing any required phrase
- [ ] Contains any forbidden phrase
- [ ] Only one orientation used (must include both)
- [ ] No non-multiples data in Section 2+

### **1.7.5 Success Criteria**

**The One Thing:** Students understand that scale selection is a strategic choice based on data characteristics—bigger ranges need bigger scales, and non-multiples of 5 require scale of 2.

**Ready for Exit Check:** Student can:

1. Identify when a scale is too small (biggest number doesn't fit)
2. Recognize when numbers aren't multiples of 5 (need scale of 2)
3. Select appropriate scale using Preview System
4. Justify choice with reasoning
5. Create bar graph with self-selected scale

**Ready for Module 6:** Student can flexibly select scales for any dataset, enabling them to focus on problem-solving with data rather than graph construction mechanics.
