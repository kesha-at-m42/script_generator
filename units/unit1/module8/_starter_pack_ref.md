# BACKBONE
---
## 1.0 THE ONE THING
Students must understand that **a multiplication expression is a precise symbolic representation of an equal groups situation, where the first number tells how many groups and the second number tells how many items in each group — and this relationship works in BOTH directions** (visual → expression AND expression → visual). M8 teaches students to BUILD the symbolic notation they were only RECOGNIZING in M7. "3 groups of 4" becomes student-produced "3 × 4."
**Critical Misconception:** Groups vs. Size Confusion (#10) — Students reverse the factors when building expressions. When shown 3 bags with 5 items each, they write "5 × 3" instead of "3 × 5." This is more dangerous in M8 than M7 because students are now PRODUCING notation — a reversal error in production gets reinforced through practice, whereas recognition errors in M7 were caught by MC options.
**Success Indicator:** Given a visual showing 4 boxes with 6 items each, student:
1. Identifies "4 groups of 6" (structure recognition — carried from M7)
2. Builds expression "4 × 6" using the Equation Builder (NEW — production)
3. Given the expression "5 × 3," selects or describes the correct visual: 5 groups with 3 items each (NEW — reverse direction)
**Biggest Risk:** Students who learned "X groups of Y" in M7 may autopilot the language but fail to transfer it to symbol production. The handoff from language → symbol is the cognitive leap: knowing "4 groups of 3" is different from independently constructing "4 × 3" in the Equation Builder. Students who skip the verbal mediation step ("What's the first number? How many groups?") will produce expressions by guessing or pattern-matching rather than reasoning from structure.

### 1.1.2 Module Bridges
**From Module 7:** Students can identify equal groups in visual contexts (bags, boxes, circles). They use "X groups of Y" language consistently and can distinguish the number of groups from items per group. They RECOGNIZE × notation when the Guide shows it and can SELECT the correct expression from MC options (2-3 late-lesson tasks). They know "multiplication," "equal groups," "times," and "product" (light touch) as vocabulary. M7 Synthesis closure explicitly previewed M8: "Next time, you'll write your own multiplication expressions."
**This Module:** Students cross the bridge from recognition to production. They BUILD multiplication expressions using the Equation Builder — first through MC selection (Method A, carried from M7 but now primary), then through tile construction (Methods C/D). The core learning is bidirectional:
- **Visual → Expression:** See 4 bags with 3 items each → build "4 × 3"
- **Expression → Visual:** See "5 × 2" → select/identify 5 groups of 2
Key learning:
- "X groups of Y" can be written as "X × Y" — students PRODUCE this, not just recognize it
- Expression = numbers and × symbol, NO equal sign
- First number = groups, second number = items per group (reinforced from M7, now applied to production)
- Bidirectional fluency: Visual ↔ Expression (both directions practiced)
**To Module 9:** M8 established how to WRITE multiplication expressions and match them bidirectionally with visuals. M9 builds FLUENCY with specific factor families (2s, 5s, 10s) — connecting back to the graph scales from M1-M6. Students will now COMPUTE products using skip-counting strategies. M8's broad factor ranges (building structural understanding) give way to M9's targeted practice with specific families. The repeated addition connection becomes a computational strategy tool in M9.

### ⚠️ Scope Warnings
> ⚠️ **EXPRESSION ≠ EQUATION:** M8 students build expressions (3 × 4) — NO equal sign, NO product. The Equation Builder uses a 2-slot template: `[___] × [___]`. That's it. Full equations (3 × 4 = 12) are M9's territory when students learn to compute.
> ⚠️ **PRODUCTION ≠ COMPUTATION:** M8 students build expressions. They do NOT find products. They do NOT count items. They do NOT skip-count. They MATCH symbols to structures and structures to symbols. That's the whole job.
> ⚠️ **BIDIRECTIONAL ≠ SYMMETRIC:** Both directions (Visual → Expression and Expression → Visual) are taught, but Visual → Expression is the PRIMARY direction and gets majority instructional time. Expression → Visual is secondary — enough to confirm understanding that notation MEANS something, not enough to become its own extended learning arc.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS
### 1.4.1 #10: Groups vs. Size Confusion (PRIMARY)
**Trigger Behavior:** Given 3 bags with 5 items each, student writes "5 × 3" instead of "3 × 5." Or given expression "4 × 2," student identifies a visual showing 2 groups of 4.
**Why It Happens:** Students focus on the most visually salient element (the items) rather than the structural organization (the groups). Without explicit verbal mediation, they grab the first number they see ("I see 5 things!") rather than reasoning through the grouping structure.
**Why Critical in M8:** In M7, students selected from options — reversal errors were caught by MC structure. In M8, students PRODUCE notation. A reversal error gets encoded through practice and becomes increasingly resistant to correction. The handoff from recognition to production is where this misconception most easily takes root.
**Prevention Strategy:**
- **Two-question routine in every Visual → Expression interaction:**
	- "How many groups?" FIRST (student answers/acts) → "That's your first number"
	- "How many in each?" SECOND (student answers/acts) → "That's your second number"
- **Build Mode decomposition:** Methods C/D force sequential placement (can't place both simultaneously)
- **MC distractors always include reversed expression:** If correct is 3 × 5, option B is 5 × 3
- **Incorrect feedback explicitly names which is which:** "5 × 3 means 5 groups of 3. But you have 3 groups. What number should be first?"
---
### 1.4.2 #11: Multiplication Only Adds (SECONDARY)
**Trigger Behavior:** Student thinks 3 × 5 means "3 plus 5" or calculates it as 8. When asked what multiplication means, says "adding numbers together."
**Why It Happens:** The word "times" can sound like "plus" in casual speech. Students may have informal exposure to "times" meaning "more" in everyday contexts. Without the equal groups model clearly established, × becomes just another symbol that might mean addition.
**Why Relevant to M8:** M8 doesn't involve computation, so this misconception won't show up in calculation errors. BUT it can show up in Expression → Visual matching: student might look for visuals that show a total of 8 when seeing "3 × 5" rather than looking for group structure.
**Prevention Strategy:**
- Equal groups language is PRIMARY and PERSISTENT: "× means groups of"
- When students encounter addition-based error, Guide contrasts: "3 plus 5 would be 8 items. But 3 groups of 5 is way more than that — it's 15 items total. × means groups, not plus."
- Module 9 will lightly connect that repeated addition (5 + 5 + 5) IS related to multiplication, but this is observational/Guide-narrated only, NOT a student method
---
### 1.4.3 #12: Symbol Pattern Matching Without Meaning (TERTIARY)
**Trigger Behavior:** Student successfully builds expressions by copying visual structure but can't explain what the expression means or select matching visuals when given expressions first.
**Why It Happens:** Students can learn procedural patterns ("count the bags, count the items, put the numbers in slots") without understanding that the notation REPRESENTS the situation. This shows up when direction reverses: they can do Visual → Expression through pattern-following but fail Expression → Visual because they never built meaning.
**Why Relevant to M8:** Bidirectional work is specifically designed to catch this. If a student can build "4 × 3" from seeing 4 bags of 3 but can't identify 4 bags of 3 when shown "4 × 3," they're pattern-matching without understanding.
**Prevention Strategy:**
- Guide narrates MEANING alongside construction: "You built 4 × 3. That MEANS 4 groups of 3."
- Reverse direction tasks force meaning-making: "This expression says 5 × 2. What does that mean? It means 5 groups with 2 in each. Which picture shows that?"
- Verbal mediation prevents pure procedural execution

### Reverse Direction Support (Expression → Visual)
For Expression → Visual tasks, the Context Visualization serves as the ANSWER rather than the prompt:
- Student sees expression (e.g., "5 × 3") displayed by Guide
- Student selects from 2-3 visual options showing different group structures
- Options include:
	- Correct (5 groups of 3)
	- Reversed (#10 distractor: 3 groups of 5)
	- Sum of factors scattered (#11 distractor: 8 items ungrouped)
	- Product scattered (correct total, ungrouped structure)
- This uses MC on the visual side (selecting the right picture), not MC on the expression side

### Method Details
**Method A: Multiple Choice (Recognition → Production Bridge)**
- 3-4 complete expressions shown as options
- Student clicks to select
- ALWAYS includes: correct expression, reversed expression (#10), one other distractor
- Example: Visual shows 3 bags of 4 items
	- A: 3 × 4 ✓
	- B: 4 × 3 (reversal)
	- C: 3 + 4 (addition distractor for #11)
	- D: 7 (nonsense/other)
**Method C: Drag-Drop Tiles**
- Click-hold tile, drag to slot, snap-to-slot on release
- Best for mouse/touch interfaces
- Visual feedback: tile enlarges slightly on pickup, glows when over valid slot
**Method D: Click-Pickup-Place**
- Click tile (lifts with animation), click slot (places with animation)
- Best for trackpad or accessibility
- Same tiles as Method C, different interaction pattern
- Guide can say: "Click a number, then click where it goes"
**Shared Tile Features (Methods C/D):**
- Rearrange: Students can pick up placed tiles and move to different slots (supports self-correction)
- Reset: Clear button returns all tiles to palette
- Two-slot template: `[___] × [___]` — NO third slot
- Method C (Drag-Drop): Click-hold tile, drag to slot, snap-to-slot on release. Best for mouse/touch.
- Method D (Click-Pickup-Place): Click tile (lifts), click slot (places). Best for trackpad. Same tiles as C.

## 1.7 LESSON (\~10-12 minutes)
### Core Purpose
Students learn to BUILD multiplication expressions from equal groups visuals using the Equation Builder, and to identify visuals that match given expressions. M8 does NOT teach computation—it teaches producing and interpreting the symbolic notation that represents equal groups structure. By the end of the lesson, students can independently build expressions using tiles and can match expressions to their corresponding visuals bidirectionally.
### Pedagogical Flow
M8 bridges from RECOGNITION (M7) to PRODUCTION. Students arrive knowing how to see equal groups and match them to expressions when given options. The Lesson teaches expression BUILDING through the CRA sequence, with M8-specific adaptations:

## 1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Building the Structure
**Purpose:** Establish the basic expression-building process with worked example, introduce "expression" vocabulary, and have student attempt with Method A (familiar from M7).
**Context:** Bags only (concrete, familiar from M7)
---
### Interaction 1.1: Transition from Warmup + Worked Example
- **Purpose:** Model the complete expression-building process step-by-step using a worked example. Introduce "expression" vocabulary formally.
- **Visual: Context Visualizations (Mode 2).** Concrete context (bags with apples). 4 bags with 3 items each. Full separation. \[WORKED EXAMPLE\] designation. Template shows `[___] × [___]` empty.
- **Guide:** "You've been matching expressions to equal groups. Now YOU get to build them — and you'll learn to go the other way too, figuring out what an expression looks like as a picture. Let me show you how building works."
- **Guide:** "First, I ask myself: how many GROUPS? Each bag is one group."
- **Visual:** Bags highlight sequentially as Guide counts
- **Guide:** "1, 2, 3, 4. Four bags. That's 4 groups."
- **Guide:** "Now I ask: how many in EACH group? Let me look inside one bag."
- **Visual:** Focus on first bag, apples highlight
- **Guide:** "1, 2, 3. Three apples. Let me check another..."
- **Visual:** Second bag highlights
- **Guide:** "Also 3. They're equal groups—same number in every bag."
- **Guide:** "So I have 4 groups of 3. Now watch how I build the expression."
- **Visual:** Equation Builder—first slot glows with subtle label "groups"
- **Guide:** "The first number is how many groups. I click 4."
- **Visual:** "4" appears in first slot (Guide demonstrates)
- **Visual:** Second slot glows with label "in each"
- **Guide:** "The second number is how many in each group. I click 3."
- **Visual:** "3" appears in second slot, complete expression shows: `4 × 3`
- **Guide:** "I built 4 times 3. This is called an EXPRESSION. An expression uses numbers and symbols to show the math. No equal sign — just the numbers and the times symbol."
- **Student Action:** Observation only (no response required).
	- This is a worked example to establish the thinking process.
> **Vocabulary Note:** "Expression" introduced explicitly after student has seen the construction process. Connects to concrete experience before naming.
---
### Interaction 1.2: Student Builds with Method A
- **Purpose:** Student attempts expression building using Method A (MC), which bridges recognition to production.
- **Visual: Context Visualizations (Mode 2) + Equation Builder (Method A).** Concrete context (bags with items). 3 bags with 6 items each. \[NEW\] initialization. Multiple Choice interaction.
- **Guide:** "Your turn to build an expression. First question: how many groups do you see? Second question: how many in each group? Now build the expression."
- **Prompt:** "Build the expression for the picture."
- **Student Action:** Multiple Choice selection.
	- **Options:** A: 3 × 6 ✓, B: 6 × 3 (reversal), C: 3 + 6 (addition), D: 6 × 6 (nonsense)
- **Correct Answer:** A (3 × 6)
- **On Correct:** "You built 3 times 6. That expression shows 3 groups of 6."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Design Note:** Two-question routine embedded in scaffolding. Options include reversal (#10), addition (#11), and nonsense distractor. This interaction completes Section 1's transition from observation to guided practice.

## 1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Pattern Across Contexts
**Purpose:** Show that the SAME structure (equal groups) appears in different visual forms. Bags and boxes both show equal groups—different containers, same mathematical structure. This prevents context-specific understanding. Introduce Methods C/D (tiles) with partial worked example.
**Context:** Bags AND boxes (showing pattern across representations)
---
### Interaction 2.1: Show Pattern—Bags AND Boxes
- **Purpose:** Relational learning moment—students see that different containers can represent the same mathematical structure.
- **Visual: Context Visualizations (Mode 2) (×2).** Split screen: Left shows concrete (bags), Right shows concrete (boxes). 2 groups of 5 items each. Same structure in both. \[RELATIONAL COMPARISON\] designation.
- **Guide:** "Look at these two. On the left—bags. On the right—boxes."
- **Visual:** Bags highlight, then boxes highlight
- **Guide:** "Different containers. But notice what's the SAME."
- **Visual:** Both visuals show "2 groups" label appear
- **Guide:** "Both show 2 groups."
- **Visual:** Items in one bag highlight, then items in one box highlight
- **Guide:** "Both have 5 in each group."
- **Guide:** "Different containers—bags or boxes—but the SAME structure. Both are 2 groups of 5. Both build the same expression: 2 times 5."
- **Visual:** Both expressions `2 × 5` highlight simultaneously
- **Guide:** "Equal groups work the same way no matter what holds them."
- **Student Action:** Observation only (no response required).
	- This is relational teaching—building conceptual understanding that structure transcends visual form.
> **Design Note:** This is the RELATIONAL phase's core moment—showing the pattern exists across different visual forms. Prevents "3×4 only means bags" misconception.
---
### Interaction 2.2: Equal vs. Unequal Groups — Can You Write an Expression?
- **Purpose:** Establish that multiplication expressions describe EQUAL groups specifically — not any collection of items in containers.
- **Visual: Context Visualizations (Mode 2) (×4).** Four different bag scenarios labeled A-D. All use bags (isolates variable to contents). Different internal quantities showing equal and unequal configurations.
- **Guide:** "All of these show bags with items inside. But can I write a multiplication expression for all of them? Remember — multiplication shows EQUAL groups. Select the pictures where every bag has the SAME number inside."
- **Prompt:** "Select ALL the pictures that show equal groups."
- **Student Action:** Multi-select from A, B, C, D.
	- **Options:** A: 3 bags with 4 items each (equal — 3 × 4) ✓, B: 3 bags with 4, 2, 6 items (unequal), C: 4 bags with 3 items each (equal — 4 × 3) ✓, D: 4 bags with 3, 3, 1, 5 items (unequal)
- **Correct Answer:** A and C
- **On Correct:** "A has 3 bags with 4 in each — equal groups, 3 times 4. C has 4 bags with 3 in each — equal groups, 4 times 3. B and D? Different amounts in the bags so they have unequal groups. We can't write a multiplication expression for those."
- **On Partially Correct (selected A and C but also B or D):** "A and C are right — equal groups. But look inside every bag in \[B/D\]. Count each one. Are they all the same?"
	- **Visual:** Items in each bag of the incorrectly selected image highlight with counts displayed.
- **On Missed A or C:** "Check inside every bag. If each bag has the same number, that's equal groups — and we can write a multiplication expression."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Design Note:** Multi-select with 4 options avoids binary response patterns. Distractor B has a first bag that matches A (both start with 4 items), forcing students to check ALL groups. Distractor D has two correct bags and two incorrect, plus a total (12) that matches C's total — testing whether students check group equality, not just the total count. All four use bags to isolate the variable: the criterion is equal contents, not container type. Placed after 2.1's "different containers, same structure" lesson to complete the conceptual pair.
---
### Interaction 2.3: Introducing Methods C/D—Tile Construction
- **Purpose:** Demonstrate the tile-based construction process (Methods C/D) that students will use for more independent building.
- **Visual: Context Visualizations (Mode 2) + Equation Builder (Methods C/D).** Concrete context (boxes with items). 5 boxes with 2 items each. Tile palette visible (tiles 1 through 10). \[PARTIAL WORKED EXAMPLE\] designation.
- **Guide:** "You've learned that you need equal groups to write multiplication expressions and that equal groups work the same way no matter what holds them. These are important ideas for multiplication."
- **Guide:** "You've been choosing complete expressions to represent equal groups. Now you'll build them piece by piece using number tiles. Watch."
- **Guide:** "Notice all of the groups are equal. Same questions: How many groups?"
- **Visual:** Boxes highlight sequentially
- **Guide:** "5 boxes. That's 5 groups. I need the 5 tile in the FIRST slot."
- **Visual (Demo):** Guide demonstrates drag or click-pickup of 5 tile to first slot
- **Visual:** Tile snaps into first slot: `5 × [___]`
- **Guide:** "How many in each group?"
- **Visual:** Items in one box highlight
- **Guide:** "2 items. I need the 2 tile in the SECOND slot."
- **Visual (Demo):** Guide demonstrates placing 2 tile
- **Visual:** Complete expression appears: `5 × 2`
- **Guide:** "I built it piece by piece—5 times 2. Now you try with tiles."
- **Student Action:** Observation only (no response required).
	- Demonstration completes before student attempts.
---
### Interaction 2.4: Student Builds with Tiles—Decomposed Prompts
- **Purpose:** Student uses tiles (Methods C/D) to build an expression with full verbal scaffolding.
- **Visual: Context Visualizations (Mode 2) + Equation Builder (Methods C/D).** Concrete context (bags with items). 2 bags with 4 items each. Tile palette shows 1 through 10. \[NEW\] initialization.
- **Guide:** "Build the expression for these bags using tiles. First number: how many groups? Second number: how many in each group?"

	- Validation: Expression must be `2 × 4`
- **Correct Answer:** 2 × 4
- **On Correct:** "You built it—2 times 4 shows 2 groups of 4."

> **Design Note:** Two-question routine embedded in scaffolding. Student manages full tile construction process. This completes Section 2's relational learning and introduction to Methods C/D.

## **1.9 SYNTHESIS**
**Purpose:** Consolidate understanding that expressions represent equal groups structure, and that the same structure can be built using different methods. Surface the insight that expressions work bidirectionally—you can build from visuals OR find visuals from expressions. Connect expression-building to real-world multiplication situations. Reflect on building strategies.

### Opening Frame
- **Purpose:** Signal shift from Practice to reflection/connection.
- **Visual:** Clean transition screen.
- **Guide:** "You've practiced building expressions from equal groups pictures. You learned different ways to build them—choosing from options and using tiles. Now let's connect what you discovered."
---
### **Interaction 1.9.1: Representation Transfer—Same Expression, Different Visuals**
- **Purpose:** Show that the SAME expression (2 × 5) can describe different visual contexts—bags, boxes, or circles. The containers change but the mathematical structure (2 groups of 5) stays constant.
- **Visual: Context Visualizations (three forms displayed side-by-side).** Visual 1: 2 bags with 5 items each. Visual 2: 2 boxes with 5 items each. Visual 3: 2 circles with 5 dots each. Below all three: Expression `2 × 5`.
- **Guide:** "These three pictures look different. Bags, boxes, circles. But they all show the same thing."
- **Prompt:** "What stays the same across all three?"
- **Student Action:** Multiple Choice selection.
	- **Options:** A: 2 groups with 5 in each ✓, B: The containers (bags/boxes/circles), C: The items inside, D: The colors or shapes
- **Correct Answer:** A
- **On Correct:** "Exactly. The CONTAINERS change—bags, boxes, circles—but the structure doesn't. 2 groups of 5 every time. That's what the expression 2 times 5 captures—the structure underneath."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** "Count the groups in each picture. Then count what's inside. What's the same?"
---
### **Interaction 1.9.2: Real-World Bridge—Where Do You See Equal Groups?**
- **Purpose:** Connect abstract expression-building to concrete real-world contexts where equal groups naturally occur. Students identify multiplication situations outside math class.
- **Visual: Four real-world scenario illustrations.** A: Egg carton (2 rows, 6 eggs each). B: Parking lot (3 rows, 4 cars each). C: Bookshelf (5 shelves, 3 books each). D: Park bench (4 benches, varying people—NOT equal groups).
- **Guide:** "Equal groups show up everywhere—not just in math problems. Which of these shows equal groups?"
- **Prompt:** "Which one shows equal groups?"
- **Student Action:** Multiple Choice selection (accept A, B, or C).
	- **Options:** A: Egg carton, B: Parking lot, C: Bookshelf, D: Park bench (unequal)
- **Correct Answer:** A, B, or C (all are equal groups; accept any)
- **On Correct (Selection A):** "The egg carton—2 rows of 6 eggs. That's 2 times 6. Equal groups in the grocery store."
- **On Correct (Selection B):** "The parking lot—3 rows of 4 cars. That's 3 times 4. Equal groups at the mall."
- **On Correct (Selection C):** "The bookshelf—5 shelves with 3 books each. That's 5 times 3. Equal groups in your room."
- **On Incorrect (Selection D):** "The benches have people, but they're not in equal groups—different numbers on each bench. Check the others for groups where each has the same amount."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Remediation Note:** "Look for groups where EVERY group has the same number. That's what makes them equal."
---
### **Interaction 1.9.3: Metacognitive Reflection—Which Direction Made More Sense?**
- **Purpose:** Help students identify which aspect of expression-building made the most sense to them. Builds metacognitive awareness about their own learning process.
- **Visual: Reminder visuals (small, non-interactive).** Top: Context Visualization (bags) → Expression. Bottom: Expression → Context Visualization selection.
- **Guide:** "Today you built expressions from pictures, and you matched pictures to expressions. Which direction made more sense to you?"
- **Prompt:** "Which helped you understand expressions better?"
- **Student Action:** Multiple Choice selection (all responses validated equally).
	- **Options:** A: Building from pictures (Visual → Expression), B: Matching pictures to expressions (Expression → Visual), C: Both helped in different ways, D: I'm still figuring it out
- **Correct Answer:** Any selection (no wrong answer in reflection)
- **On Selection A:** "Building from pictures lets you see where the numbers come from. That's a strong way to learn."
- **On Selection B:** "Working backwards—from expression to picture—shows you what expressions MEAN. That's powerful too."
- **On Selection C:** "Different directions for different moments. That's flexible thinking."
- **On Selection D:** "Still figuring it out is totally fine. You'll notice what works as you keep practicing."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
---
### **Identity-Building Closure**
- **Purpose:** Specific, growth-oriented affirmation connecting to future learning. Bridge to M9.
- **Visual:** Clean screen. Brief display of an expression `4 × 3`.
- **Guide:** "You built expressions from equal groups pictures. You learned that 4 times 3 MEANS 4 groups with 3 in each. You know what the numbers represent—groups and items."
- **Guide:** "Next time, you'll find out what 4 times 3 EQUALS. You'll learn to figure out the total. You're ready because you already know what the expression means."

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS
### KDD 1: Progressive Method Unlock (A → A+C/D)
Students begin Lesson with Method A (MC selection), which bridges from M7's recognition work and lowers cognitive load during initial expression-building. Once comfortable with the structure and vocabulary, Methods C/D (tile construction) unlock. This phasing prevents cognitive overload: students master the conceptual work (identifying groups and items) before managing the interface complexity of tile manipulation.
### KDD 2: Bidirectional Learning with Expression → Visual as Secondary
Both Visual → Expression and Expression → Visual are taught in the Lesson, but Visual → Expression receives the majority of time (11 of 12 interactions). This asymmetry is deliberate: students must PRODUCE expressions to internalize the structure, and production precedes deep reverse-direction fluency. Expression → Visual serves as a meaning-check ("Do you know what this expression represents?") rather than a parallel learning goal.
### KDD 3: Context Progression (Bags → Boxes → Circles)
The Lesson moves from familiar-concrete (bags, same from M7) to increasingly abstract (boxes, circles with dots). This progression allows students to apply stable conceptual understanding (expression building from equal groups structure) across visual variations. The pattern shows students that structure transcends visual form, preventing context-dependent understanding.
### KDD 4: Two-Question Routine as Prevention for #10
Every Visual → Expression interaction includes the same verbal sequence: "How many groups? \[Student acts/answers\] That's your first number. How many in each? \[Student acts/answers\] That's your second number." This routine prevents Groups vs. Size Confusion (#10) by making factor order meaningful and grounded in structure rather than visual salience.
### KDD 5: No Equal Sign, No Product Computation
M8 strictly avoids equations and totals. The Equation Builder template is `[___] × [___]` with no third slot. This boundary preserves the focus on expression production without confusing it with equation-solving or computation. M9 introduces products and equations.
### KDD 6: Method C/D Decomposition Prevents Pattern-Only Learning
Methods C/D force sequential tile placement—students must place "groups" first, then "items per group." This decomposition prevents students from randomly placing numbers and then checking if the result "looks right." Sequential placement enforces thinking through the structure before acting.
### KDD 7: Distractors Target Known Misconceptions
All MC options and tile palettes include specific distractors designed to surface and prevent misconceptions. Reversed expressions (#10), addition (#11), and product distractors (#12, though computation not taught) appear consistently. This targeting helps teachers diagnose thinking errors and guides Remediation Pipeline toward the most common student struggles.
### KDD 8: Build Mode for Reverse Direction Adds Variety
The Lesson includes a single Build Mode interaction (3.4) for reverse direction to add interaction variety and show students the bidirectional process. However, the primary reverse-direction method remains MC visual selection (3.3, 4.3, EC.2). This balance prevents reverse direction from becoming burdensome while ensuring students experience the full bidirectionality.
