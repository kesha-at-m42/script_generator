## **1.7 LESSON (10-12 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

---

### **Core Purpose**

Teach the critical leap from 1:1 to 1:2 scale: each symbol represents 2 items, not 1\. Students learn to create scaled picture graphs by grouping data, understanding that even numbers produce whole symbols and odd numbers require half-symbols. Students apply graph reading skills from M1 to scaled contexts.

---

### **Pedagogical Flow**

M2 introduces a genuinely new concept (scaled representation), so the full CRA sequence applies:

| CRA Phase | M2 Application |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Concrete** | Grouping Animation transforms items into groups of 2, then into symbols |
| **Relational** | Students notice pattern: 10 items \= 5 groups \= 5 symbols; odd numbers have "leftover" |
| **Abstract** | Formalize "scale of 2," "each symbol \= 2," introduce "half-symbol" terminology |
| **Application** | Create graphs independently; answer comparison questions using scaled graphs |

---

### **Lesson Structure**

| Section | Focus | Interactions |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | The Critical Leap: Scaled Graphs with Even Numbers | 7 |
| Section 2 | Half-Symbols for Odd Numbers | 6 |
| Section 3 | Full Independence & Problem Solving | 6 |

**Total: 19 interactions**

---

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: The Critical Leap (Even Numbers)**

**Purpose:** Establish that each symbol \= 2 items through animation, vocabulary formalization, and guided graph creation. All values are even numbers—students master whole symbols before encountering half-symbols.

---

**Interaction 1.1: Your Scaled Graph**

- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Completed Warmup graph (3 categories, even values, 1:2 scale). Key visible.
- **Visual: Data Tables (Numeric Display).** Same data displayed alongside.
- **Guide:** "You already read this graph—you counted \[Y÷2\] symbols for \[Category B\]. Each symbol shows 2 items. This is called a SCALED picture graph."
- **Guide:** "But HOW do you know each symbol shows 2? Let's find out."
- **No student prompt** (brief transition to 1.2 where Key/Scale is taught)
- **Vocabulary Introduced:** "scaled picture graph"

**Design Note:** This interaction acknowledges Warmup success and creates curiosity about the KEY—setting up Interaction 1.2.
---

**Interaction 1.2: What Is a Key? What Is Scale?**

- **Visual:** Completed scaled picture graph from animation. Key area highlighted: "Each ⭐ \= 2". Data table visible.
- **Guide:** "Every graph has a KEY. The key is like a decoder—it tells you what each symbol MEANS."
- **Guide:** "This key says: each star equals 2\. That '2' is called the SCALE."
- **Guide:** "Scale of 1 means each symbol represents 1 item. So what does scale of 2 mean?"
- **Prompt:** "This graph has a scale of 2\. What does that mean?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice:
  - "There are 2 symbols"
  - "Each symbol shows 2 items"
  - "There are 2 categories"
  - "Add 2 to each number"\]
- **Correct Answer:** "Each symbol shows 2 items"
- **Answer Rationale:**
  - "Each symbol shows 2 items" \= Correct (definition of scale)
  - "There are 2 symbols" \= Misconception: confusing scale with count
  - "There are 2 categories" \= Misconception: confusing scale with structure
  - "Add 2 to each number" \= Misconception \#2 (Scale Means Addition)
- **On Correct:** "Scale of 2 means each symbol represents 2 items. The KEY tells you the scale. Always check the key first."
- **Vocabulary Formalized:** "key" (the decoder), "scale" (how many each symbol represents)

---

**Interaction 1.3: Using the Key to Read**

- **Visual:** NEW scaled picture graph, vertical orientation. "Favorite Playground Equipment" with 4 categories. Key highlighted: "Each ⭐ \= 2 votes." Values: Swings=8 (4 symbols), Slide=6 (3 symbols), Monkey Bars=10 (5 symbols), Sandbox=4 (2 symbols).
- **Guide:** "New graph. First step: check the key. What is the scale of this graph?"
- **Prompt:** "What is the scale of this graph?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: 1, 2, 4, 5\]
- **Correct Answer:** 2
- **On Correct:** "Scale of 2\. Each symbol equals 2 votes. Now you can read the graph."
- **Guide:** "Monkey Bars has 5 symbols. Each symbol equals 2\. So how many voted for Monkey Bars?"
- **Prompt:** "How many students chose Monkey Bars?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: 5, 7, 10, 12\]
- **Correct Answer:** 10
- **Answer Rationale:**
  - 10 \= Correct (5 symbols × 2 \= 10, or count by 2s: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10\)
  - 5 \= Error: Counted symbols, ignored scale
  - 7 \= Error: Added instead of scaled (5 \+ 2\)
  - 12 \= Counting error
- **On Correct:** "10 students. 5 symbols, scale of 2\. Count by 2s: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10."
- **Remediation (Light):** "Check the key. Scale of 2 means count by 2s. 5 symbols: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10."

---

**Interaction 1.4: Your Turn to Create (Guided)**

- **Visual:** NEW Data Table, vertical orientation, showing "Favorite Snacks" data: Popcorn=8, Pretzels=10, Fruit=12, Crackers=14, Chips=10. Empty picture graph template, vertical orientation, with 3 categories pre-filled (Pretzels=5 symbols, Fruit=6 symbols, Crackers=7 symbols). Popcorn and chips columns empty.
- **Guide:** "Now you create. The table shows 8 students chose Popcorn."
- **Guide:** "8 is an EVEN number—it groups perfectly into 2s. 8 splits into 4 groups of 2\. Place 4 symbols for Popcorn."
- **Prompt:** "Place 4 symbols for Popcorn."
- **Student Action:** Hover over Popcorn row, click to place symbols (4 placements)
- **Correct Answer:** 4 symbols placed
- **On Correct:** "4 symbols. Each shows 2 votes. 4 groups of 2 is 8."
- **Remediation (Light):** "8 votes needs how many symbols? Group by 2s: 2, 4, 6, 8\. That's 4 groups."

---

**Interaction 1.5: More Practice with Even Numbers**

- **Visual:** Same graph. Shows: Popcorn=4 symbols (student created), Pretzels=5 symbols (pre-filled), Fruit=6 symbols (pre-filled), Crackers=7 symbols (pre-filled), Chips=empty.
- **Guide:** "Good work on Popcorn. Now try Chips. The table shows 10 students chose Chips."
- **Guide:** "10 is also an EVEN number. It groups into 2s perfectly. Place symbols for Chips."
- **Prompt:** "Place symbols for Chips."
- **Student Action:** Click to place 5 symbols
- **Correct Answer:** 5 symbols placed
- **On Correct:** "5 symbols for 10 votes. You're getting this—group by 2s, and even numbers give us whole symbols."
- **Remediation (Light):** "10 votes needs how many symbols? Group by 2s: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10\. That's 5 groups."

**Design Note:** This second creation rep builds confidence with even numbers BEFORE Section 2 introduces the complexity of half-symbols for odd numbers.

---

**Interaction 1.6: Checking the Graph (Two-Step)**

- **Visual:** Completed "Favorite Snacks" graph only (no data table visible). Key shows: "Each ⭐ \= 2 votes"
- **Purpose:** Force students to apply scale—they cannot read the value from a table.

**Step A: Identify**

- **Guide:** "You made a scaled graph\! Let's check: which snack got the most votes?"
- **Prompt:** "Click on the category with the most votes."
- **Student Action:** Click on Crackers row (7 symbols)
- **On Correct:** "Crackers has the most symbols."

**Step B: Apply Scale**

- **Guide:** "How many votes did Crackers get?"
- **Prompt:** How many votes did Crackers get?
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: 7, 9, 14, 16\]
- **Correct Answer:** 14
- **Answer Rationale:**
  - 14 \= Correct (7 symbols × 2 \= 14 votes)
  - 7 \= Error: Counted symbols, didn't apply scale
  - 9 \= Error: Added instead of multiplied (7 \+ 2\)
  - 16 \= Error: Miscounted symbols as 8
- **On Correct:** "14 votes\! You counted 7 symbols, and each symbol equals 2\. 7 groups of 2 is 14."
- **Remediation (Light):** "Count the symbols: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7\. Each symbol \= 2 votes. Count by 2s: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 14."

**Design Note:** Removing the data table ensures students must apply the scale to determine values. The "7" distractor specifically catches students who count symbols without scaling.

---

**Interaction 1.7: How Many More?**

- **Visual:** Same graph. Data table not shown.
- **Guide:** "How many MORE students chose Crackers than Popcorn?"
- **Prompt:** How many MORE students chose Crackers than Popcorn?
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: 3, 6, 14, 22\]
- **Correct Answer:** 6
- **Answer Rationale:**
  - 6 \= Correct (14 \- 8 \= 6\)
  - 3 \= Error: Subtracted symbols (7 \- 4\) instead of values
  - 14 \= Error: Used Crackers value only
  - 22 \= Error: Added values (14 \+ 8\)
- **On Correct:** "6 more students. Crackers has 14, Popcorn has 8\. The difference is 6."
- **Remediation (Light):** Crackers (7 symbols) and Popcorn (4 symbols) highlighted.
  "First find the number of votes for each snack. Crackers: 7 symbols: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 14\. Popcorn: 4 symbols: 2, 4, 6, 8\. Now subtract."

**→ SECTION 1 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 2\.**

### **1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Half-Symbols for Odd Numbers**

**Purpose:** Introduce half-symbols for odd values. Explicitly teach "half of 2 \= 1" and connect to even/odd understanding. Students practice creating graphs with half-symbols.

---

**\[SECTION TRANSITION\]**

- **Guide:** "You made graphs with numbers like 8, 10, 12, 14\. Those are EVEN numbers—they group into 2s perfectly. But what happens with ODD numbers?"

---

**Interaction 2.1: The Problem with 7**

- **Visual:** New Data Table appears Horizontal Table showing "Animals We Saw" data: Birds=7, Squirrels=10, Rabbits=8, Deer=6. Key showing "Each ⭐ \= 2".
- **Guide:** "The Key shows "Each ⭐ \= 2" so we need to group into 2s to make the graph. Look at Birds: 7 sightings. Let's try to group 7 into 2s."
- **Animation (Auto-play, showing the problem):**
  - 7 items appear
  - Groups form: 2, 2, 2... 1 left over
  - "7 items... 3 groups of 2... and 1 left over."
  - "We have 6 put into groups of 2, but 1 more. What do we do with that 1?"
- **Prompt:** "7 makes 3 full groups of 2, with 1 left over. How do we show 1 with our symbols?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice:
  - "Ignore the leftover"
  - "Use a half-symbol for 1"
  - "Round up to 8"
- **Correct Answer:** "Use a half-symbol for 1."
- **On Correct:** "A half-symbol shows 1 item. Let's see how that works."

---

**Interaction 2.2: Half-Symbol Teaching**

- **Visual:** Animation continues showing half-symbol solution.
- **Animation:**
  - Key highlighted to show "Each ⭐ \= 2"
  - 3 full symbols appear for the 6 grouped items
  - Half-symbol appears for the 1 remaining: "Half of 2 is 1\. This half-symbol shows 1 item."
  - "7 \= \[3 full symbols\] \+ \[1 half-symbol\]"
- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Graph showing the odd-value category with half-symbol visible. Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "Half of 2 is 1\. So a half-symbol shows 1 item. How many sightings does 3 and a half symbols show?"
- **Prompt:** "How many sightings does 3 and a half symbols show?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: 3, 6, 7, 8\]
- **Correct Answer:** 7
- **On Correct:** "7 sightings. 3 full symbols show 6, plus the half-symbol shows 1 more. 6 plus 1 is 7."
- **Vocabulary Introduced:** "half-symbol," "half of 2 \= 1"

---

**Interaction 2.3: Create with Half-Symbols (Guided)**

- **Visual: Data Tables (Symbol Display).** Context: "Animals We Saw," 4 categories. Values: Squirrels=10, Deer=6, Birds=7, Rabbits=8. Horizontal.
- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Horizontal. 2 of 4 categories pre-filled (Squirrels=5 symbols, Deer=3 symbols). Birds and Rabbits rows empty. Key: "Each ⭐ \= 2."
- **Guide:** "Now you place the symbols to show 7 Bird sightings. Place symbols for Birds. Remember: 7 needs 3 full symbols and 1 half-symbol."
- **Prompt:** "Place symbols for Birds."
- **Student Action:** Hover and click to place 3 full symbols \+ 1 half-symbol
- **Correct Answer:** 3½ symbols placed
- **On Correct:** "3 and a half symbols for 7\. Each full symbol is 2 sightings, the half is 1 sighting. 6 plus 1 is 7."
- **Remediation (Light):** "Count the symbols. 7 is 6 plus 1\. 6 needs 3 full symbols. 1 needs a half-symbol."

---

**Interaction 2.4: Complete the Graph**

- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Same "Animals We Saw" graph (horizontal). Birds filled (3½ symbols). Rabbits row empty. Data Tables (Symbol Display) visible.
- **Guide:** "Rabbits have 8 sightings. Does 8 need a half-symbol? Fill in the picture graph for rabbits."
- **Prompt:** "Place symbols for Rabbits."
- **Student Action:** Place 4 full symbols
- **Correct Answer:** 4 symbols (no half-symbol needed)
- **On Correct:** "4 symbols for 8\. 8 is even—it groups perfectly into 2s. No half-symbol needed."

---

**Interaction 2.5: How Many In All?**

- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Completed "Animals We Saw" graph (horizontal, 4 categories). Key visible. Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "How many animals sighted were Birds and Deer IN ALL?"
- **Prompt:** How many animals sighted were Birds and Deer IN ALL?
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: 6, 7, 13, 16\]
- **Correct Answer:** 13
- **On Correct:** "13 birds and deer in all. 7 Birds were sighted, 6 Deer were sighted. 7 plus 6 is 13."
- **Remediation (Light)**: Birds (7 \= 3½ symbols) and Deer (6 \= 3 symbols).

---

**Interaction 2.6: Comparison with Odd Values**

- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Same "Animals We Saw" graph (horizontal). System highlights Birds (3½ symbols) and Squirrels (5 symbols). Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "How many FEWER Birds were sighted than Squirrels?"
- **Prompt:** How many FEWER Birds were sighted than Squirrels?
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: 1, 2, 3, 7\]
- **Correct Answer:** 3
- **On Correct:** "3 fewer. 10 Squirrels were sighted, 7 Birds were sighted. The difference is 3."
- **Remediation (Light):** Birds (7 \= 3½) and Squirrels (10 \= 5).

**→ SECTION 2 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 3\.**

### **1.7.3 LESSON SECTION 3: Full Independence & Problem Solving**

**Purpose:** Remove scaffolding. Students create full graphs from numeric tables without animation. Multiple odd numbers require half-symbols. Students answer multi-step comparison questions.

---

**\[SECTION TRANSITION\]**

- **Visual:** Previous graph clears.
- **Visual: Data Tables (Numeric Display).** NEW data appears. Numbers only — no symbol display. This is the first use of Numeric Display in M2.
- **Guide:** "You created scaled picture graphs with EVEN numbers—those grouped perfectly into 2s. You added HALF-SYMBOLS for ODD numbers—because there's always 1 left over. Now let's see if you can handle BOTH together—a mix of even and odd—all on your own."

---

**Interaction 3.1: Create with Mixed Values**

- **Visual: Data Tables (Numeric Display).** Horizontal. Context: "Books Read This Week," 4 categories. Values: Maya=9, James=12, Sofia=7, Aiden=11.
- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Vertical. 0 categories pre-filled. Key: "Each ⭐ \= 2 books."
- **Guide:** "Build Maya's row first. Notice the key \- Each ⭐ \= 2 books. She read 9 books. Think: is 9 even or odd? Will you need a half-symbol?"
- **Prompt:** "Place symbols for Maya's 9 books read."
- **Student Action:** Click to place 4 full symbols \+ 1 half-symbol
- **Correct Answer:** 4½ symbols placed
- **On Correct:** "4 and a half symbols. 9 is odd—8 needs 4 full symbols, plus that leftover 1 needs a half-symbol."
- **Remediation (Light):** "9 is odd. Group what you can: 2, 4, 6, 8... that's 4 full symbols. What about the 1 left over?"

---

**Interaction 3.2: Create Full Graph**

- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Same "Books Read" graph (vertical). Maya filled (4½ symbols). James, Sofia, Aiden empty. Data Tables (Numeric Display) visible.
- **Guide:** "Build the whole picture graph. Use the key and the table to figure out how many symbols each person needs. Place symbols for all remaining categories."
- **Prompt:** "Place symbols for all remaining categories."
- **Student Action:** Click to place symbols for each column:
  - James: 6 symbols (12 books)
  - Sofia: 3½ symbols (7 books)
  - Aiden: 5½ symbols (11 books)
- **Correct Answer:** All four columns correctly filled
- **On Correct:** "You built the whole graph. You figured out which numbers needed half-symbols."
- **Remediation (by column, Light):** "Check \[name\]'s number. Is it even or odd? Even numbers don't need half-symbols. Odd numbers do."

---

**Interaction 3.2b: Scale Reflection — Same Data, Different Scale \[OBSERVATION ONLY\]**

- **Purpose:** Leverage cognitive conflict (Watson & Moritz 2001\) — students just created a complete graph independently and now observe what the same data looks like at scale of 1\. Builds metarepresentational competence (English 2010/2012) by making scale a visible, comparable choice rather than an invisible given.
- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Student's completed 1:2 graph on LEFT. Key: "Each ⭐ \= 2."
- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 1: Reading).** System-generated 1:1 graph of SAME data on RIGHT. Key: "Each ⭐ \= 1." Both visible simultaneously. Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "Here's the same data — but with scale of 1\. Every book gets its OWN symbol."
- **Guide:** "Look at James. Your graph shows 6 symbols. The scale-of-1 graph shows 12 symbols. Same number of books\!"
- **Prompt:** "Which graph uses fewer symbols to show the same data?"
- **Student Action:** \[Click to select: "Scale of 1" or "Scale of 2"\]
- **Correct Answer:** Scale of 2
- **On Correct:** "Scale of 2 — just 6 symbols instead of 12 for James. Same data, fewer symbols. That's why we use scales."
- **On Incorrect:** "Both graphs show the same data\! But count the symbols — scale of 2 uses fewer. That's what makes scaled graphs useful."
- **Guide:** "Now let's use YOUR graph to answer some questions."
- **Duration:** 30–40 seconds
- **No student graph creation** (observation and comparison only)

**Design Notes:**

- System generates the 1:1 graph — student does NOT create it. This preserves the "problem-solve with scaled graph" principle.
- The 1:1 graph disappears after this interaction. Interactions 3.3–3.5 use ONLY the student's 1:2 graph.
- Placed AFTER successful independent creation (3.2) and BEFORE problem-solving (3.3) — students reflect on what they built before using it analytically.
- Research alignment: Watson & Moritz (2001) Study 3 showed cognitive conflict via alternative representations improved pictograph reasoning. English (2010, 2012\) showed making representation decisions builds metarepresentational competence.
- Total time cost: \~35 seconds. Minimal impact on Lesson pacing.

---

**Interaction 3.3: Who Read the Most?**

- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Student's completed "Books Read" graph (vertical, 1:2 scale). 1:1 comparison graph removed. Key visible. Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "Use your graph to answer: Who read the most books?"
- **Prompt:** Click on the category with the most books.
- **Correct Answer:** James (12 books \= 6 symbols)
- **On Correct:** "James read the most—12 books. 6 symbols, each worth 2."

---

**Interaction 3.4: How Many in All?**

- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Same "Books Read" graph. System highlights James (6 symbols) and Lily (5½ symbols). Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "How many books did James and Lily read IN ALL?"
- **Prompt:** How many books did James and Lily read IN ALL?
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: 11, 17, 23, 28\]
- **Correct Answer:** 23
- **Answer Rationale:**
  - 23 \= Correct (12 \+ 11 \= 23\)
  - 11 \= Error: Used only one value (Lily's count)
  - 17 \= Error: Added symbol counts (6 \+ 5.5 ≈ 11-12, then miscalculated)
  - 28 \= Error: Added incorrectly or included extra category
- **On Correct:** "23 books in all. James read 12, Lily read 11\. 12 plus 11 is 23."

---

**Interaction 3.5: Two-Step Problem (Chained)**

- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Same "Books Read" graph. No categories highlighted initially. Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "Here's a two-step problem. How many MORE books did Maya and Sofia read together than James?"
- **Guide:** "Step 1: How many books did Maya and Sofia read together?"
- **Prompt:** "How many books did Maya and Sofia read in all?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: 9, 12, 16, 19\]
- **Correct Answer:** 16
- **On Correct:** "16 books together."
- **Visual Update:** Step 1 total displayed on screen. Comparison category highlights for Step 2\.
- **Guide:** "Step 2: Compare James to that total."
- **Prompt:** "James read 12\. Maya and Sofia read 16\. Who read more, and by how many?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: "James read 4 more", "Maya and Sofia read 4 more", "James read 28 more", "They read the same"\]
- **Correct Answer:** "Maya and Sofia read 4 more"
- **On Correct:** "Maya and Sofia read 4 more books together than James. 16 minus 12 is 4."

**→ SECTION 3 COMPLETE.**

### **Bridge to Exit Check**

- **Visual:** Final graph remains briefly.
- **Guide:** "You made scaled picture graphs. You know each symbol equals 2\. You know half-symbols show 1\. Let's see what you know."

**→ LESSON COMPLETE.**

### **Required Phrases (Must Appear in Script)**

- "Each symbol shows 2 items, not 1" (the critical leap)
- "The KEY tells you the scale" / "The key is like a decoder"
- "Scale of 2" / "Each symbol equals 2"
- "Scale of 1 means each symbol shows 1 item. Scale of 2 means each symbol shows 2 items."
- "Always check the key first"
- "Groups of 2" (multiplication language foundation)
- "Half of 2 is 1" (half-symbol teaching)
- "Odd numbers need half-symbols" / "\[Number\] is odd"
- "\[Number\] is even—it groups perfectly into 2s"
- "\[Number\] is an EVEN number—it groups perfectly into 2s"

---

### **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**

- ❌ "Each symbol represents 5/10" (M3-M4 scales)
- ❌ "Multiply by 2" / "Times 2" / "×" (multiplication notation is M8)
- ❌ "Skip count" as formal instruction (casual use okay; formal is M3)
- ❌ "The axis shows..." (axis vocabulary is M4)
- ❌ "Bar graph" (M3-M4)
- ❌ "You thought carefully" / "You're so smart\!" (assumed states, identity labels)
- ❌ "Perfect\!" / "Amazing\!" / "Excellent\!" (overpraise)

---

### **Misconception Prevention**

**\#1 Counting Symbols Instead of Scaling:**

- Animation explicitly shows transformation (items → groups → symbols)
- Interaction 1.2 tests understanding of what "scale of 2" means with distractors
- Interaction 1.3 explicitly asks "What is the scale?" before reading values
- Every reading interaction requires calculating actual value, not symbol count
- Interaction 1.6 and 3.4 include distractors that ARE symbol counts (catching the error)
- Guide consistently narrates: "5 symbols, each worth 2, that's 10"

**\#2 Scale Means Addition:**

- Interaction 1.2 includes "Add 2 to each number" as explicit distractor
- "Groups of 2" language throughout (not "plus 2" or "add 2")
- Animation shows GROUPING, not adding
- Remediation uses count-by-2s, not addition equations

**\#3 Can't Create Fractional Symbols:**

- Dedicated Section 2 for half-symbol introduction
- Explicit "half of 2 \= 1" teaching
- Even/odd connection made explicit
- Multiple practice opportunities with odd numbers in Section 3

### **1.7.5 Success Criteria**

**The One Thing:** Students understand that in a scaled picture graph, each symbol represents 2 items (not 1), and they can create graphs using this understanding—including half-symbols for odd numbers.

**Ready for Exit Check:** Students can:

- Explain that the KEY shows the SCALE (what each symbol means)
- Explain that "scale of 2" means each symbol \= 2 items
- Create scaled picture graphs from data tables
- Determine correct number of symbols for even numbers
- Use half-symbols correctly for odd numbers
- Answer "how many more/fewer" and "in all" questions using scaled graphs

**Ready for Module 3:** Students have solid 1:2 scale foundation including half-symbols. They understand grouping language ("groups of 2") which will connect to skip-counting in M3 (groups of 5\) and multiplication in M7.
