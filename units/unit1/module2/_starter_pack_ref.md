## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**Students must understand that in a scaled picture graph, each symbol represents MORE than one item—specifically, each symbol \= 2 items, so we count by 2s (not by 1s) to find totals.**

**Critical Misconception:** Counting Symbols Instead of Scaling (\#1) — Students ignore the scale value and count pictures directly. When scale says "each symbol \= 2" and there are 5 symbols, student answers "5" instead of "10."

**Success Indicator:** Student correctly creates a picture graph where 10 items \= 5 symbols AND can explain "each symbol shows 2."

**Biggest Risk:** Students who don't internalize "symbol ≠ item" will struggle with all subsequent scale work (M3: 1:5, M4: 1:10) and the multiplication bridge in M7.

## **1.1 LEARNING GOALS**

*Verbatim from OUR Curriculum — Script Must Achieve These*

**L1:** "Interpret scaled picture graphs to generate questions about the data."

**Module Goal (Student-Facing):** "You'll learn to read and create picture graphs where each symbol shows MORE than one item. One picture \= 2 items. Let’s see how that works That changes everything\!"

**Exit Check Tests:**

* Can student create a scaled picture graph (1:2) from a data table?
* Can student determine correct number of symbols for even values (8 items \= 4 symbols)?
* Can student determine correct number of symbols for odd values (7 items \= 3½ symbols)?

* Can student answer "how many more/fewer" questions using their created graph?

### **1.1.2 Module Bridges**

**From Module 1:** Students read picture graphs and bar graphs with 1:1 scale. They understand that "the KEY shows the SCALE" and practiced "scale of 1" language. They can solve "how many more/fewer" problems by subtracting and "in all" problems by adding. They understand both horizontal and vertical orientations.

**This Module:** Students experience THE critical leap: each symbol can represent MORE than one item. Through:

* Watching their collected data TRANSFORM via Grouping Animation (10 items → 5 groups of 2 → 5 symbols)
* Creating scaled picture graphs (1:2) from data tables
* Learning half-symbols for odd numbers ("half of 2 \= 1")
* Applying M1's comparison skills to their created graphs

**To Module 3:** Students will apply their understanding of "each symbol \= multiple items" to scale of 5\. The grouping concept (groups of 2 → groups of 5\) and half-symbol understanding transfer directly. Skip-counting connection becomes more explicit.

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

* THE CRITICAL CONCEPT: Each symbol \= 2 items (not 1\)
* Grouping Animation: Visual proof that items group into symbols
* Reading scaled picture graphs (scale of 2\)
* Creating scaled picture graphs from data tables
* Even numbers: Clean symbol counts (8 items \= 4 symbols)
* Odd numbers: Half-symbol requirement (7 items \= 3½ symbols)
* Mathematical precision: "Half of 2 \= 1"
* Even/odd connection: Predict when half-symbols are needed
* "Groups of" language (planting seeds for M7)
* Both horizontal and vertical picture graph orientations
* "How many more/fewer" and "in all" questions after graph creation
* **Scale reflection (observation only):** After independent graph creation, students OBSERVE same data displayed as 1:1 alongside their 1:2 graph. Purpose: cognitive conflict ("same data, different representation") builds metarepresentational competence. Students do NOT create at scale of 1 — they observe and compare. (Research basis: English 2010/2012 data modeling; Watson & Moritz 2001 cognitive conflict; diSessa 1991 metarepresentational expertise)

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* Scale of 5 or scale of 10 (Modules 3-4)
* Bar graphs (Module 4\)
* Scale selection/comparison (Module 5\)
* Two-step problems as single submissions (M6 deep practice)
* Formal multiplication notation (Module 7+)
* Creating graphs with more than 4 categories

⚠️ **THE CRITICAL LEAP PRINCIPLE:** Research identifies scaled graphs as the most difficult elementary graphing concept. This module exists to thoroughly establish that symbols can represent multiple items. Everything else is secondary to this understanding.

⚠️ **ANIMATION NON-NEGOTIABLE:** The Grouping Animation (items → groups → symbols) must appear in Warmup and Early Lesson activities. Research shows visual transformation has effect size 0.50-0.77 for this concept.

⚠️ **EVEN-BEFORE-ODD PRINCIPLE:** Students must succeed with even numbers (clean symbol counts) before encountering odd numbers (half-symbols required). Half-symbols are introduced Mid-lesson, NOT Early.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Counting Symbols Instead of Scaling (PRIMARY) — Misconception \#1**

**Trigger Behavior:** When scale says "each symbol \= 2" and there are 5 symbols, student answers "5" instead of "10." Student counts the pictures directly, ignoring the scale/key.

**Why It Happens:**

* Grade 2 experience was exclusively 1:1 (symbol \= item)
* Natural inclination to count what you see
* Key/scale may be visually small or overlooked
* Abstract concept that picture "stands for" something other than itself

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Animation is key:** Grouping Animation visually SHOWS transformation (items → symbols)
2. **Explicit key attention:** "ALWAYS check the key first. What does each symbol represent?"
3. **Count-by-2s practice:** When reading graphs, model "2, 4, 6, 8, 10" not "1, 2, 3, 4, 5"
4. **~~Reversal checks:~~** ~~After creating, verify: "5 symbols × 2 \= 10 items ✓"~~

**Remediation Approach:**

* **Light:** "Check the key. Each symbol \= how many?"
* **Medium:** Return to grouping animation; show 5 symbols unpacking to 10 items
* **Heavy:** Side-by-side: same 10 items shown as 10 symbols (1:1) vs 5 symbols (1:2)

---

### **1.4.2 Scale Means Addition (SECONDARY) — Misconception \#2**

**Trigger Behavior:** With "each symbol \= 2" and 5 symbols shown, student calculates 5+2=7 instead of 5×2=10.

**Why It Happens:**

* Addition is the dominant operation in Grades 1-2
* "Each symbol \= 2" might be interpreted as "add 2 to the count"
* Multiplication concept not yet formalized

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **"Groups of" language:** "5 symbols means 5 groups of 2—that's 2, 4, 6, 8, 10"
2. **Visual unpacking:** Show each symbol "opening up" to reveal 2 items
3. **Skip-counting:** Count by 2s rather than emphasizing any operation
4. **Avoid multiplication language:** Don't say "multiply"—use counting patterns

**Remediation Approach:**

* **Light:** "Let's count by 2s: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10\. Five 2s is 10."
* **Medium:** Animate symbols splitting into items; count all items
* **Heavy:** Physical grouping with manipulatives or interactive grouping tool

---

### **1.4.3 Can't Create Fractional Symbols (TERTIARY) — Misconception \#3**

**Trigger Behavior:** With scale of 2 and data showing 7, student draws 3 or 4 complete symbols instead of 3½.

**Why It Happens:**

* Half-symbols are conceptually new
* Student may not realize "leftover" items need representation
* May round to nearest whole number of symbols

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Explicit half-symbol teaching:** Dedicated interaction for first odd number
2. **Mathematical precision:** "Half of 2 \= 1\. A half-symbol shows 1 item."
3. **Even/odd prediction:** "Is 7 even or odd? Odd numbers need half-symbols."
4. **Visual confirmation:** Half-symbol clearly shows half the image

**Remediation Approach:**

- **Light:** "7 is 6 plus 1 more. How many symbols for 6? Now add a half for the 1."
- **Medium:** Animate: 7 items → 3 groups of 2 with 1 leftover → 3½ symbols
- **Heavy:** Side-by-side comparison of 6 (3 symbols) vs 7 (3½ symbols)

## Practice Phase Notes

### Scale Analysis Templates (NEW — Cognitive Conflict)

M2 Practice SHOULD include 2–3 templates targeting scale analysis. These build toward state test questions presenting multiple graph options that require analyzing what is right/wrong with each graph.

**Research basis:** Watson & Moritz (2001) cognitive conflict; diSessa (1991) metarepresentational expertise. Showing students the same data with different scales and asking them to reason about the representations.

**New Skill for Goal Decomposition:**

* M2-NEW: Student can identify whether a picture graph correctly represents data at a given scale
  * Builds on: M2 scale/key understanding (Lesson 1.2) \+ symbol calculation (Lesson 1.4–2.4)
  * Lesson: Interaction 3.2b (scale reflection) provides conceptual foundation
  * Verbs: identify, compare
  * Tiers: baseline, stretch

**Template Types:**

1. **"Same data, two graphs"** — Show two picture graphs of identical data (one at scale 1, one at scale 2). Student answers: "Do these show the same information?" \[Yes/No\] followed by "How do you know?" \[MC: "Same values for each category" / "Same number of symbols" / "Same key"\]. Targets: understanding that scale changes representation, not data.

2. **"Which graph matches the data?"** — Show a data table and two picture graphs. One is correctly drawn at scale 2\. The other has the correct key ("Each ⭐ \= 2") but uses scale-of-1 symbol counts (i.e., 8 symbols for 8 items when it should be 4 symbols). Student selects the correct graph. Targets: Misconception \#1 (counting symbols instead of scaling) from an analytical angle.

3. **"What's the scale?"** — Show a completed picture graph WITHOUT a visible key, alongside a data table with matching values. Student identifies the scale used \[MC: 1, 2, 5, 10\]. Builds scale-identification fluency for later modules.

**Distribution:** 2–3 templates, primarily conceptual/transfer at baseline and stretch tiers. These supplement (not replace) existing CREATE and IDENTIFY templates. Target: \~4–6 problems in pool (\~8–10% of total).

**Constraint:** These templates use COMPLETED graphs for analysis. Students do NOT create graphs at scale of 1 in Practice. All creation problems remain at scale of 2\.

## **1.6 WARMUP (3-4 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

---

### **Purpose**

Activate prior knowledge of picture graphs from M1 while introducing the critical new concept: scaled representation. Students collect personal data, PREDICT how grouping will work, WATCH their prediction confirmed through animation, then VERIFY they can read the result. This creates ownership ("my data"), cognitive engagement ("was I right?"), and confidence ("I can do this") before Lesson formalizes vocabulary and has students create.

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 4 interactions (Game \+ Prediction \+ Animation \+ Verification/Bridge) |
| Active Participation | 3 of 4 require student response |
| Format | Data Collection Game → Prediction → Grouping Animation → Verification \+ Bridge |
| Cognitive Load | 20-30% (prediction and verification are accessible) |
| Remediation | Light only (prediction has gentle correction) |
| Tone | Medium-High energy, curious, builds anticipation |
| Engagement Anchors | Narrative Setup (Minis), Personalization (their data), Curiosity Gap (prediction check) |

### **Constraints**

| USE | AVOID |
| :---- | :---- |
| Informal language ("Watch this," "Check it out") | Formal phrasing ("Observe the transformation") |
| Contractions naturally | Stiff language |
| "Groups of 2" (casual, sets up Lesson) | "Scale of 2" (formal vocabulary—save for Lesson) |
| Observable acknowledgments ("You counted 8") | Assumed states ("You paid attention") |
| 3 categories | 2 or 4+ categories |
| Even values only (6-14 range) | Odd values (half-symbols are Lesson content) |

---

### **Warmup Type Rationale**

**Standard Playbook Types:** Binary Choice, WODB, Number Talk, Notice & Wonder, Estimation 180, Visual Patterns, Mystery Reveal

**M2 Approach:** Data Collection Game \+ Grouping Animation

**Why this serves the concept:**

* Unit 1 is about DATA—personal data creates investment
* The ANIMATION is the critical teaching tool for scaled graphs (effect size 0.50-0.77)
* Watching transformation with THEIR data makes abstract concept concrete
* "Watch then do" structure: Warmup \= observe transformation, Lesson \= create graphs
* Follows Playbook PRINCIPLES (low cognitive load, engagement anchors, curiosity gap) while adapting STRUCTURE for the critical conceptual leap

**Test:** If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for Lesson? YES—they would miss the animated transformation that makes "each symbol \= 2 items" visible and concrete.

**Interaction W.2: Review \+ Prediction**

- **Purpose:** Confirm data, then create cognitive engagement BEFORE animation through prediction. No feedback—the animation reveals the answer.
- **Visual:** Results displayed clearly: "\[Category A\]: \[X\], \[Category B\]: \[Y\], \[Category C\]: \[Z\]"
- **Guide:** "You counted \[X\] \[Category A\], \[Y\] \[Category B\], and \[Z\] \[Category C\]. That's *your* data."
- **Guide:** "Here's something new: what if each symbol showed 2 items instead of 1?"
- **Guide:** "You have \[X\] \[Category A\]. If you group them into 2s..."
- **Prompt:** "How many groups of 2 can you make from \[X\]?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: X÷2 (correct), X, X+2, X-2\]
- **On Submit:** No correct/incorrect feedback. Student's selection is recorded.
- **Guide:** "You think \[student's answer\] groups. Let's see if you're right..."
- **Engagement Anchor:** Curiosity Gap (will my prediction be right?)
- **Duration:** 30-45 seconds

**Design Note:** This is a prediction, not an assessment. Withholding feedback creates genuine curiosity—the animation becomes the "answer." Students watch actively to check their thinking.

---

**Interaction W.3: Grouping Animation (THE KEY MOMENT)**

* **Purpose:** Visually demonstrate the critical leap—items grouping into 2s, then becoming symbols. This is the conceptual foundation for all of M2.
* **Engagement Anchor:** Curiosity Gap (what will happen to my data?)
* **Visual:** Animation workspace showing student's collected data on a picture graph.
* **Animation Sequence (45-60 seconds):**

**Phase 1: Items Appear**

* First category items appear (e.g., 8 Red Minis)
* Guide: "You counted 8 Red Minis. Watch what happens..."

**Phase 2: Grouping**

* Items visually pair up: 2, 2, 2, 2
* Guide: "8 items... break/split/show into 4 groups of 2..."

**Phase 3: Transformation**

* Each group of 2 becomes one symbol on the graph
* Guide: "Each group of 2 becomes ONE symbol."
* "4 groups... 4 symbols."

**Phase 4: Key Reveal**

* Key appears: "Each ⭐ \= 2"
* Guide: "Each symbol shows 2 items, not 1\. That's different from before\!"

**Phase 5: Remaining Categories (Condensed)**

* Second and third categories transform (faster, same pattern)
* Guide: "\[Y\] items... \[Y÷2\] groups of 2... \[Y÷2\] symbols."
* Guide: "\[Z\] items... \[Z÷2\] groups of 2... \[Z÷2\] symbols."

**Phase 6: Complete Graph**

* Full scaled picture graph visible with all 3 categories
* Guide: "Your data is now a SCALED picture graph\!"
* **No student prompt** (observation interaction)
* **On Complete:** Scaled picture graph with student's data remains visible.

---

**Interaction W.4: Verification \+ Bridge**

- **Purpose:** Confirm students can read the scaled graph. Create anticipation for Lesson.
- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Completed 1:2 scaled picture graph from Warmup animation. 3 categories, all even values. Key visible: "Each \[symbol\] \= 2." Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "Now you've seen how grouping works\! Each group of 2 became one symbol."
- **Prompt:** "Look at \[Category B\]. How many symbols do you see?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice showing symbol counts\]
- **Correct Answer:** Actual symbol count for Category B (Y÷2)
- **On Correct:** "\[Y÷2\] symbols. And each one shows 2 items. So that's \[Y\] total\!"
- **Guide (Bridge):** "You can already read a scaled graph\! Now let's learn more about how they work."
- **Duration:** 30-40 seconds

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: The Critical Leap (Even Numbers)**

**Purpose:** Establish that each symbol \= 2 items through animation, vocabulary formalization, and guided graph creation. All values are even numbers—students master whole symbols before encountering half-symbols.

**Interaction 1.1: Your Scaled Graph**

- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Completed Warmup graph (3 categories, even values, 1:2 scale). Key visible.

- **Guide:** "You already read this graph—you counted \[Y÷2\] symbols for \[Category B\]. Each symbol shows 2 items. This is called a SCALED picture graph."

**Interaction 3.2b: Scale Reflection — Same Data, Different Scale \[OBSERVATION ONLY\]**

- **Purpose:** Leverage cognitive conflict (Watson & Moritz 2001\) — students just created a complete graph independently and now observe what the same data looks like at scale of 1\. Builds metarepresentational competence (English 2010/2012) by making scale a visible, comparable choice rather than an invisible given.
- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** Student's completed 1:2 graph on LEFT. Key: "Each ⭐ \= 2."
- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 1: Reading).** System-generated 1:1 graph of SAME data on RIGHT. Key: "Each ⭐ \= 1." Both visible simultaneously. Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "Here's the same data — but with scale of 1\. Every book gets its OWN symbol."
- **Guide:** "Look at James. Your graph shows 6 symbols. The scale-of-1 graph shows 12 symbols. Same number of books\!"
- **Prompt:** "Which graph uses fewer symbols to show the same data?"
- **Student Action:** \[Click to select: "Scale of 1" or "Scale of 2"\]
- **Correct Answer:** Scale of 2
- **On Correct:** "Scale of 2 — just 6 symbols instead of 12 for James. Same data, fewer symbols. That's why we use scales."
- **On Incorrect:** "Both graphs show the same data\! But count the symbols — scale of 2 uses fewer. That's what makes scaled graphs useful."
- **Guide:** "Now let's use YOUR graph to answer some questions."
- **Duration:** 30–40 seconds
- **No student graph creation** (observation and comparison only)

**Design Notes:**

- System generates the 1:1 graph — student does NOT create it. This preserves the "problem-solve with scaled graph" principle.
- The 1:1 graph disappears after this interaction. Interactions 3.3–3.5 use ONLY the student's 1:2 graph.
- Placed AFTER successful independent creation (3.2) and BEFORE problem-solving (3.3) — students reflect on what they built before using it analytically.
- Research alignment: Watson & Moritz (2001) Study 3 showed cognitive conflict via alternative representations improved pictograph reasoning. English (2010, 2012\) showed making representation decisions builds metarepresentational competence.
- Total time cost: \~35 seconds. Minimal impact on Lesson pacing.

### **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**

- ❌ "Each symbol represents 5/10" (M3-M4 scales)
- ❌ "Multiply by 2" / "Times 2" / "×" (multiplication notation is M8)
- ❌ "Skip count" as formal instruction (casual use okay; formal is M3)
- ❌ "The axis shows..." (axis vocabulary is M4)
- ❌ "Bar graph" (M3-M4)
- ❌ "You thought carefully" / "You're so smart\!" (assumed states, identity labels)
- ❌ "Perfect\!" / "Amazing\!" / "Excellent\!" (overpraise)

### **Misconception Prevention**

**\#1 Counting Symbols Instead of Scaling:**

- Animation explicitly shows transformation (items → groups → symbols)
- Interaction 1.2 tests understanding of what "scale of 2" means with distractors
- Interaction 1.3 explicitly asks "What is the scale?" before reading values
- Every reading interaction requires calculating actual value, not symbol count
- Interaction 1.6 and 3.4 include distractors that ARE symbol counts (catching the error)
- Guide consistently narrates: "5 symbols, each worth 2, that's 10"

**\#2 Scale Means Addition:**

- Interaction 1.2 includes "Add 2 to each number" as explicit distractor
- "Groups of 2" language throughout (not "plus 2" or "add 2")
- Animation shows GROUPING, not adding
- Remediation uses count-by-2s, not addition equations

**\#3 Can't Create Fractional Symbols:**

- Dedicated Section 2 for half-symbol introduction
- Explicit "half of 2 \= 1" teaching
- Even/odd connection made explicit
- Multiple practice opportunities with odd numbers in Section 3

**The One Thing:** Students understand that in a scaled picture graph, each symbol represents 2 items (not 1), and they can create graphs using this understanding—including half-symbols for odd numbers.

**Task S.1: Representation Transfer (Type D) — Scale Toggle Exploration**

- **Purpose:** Surface understanding that scaled graphs use fewer symbols for the same data. Connect scale to efficiency. Student toggles between scales themselves — agency in switching views reinforces metarepresentational competence (English 2010/2012) more powerfully than static side-by-side observation.
- **Visual:** Numeric Data Table visible (Cats=8, Dogs=6, Birds=10, Fish=4). Picture Graph displayed alongside, defaulting to scale of 2 (1:2). Scale toggle control visible with options: "Each ⭐ \= 1" and "Each ⭐ \= 2." Key updates dynamically when toggled.
- **Guide:** "Here's data you know — and a graph with scale of 2\. But watch what happens when you change the scale."
- **Prompt:** "Toggle the scale to 1\. What happens to the graph?"
- **Student Action:** Click scale toggle from 2 to 1\. Graph animates: symbols double in count (4 symbols → 8 for Cats, 3 → 6 for Dogs, 5 → 10 for Birds, 2 → 4 for Fish). Key updates to "Each ⭐ \= 1."
- **Guide:** "Look at that\! Cats went from 4 symbols to 8\. Same 8 cats — but now every cat gets its own symbol."
- **Prompt:** "Toggle back to scale of 2\. Which scale uses fewer symbols?"
- **Student Action:** Click scale toggle from 1 back to 2\. Graph animates: symbols halve. Then select answer: \[Multiple choice: "Scale of 1" / "Scale of 2"\]
- **Correct Answer:** Scale of 2
- **On Correct:** "Scale of 2 — fewer symbols, same data. That's what makes scaled graphs efficient."
- **On Incorrect:** "Toggle back and forth one more time. Count the symbols each way. Scale of 2 always uses fewer."
- **Connection (Guide states):** "You just saw the same data change shape. The INFORMATION stays the same — only the number of symbols changes. That's the power of scale."

**Design Notes:**

- Student toggles TWICE (2→1, then 1→2). The back-and-forth is the point — it makes the scale-representation relationship tangible and repeatable, not a one-time observation.
- Animation between states should be smooth enough to show symbols appearing/disappearing, reinforcing that the same data produces different visual density. A brief transition (\~0.5s) is ideal — fast enough to feel responsive, slow enough to see the change.
- The toggle remains active only during S.1. It is NOT available during S.2–S.4.
- This replaces the static three-way display (data table \+ 1:1 graph \+ 1:2 graph) with a two-state toggle (1:1 ↔ 1:2) plus data table. The data table serves as the stable anchor while the graph transforms.
- The MC question at the end confirms understanding — it's not just about playing with the toggle.

**Task S.2: Pattern Discovery (Type A) — Half-Symbol Mastery**

- **Purpose:** Confirm understanding of when half-symbols are needed. Students must apply even/odd reasoning without visual scaffolding.
- **Visual: Picture Graphs (Mode 2: Creating).** NEW graph context. Key visible: "Each \[symbol\] \= 2." Mix of even and odd values displayed. Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "You learned about half-symbols. If you made a graph using this key, which numbers would need a half-symbol?"
- **Prompt:** "If you made a graph with scale of 2, which numbers would need a half-symbol? Select all that apply."
- **Student Action:** \[Multi-select: ☐ 8  ☐ 7  ☐ 12  ☐ 9\]
- **Correct Answer:** 7 and 9 (odd numbers)
- **On Correct:** "7 and 9 are ODD numbers. They don't split evenly into groups of 2, so they need a half-symbol for the leftover 1."
- **On Incorrect (missed one):** "Check again. Which numbers are odd? Odd numbers always need a half-symbol with scale of 2."
- **Guide (Follow-up):** "What does a half-symbol show?"
- **Prompt:** What does a half-symbol show?
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice:
  - "Half of 2, which is 1"
  - "Half of the total"
  - "2 items"
  - "Nothing—it's decoration"\]
- **Correct Answer:** "Half of 2, which is 1."
- **Connection (Guide states):** "Half of 2 is 1\. A half-symbol shows 1 item. That's how we show odd numbers exactly—the full symbols show groups of 2, the half shows the 1 left over."

**Design Note:** Key provides context without scaffolding the answer. Students must reason about even/odd rather than just visually identifying half-symbols.
