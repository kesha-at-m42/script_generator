## **1.7 LESSON (10-12 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

---

### **Core Purpose**

Teach students to recognize when problems need two steps and execute a reliable strategy: **"Find the combined total first, then compare."** This is STRATEGY instruction, not concept introduction—students already know graph reading and comparison language from M1-M5.

---

### **Pedagogical Flow**

M6 Lesson differs from typical CRA sequence because we're teaching a problem-solving STRATEGY, not a new concept. The flow is:

| Phase | M6 Application |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Activation** | Verify M1 language is intact ("how many more" = subtract) |
| **Strategy Introduction** | Worked example with think-aloud: Guide models the strategy |
| **Guided Practice** | Student tries with full scaffolding ("What should we find first?") |
| **Fading** | Scaffolding reduces; student takes ownership of planning |
| **Bridge** | Transition to Exit Check with confidence |

**Key Principle:** The strategy must be TAUGHT explicitly through worked examples before students attempt independently. Students should hear the Guide's planning process modeled before they plan themselves.

---

### **Lesson Structure**

| Section | Focus | Interactions |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | Single-Step Activation (BRIEF) | 2 |
| Section 2 | Two-Step Strategy Introduction | 4 |
| Section 3 | Scaffolded Practice with Fading | 4 |
| **Total** | | **10** |

---

## **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Single-Step Activation**

**Purpose:** Quickly verify M1 comparison language is intact. Students solve 1-2 single-step problems to activate "how many more = subtract" before extending to two-step. This is NOT re-teaching—it's activation.

---

**Interaction 1.1: Transition from Warmup**

- **Visual:** Bar graph appears (horizontal, scale of 5, 4 categories: Pizza 45, Tacos 25, Salad 10, Burgers 15). Title: "Favorite Lunch Foods"
- **Guide:** "In Warmup, you read graphs with different scales. Now let's use graphs to solve problems. Here's data about students' favorite lunch foods."
- **Guide:** "First, a quick check on something you learned before."
- **No student action** (setup for 1.2)

**Design Note:** Brief transition acknowledges Warmup, establishes problem-solving context.

---

**Interaction 1.2: Single-Step Activation**

- **Visual:** Same bar graph. Question appears: "How many more students chose pizza than salad?"
- **Guide:** "How many more students chose pizza than salad? Remember what 'how many more' tells us to do."
- **Prompt:** How many more students chose pizza than salad?
- **Options:** [A) 55, B) 35, C) 30, D) 15]
- **Correct Answer:** B (45 - 10 = 35)
- **On Correct:** "35 more. 'How many more' means compare—we subtract. Pizza is 45, salad is 10, so 45 minus 10 is 35."
- **On Incorrect (A - added):** "'How many more' means compare, not combine. When we compare, we subtract to find the difference."
- **On Incorrect (C or D - read wrong value):** "Check the bars again. Pizza is 45, salad is 10. 'How many more' means subtract: 45 minus 10."
- **Remediation:** Light only (this is activation, not new teaching)

**Design Note:** This verifies M1 foundation. If students struggle here, the issue is prior knowledge, not M6 content. Light remediation only.

---

**[SECTION TRANSITION]**

- **Visual:** Same bar graph remains.
- **Guide:** "Good—'how many more' means compare, and compare means subtract. You've got that. Now here's something new: What if the problem asks about MORE than one category?"

---

## **1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Two-Step Strategy Introduction**

**Purpose:** Introduce two-step problems through explicit worked example with Guide think-aloud. Teach the strategy: "Find the combined total first, then compare." Students see the strategy modeled before attempting.

---

**Interaction 2.1: Return to the Warmup Question**

- **Visual:** Same bar graph from Warmup (Favorite Lunch: Pizza 45, Tacos 25, Salad 10, Burgers 15). Question displayed: "How many more students chose pizza than tacos and salad together?"
- **Guide:** "Remember this question from Warmup? You noticed it needed more than one step. Now let's figure out HOW to solve it."
- **Guide:** "First, let's find the categories we need. Which categories does the question ask about? Select all of the needed categories."
- **Prompt:** "Which categories do we need?" (multi-select)
- **Options:** [Pizza, Tacos, Salad, Burgers]
- **Correct Answer:** Pizza, Tacos, Salad (3 selected)
- **On Correct:** "Those three. Notice we DON'T need burgers—the question doesn't ask about burgers."
- **On Incorrect (includes Burgers):** "Read the question again. Does it mention burgers?"
- **On Incorrect (missing one):** "The question mentions pizza, tacos, AND salad. Select all three."

---

**Interaction 2.2: Worked Example with Think-Aloud**

- **Visual:** Same bar graph (Pizza 45, Tacos 25, Salad 10, Burgers 15). Guide avatar appears. Question displayed: "How many more students chose pizza than tacos and salad together?"
- **Guide:** "Let me show you how I solve this. First, I ask myself: What should I find FIRST?"
- **Guide:** "The question says 'tacos and salad together.' That word 'together' tells me to ADD those first. Let me find that total."
- **Visual:** Tacos bar (25) and Salad bar (10) highlight together. Animation shows: 25 + 10 = 35
- **Guide:** "Tacos is 25, salad is 10. Combined, that's 35."
- **Guide:** "NOW I can compare. How many more is pizza than that total of 35?"
- **Visual:** Pizza bar (45) highlights. Shows: 45 compared to 35.
- **Guide:** "Pizza is 45 while tacos and salad combined is 35. 45 minus 35 is 10. Pizza is 10 MORE than tacos and salad combined."
- **No student action** (worked example—student observes)

**Design Note:** This is a FULL worked example with think-aloud. The Guide models planning ("What should I find first?"), identifies the trigger word ("together"), executes both steps, and states the answer clearly. Students observe before attempting. The question is straightforward—"more" means more—so cognitive load stays on the STRATEGY, not on linguistic reversals.

---

**Interaction 2.3: Strategy Naming**

- **Visual:** Strategy statement appears on screen: **"Find the combined total first, then compare."**
- **Guide:** "Here's the strategy: When you see 'combined' or 'together' or 'altogether,' find the total of those categories FIRST. Then compare to the other given category."
- **Guide:** "Find the combined total first, then compare. That's the key to these problems. What do we find first when we see 'combined'?"
- **Prompt:** "What do we find first when we see 'combined'?"
- **Options:** [A) The total, B) The difference, C) The largest number]
- **Correct Answer:** A
- **On Correct:** "Find the total by adding. That's always our first step when we see 'combined.'"
- **On Incorrect:** "When the question says 'combined,' we add those categories first. That's our first step."

**Design Note:** Explicit strategy naming. The strategy is stated, displayed, and verified. This is the key learning of M6.

---

**Interaction 2.4: Guided Practice (Full Scaffolding)**

- **Visual:** NEW bar graph appears (vertical, scale of 10, 4 categories: Skateboards 15, Rollerblades 10, Bikes 65, Scooters 30). Title: "Favorite Ways to Roll"
- **Guide:** "Your turn. Here's a new graph."
- **Visual:** Question appears: "How many more students chose bikes than scooters and skateboards together?"
- **Guide:** "This problem needs two steps. What should we find first?"
- **Prompt:** Select what to find first.
- **Options:** [A) Add scooters and skateboards, B) Compare bikes to scooters, C) Add all four categories]
- **Correct Answer:** A
- **On Correct:** "Right—scooters and skateboards combined. Let's find that total."
- **On Incorrect (B):** "Not yet. The question asks about 'scooters and skateboards together.' We need to find that total FIRST, then compare to bikes."
- **On Incorrect (C):** "The question only mentions three categories: bikes, scooters, and skateboards. We don't need rollerblades."

**[CHAINED - Step 2]**

- **Visual:** Scooters (30) and Skateboards (15) highlight. Shows: 30 + 15 = 45
- **Guide:** "Scooters and skateboards combined is 45. Now we compare. How many more is bikes than 45?"
- **Prompt:** How many more is bikes than 45?
- **Options:** [A) 110, B) 65, C) 45, D) 20]
- **Correct Answer:** D (65 - 45 = 20)
- **On Correct:** "20 more. Bikes is 65, combined is 45, so 65 minus 45 is 20. You used the strategy: find the combined total first, then compare."
- **On Incorrect (A - added all):** "You added instead of compared. After finding the combined total, we SUBTRACT to compare."
- **On Incorrect (B - just bikes value):** "65 is bikes, but the question asks how many MORE than the combined total. Subtract: 65 minus 45."
- **On Incorrect (C - combined total):** "45 is the combined total—good first step! But now we need to compare: how many more is 65 than 45?"

**Design Note:** Full scaffolded problem with chained submission. Student identifies first step, then completes second step. Guide provides explicit feedback connecting to strategy.

---

**[SECTION TRANSITION]**

- **Visual:** Graph clears.
- **Guide:** "You're getting it. Find the combined total first, then compare. Let's practice more—and I'll give you less help each time."

---

## **1.7.3 LESSON SECTION 3: Scaffolded Practice with Fading**

**Purpose:** Students practice the two-step strategy with scaffolding that fades. By the end, students identify the first step without explicit prompting.

---

**Interaction 3.1: Reduced Scaffolding (3-Step Chain)**

- **Visual:** NEW bar graph (horizontal, scale of 5, 4 categories). Title: "Fruit Stand Sales". Values: Grapes 15, Apples 45, Bananas 30, Oranges 25.
- **Visual:** Question appears: "How many fewer oranges and grapes combined were sold than apples?"
- **Guide:** "Here's another one. What should we find first?"

**[CHAINED - Step 1: Identify Strategy]**

- **Prompt:** Select what to find first.
- **Options:** [A) Add oranges and grapes, B) Subtract grapes from oranges, C) Compare apples to oranges]
- **Correct Answer:** A
- **On Correct:** "Right—oranges and grapes combined. Find that total."
- **On Incorrect:** "Look for 'combined' in the question. Oranges and grapes combined—add those first."

**[CHAINED - Step 2: Execute Combining]**

- **Visual:** Oranges bar (25) and Grapes bar (15) highlight.
- **Guide:** "Oranges is 25. Grapes is 15. What's the combined total?"
- **Prompt:** How many oranges and grapes combined?
- **Options:** [A) 10, B) 30, C) 40, D) 45]
- **Correct Answer:** C (25 + 15 = 40)
- **On Correct:** "40 combined."
- **On Incorrect (A):** "You subtracted. 'Combined' means add: 25 plus 15."
- **On Incorrect (B or D):** "Check your addition. 25 plus 15 equals..."

**[CHAINED - Step 3: Execute Comparison]**

- **Visual:** Combined value (40) displayed. Apples bar (45) highlights.
- **Guide:** "Oranges and grapes combined is 40. Apples is 45. Now find how many fewer."
- **Prompt:** How many fewer oranges and grapes combined than apples?
- **Options:** [A) 85, B) 45, C) 40, D) 5]
- **Correct Answer:** D (45 - 40 = 5)
- **On Correct:** "5 fewer. You found the combined total first, then compared."
- **On Incorrect (A):** "You added. 'How many fewer' means compare—subtract."
- **On Incorrect (B or C):** "Those are values from the problem. The question asks for the difference: 45 minus 40."

**Design Note:** Reduced scaffolding—Guide asks "What should we find first?" but doesn't say "This needs two steps." Three-step chain ensures student EXECUTES both operations, not just identifies them.

---

**Interaction 3.2: Minimal Scaffolding (3-Step Chain)**

- **Visual:** NEW bar graph (horizontal, scale of 10, 4 categories). Title: "Pets at the Shelter". Values: Dogs 65, Cats 40, Birds 25, Fish 30.
- **Visual:** Question appears: "How many more dogs are there than fish and birds combined?"
- **Guide:** "One more. Think about what to do first."

**[CHAINED - Step 1: Identify Strategy]**

- **Prompt:** Select what to find first.
- **Options:** [A) Add fish and birds, B) Compare dogs to fish, C) Subtract birds from dogs, D) Add all pets]
- **Correct Answer:** A
- **On Correct:** "Fish and birds combined. Find that total."
- **On Incorrect:** "The question says 'fish and birds combined.' Find that total first."

**[CHAINED - Step 2: Execute Combining]**

- **Visual:** Fish bar (30) and Birds bar (25) highlight.
- **Guide:** "Fish is 30. Birds is 25. What's the combined total of fish and birds?"
- **Prompt:** How many fish and birds combined?
- **Options:** [A) 5, B) 45, C) 55, D) 65]
- **Correct Answer:** C (30 + 25 = 55)
- **On Correct:** "55 combined."
- **On Incorrect (A):** "You subtracted. 'Combined' means add: 30 plus 25."
- **On Incorrect (B or D):** "Fish is 30. Birds is 25. Check your addition."

**[CHAINED - Step 3: Execute Comparison]**

- **Visual:** Combined value (55) displayed. Dogs bar (65) highlights.
- **Guide:** "Fish and birds combined is 55. Now find how many more dogs there are."
- **Prompt:** How many more dogs than fish and birds combined?
- **Options:** [A) 120, B) 65, C) 55, D) 10]
- **Correct Answer:** D (65 - 55 = 10)
- **On Correct:** "10 more dogs. You found the combined total first, then compared."
- **On Incorrect (A):** "You added. 'How many more' means compare—subtract."
- **On Incorrect (B or C):** "Those are values from the problem. The question asks for the difference: 65 minus 55."

**Design Note:** Minimal scaffolding—just "Think about what to do first." No explicit mention of two steps or strategy. Three-step chain ensures student executes both operations.

---

**Interaction 3.3: Mixed Problem (Single-Step Check)**

- **Visual:** Same bar graph (Dogs 65, Cats 40, Birds 25, Fish 30).
- **Guide:** "How about this one? How many more dogs are there than cats?"
- **Prompt:** How many more dogs are there than cats?
- **Options:** [A) 105, B) 65, C) 30, D) 25]
- **Correct Answer:** D (65 - 40 = 25)
- **On Correct:** "25 more dogs than cats. This one was just one step—no 'combined,' so straight to comparing."
- **On Incorrect (A):** "You added. 'How many more' means subtract."
- **On Incorrect (B or C):** "Dogs is 65, cats is 40. The difference is 65 minus 40."

**Design Note:** Single-step problem mixed in to ensure students don't assume EVERY problem needs two steps. This prevents over-application of the new strategy.

---

**Interaction 3.4: Final Two-Step (Independence Check)**

- **Visual:** NEW bar graph (vertical, scale of 5, 5 categories: Yellow 20, Red 45, Green 25, Purple 15, Blue 45). Title: "Favorite Colors"
- **Visual:** Question appears: "How many more students chose red than chose green or yellow together?"
- **Guide:** "Last one before we check what you know."
- **Prompt:** Select what to find first.
- **Options:** [A) Add green and yellow, B) Compare red to green, C) Add all colors, D) Subtract yellow from green]
- **Correct Answer:** A
- **On Correct:** "Total green and yellow first."
- **On Incorrect:** "Green and yellow together—add those first."

**[CHAINED - Step 2]**

- **Visual:** Green (25) and Yellow (20) highlight. Shows: 25 + 20 = 45
- **Guide:** "Green and yellow combined is 45. Now compare to red."
- **Prompt:** How many more students chose red than green and yellow combined?
- **Options:** [A) 90, B) 45, C) 10, D) 0]
- **Correct Answer:** D (45 - 45 = 0)
- **On Correct:** "Zero! Red is 45, combined is 45—they're equal. No difference."
- **On Incorrect:** "Red is 45, combined is 45. What's the difference? 45 minus 45 is 0."

**Design Note:** This problem has a "0" answer—students must recognize when values are equal. This prevents assumption that there's always a positive difference.

---

### **Bridge to Exit Check**

- **Visual:** Graph clears. Strategy statement reappears: **"Find the combined total first, then compare."**
- **Guide:** "You've got the strategy: find the combined total first, then compare. Let's see what you know."

---

## **Required Phrases (Must Appear in Script)**

- "How many more" (comparison language—multiple times)
- "Combined" / "together" / "altogether" (trigger words for two-step)
- "What should we find first?" (planning prompt)
- "Find the combined total first, then compare" (strategy statement)
- "'How many more' means compare—we subtract" (M1 callback)

## **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**

- "Two-step problem" (teacher language—don't label for students)
- "First step" / "Second step" as formal labels (use natural language)
- "You need to..." / "You have to..." (controlling—use "Let's..." or "Try...")
- "Perfect!" / "Excellent!" / "Amazing!" (overpraise)
- "You're thinking carefully" (assumed internal state)

---

## **Misconception Prevention**

**#17: All Data Must Be Used**

- Interaction 2.1 explicitly asks students to identify relevant categories
- All graphs have 4-5 categories; problems use only 2-3
- Feedback addresses "you don't need [category]" when students include extras

**#6: "More Than" Means Add**

- Interaction 1.2 activates "how many more = compare = subtract"
- Step 2 of every chained problem requires subtraction
- Distractor A in most problems is the "added" answer with explicit remediation

**#7: Combining Before Comparing (Wrong First Step)**

- Worked example (2.2) explicitly models "What should I find first?"
- Every two-step problem asks students to identify first step before calculating
- Distractors include "skip to comparison" option with remediation

---

## **1.7.4 Incomplete Script Flags**

**If ANY of these are true, STOP and complete the script:**

- [ ] Missing worked example with think-aloud (Interaction 2.2)
- [ ] Missing strategy naming (Interaction 2.3)
- [ ] Missing single-step activation (Interaction 1.2)
- [ ] Missing mixed single-step problem (Interaction 3.3)
- [ ] Total interaction count below 10
- [ ] No chained submissions in two-step problems
- [ ] Missing any required phrase
- [ ] Contains any forbidden phrase

---

## **1.7.5 Success Criteria**

**The One Thing:** Students understand that some problems need two steps—and figuring out what to do FIRST is the key. The strategy is: find the combined total first, then compare.

**Ready for Exit Check:** Student can:

- Recognize when a problem needs one step vs. two steps
- Identify what to find first when they see "combined/together/altogether"
- Execute the two-step strategy with chained submissions
- Solve single-step comparison problems fluently

**Ready for Practice:** After Exit Check, students will practice with scaffolding that continues to fade, eventually reaching full independence (single submissions, no prompts).
