## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

Students must understand that **some problems need two steps—and figuring out what to do FIRST is the key to solving them.** Comparison language ("how many more/fewer" \= subtract, "in all" \= add) was taught in M1 and practiced in M3-M4. M6's NEW learning is recognizing when a problem needs multiple operations and executing a reliable strategy: **find the combined total first, then compare.**

**Critical Misconception:** All Data Must Be Used (\#17) — Students incorporate ALL categories into their answer when the question only requires specific categories. This is especially problematic when problems need multiple operations and students combine irrelevant data.

**Success Indicator:** Given a problem like "How many more students chose pizza and tacos together than salad?", student:

1. Recognizes this needs two steps
2. Identifies what to find first ("I need to add pizza and tacos")
3. Completes the comparison ("Now I compare that total to salad")
4. Executes both steps accurately

**Biggest Risk:** Students who don't pause to think "What should I find first?" will jump to calculations without a plan, often grabbing the first numbers they see. Students may also regress on comparison skills under increased cognitive load—M6 must activate prior learning before extending it.

**Terminology Note:** "One-step" and "two-step" are TEACHER/STANDARDS language for sequencing complexity. Students are not told "this is a two-step problem"—they learn to recognize when problems need multiple operations through scaffolded prompts like "What should we find first?"

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

**Single-Step Problems (ACTIVATION, not new instruction):**

- Quick callback: "Remember, 'how many more' means compare—we subtract"
- Fluency activation in Warmup and early Lesson
- Single-step problems appear in mixed practice to maintain discrimination

**Two-Step Problems (NEW LEARNING):**

*Strategy We Teach:* **"Find the combined total first, then compare."**

This is the most intuitive, generalizable approach for two-step comparison problems. While other valid solution paths exist (e.g., sequential subtraction), we teach ONE reliable strategy to build automaticity and reduce cognitive load.

*Problem Types Included:*

| Type | Structure | Example | Scaffolding Prompt |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Combine then compare** | $(A \+ B) \- C$ | "How many more Goldfish than Guppies and Tetras combined?" | "What should we find first?" → Add Guppies \+ Tetras |
| **Combine then compare (flipped)** | $C \- (A \+ B)$ | "How many fewer Guppies and Tetras combined than Goldfish?" | "What should we find first?" → Add Guppies \+ Tetras |
| **Target minus combined** | $Target \- (A \+ B)$ | "The goal is 100\. How many more needed than Apples and Bananas sold?" | "What should we find first?" → Add Apples \+ Bananas |

*Scaffolding Progression:*

- **Full scaffolding (Activities 3-4):** "This problem needs two steps. What should we find first?" \+ chained submissions with step prompts
- **Reduced scaffolding (Activities 5-6):** "What should we find first?" only \+ chained submissions
- **Minimal scaffolding (Activities 7-9):** Chained submissions without explicit prompts
- **Independent (Activities 10-12):** Single submission (non-chained); student plans and executes without prompting

**Problem-Solving Skills:**

- Identifying relevant categories: "What information do you need?"
- Ignoring extraneous data (graphs have 3-5 categories; problems typically use only 2-3)
- Reading bar graphs with scales of 2, 5 and 10 (both orientations)

### **❌ Must Not Include**

**Problem Types Excluded:**

| Type | Structure | Why Excluded |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Compare then combine | $(A \- B) \+ C$ | Unnatural phrasing; "What first?" is less obvious |
| Sequential subtraction (as taught path) | $C \- A \- B$ | Valid but not taught; students may discover independently |
| Hypothetical/conditional | $(A ± X) \- B$ | Requires tracking value changes; too complex |
| Compare two totals (non-chained) | (A+B)−(C+D) in single submission | Beyond Lesson scope; chained version permitted in Practice (see Practice Extensions below) |

### **Practice Phase Extensions (Not in Lesson/Exit Check)**

These problem types are valid for Practice but not explicitly taught in Lesson:

| Type | Structure | Delivery | Example |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Compare two totals** | (A+B)−(C+D) | Always chained: Step 1 finds first total, Step 2 finds second total, Step 3 compares | "How many more books did Maya and Ben read together than Carla and David together?" |
| **Sums of 3+ categories** | A+B+C (or more) | Single or chained | "How many total books did all four students read?" |

**Rationale:** Students have the component skills—these are natural complexity progressions. Keeping them in Practice (not Lesson) maintains Lesson focus on the core "combine two, then compare" strategy while allowing transfer and extension.

**Other Exclusions:**

- Extended single-step instruction (already taught in M1, practiced in M3-M4)
- Graph creation as primary activity (limited to Activities 1-2 only)
- Picture graphs (not part of 3.MD.B.3; bar graphs only)
- Scale selection (M5 skill; scales are PRE-SET in M6)
- Three-operation problems (beyond 3.MD.B.3 scope)
- More than 5 categories per graph (cap at 5; typical is 4\)
- Multiplication notation or "groups of" language (save for M7)

### **⚠️ Scope Warnings**

**TERMINOLOGY:**

- "One-step" and "two-step" are TEACHER/STANDARDS language only
- Student-facing scaffolding: "This problem needs two steps" → "What should we find first?" → (no prompt)
- Avoid repetitive labeling; goal is recognition through structure, not memorization of classification

⚠️**CATEGORY COUNT:** Graphs use 3-5 categories, with 4 as the typical count. This matches state test format and provides sufficient extraneous data for students to practice identifying relevant categories without overwhelming. Variety across activities (some 3, mostly 4, occasional 5\) prevents students from assuming all graphs look the same.

⚠️**SINGLE-STEP AS ACTIVATION:** M6 does NOT re-teach "how many more \= subtract." This was explicitly taught in M1 Section 3 and practiced across M3-M4. Warmup activates this prior knowledge; Lesson moves to two-step extension after brief activation.

⚠️**CHAINED → SINGLE SUBMISSION:** Multi-operation problems use chained submissions (Activities 3-9) to enable step-by-step remediation. Activities 10-12 shift to single submissions to test true independence and prepare for state test format.

⚠️**SINGLE STRATEGY INSTRUCTION:** We teach "find the combined total first, then compare" as THE strategy. Other valid paths (e.g., sequential subtraction) are not explicitly taught. If a student uses an alternative valid approach and gets the correct answer, accept it—but remediation always models the taught strategy.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 All Data Must Be Used (PRIMARY) — Misconception \#17**

**Trigger Behavior:** When asked "How many more chose pizza and tacos together than salad?", student incorporates ALL categories (pizza \+ tacos \+ salad \+ pasta) into their calculation.

**Why It Happens:**

- Students have learned that "using the information" often means using ALL the information
- Two-step problems increase cognitive load, making it harder to filter relevant data
- Graphs with 4-5 categories present more "tempting" data to include
- Students may not pause to identify which categories the question actually asks about

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Explicit category identification:** Before solving, ask "Which categories do you need for this question?"
2. **Visual highlighting:** System highlights only relevant bars when student correctly identifies categories
3. **Multi-select interactions:** Students select relevant categories before solving (M1 pattern)
4. **Extraneous data exposure:** All graphs have 3-5 categories, but problems typically use only 2-3

**Remediation Approach:**

- **Light:** "Read the question again. Which categories does it ask about? Just those ones."

---

### **1.4.2 "More Than" Means Add (SECONDARY) — Misconception \#6**

**Trigger Behavior:** For "How many more chose pizza than tacos?", student adds (35 \+ 20 \= 55\) instead of subtracting (35 \- 20 \= 15).

**Why It Happens:**

- "More" sounds like "add more"
- Cognitive load from two-step context may cause regression to familiar (but incorrect) pattern
- Student may have weak M1 foundation that resurfaces under pressure
- Larger numbers (scale of 5 and 10\) make mental math harder, increasing error likelihood

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Activation callback:** Warmup explicitly recalls "how many more \= compare \= subtract"
2. **Language consistency:** Always pair "how many more/fewer" with "compare" and "difference"
3. **Visual support:** Highlight both bars being compared before asking comparison question

**Remediation Approach:**

- **Light:** "This is a comparison. 'How many more' means find the difference. Subtract."

---

### **1.4.3 Combining Before Comparing (TERTIARY) — Misconception \#7**

**Trigger Behavior:** For "How many more chose pizza and tacos together than salad?", student starts by comparing (pizza vs. salad) instead of combining (pizza \+ tacos first). Student skips the combining step entirely or performs operations in wrong order.

**Why It Happens:**

- Student doesn't pause to analyze problem structure
- "How many more" triggers immediate comparison response
- Student grabs first numbers seen without planning
- Two-step problems are new; students lack strategic approach

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Explicit planning prompt:** "What should we find first?" before any calculation
2. **Step-by-step scaffolding:** Early problems break into explicit chained submissions
3. **Wrong-step feedback:** If student attempts comparison first, Guide redirects: "Wait—we need to find the combined total first."
4. **Strategy language:** Reinforce "Find the combined total first, then compare"

**Remediation Approach:**

- **Light:** "Read it again. What does 'pizza and tacos together' mean? Find that first."

## **1.5 TOY SPECIFICATIONS**

### **1.5.1 Bar Graphs (Mode 1: Reading) — PRIMARY TOY**

**Purpose:** Students read pre-made bar graphs to extract values for problem-solving. This is the PRIMARY interaction mode for M6.

#### Core Specifications

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Scales** | 1:2, 1:5 and 1:10 (mix across activities) |
| **Orientations** | Both horizontal and vertical |
| **Categories** | 3-5 per graph (4 most common) |
| **Axis Markings** | Tick marks at scale increments; ghost gridlines at halfway points |
| **Values** | All  values are clean multiples of scale or half-scale (no estimation needed);  exclude values requiring true estimation (e.g., 23 on scale of 10, 7 on scale of 5\) |
| **Interaction** | Click bar to highlight; click two bars to compare (both highlight) |

#### M6-Specific Constraints

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Mix scales across activities (not within single graph) | Use only one scale throughout |
| Mix orientations across activities | Restrict to one orientation |
| Use 3-5 categories with 4 most common | Use 6+ categories |
| Ensure all values are multiples of 5 | Require estimation (no values like 37\) |
| Support multi-bar selection for comparison | Limit to single-bar selection |

#### Bar Selection for Problem-Solving

**Single-bar selection:** Student clicks one bar → bar highlights
**Multi-bar selection:** Student clicks two bars → both highlight
**Clear selection:** Click elsewhere or "Clear" button to reset

**Visual Feedback:**

- Selected bars show highlight color
- Unselected bars dim slightly (for problems requiring specific categories)
- Value labels appear on selected bars only after student has read or calculated and submitted the values

**Value Distribution Guidance:**

* **Scale of 2:** Any whole number (evens land on tick marks, odds land on halfway points—e.g., 6, 7, 8, 12, 13, 14)
* **Scale of 5:** Values are multiples of 5 (e.g., 15, 20, 25, 30\)
* **Scale of 10:** Values are multiples of 5, including halfway points (e.g., 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45\)

Include at least one halfway-point value per scale-of-10 graph to maintain M4 interpolation fluency. Students should not perceive "bars always land on lines" as a rule.

## **1.7 LESSON (10-12 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

---

### **Core Purpose**

Teach students to recognize when problems need two steps and execute a reliable strategy: **"Find the combined total first, then compare."** This is STRATEGY instruction, not concept introduction—students already know graph reading and comparison language from M1-M5.

---

### **Pedagogical Flow**

M6 Lesson differs from typical CRA sequence because we're teaching a problem-solving STRATEGY, not a new concept. The flow is:

| Phase | M6 Application |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Activation** | Verify M1 language is intact ("how many more" \= subtract) |
| **Strategy Introduction** | Worked example with think-aloud: Guide models the strategy |
| **Guided Practice** | Student tries with full scaffolding ("What should we find first?") |
| **Fading** | Scaffolding reduces; student takes ownership of planning |
| **Bridge** | Transition to Exit Check with confidence |

**Key Principle:** The strategy must be TAUGHT explicitly through worked examples before students attempt independently. Students should hear the Guide's planning process modeled before they plan themselves.

### **Lesson Structure**

| Section | Focus | Interactions |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | Single-Step Activation (BRIEF) | 2 |
| Section 2 | Two-Step Strategy Introduction | 4 |
| Section 3 | Scaffolded Practice with Fading | 4 |
| **Total** |  | **10** |

## **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Single-Step Activation**

**Purpose:** Quickly verify M1 comparison language is intact. Students solve 1-2 single-step problems to activate "how many more \= subtract" before extending to two-step. This is NOT re-teaching—it's activation.

---

**Interaction 1.1: Transition from Warmup**

- **Visual:** Bar graph appears (horizontal, scale of 5, 4 categories: Pizza 45, Tacos 25, Salad 10, Burgers 15). Title: "Favorite Lunch Foods"
- **Guide:** "In Warmup, you read graphs with different scales. Now let's use graphs to solve problems. Here's data about students' favorite lunch foods."
- **Guide:** "First, a quick check on something you learned before."

## **Required Phrases (Must Appear in Script)**

- ✅ "How many more" (comparison language—multiple times)
- ✅ "Combined" / "together" / "altogether" (trigger words for two-step)
- ✅ "What should we find first?" (planning prompt)
- ✅ "Find the combined total first, then compare" (strategy statement)
- ✅ "'How many more' means compare—we subtract" (M1 callback)

## **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**

- ❌ "Two-step problem" (teacher language—don't label for students)
- ❌ "First step" / "Second step" as formal labels (use natural language)
- ❌ "You need to..." / "You have to..." (controlling—use "Let's..." or "Try...")
- ❌ "Perfect\!" / "Excellent\!" / "Amazing\!" (overpraise)
- ❌ "You're thinking carefully" (assumed internal state)

## **Misconception Prevention**

**\#17: All Data Must Be Used**

- Interaction 2.1 explicitly asks students to identify relevant categories
- All graphs have 4-5 categories; problems use only 2-3
- Feedback addresses "you don't need \[category\]" when students include extras

**\#6: "More Than" Means Add**

- Interaction 1.2 activates "how many more \= compare \= subtract"
- Step 2 of every chained problem requires subtraction
- Distractor A in most problems is the "added" answer with explicit remediation

**\#7: Combining Before Comparing (Wrong First Step)**

- Worked example (2.2) explicitly models "What should I find first?"
- Every two-step problem asks students to identify first step before calculating
- Distractors include "skip to comparison" option with remediation

### **Opening Frame**

- **Purpose:** Signal shift from Practice to reflection/connection.
- **Visual:** Clean transition screen. Strategy statement appears: "Find the combined total first, then compare."
- **Guide:** "You've solved problems that needed one step AND problems that needed two steps. Now let's think about HOW you knew what to do."

---

**Task S.1: Pattern Discovery (Type A) — Recognizing Trigger Words**

- **Purpose:** Surface the pattern that certain words signal two-step problems. Students identify the linguistic cues they've been using.
- **Visual:** Three problem questions displayed (text only, no graphs needed):
  1. "How many more dogs are there than cats?"
  2. "How many more dogs are there than cats and birds combined?"
  3. "How many students chose red or blue altogether?"
- **Guide:** "Look at these three questions. Two of them need two steps. One needs just one step. Which one?"
- **Prompt:** "Which question needs just ONE step?"
- **Options:** \[A) Question 1, B) Question 2, C) Question 3\]
- **Correct Answer:** A (Question 1 has no "combined/altogether")
- **On Correct:** "Question 1\. It just compares dogs to cats—no combining first."
- **On Incorrect:** "Look for the words 'combined' or 'altogether.' Those tell you to add first."
- **Guide (Follow-up):** "What's the clue that tells you a problem needs two steps?"
- **Prompt:** What's the clue that tells you a problem needs two steps?.
- **Options:** \[A) The word "more", B) Words like "combined" or "altogether", C) Big numbers, D) Two categories are used
- **Correct Answer:** B
- **Connection (Guide states):** "Words like 'combined,' 'together,' and 'altogether' are your signal. When you see them, you know: find that total FIRST, then compare."

**Design Note:** This makes the linguistic pattern explicit. Students have been using these cues implicitly—now they can name them.

---

**Task S.2: Consolidation (Type B) — Sorting Problems by Type \[ACTIVE TASK\]**

- **Purpose:** Consolidate the strategy as a deliberate decision process through active sorting. Students demonstrate discrimination between one-step and two-step problems.
- Visual: Three problem questions on LEFT side:
  1. "How many more chose pizza than tacos?"
  2. "How many more chose pizza and tacos combined than salad?"
  3. "How many fewer chose burgers and hot dogs together than pizza?"
- Two drop zones on RIGHT side labeled: "JUST COMPARE" and "COMBINE FIRST, THEN COMPARE"
- Guide: "Here's what you've been doing. You look at a problem and decide: Do I just compare? Or do I combine first, then compare? Sort these questions."
- **Prompt:** "Match the task to the group”
- **Interaction:** Student drags each question to the appropriate zone (MATCHING task)
- **Correct Sorting:**
  - JUST COMPARE: Question 1 (direct comparison)
  - COMBINE FIRST, THEN COMPARE: Questions 2 and 3 (both have "combined/together")
- **On All Correct:**  "You sorted them all. Words like 'combined' and 'together' tell you to find a total first."
- **On Partial/Incorrect:** "Look for the words 'combined' or 'together.' Those tell you there's an extra step."
- **Follow-up (after sorting):**
  - **Guide:** "What question do you ask yourself to decide?"
  - **Prompt:** Select the key question.
  - **Options:** \[A) "What's the biggest number?", B) "Does it say 'combined' or 'together'?", C) "How many categories are there?", D) "Should I add or subtract?"\]
  - **Correct Answer:** B
  - **On Correct:** "That's the key question. The answer tells you whether it's one step or two."
  - **Incorrect (C):** "The number of categories can be something to look at, but it doesn't guarantee that it is one step or two step."
- **Connection (Guide states):** "This is strategic thinking. You're not just doing math—you're PLANNING your approach based on what the problem says."

**Design Note:** Active sorting task ensures students demonstrate discrimination physically, not just through selection. The follow-up MC consolidates the metacognitive rule. Per Synthesis Playbook: "At least one task requires placement, matching, or creation (not all multiple-choice)."

---

**Task S.3: Real-World Bridge (Type C) — Beyond Math Class**

- **Purpose:** Connect the "combine first, then compare" pattern to real-world decision-making.
- **Visual:** Three scenarios displayed:
  1. "Who has more stickers—just Maya, or Ben and Carlos put together?"
  2. "Which team scored more—the Red team alone, or the Blue and Green teams combined?"
  3. "Did I spend more on only snacks, or on drinks and dessert altogether?"
- **Guide:** "This kind of thinking shows up all the time—not just in math problems. What would you need to DO to answer all of these?"
- **Prompt:** "What would you need to DO to answer all of these?"
- **Options:** \[A) Count everything, B) Combine some things first, then compare, C) Find the biggest number, D) Subtract twice\]
- **Correct Answer:** B
- **On Correct:** "Combine first, then compare. That pattern shows up everywhere."
- **On Incorrect (A):** "Counting everything doesn't answer the question—you need to combine specific things, then compare."
- **On Incorrect (C):** "Finding the biggest number doesn't tell you how they compare to a combined group."
- **On Incorrect (D):** "You subtract once—after you combine. The pattern is: combine first, THEN compare."

**Design Note:** This transfers the mathematical pattern to everyday reasoning, cementing its usefulness beyond school.

---

**Task S.4: Metacognitive Reflection (Type 1 — Strategy Identification)**

- **Purpose:** Students reflect on what helped them approach these problems successfully.
- **Visual:** Strategy statement remains visible: "Find the combined total first, then compare."
- **Guide:** "What helped you most when figuring out what to do first?"
- **Prompt:** Select what helped most.
- **Options:**
  - A) Looking for words like "combined" or "altogether"
  - B) Reading the question carefully
  - C) Thinking about what the question is really asking
  - D) Remembering the strategy
- **On Selection (A):** "Spotting those trigger words is huge. They tell you exactly what to do."
- **On Selection (B):** "Careful reading pays off. The question tells you what it needs."
- **On Selection (C):** "Thinking about what the question REALLY asks—that's mathematical reasoning."
- **On Selection (D):** "Having a strategy ready helps. 'Find combined total first, then compare' works every time."

**Design Note:** All responses are validated. The goal is reflection, not assessment. Per Playbook guidance for Modules 4-6, Type 1 (Strategy Identification) is preferred.

### **Identity-Building Closure**

- **Purpose:** Specific, growth-oriented affirmation. NO M7 preview (per backbone 1.1.2).
- **Visual:** Strategy statement fades. Brief celebratory visual or clean screen.
- **Guide:** "You learned something important today. You learned to LOOK at a problem before jumping in. You ask: 'What should I find first?'"
- **Guide:** "That's not just math—that's strategic thinking. You're becoming someone who plans before solving."

**Voice Note:** Specific to what student learned (planning, recognizing problem structure). Not generic praise. Connects to identity ("becoming someone who...") without over-claiming. Does NOT preview M7.

**Key Design Decisions Summary:**

1. **No True Estimation** — All graph values are multiples of 5 (landing on tick marks OR halfway points). Scale-of-10 graphs include halfway-point values (25, 35, etc.) to maintain M4 interpolation fluency. No values requiring estimation beyond the midpoint.
2. **Limited Graph Creation** — M6 creation activities (1-2 only) connect creation to problem-solving context; students see how graphs they make become tools for answering questions.
3. **Bar Graphs Only** — Picture graphs are not part of the 3.MD.B.3 standard being addressed. M6 uses bar graphs exclusively to consolidate fluency before M7's multiplication transition.
4. **Two-Step as Chained Interactions** — Two-step problems implemented as separate sequential submissions in scaffolded activities (Activities 3-9). Enables targeted remediation at each stage.
5. **Independence Progression** — Activities 10-12 use single submissions (non-chained) to test true independence before Exit Check.
6. **Single-Step as Activation Only** — "How many more" \= subtract was taught in M1, practiced in M3-M4. M6 activates this prior knowledge, then moves to two-step extension.
7. **Scale of 2 in Warmup Only** — Warmup activates M5 "all scales" knowledge. Lesson uses only scales 5 and 10 to minimize cognitive load during new instruction.
8. **Single Strategy Instruction** — M6 teaches ONE reliable two-step strategy: "Find the combined total first, then compare." Alternative valid paths are not explicitly taught.
