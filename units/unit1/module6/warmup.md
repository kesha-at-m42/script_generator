## **1.6 WARMUP (3-4 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure + parameters (AI writes dialogue)

---

### **Purpose**

Activate prior knowledge of graph reading across scales (including scale of 2) and comparison language ("how many more" = subtract) from M1-M5. Build confidence with familiar skills before Lesson introduces the new challenge: problems that need more than one step.

**Key Function:** This is the ONLY place scale of 2 appears in M6. Warmup activates M5's "all scales" knowledge before Lesson focuses on scales 5 and 10 only.

---

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 3 meaningful interactions + bridge |
| Active Participation | 3 of 3 require student response |
| Format | Graph Reading (scale 2) → Comparison Question → Scale Variety Check → Bridge |
| Cognitive Load | 20-30% (light, accessible—all prior skills) |
| Remediation | Light only |
| Tone | Medium-High energy, confident, activating |
| Engagement Anchors | Curiosity Gap (teasing "bigger" problems), Quick Success (familiar skills) |

---

### **Constraints**

| USE | AVOID |
| :---- | :---- |
| Informal language ("Let's warm up," "Check this out") | Formal phrasing ("Observe the following data") |
| Observable acknowledgments ("You found 24") | Assumed states ("You remembered that well") |
| Scale of 2 (activation of M5) | Teaching scale reading (they know this) |
| Single-step comparison only | Two-step problems (save for Lesson) |
| "How many more" = subtract callback | Extended re-teaching of comparison |

---

### **Warmup Type Rationale**

**Standard Playbook Types:** Binary Choice, WODB, Number Talk, Notice & Wonder, Estimation 180, Visual Patterns, Mystery Reveal

**M6 Approach:** Quick Activation Sequence

**Why this serves the concept:**

- M6's new learning is problem-solving STRATEGY, not a new concept
- Students need confident activation of prior skills (graph reading, comparison language) before strategy instruction
- Scale of 2 must appear somewhere—Warmup is the only appropriate place
- Unlike M1-M4, we don't need Data Collection Game because M6 isn't about graph creation
- Quick, successful activation builds confidence for Lesson's new challenge

**Test:** If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for Lesson? YES—they would enter Lesson without activating comparison language ("how many more" = subtract), which is foundational to the new strategy.

---

## **Warmup Interactions**

**Interaction W.1: Graph Reading with Scale of 2**

- **Purpose:** Activate M5 "all scales" knowledge. Confirm students can read graphs with scale of 2 (only appearance in M6).
- **Hook (first 15-20 seconds):**
  - **Visual:** Bar graph appears (horizontal, scale of 2, 4 categories: Soccer 12, Basketball 7, Tennis 6, Swimming 11). Title: "After-School Activities"
  - **Guide:** "Let's warm up your graph skills. Here's a bar graph—check the scale. How many students chose soccer?"
- **Prompt:** "How many students chose soccer?"
- **Options:** [A) 6, B) 10, C) 12, D) 24]
- **Correct Answer:** C (6 axis marks × 2 = 12)
- **On Correct:** "12 students. Scale of 2—each mark on the axis shows 2 students."
- **On Incorrect (A - counted bars):** "Check the scale. Each axis mark is 2 students, not 1. Count by 2s."
- **On Incorrect (B or D):** "Look at the soccer bar and count by 2s using the scale."
- **Remediation:** Light only

**Design Note:** This is the ONLY scale-of-2 graph in M6. Brief, successful reading activates M5 knowledge.

---

**Interaction W.2: Comparison Question (Single-Step)**

- **Purpose:** Activate M1 comparison language: "how many more" = subtract. This is the foundation for Lesson's two-step strategy.
- **Visual:** Same bar graph remains (Soccer 12, Basketball 7, Tennis 6, Swimming 11).
- **Guide:** "Now a question about this data. How many more students chose soccer than tennis?"
- **Prompt:** "How many more students chose soccer than tennis?"
- **Options:** [A) 18, B) 12, C) 6, D) 3]
- **Correct Answer:** C (12 - 6 = 6)
- **On Correct:** "6 more. 'How many more' means compare—we subtract. Soccer is 12, tennis is 6, difference is 6."
- **On Incorrect (A - added):** "'How many more' means compare, not combine. Subtract to find the difference."
- **On Incorrect (B or D):** "Soccer is 12, tennis is 6. 'How many more' means find the difference: 12 minus 6."
- **Remediation:** Light only

**Design Note:** Explicit callback to M1 language. The phrase "'how many more' means compare—we subtract" should echo in Lesson Section 1.

---

**Interaction W.3: Quick Scale Check (Lesson Context Setup)**

- **Purpose:** Activate scale-of-5 reading while introducing the context students will see again in Lesson 2.1-2.2.
- **Visual:** Bar graph appears (vertical, scale of 5, 4 categories: "Favorite Lunch"). Values: Pizza 45, Tacos 25, Salad 10, Burgers 15.
- **Guide:** "Different graph, different scale. Quick check."
- **Task:** "How many students chose pizza?"
- **Options:** [A) 9, B) 25, C) 45, D) 50]
- **Correct Answer:** C (9 axis marks × 5 = 45)
- **On Correct:** "45 students. Scale of 5—you've got it."
- **On Incorrect:** "Check the scale—it's 5. Count by 5s."
- **Remediation:** Light only

**Design Note:** This context (Favorite Lunch) returns in Lesson 2.1-2.2. Students will recognize it when the two-step question appears.

---

**Interaction W.4: Bridge to Lesson**

- **Purpose:** Tease the exact question students will work through in Lesson 2.2. Creates anticipation with a promise of resolution.
- **Visual:** Same bar graph remains (Pizza 45, Tacos 25, Salad 10, Burgers 15). Question appears: "How many more students chose pizza than tacos and salad together?"
- **Guide:** "You've got graph reading down. You've got comparison down. But look at THIS question..."
- **Guide:** (reading question) "How many more students chose pizza than tacos and salad together?"
- **Guide:** "See that word 'together'? This question needs more than one step. We'll come back to THIS question in the lesson—and you'll be able to answer it."
- **No student action** (bridge only—Lesson 2.2 resolves this)

**Design Note:** The exact question from W.4 becomes the worked example in Lesson 2.2. Students experience the full arc: "I couldn't answer this → Guide showed me how → Now I can."
