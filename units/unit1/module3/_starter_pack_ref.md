## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**Students must understand that scale of 5 means each symbol represents 5 items, and they can use skip-counting by 5s—a skill they already know—to find totals when reading and creating scaled graphs.**

**Critical Misconception:** Counting Symbols Instead of Scaling (\#1) — Students ignore the scale value and count pictures directly. When scale says "each symbol \= 5" and there are 4 symbols, student answers "4" instead of "20."

**Success Indicator:** Student correctly creates a picture graph where 20 items \= 4 symbols AND can explain "I counted by 5s: 5, 10, 15, 20—that's 4 groups of 5."

**Biggest Risk:** Students who don't connect skip-counting to symbol meaning will struggle when M4 introduces scale of 10 and requires reading values between tick marks on bar graphs.

## **1.1 LEARNING GOALS**

*Verbatim from OUR Curriculum — Script Must Achieve These*

**L1:** "Represent data using scaled picture graphs."

**Module Goal (Student-Facing):** "You'll learn to make picture graphs where each symbol shows 5 items—not 2, but 5\! You already know how to count by 5s. That same skill helps you read and create these graphs."

**Exit Check Tests:**

* Can student answer "how many more/fewer" and "in all" questions using their created graph?
* Can student recognize that scale of 5 is more efficient than scale of 2 for larger numbers?

**From Module 2:** Students learned THE critical leap: each symbol can represent multiple items (not just 1). They mastered scale of 2, including half-symbols for odd numbers. ## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

* Scale of 5: Each symbol represents 5 items
* Skip-counting connection: "When scale is 5, count by 5s to find totals"
* Hand/finger reference (Guide dialogue): "5 fingers on each hand—easy to remember"
* Grouping Animation: Visual proof that items group into 5s, then become symbols

* **Bar graph FORMAT introduction:** Same data shown as Picture (1:5) AND Bar (1:5) side-by-side
* **Informal "axis" vocabulary:** "The numbers along the side are called the axis" (plural: axes)
* **Students create BOTH formats** (picture graph and bar graph) for same data
* **Three-scale reflection (observation only):** After independent graph creation, students OBSERVE same data displayed at 1:1, 1:2, AND 1:5 alongside their own graph. Purpose: extends M2's binary comparison to a three-way pattern ("bigger scale \= fewer symbols"), deepening metarepresentational competence. Students do NOT create at scale of 1 or 2 — they observe and compare. Builds on M2 Interaction 3.2b.
* Both horizontal and vertical orientations

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* Half-symbols or non-multiples of 5 (return in Module 4\)
* Scale of 10 (Module 4\)
* Scale selection/choosing between scales (Module 5\)
* Bar graph interpolation (reading values between tick marks—Module 4\)
* Two-step problems as single submissions (M6 deep practice)
* Multiplication notation (Module 7+)

⚠️ **MULTIPLES-ONLY PRINCIPLE:** All values in M3 are multiples of 5 (5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40). This keeps focus on the scale concept and skip-counting connection without half-symbol complexity. SME confirmed: "Fifths of a symbol" would be too complex and doesn't add pedagogical value.

⚠️ **FORMAT BEFORE SCALE PRINCIPLE:** M3 introduces bar graph FORMAT while keeping scale at 5\. This separates the cognitive shifts: students learn "bars show the same info as symbols" (format change) BEFORE M4 introduces scale of 10 (scale change). This reduces cognitive load.

⚠️ **FRONT-LOADED SCALE COMPARISON:** The 1:2 vs 1:5 efficiency comparison happens in Warmup/Early Lesson (not saved for Synthesis). Students need to see WHY scale of 5 matters before creating graphs with it.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Counting Symbols Instead of Scaling (PRIMARY) — Misconception \#1**

**Trigger Behavior:** When scale says "each symbol \= 5" and there are 4 symbols, student answers "4" instead of "20." Student counts the pictures directly, ignoring the scale/key.

**Why It Happens:**

* Grade 2 experience was 1:1 (symbol \= item); M2 introduced 1:2 but habit may not be solid
* Natural inclination to count what you see
* Key/scale may be visually small or overlooked
* Larger scale (5 vs 2\) makes the gap between symbol count and actual value bigger

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **"Check the key first" routine:** Every graph reading starts with "What does each symbol show?"
2. **Scale comparison:** Show same data as 1:2 AND 1:5 side-by-side—makes scale impact visible
3. **Count-by-5s practice:** When reading graphs, model "5, 10, 15, 20" not "1, 2, 3, 4"
4. **Reversal checks:** After creating, verify: "4 symbols, each shows 5, that's 20 ✓"

**Remediation Approach:**

* **Light:** "Check the key. Each symbol shows how many? Now count by 5s."
* **Medium:** Return to grouping animation; show 4 symbols unpacking to 20 items
* **Heavy:** Side-by-side: same 20 items shown as 10 symbols (1:2) vs 4 symbols (1:5)

---

### **1.4.2 "More Than" Means Add (SECONDARY) — Misconception \#6**

**Trigger Behavior:** For "How many more chose A than B?" student adds the values instead of subtracting. More likely with larger numbers (25 \+ 35 \= 60 instead of 35 \- 25 \= 10).

**Why It Happens:**

* "More" sounds like "add more"
* Larger numbers in M3 (vs M1-M2) make mental math harder, increasing errors
* Students may not have solidified M1's teaching that "how many more" \= compare \= subtract

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **Explicit callback:** "Remember from before: 'how many MORE' means COMPARE. We find the DIFFERENCE."
2. **Language consistency:** Always pair "how many more/fewer" with "find the difference" and "subtract"
3. **Visual support:** Highlight both bars/symbols being compared before asking the question

### **1.4.3 Scale Means Addition (TERTIARY) — Misconception \#2**

**Trigger Behavior:** With "each symbol \= 5" and 4 symbols shown, student calculates 4+5=9 instead of counting by 5s to get 20\.

**Why It Happens:**

* Addition is the dominant operation students know
* "Each symbol \= 5" might be interpreted as "add 5 to the count"
* "Groups of" concept not yet solidified

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. **"Groups of" language:** "4 symbols means 4 groups of 5"
2. **Skip-counting as bridge:** "Count by 5s: 5, 10, 15, 20\. That's what 4 groups of 5 gives you."

## PRACTICE PHASE NOTES FOR TEMPLATE GENERATION

### Bar Graph Creation Coverage (CRITICAL)

M3 Practice MUST include bar graph creation problems, not just picture graphs.

Rationale: M4 assumes bar graph creation proficiency. The Lesson introduces bar graphs in Section 2 (6 interactions), but mastery requires Practice reinforcement.

Target distribution for M3 Practice:

- Picture graph creation (scale of 5): 40-50%
- Bar graph creation (scale of 5): 40-50%
- Mixed comparison problems: 10-20%

Skill tracking for Goal Decomposition:

- M3-0X: Student can create scaled bar graph (1:5) given data table Builds on: M1 bar graph READING \+ M3 scale-of-5 understanding Lesson: Section 2 interactions Verbs: create Tiers: support, baseline, stretch

### Scale Analysis Templates (NEW — Cognitive Conflict / Three-Scale Comparison)

M3 Practice SHOULD include 3–4 templates targeting scale analysis across three scales (1, 2, 5). These build on M2's scale analysis templates and progress toward state test questions presenting multiple graph options.

**Research basis:** Watson & Moritz (2001) cognitive conflict; English (2010/2012) metarepresentational competence; diSessa (1991) inventing graphing. Multiple-scale comparison develops reasoning about representations.

**New Skills for Goal Decomposition:**

- M3-NEW-A: Student can identify whether a picture graph correctly represents data at a given scale (extends M2-NEW to scale of 5\)

  - Builds on: M2-NEW (scale analysis at 1:2) \+ M3 scale-of-5 understanding
  - Lesson: Interaction 3.2b (three-scale comparison) provides conceptual foundation
  - Verbs: identify, compare
  - Tiers: baseline, stretch


- M3-NEW-B: Student can determine which scale was used for a given picture graph

  - Builds on: M2-NEW \+ M3 skip-counting fluency
  - Lesson: Interaction 3.2b \+ Section 1 (scale meaning)
  - Verbs: identify
  - Tiers: baseline, stretch, challenge

**Template Types:**

1. **"Three graphs, same data"** — Show three picture graphs of identical data at scales 1, 2, and 5\. Ask: "Which graph has the most symbols?" \[MC\] and "Do all three graphs show the same information?" \[Yes/No\]. Targets: understanding that scale changes representation, not data. Extends M2 template type 1 to three scales.

2. **"Match the scale to the graph"** — Show three picture graphs (unlabeled keys) and three key options (Each ⭐ \= 1, Each ⭐ \= 2, Each ⭐ \= 5). Student matches each key to the correct graph by examining symbol counts against a visible data table. Targets: scale identification fluency.

3. **"What's wrong with this graph?"** — Show a picture graph with an incorrect number of symbols for the stated key. For example: key says "Each ⭐ \= 5" but the graph shows 20 symbols for a category that should have 20 items (correct symbol count \= 4). Student identifies the error from MC options. Targets: analytical graph evaluation — directly builds toward 4-option state test format.

4. **"Which graph correctly shows the data?"** — Show a data table and 3–4 picture graph options, each with a different scale or error. Student selects the correct graph(s). This is the closest approximation to the state test's multiple-graph-option format. Targets: integrated scale reasoning. Tier: stretch and challenge only.

**Distribution:** 3–4 templates, primarily conceptual/transfer at baseline, stretch, and challenge tiers. Target: \~5–8 problems in pool (\~8–12% of total).

**Constraint:** These templates use COMPLETED graphs for analysis. Students do NOT create graphs at scale of 1 or 2 in M3 Practice. All creation problems remain at scale of 5\.

## **1.6 WARMUP (3-4 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

### **Purpose**

Activate prior knowledge of scaled graphs (1:2) from M2 while introducing the key M3 concept: scale of 5 is more efficient for larger numbers. Students collect personal data, then OBSERVE the same data displayed as BOTH 1:2 and 1:5 picture graphs side-by-side. The discovery that "scale of 5 uses fewer symbols" creates curiosity and sets up Lesson's formalization of the skip-counting connection.

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 4 interactions (Game \+ Scale Comparison Display \+ Efficiency Discovery \+ Bridge) |
| Active Participation | 3 of 4 require student response |
| Format | Data Collection Game → Side-by-Side Scale Comparison → Efficiency Discovery → Bridge |
| Cognitive Load | 20-30% (observation and comparison are accessible) |
| Remediation | Light only |
| Tone | Medium-High energy, curious, builds anticipation |
| Engagement Anchors | Narrative Setup (Minis), Personalization (their data), Curiosity Gap (which is better?) |

### **Constraints**

| USE | AVOID |
| :---- | :---- |
| Informal language ("Check this out," "Look at this") | Formal phrasing ("Observe the comparison") |
| "Scale of 2" and "Scale of 5" (familiar from M2) | Formal teaching of new vocabulary |
| Observable acknowledgments ("You counted 20") | Assumed states ("You noticed the difference") |
| 3 categories | 2 or 4+ categories |
| Multiples of 5 only (10, 15, 20, 25, 30\) | Non-multiples |
| Values that show clear efficiency difference | Values too small to see benefit (e.g., 5\) |
| Half-symbols acceptable on 1:2 graph (reinforces M2) | Avoiding half-symbols unnecessarily |

---

### **Warmup Type Rationale**

**Standard Playbook Types:** Binary Choice, WODB, Number Talk, Notice & Wonder, Estimation 180, Visual Patterns, Mystery Reveal

**M3 Approach:** Data Collection Game \+ Scale Efficiency Comparison

**Why this serves the concept:**

* Unit 1 is about DATA—personal data creates investment
* M3's core insight is EFFICIENCY: same data, fewer symbols with scale of 5
* Side-by-side comparison makes abstract concept visible and concrete
* Students SEE the benefit before Lesson teaches the HOW (skip-counting)
* Follows Playbook PRINCIPLES (low cognitive load, engagement anchors, curiosity gap) while adapting STRUCTURE for the efficiency discovery

**Test:** If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for Lesson? YES—they would miss the motivation for WHY scale of 5 matters (efficiency), making Lesson feel like arbitrary new content rather than a meaningful improvement.

**Key Difference from M2 Warmup:**

* M2: Introduces SCALING concept (1 symbol \= multiple items) via grouping animation
* M3: Introduces EFFICIENCY concept (same data, fewer symbols) via side-by-side comparison
* M3's grouping animation moves to Lesson Section 1 (skip-counting connection is the new learning)

---

**Interaction W.1: Data Collection Game**

* **Purpose:** Generate personalized data. Create investment through Minis context. Provide concrete numbers for scale comparison.
* **Hook (first 15-20 seconds):**
  * **Visual:** Minis characters appear in animated counting scenario.
  * **Guide:** "The Minis are having a busy day\! Help count what they're up to."
* **Engagement Anchor:** Narrative Setup (Minis context), Personalization (their counts)
* **Game Specifications:**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Style | Mario Party-style counting game |
| Categories | 3 (e.g., Red Minis, Blue Minis, Green Minis) |
| Values | Multiples of 5 only: 10, 15, 20, 25, or 30 per category |
| Duration | \~60 seconds |
| Student Interaction | Count items; confirm/input count for each category |

* **Task:** Student participates in counting activity, tracking count for each of 3 categories.
* **On Complete:** Game ends; counts recorded for next interaction.

**Design Note:** All values MUST be multiples of 5 AND large enough to show efficiency difference. Target range: 10-30. Values like 20, 25, 30 show the clearest difference between scales.

---

**Interaction W.2: Scale Comparison Display (THE KEY MOMENT)**

* **Purpose:** Visually demonstrate that the SAME data looks different with different scales. Set up the efficiency discovery.
* **Engagement Anchor:** Curiosity Gap (what's different about these two graphs?)
* **Visual:** Picture Graphs (side-by-side comparison). Same student data shown as 1:2 picture graph AND 1:5 picture graph. Both vertical. Keys visible on both: "Each ⭐ \= 2" and "Each ⭐ \= 5." Data Table not visible. Half-symbols acceptable on 1:2 graph.
* **Major Beats:**
  * LEFT graph builds first
  * **Guide:** "Here's your data with scale of 2\. Each symbol shows 2 items."
  * RIGHT graph builds (noticeably fewer symbols)
  * **Guide:** "And here's the SAME data with scale of 5\. Each symbol shows 5 items."
  * Both graphs now visible side-by-side
  * **Guide:** "Same data. Same totals. But look at the graphs… What do you notice about the two graphs?"
* **Prompt:** "What do you notice about the two graphs?"
* **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice:
  * "The scale-of-5 graph has fewer symbols"
  * "The scale-of-5 graph has more symbols"
  * "They have the same number of symbols"
  * "The graphs show different data"\]
* **Correct Answer:** "The scale-of-5 graph has fewer symbols"
* **On Correct:** "Fewer symbols for the same data. When each symbol shows MORE, you need FEWER of them."

**Design Note:** This is the conceptual payoff. Students SEE the efficiency before Lesson explains the mechanism (skip-counting).

---

**Interaction W.3: Efficiency Discovery**

* **Purpose:** Solidify the efficiency insight. Students quantify the difference.
* **Visual: Picture Graphs (side-by-side comparison).** Same display as W.2. System highlights symbol counts for comparison.
* **Guide:** "Look at \[category name\]. It has 30 items."
* **Guide:** "With scale of 2... that's 15 symbols."
* **Guide:** "With scale of 5... that's only 6 symbols.Which scale is more efficient for showing 20 items?"
* **Prompt:** "Which scale is more efficient for showing 20 items?"
* **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: Scale of 2, Scale of 5\]
* **Correct Answer:** Scale of 5
* **On Correct:** "Scale of 5\. When numbers get bigger, scale of 5 saves space. Fewer symbols, same information."

---

**Interaction W.4: Bridge to Lesson**

* **Purpose:** Create anticipation for Lesson without teaching the mechanism.
* **Visual: Picture Graphs (1:5 graph).** Scale-of-5 graph from W.2 remains. 1:2 graph fades. Data Table not visible.
* **Guide:** "You discovered something important: Scale of 5 uses fewer symbols for the same data."
* **Guide:** "But HOW do you know how many symbols to use? There's a skill that helps..."
* **Guide:** "Do you remember how to count by 5s?"
* **Bridge Statement:** "Let's learn how skip-counting and scale of 5 work together."
* **No student prompt**

## **1.7 LESSON (10-12 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

---

### **Core Purpose**

Extend students' understanding of scaled graphs from scale of 2 to scale of 5\. Connect scale to skip-counting by 5s (a familiar Grade 2 skill). Introduce bar graph CREATION while maintaining familiar scale (students read bar graphs in M1). Students create both picture graphs and bar graphs with scale of 5, recognizing that both formats show the same data.

---

### **Pedagogical Flow**

M3 extends an established concept (scaling) to a new value (5) and introduces a new skill (bar graph creation). The CRA sequence applies:

| CRA Phase | M3 Application |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Concrete** | Grouping Animation transforms items into groups of 5, then into symbols |
| **Relational** | Students notice efficiency (fewer symbols than scale of 2); connect to skip-counting |
| **Abstract** | Formalize "scale of 5"; transition from reading bar graphs to creating them |
| **Application** | Create both picture graphs and bar graphs; solve comparison problems |

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Scale of 5 — Skip-Counting Connection**

**Interaction 1.1: Building on Scale Comparison**

- **Visual: Picture Graphs (side-by-side comparison).** Brief callback — same data as 1:2 and 1:5 picture graphs (both vertical). Key highlighted on 1:5 graph: "Each ⭐ \= 5." Data Table not visible.
- **Guide:** "In Warmup, you saw something important. Same data, but the scale-of-5 graph needed fewer symbols. That's because each symbol represents MORE."

- **Prompt:** "What does each symbol represent when the scale is 5?"

- **Correct Answer:** 5
- **On Correct:** "Each symbol represents 5 items. That's the scale."
- **Remediation (Light):** "Check the key. It says 'Each symbol \= 5.' That's the scale: 5."

**Interaction 1.2: Grouping Animation — Groups of 5 \[WORKED EXAMPLE\]**

- **Visual: Data Tables (Numeric Display).** "Favorite Playground Activities" — Swings: 20, Slides: 15, Monkey Bars: 25, Sandbox: 10\. Vertical.

**Interaction 3.2b: Three-Scale Toggle — Same Data, Your Choice of Scale \[OBSERVATION ONLY\]**

- **Purpose:** Extend M2's binary scale comparison to a three-way pattern. Student toggles between scales 1, 2, and 5 on their own graph data, discovering that "bigger scale \= fewer symbols" is a general principle. Builds metarepresentational competence (English 2010/2012) through direct manipulation — the student causes the transformation, making scale a visible choice rather than an invisible given.
- **Visual: Picture Graphs (side-by-side comparison).** Student's completed 1:5 horizontal "Books Read" graph displayed. Scale toggle control with three options: "Each ⭐ \= 1," "Each ⭐ \= 2," "Each ⭐ \= 5." Defaults to scale of 5\. Data Table visible as stable anchor (Liam=35, Emma=20, Noah=25, Olivia=40). Key updates dynamically. Toggle sequence: 5→1 (Olivia: 8→40 symbols), 1→2 (Olivia: 40→20 symbols; half-symbols appear for Liam=17½, Noah=12½), 2→5 (return to original).
- **Guide:** "You built this graph with scale of 5\. But what would it look like with a different scale? Try it."
- **Prompt:** "Toggle the scale to 1\. What happens?"
- **Student Action:** Click scale toggle from 5 to 1\. Graph animates: symbols multiply (Olivia: 8 symbols → 40 symbols; Liam: 7 → 35; Noah: 5 → 25; Emma: 4 → 20). Key updates to "Each ⭐ \= 1."
- **Guide:** "Olivia went from 8 symbols to 40\! That's a LOT of symbols. Now try scale of 2."
- **Student Action:** Click scale toggle from 1 to 2\. Graph animates: symbols reduce (Olivia: 40 → 20; Liam: 35 → 17½; Noah: 25 → 12½; Emma: 20 → 10). Key updates to "Each ⭐ \= 2."
- **Guide:** "Fewer symbols — but still more than scale of 5\. Toggle back to 5."
- **Student Action:** Click scale toggle from 2 to 5\. Graph animates back to original state (Olivia: 20 → 8; Liam: 17½ → 7; etc.). Key updates to "Each ⭐ \= 5."
- **Guide:** "Same 40 books for Olivia every time. Scale of 1: 40 symbols. Scale of 2: 20 symbols. Scale of 5: just 8."
- **Prompt:** "Which scale uses the fewest symbols?"
- **Student Action:** \[Multiple choice: Scale of 1, Scale of 2, Scale of 5\]
- **Correct Answer:** Scale of 5
- **On Correct:** "Scale of 5\. Bigger scale, fewer symbols — same data."
- **Remediation (Light):** "Toggle through all three again and count Olivia's symbols each time. 40, then 20, then 8\. Which is fewest?"
- **Guide:** "All three showed the same information. The scale just changes how many symbols you need. Now let's use YOUR graph to answer some questions."
- **Duration:** 45–55 seconds
- **No student graph creation** (toggle is observation/exploration only)

**Design Notes:**

- Student toggles through all three scales in sequence (5→1→2→5). The full cycle matters — they see the extremes (scale 1 is overwhelming) and the middle (scale 2 is better but still more than 5), then return home to their own graph. This progression makes scale of 5's efficiency feel earned, not asserted.
- Half-symbols appear naturally at scale of 2 for odd values (Liam=35 → 17½ symbols, Noah=25 → 12½ symbols). Guide does NOT call attention to this — it's a background observation. Students already learned half-symbols in M2. If they notice, great. If not, it's not the point of this interaction.
- Animation between states should be smooth (\~0.5s transition) — fast enough to feel responsive, slow enough to see symbols appearing/disappearing.
- The toggle becomes inactive after the MC question is answered. Interactions 3.2a onward use ONLY the student's 1:5 graph. Toggle is NOT available during problem-solving.
- The data table stays visible throughout as the stable reference point. The graph transforms; the data doesn't. This reinforces the core insight: scale changes representation, not information.
- Guide narrates Olivia specifically because she has the largest value (40), making symbol count differences most dramatic across all three scales (40 → 20 → 8).
- Compared to M2's toggle (two states, back and forth): M3 adds a third state and a guided sequence through all three. This is appropriate because M3's conceptual point is the PATTERN across scales, not just a binary comparison. The guided sequence (5→1→2→5) prevents aimless clicking while still giving the student agency.
- Total time cost: \~50 seconds. Slightly longer than M2's 3.2b (\~35s) because three states require more transitions, but justified by the richer insight.

### **Misconception Prevention**

**\#1 Counting Symbols Instead of Scaling:**

- Interaction 1.3 explicitly tests scale understanding with "4" as distractor
- Interaction 2.2 includes "3" as distractor (symbol count instead of value)
- Every reading task requires calculating actual value, not symbol count
- Skip-counting modeled aloud: "5, 10, 15, 20"
- Guide consistently narrates: "X symbols, that's \[total\]"

**\#6 "More Than" Means Add:**

- Interaction 2.6 includes "45" distractor (sum instead of difference)
- Frame as callback: "You already know how to compare"
- Light remediation available: "Compare means find the difference"

**\#2 Scale Means Addition:**

- Interaction 1.3 includes "9" distractor (4+5)
- "Groups of 5" language throughout
- Skip-counting as bridge (not addition)

# **END OF MODULE 3 STARTER PACK**

---

**Key Design Decisions Summary:**

1. **Front-loaded scale comparison:** Warmup shows 1:2 vs 1:5 side-by-side (efficiency discovery BEFORE creation)
2. **Skip-counting as central strategy:** Every calculation uses "count by 5s" (Grade 2 foundation)
3. **Bar graph FORMAT introduction:** Same scale (5), new creation skill—separates format change from scale change
4. **Multiples-only:** All values 10-40 in multiples of 5—no half-symbols (reduces cognitive load and maintains precision)
5. **Orientation progression:** Vertical (Sections 1-2) → Horizontal (Section 3\) with explicit acknowledgment
6. **Lean Synthesis:** 3 interactions appropriate for thinner conceptual depth vs M2
7. **M1 callback for bar graphs:** "You've read bar graphs before"—creation is new, format is familiar
