# MODULE 7: Multiplying by Multiples of 10

# BACKBONE

---

## 1.0 THE ONE THING

M7 is a PLACE VALUE module, not a computation tricks module. Students must understand WHY 4 × 30 = 120 — not just compute it. The core insight: when you multiply by a multiple of 10, you're making groups of tens. "4 groups of 3 tens = 12 tens = 120." The ones place is 0 because there are no ones — only tens.

**CRA Stage:** Concrete (Base-10 Blocks — physically grouping ten-rods) → Representational (Place Value Chart digit positions + unit language) → Abstract (decomposition chains in Equation Builder). The concept is narrow (multiplying by 10s) but the reasoning is deep (place value). Full CRA within one module is appropriate.

**Critical Misconception:** U4.5 — "Add a zero" as a computation shortcut without place value understanding. Works for whole numbers but creates catastrophic errors with decimals (Grade 5+). Students are "nearly guaranteed" to encounter this shortcut from adults outside our app. The module's job: make place value reasoning SO clear that students choose it over the shortcut.

**Success Indicator:** Student can produce the decomposition chain (4 × 30 = 4 × 3 × 10 = 12 × 10 = 120), state unit language ("4 groups of 3 tens = 12 tens"), AND explain why the ones digit is 0 ("because we're multiplying tens — tens always have 0 in the ones place"). All three. Computation alone is insufficient.

**Biggest Risk:** Teaching "add a zero" avoidance as a rule rather than through understanding. If the module feels like "don't do X, do Y instead," students may follow the procedure without gaining insight. The blocks and unit language must make tens VISIBLE as units — the understanding must come from seeing, not from being told.

**Cross-Unit Bridge:** Students discovered in Unit 1 M9 that "multiples of 10 always end in 0" — a true pattern observation. M7 transforms that pattern observation INTO place value understanding: the pattern is true BECAUSE tens always have 0 in the ones place.

**Strategy Shift from M6:** M6 used the distributive property (unnamed — "breaking apart") with area models. M7 uses associative reasoning (unnamed — "grouping factors") with Base-10 Blocks. The tool switch (Grid Rectangles → Base-10 Blocks) signals the strategy switch. Both are multiplication strategies; they serve different number types.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

**[REQUIRED]**

### 1.4.1 U4.5: Incorrect Place Value in Multiplication by Multiples of 10 (PRIMARY)

**Description:** Students who "add a zero" without understanding will compute correctly for whole numbers but cannot explain WHY. They multiply the non-zero digits correctly but fail to account for place value, or apply the shortcut without understanding. This creates catastrophic errors with decimals in later grades (3 × 0.4 ≠ 0.12 with "added zero"). Weak connection to associative property reasoning. Alanazi (2024) documented Grade 5 students still making these errors when the shortcut was taught without conceptual grounding.

**Observable Behavior:**
* Student computes 4 × 30 = 12 (multiplied digits, dropped the zero)
* Student can produce 120 but says "you just add a zero" — cannot explain WHY
* When asked "what does the 3 in 30 represent?" cannot connect to "3 tens"
* May produce correct answers via rote but fail on unfamiliar formats
* Errors increase when place value gets more complex (e.g., 4 × 300)

**Prevention Strategy in This Module:**
* Base-10 Blocks Grouping Mode makes tens VISIBLE as physical units — "4 groups of 3 tens" is concrete, not abstract
* Unit language bridges concrete to symbolic: "4 groups of 3 tens = 12 tens = 120"
* Place value chart shows digit positions: 1 hundred, 2 tens, 0 ones — the structure IS the explanation
* Decomposition chain captures the reasoning symbolically: 4 × 30 = 4 × 3 × 10 = 12 × 10 = 120
* Proactive "add a zero" redirect in Late: acknowledge the pattern, redirect to place value reasoning. Per SME (Andrea): Do NOT explain why it fails (involves math beyond Grade 3). DO acknowledge briefly, redirect, move on.
* Cross-unit bridge: Unit 1 M9 pattern ("multiples of 10 end in 0") is the springboard INTO place value understanding — the pattern is true BECAUSE tens have 0 in the ones place

**Where U4.5 Surfaces in This Module:**
* Lesson S1: Students who arrive with "add a zero" may produce correct answers without understanding — watch for inability to state unit language
* Lesson S3 (Late): Proactive redirect — Guide addresses the pattern directly
* EC.3: Tests whether student understands WHY (not just WHAT) — visual-selection format targeting the place value principle

**Related Mathematical Practices:** MP5 (Use appropriate tools — blocks make reasoning visible), MP7 (Look for structure — place value IS the structure)

## 1.6 WARMUP (2–3 minutes)

### Core Purpose

Activate the Unit 1 M9 pattern observation — "multiples of 10 always end in 0" — and transform it into a driving question: WHY? Students recall a true pattern they already know, extend it to larger multiples of 10, and arrive at the Lesson entry point: the pattern is always true — what makes it true? The Warmup does NOT explain place value, introduce any vocabulary, or teach the ×10 strategy. It creates the question that the Lesson answers.

**M6 Bridge Acknowledgment:** M6's Identity Closure told students: "Next time, you'll look at what happens when one factor is a multiple of 10... a different kind of strategy... place value instead of breaking apart." Students arrive expecting a place value strategy. The Warmup honors this by framing the ×10 pattern as something they've already noticed, positioning the Lesson as the explanation.

Notice & Wonder elements (the "why?" question) serve the activation, not the other way around. WODB would require four items with multiple valid "doesn't belong" reasons — not a natural fit for a pattern-confirmation task. The extension to larger multiples (W.2) adds a judgment dimension: "Does the pattern hold for these too?" This creates genuine curiosity without teaching.

### Interaction W.1: The ×10 Pattern [ACTIVATION]

* **Purpose:** Recall the U1 M9 pattern — when you multiply by 10, the product always ends in 0. Hook in first 15 seconds via personalization callback to student's own Unit 1 discovery.
* **Visual:** Display — column of ×10 facts: 1 × 10 = 10, 2 × 10 = 20, 3 × 10 = 30, 4 × 10 = 40, 5 × 10 = 50. Products highlighted.
* **Guide:** "Back in Unit 1, you discovered something about multiplying by 10. Look at these products: 10, 20, 30, 40, 50. What do they all have in common? A: They all end in 0. B: They are all even. C: They are all greater than 10. D: They go up by 5."
* **Prompt:** "What is true about all these products? A: They all end in 0. B: They are all even. C: They are all greater than 10. D: They go up by 5."
* **Student Action:** Select (single)
  * **Options:**
  A. They all end in 0
  B. They are all even
  C. They are all greater than 10
  D. They go up by 5
* **Correct Answer:** A (They all end in 0)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - A (They all end in 0): Correct — the core U1 M9 pattern observation
  - B (They are all even): True but not the salient pattern — doesn't capture the ×10 insight
  - C (They are all greater than 10): True for most but 10 itself is not "greater than 10" — not the defining feature
  - D (They go up by 5): False — they go up by 10
* **On Correct:** "They all end in 0. You spotted that in Unit 1."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

### Core Purpose + Pedagogical Flow

**Purpose:** Teach WHY multiplying by multiples of 10 always produces a product ending in 0 — through place value reasoning, not computation shortcuts. Students progress from physically seeing groups of tens (Base-10 Blocks) to naming them with unit language ("4 groups of 3 tens = 12 tens = 120") to capturing the reasoning symbolically in decomposition chains (4 × 30 = 4 × 3 × 10 = 12 × 10 = 120).

**Pedagogical Flow:**

| CRA Phase | Module Implementation | Key Pedagogical Move |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Concrete (S1)** | Base-10 Blocks Grouping Mode + Place Value Chart alongside | Guide demonstrates unit language with running count; student produces unit language with prompting. Associative grouping moment grounds the "multiply in any order" concept informally. |
| **Representational → Abstract (S2)** | Place Value Chart primary + Equation Builder chain display | Guide introduces 3-step decomposition chain; student builds chains with decreasing support. "Place value" shifts from general concept to specific strategy anchor. |
| **Abstract (S3)** | Equation Builder only | Student constructs chains independently across full factor range. Proactive "add a zero" redirect after 2 successful chains. |

### Misconception Prevention

**U4.5 (PRIMARY) — "Add a zero" without place value understanding:**

Prevention is embedded across the full CRA progression:

* **S1 (Concrete):** Base-10 Blocks Grouping Mode makes tens VISIBLE as physical units. Unit language ("4 groups of 3 tens = 12 tens = 120") names what students see. The blocks contain NO ones — the absence of ones IS the pedagogical point. Students who arrive with "add a zero" may produce correct answers but will struggle to state unit language — the unit language requirement is the diagnostic.
* **S2 (Representational/Abstract):** Decomposition chains (6 × 40 = 6 × 4 × 10 = 24 × 10 = 240) capture the place value reasoning symbolically. The chain makes associative reasoning visible without naming the property (D5). The middle step (6 × 4 × 10 → 24 × 10) IS the understanding that "add a zero" skips.
* **S3 (Abstract):** Proactive redirect (Interaction 3.3) — Guide acknowledges the shortcut, redirects to place value reasoning. Per SME: don't explain why it fails, don't dwell. Students evaluate the shortcut against their own demonstrated understanding.
* **MC Distractor Design:** In S1 select interactions, distractor C ("6 tens = 6" / "20 tens = 20") diagnoses the critical failure: can the student convert between unit language and standard form? This is the exact gap that "add a zero" masks.

---

### Incomplete Script Flags (§1.7.4)

🚩 **If the grouping moment (1.1) is skipped,** students have no informal grounding for why 4 × 30 = 4 × 3 × 10. The decomposition chain in S2 will feel like an arbitrary procedure rather than a natural extension of "you can group factors differently."

🚩 **If unit language is not stated in EVERY interaction where a product is computed,** students may learn to build chains procedurally without understanding that 12 × 10 means "12 tens." Unit language is the non-negotiable reasoning anchor — it's in On Correct for every interaction.

🚩 **If the "add a zero" redirect (3.3) is omitted,** students who encounter the shortcut from adults outside the app have no place value counter-frame. The redirect is brief but essential.

🚩 **If Base-10 Blocks are removed from S1,** the concrete anchor for "groups of tens" is lost. Students need to SEE 4 groups of 3 ten-rods before they can understand why "4 × 30 = 4 × 3 × 10."

### Success Criteria (§1.7.5)

✅ Student can state unit language for a ×10 problem: "4 groups of 3 tens = 12 tens = 120" (tested in S1 select interactions and On Correct throughout).

✅ Student can build a 3-step decomposition chain in Equation Builder: a × b0 = a × b × 10 = (a×b) × 10 = product (tested in S2 and S3 chain-building interactions).

✅ Student can explain WHY a ×10 product ends in 0 — connecting to place value ("no ones — only tens") or unit language ("12 tens has 0 in the ones place") (tested in On Correct language; formally assessed in EC.3).

✅ Student can construct chains independently across the full factor range (up to 9 × 90) without Guide scaffolding (tested in S3 interactions 3.1, 3.2, 3.4).
