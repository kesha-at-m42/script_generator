TVP: MODULE 6 — Multiplication Strategies — Area Models
========================================================
OUR Lessons: 10, 11
Standards: 3.OA.B.5 (properties of operations), 3.MD.C.7c (distributive property via area)

Learning Goal:
  Use area diagrams to explore strategies based on properties of multiplication.
  Apply the distributive property (unnamed) to find products within 100. Recognize
  that breaking apart a factor produces partial products that sum to the original product.

Key Teaching Points:
  1. Students already know area models from Unit 2 — Grid Rectangles, decomposition,
     dimension labels. What's NEW in Unit 4: connecting the decomposition to
     multiplication EXPRESSIONS.
  2. "Breaking apart" a factor into friendlier numbers creates two simpler multiplication
     problems whose products sum to the original. E.g., 7×6 split at 5 →
     (5×6) + (2×6) = 30 + 12 = 42.
  3. THE BIG INSIGHT: The area model makes the distributive property VISIBLE. Both
     sub-rectangles on screen simultaneously — prevents "forgetting to multiply both
     parts" (U4.6) by design.
  4. Strategic choice matters: different students may prefer different decomposition
     points. Per SME: "choice is extremely important."
  5. Language: "breaking apart," "decompose," "partial products" (informal: "the

  CONNECT (Transfer) — linking Unit 2 area models to Unit 4 multiplication strategy
  CREATE (Procedural) — decomposing rectangles and writing matching expressions
  COMPARE (Conceptual) — discovering different decompositions produce same product
  APPLY (Transfer) — choosing decomposition strategy based on personal fact fluency

Misconceptions Targeted:
  U4.6: Distributive property applied incorrectly (PRIMARY)

  Guide draws partition line: 4×6 becomes 4×4 + 4×2. "4 times 4 is 16, plus 4 times 2
  is 8. 16 + 8 = 24. Same area — but we turned one problem into two easier ones."
  Purpose: Reactivate Unit 2 area model knowledge, introduce strategic framing.

  Early (Guided decomposition on gridded area models):
    Grid Rectangles (full grid, partition line) + Equation Builder (expression mode)

      System shows guided decomposition. Both sub-rectangles color-coded.
      Equation Builder displays: (5 × 10) + (5 × 3) = 65.

      parentheses, are like a box around each part. THIS part [highlight left
      sub-rectangle] is 5 × 10. THAT part [highlight right sub-rectangle] is 5 × 3.
      We figure out each part, then add them together."

    expression in Equation Builder. Grid visible throughout for verification.

  Mid (Student-chosen decomposition on gridded models):

      "To make 7 × 8 easier, you break the 8 into 5 and 3: 7×5 + 7×3. Your friend
      breaks the 8 into 4 and 4: 7×4 + 7×4. And another friend breaks the 7 instead
      — into 5 and 2: 5×8 + 2×8." Show three different decompositions side by side,

      "Different strategies, same answer. Which feels easier to YOU?"

    Grid Rectangles (ungridded — outline + dimension labels only, no grid squares)

    3–4 problems. Decompositions that break at 10 produce sub-problems with known facts.
    SME note: Tighter than original "open-ended by end of lesson." Rationale: students
    simultaneously lose counting fallback AND work with larger factors — constraining

## Section Plan

**Warmup (~2-3 min):** Unit 2 Area Model Callback
- Type: ACTIVATION — reactivating prior knowledge from Unit 2 (Grid Rectangles, area, decomposition)
- Bridge from M5: M5 closure promised "area models to discover strategies for multiplying larger numbers"
- **⚠️ NON-NEGOTIABLE REFRAMING (PE-03):** Warmup MUST explicitly reframe Grid Rectangles from "area measurement tool" (Unit 2 context) to "multiplication strategy tool" (M6 context). Students last used Grid Rectangles for measuring area — they need a clear signal that the SAME tool now serves a different purpose. Guide language must include something like: "You measured area with these grids. Now we're using the SAME rectangles as a trick to make hard multiplication easier." Without this reframing, students may not connect Unit 2's tool to M6's strategy.
- Key beat: Guide demonstrates partitioning a familiar gridded rectangle into two parts, computes partial products, adds. "Same area — but we turned one problem into two easier ones."
- Ends with bridge: "What if we use this to make HARDER multiplication easier?"
- 2-3 interactions: familiar area model display → guided partition demonstration → anticipation bridge

**Lesson Section 1 (Early — ~3-4 min): Guided Decomposition on Gridded Area Models**
- CRA: Representational (gridded — can count to verify)
- Pedagogical moves: Expression-to-diagram connection, parentheses introduction, guided decomposition at benchmark numbers
- **Scaffolding Fade Plan (PE-04):** Interactions 1.1–1.2 = full scaffold (Guide models decomposition + expression writing with tagged think-aloud). Interaction 1.3+ = independent check (student writes expression from shown partition, grid still visible for verification). This ensures 2 full scaffolded examples before independent work per Grade 3 baseline.
- Think-aloud: Guide models decomposition reasoning with tagged elements — first think-aloud must model the cognitive move of identifying sub-rectangles BEFORE unpacking notation (observation-to-plan sequence, not just the answer)
- Key: Equation Builder appears alongside Grid Rectangles from the start (D4)
- Vocabulary: "breaking apart," "decompose," parentheses introduced via visual-to-notation unpacking

**Lesson Section 2 (Mid — ~3-4 min): Student-Chosen Decomposition**
- CRA: Relational (comparing decompositions)
- Pedagogical moves: Student choice, multiple valid decompositions, "same total" discovery
- Key beat: Three different decompositions of same rectangle → same product
- **⚠️ SEQUENTIAL REVEAL REQUIRED (PE-02):** The "three decompositions, same total" discovery is the relational peak of the module. Three decompositions shown sequentially with comparison prompts after each reveal, NOT simultaneously. Grade 3 working memory (5–7 items max) cannot hold three decompositions at once for meaningful comparison. Sequence: (1) student's own → (2) "Here's a different way" (alternative partition of same factor) → (3) "And another way — this time breaking the OTHER factor." Brief comparison prompt after each reveal. This preserves the "different paths, same answer" insight without overloading. (Aligns with DN-4.)
- Vocabulary: "partial products" / "the parts" formalized after experience
- Independence building: student places partition, writes expression

**Lesson Section 3 (Late — ~3-4 min): Ungridded/Open Area Models**
- CRA: Abstract (no counting fallback)
- Pedagogical moves: Progressive formalization — remove grid, keep dimensions
- Partition constraint: benchmark numbers (2, 5, or 10) per AF3 — fully open in Practice
- 3-4 problems with increasing independence

**Exit Check (~2-3 min): 3 problems**
- EC.1: Gridded → expression (IDENTIFY — same toy/mode as Early)

## Task 2 Design Notes (from Gate 1 Review)

**DN-1 (Voice — P-1/R9):** The Mid "three decompositions" beat must NOT use "your friend" framing. Use "another student might do it this way" or sequential reveal: "Here's one way... Here's a different way... And here's yet another way — this time breaking the OTHER factor." Sequential reveal manages cognitive load better than simultaneous display in 1:1 digital.

**DN-2 (Warmup Values — P-3/R11):** TVP Warmup example 4×6 partitioned at 4 is atypical — benchmark strategy message is 2, 5, or 10. Consider: (a) 3×12 → 3×10 + 3×2 (benchmark 10, natural), or (b) 4×6 partitioned at 5 → 4×5 + 4×1 (benchmark 5). Either foreshadows the benchmark strategy cleanly.

**DN-3 (Terminology — P-4/R10):** "Expression" = left side without equals (e.g., (5 × 10) + (5 × 3)). "Equation" = full statement with equals (e.g., (5 × 10) + (5 × 3) = 65). Guide dialogue should use "partial-product expression" when referring to the decomposition notation, and "equation" when the student completes it with the equals sign and total. Be consistent.

**DN-4 (Mid Reveal Sequence — P-1):** Three decompositions of the same rectangle should be revealed sequentially in the 1:1 app, not simultaneously. Sequence: (1) student's own decomposition → (2) "Here's a different way" (alternative partition of same factor) → (3) "And another way — this time breaking the OTHER factor." Brief comparison prompt after each reveal. This maintains the "same total" discovery while managing working memory for Grade 3.

**DN-5 (EC.3 — CA-1/R1):** EC.3 uses Version B from TVP: student produces an ALTERNATIVE decomposition of a rectangle (one decomposition shown, student generates a different one). This is CREATE cognitive type — the highest-demand EC item. Version A (verify two decompositions match) is IDENTIFY and would be a weaker assessment. Version B confirmed by author.

**DN-6 (Parentheses Convention — G1-04):** Parentheses are a notation convention, NOT a `[vocab]`-tagged term. Task 2 Guide dialogue must introduce them via visual unpacking ("These curved lines, called parentheses, are like a box around each part"), not as a formal vocabulary definition. Students learn the convention by seeing it used, not by defining it. Script writers: no `[vocab]` tags on "parentheses" — it appears untagged in all contexts.

**DN-7 (Distractor Design for U4.6 — PE-05):** MC distractors for expression-writing items (EC.1, any Lesson MC) should diagnostically target U4.6. Primary distractor: partial-product expression missing the common factor in one term (e.g., "5 × 10 + 3" instead of "5 × 10 + 5 × 3" for a 5 × 13 rectangle). This directly reveals which students have "forgotten to multiply both parts." Secondary distractors: incorrect totals from arithmetic errors, or decomposition of the other factor (valid alternative strategy, not an error — use as a "technically correct but not what was shown" option).
