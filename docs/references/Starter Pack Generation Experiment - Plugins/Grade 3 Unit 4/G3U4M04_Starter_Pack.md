## 1.0 THE ONE THING

**[REQUIRED]**

A fact family proves that multiplication and division are the same relationship described four ways — one set of three numbers (e.g., 4, 7, 28) generates two multiplication equations AND two division equations — and knowing ANY one fact in the family unlocks the other three, making multiplication a deliberate STRATEGY for solving division.

**CRA Stage:** Representational → early Abstract. Students transition from M3's array-grounded insight (division IS finding a missing factor) to systematic fact family production. Early activities ground construction in word problems and arrays. Mid removes the construction step (arrays given, not built). Late removes the visual entirely — students produce fact families from equations alone. The array is internalized as a mental model, not a required scaffold.

**Critical Misconception:** U4.3 — Cannot connect multiplication and division as inverse operations. M3 established the conceptual insight; M4 tests whether students can APPLY it systematically. Students who produce fact families by shuffling numbers without understanding will be caught by the construction requirement and sense-making checks. A2 (equals sign as "do something") compounds the risk — fact families require writing equations in varied formats, including product-first.

**Biggest Risk:** Students learn to generate four equations from three numbers as a procedural pattern-matching exercise — swapping number positions mechanically — without understanding WHY the four facts are related. The array visual anchor, the word-problem grounding in Early, and the sense-making checks (student explains HOW the array shows the division equation) are the safeguards. If fact families become a number-shuffling trick, the "think-multiplication" strategy in Late won't transfer — students will have the procedure without the understanding.

**Success Indicator:** Given a multiplication equation (e.g., 6 × 7 = 42), the student produces all four fact family members, uses a known multiplication fact to solve a division problem (think-multiplication — primary strategy outcome: "I don't know 42 ÷ 6, but I know 6 × 7 = 42, so it's 7"), AND can verify a division answer using multiplication (check-by-inverse — core teaching beat per SME, secondary to think-multiplication as a fluency strategy: "Does 42 ÷ 6 = 7? Check: 7 × 6 = 42 ✓"). Per SME: the test is whether the inverse relationship has become a STRATEGY, not just an observation. Note: Module Mapping labels check-by-inverse as "EXTENSION" — this refers to its extension from the M3 discussion (placed in M4 per SME because students now actively find unknowns), not an optional extension within M4. It appears in the TVP's Late activities and Exit Check as a standard teaching beat.

## 1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES

**[REQUIRED]**

### ✅ Must Teach

* Fact family as a formal concept: a set of four related equations (2×, 2÷) using the same three numbers, named and systematized
* Complete fact family production: given an array, word problem, or single equation, student generates all four family members
* Think-multiplication as a named strategy: "I don't know 35 ÷ 7, but I know 5 × 7 = 35, so 35 ÷ 7 = 5"
* Check-by-inverse: use multiplication to verify division answers ("Does 42 ÷ 6 = 7? Check: 7 × 6 = 42 ✓")
* Word problem → array construction → dual equation production (both × and ÷ for the same situation)
* □ as unknown symbol (introduced alongside existing ?) per D7 revised
* Letter unknowns (n, p, etc.) as unknown symbol (introduced alongside ? and □) per D7 revised — all three interchangeable from M4 onward
* Vocabulary retrieval: students actively identify dividend, divisor, and quotient in both × and ÷ contexts (M3 was exposure; M4 is the retrieval loop per SME)
* "Inverse" introduced informally as "opposite operation" — first time the relationship is named, though M3 showed it
* "Related facts" as synonym for fact family members
* Nonstandard equation formats at ≥30% per D8: 24 = 4 × 6, 7 = 42 ÷ 6, n × 5 = 20
* Both partitive and quotitive word problems in Early activities (per D2: 50-50 target)
* Bidirectional reasoning: situation → array + equations AND equation → matching array
* Increasing fact complexity across full single-digit range per SME: NOT gated by fluency (factors 2–6, products ≤30 in Early → factors 2–9, products ≤100 in Mid → factors 2–10, products ≤100 in Late)
* Reinforcement of all M1-M3 and Unit 1 vocabulary in context

### ❌ Must Not Include

* Commutativity as a named property or discovery activity (M5 — commutativity is implicitly present in fact families but NOT the focus; if a student asks "why can't I switch the ÷ numbers?", address briefly, don't teach formally)
* Multiplication table exploration or patterns (M5)
* Problems with remainders — all M4 divisions are exact
* Division of numbers requiring multi-digit computation (products within 100, factors 2–10)
* Self-assessment of fact fluency or known/unknown fact sorting (removed per SME; fluency gating belongs at M11, D10)
* "Add a zero" or place value multiplication strategies (M7+)
* True/false number sentence activities (D8 — eliminated)
* Area models or decomposition strategies (M6+)
* Think-multiplication as a fully developed, assessed strategy (M4 NAMES it; M12 is where it becomes the primary division method for larger numbers)
* Partial quotients or any multi-step division strategies (M12)
* Two-step word problems (M10+)
* Backward step to Equal Groups as a primary tool — remediation stays within Arrays per SME

⚠️ **CONSTRUCTION OVER PRODUCTION:** M4's purpose is not just generating four equations — it's proving that students understand WHY the four facts are related through construction. Every Early activity requires building the array from a word problem before producing equations. If construction drops out and students skip straight to equation shuffling, fact families become procedural. The array is the sense-making anchor.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

**[REQUIRED]**

### 1.4.1 #U4.3: Cannot Connect Multiplication and Division (PRIMARY — CRITICAL, CONTINUED)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student knows 7 × 8 = 56 but cannot quickly derive 56 ÷ 8 = 7. Uses counting or repeated subtraction for division even when the related multiplication fact is known. When asked "how does 3 × 5 = 15 help you solve 15 ÷ 3?", cannot explain the connection. May produce fact families procedurally (shuffling numbers) without understanding why four facts exist from one arrangement.

**Why It Happens:** Robinson & LeFevre (2012) found conceptual understanding of multiplicative inversion is significantly harder than additive inversion. Robinson et al. (2016) found repeated exposure alone does not reliably build this understanding — explicit instruction is required. M3 provided the initial conceptual foundation; M4 tests whether students can apply it systematically across the full fact range.

**Visual Cue:** One array with all four fact family equations displayed. Highlighting shows row-reading (×) vs. column-counting (÷) from the same image. The array IS the proof that four equations share the same structure.

**Prevention Strategy:** (1) Construction requirement — students BUILD the array from word problems before producing equations, preventing pure number-shuffling. (2) Sense-making checks — at least two interactions where students explain HOW the array shows the division equation, not just produce it. (3) Increasing complexity — as facts get harder (7×8, 8×9), students who rely on pattern-matching without understanding will falter; the array provides visual support. (4) Bidirectional reasoning — given equation → select/build matching array prevents single-direction procedural learning.

### 1.4.2 #A2: Equals Sign as "Do Something" (SECONDARY — CONTINUED)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student rejects 24 = 4 × 6 ("the answer should be on the right"). Always puts the product on the right when writing fact families. Struggles with □ × 5 = 40 format but can solve 5 × □ = 40. May reject n = 42 ÷ 6 format.

**Why It Happens:** Behr et al. (1980) and McNeil & Alibali (2005). Fact family writing is especially revealing — students must write equations where the product appears on both sides of =. Nonstandard formats are unavoidable in complete fact families.

**Visual Cue:** Fact family displayed with all four equations in varied formats: 4 × 7 = 28, 7 × 4 = 28, 28 ÷ 4 = 7, 28 ÷ 7 = 4. Also: 28 = 4 × 7 shown alongside standard format.

**Prevention Strategy:** Per D8 — ≥30% nonstandard formats throughout. Fact families naturally produce varied formats. Guide uses "is the same as" language when presenting nonstandard forms. Equation Builder supports both standard and product-first entry.

### 1.4.3 #A4: Associative Interference in Fact Recall (MONITOR)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student answers 6 × 8 = 42 (confusing with 6 × 7) or 7 × 8 = 54 (confusing with 9 × 6). Errors cluster around facts with shared operands or nearby products. May self-correct after a pause.

**Why It Happens:** Dotan et al. (2022) found 50–90% of multiplication errors are caused by interference between similar facts. The 6, 7, 8, and 9 tables dominate the hardest facts because they share operands and have nearby products. As M4 increases fact complexity through Mid and Late activities, these interference errors become more likely.

**Visual Cue:** Array remains available as verification tool — students can count rows/columns to confirm when uncertain.

**Prevention Strategy:** (1) Familiar facts for unknown-factor problems (cognitive demand on the STRATEGY, not recall). (2) Harder facts appear where all numbers are given (produce-the-family tasks). (3) Array available as remediation scaffold in Late to prevent recall errors from blocking relationship work. (4) Avoid pairing confusable facts in adjacent problems (don't put 6 × 7 and 6 × 8 in the same interaction set). *Note: Detail here reflects problem-design guidance for SP scripting, not student-facing instructional emphasis proportional to PRIMARY. A4 remains MONITOR status — these constraints guide the author's value selection, not the lesson's teaching arc.*

## 1.6 WARMUP

**[REQUIRED]**

### Core Purpose

**Purpose:** Reactivate M3's four-equation pattern from a single array and formally name it as a "fact family," framing M4 as the module where students BUILD fact families themselves and use them as a division STRATEGY.

**Key Function:** The Warmup retrieves the four-equation pattern students discovered in M3 Synthesis (where they saw 4×9=36 generate four related equations and heard "fact family" for the first time). By showing the familiar 3×5=15 array — M3's opening dual-read value — with all four equations, the Warmup creates a bookend: the same array they first dual-read now appears with its full family named. This positions the Lesson to shift from recognition ("I see the pattern") to construction ("I can build this myself").

**Why this serves the concept:**

* Fulfills the M3→M4 bridge promise ("Next time, you'll build fact families on your own and use your multiply knowledge as a division STRATEGY")
* Formally names "fact family" — the organizing concept for the module — using a value students already know, so the naming attaches to existing understanding rather than competing with new content
* Creates the motivation gap: "You can recognize a fact family — but can you BUILD one from a word problem?"

**Test:** If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson? YES — students would enter S1 without the "fact family" label, without reactivation of the four-equation pattern, and without the frame that today's work is CONSTRUCTION, not just recognition. The Lesson's worked example assumes students recognize the pattern they're about to build.

| Parameter | Value |

> **Warmup Type Rationale:** Activation (M3 Callback) is the right type because M4 directly extends M3's discovery. The warmup's job is retrieval (reactivate the four-equation pattern) + naming (formally label it "fact family") + framing (shift from recognition to construction). WODB or Notice & Wonder would work for a module introducing a genuinely new concept, but M4 builds directly on M3's closing moment. The callback creates continuity.

> **Design Note — Vocabulary in Warmup:** The Warmup Playbook prohibits introducing new vocabulary. "Fact family" is treated as an exception because it was previewed in M3 Synthesis S.3 (On Correct: "Mathematicians call this set of equations a [vocab]fact family[/vocab]"). Students have already heard the term attached to the pattern they discovered. M4's Warmup formalizes/retrieves a familiar label, not introduces a new concept. This is closer to "using terms students already know from prior modules" (permitted) than "formal vocabulary introduction" (forbidden). If this feels like a boundary violation, the naming could move to the Purpose Frame — but that delays the organizing label by 2 minutes, during which the Guide would refer to "this group of four equations" repeatedly.

### Interaction W.1: Four Equations, One Family [ACTIVATION]

**Purpose:** Retrieve M3's four-equation pattern, formally name "fact family," establish that one set of three numbers generates the whole family.

**Visual: Arrays (dot array, display).** Horizontal. 3 rows × 5 columns = 15 dots. Completed display. Equation display alongside: 3 × 5 = 15, 5 × 3 = 15, 15 ÷ 3 = 5, 15 ÷ 5 = 3. All four equations visible simultaneously. Three numbers (3, 5, 15) color-highlighted across all equations.

**Guide:** "Look what's back — the array you read four different ways last time. Three rows of five. And here are the four equations that go with it: 3 × 5 = 15, 5 × 3 = 15, 15 ÷ 3 = 5, and 15 ÷ 5 = 3. See how every equation uses the same three numbers — 3, 5, and 15? Mathematicians call this a [vocab]fact family[/vocab]. Which of these is a [vocab]fact family[/vocab] — a set of equations that ALL use the same three numbers?"

**Prompt:** "Which set is a fact family — all equations use the same three numbers?"

**Student Action:** Select (single)

**Options:**
A. 3 × 5 = 15, 5 × 3 = 15, 15 ÷ 3 = 5, 15 ÷ 5 = 3
B. 3 × 5 = 15, 5 × 3 = 15, 12 ÷ 3 = 4, 15 ÷ 5 = 3
C. 3 × 5 = 15, 3 + 5 = 8, 15 ÷ 3 = 5, 15 − 5 = 10
D. 3 × 5 = 15, 5 × 3 = 15, 15 ÷ 3 = 5, 20 ÷ 5 = 4

**Correct Answer:** A

**Answer Rationale:**

* A (correct): All four equations use exactly 3, 5, and 15. Two ×, two ÷ — a complete [vocab]fact family[/vocab].
* B: 12 ÷ 3 = 4 breaks the family — uses 12 and 4 instead of 15 and 5. Tests whether student checks ALL equations.
* C: Addition and subtraction equations use different results (8 and 10). Tests whether student understands fact families are × and ÷ only.
* D: 20 ÷ 5 = 4 breaks the family — uses 20 and 4 instead of 15 and 3. Tests careful reading.

**On Correct:** "All four equations use 3, 5, and 15. Two multiply, two divide — one [vocab]fact family[/vocab]."

**Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Engagement Anchors:** (1) Personalization — "the array you read four different ways last time" (callback to M3 experience). (2) Narrative Setup — naming the pattern creates a "leveling up" feeling.

> **Design Note — Bridge Fragility (During-Task-2 item #9):** If students don't recognize the 3×5 pattern from M3, the Guide's opening narration establishes the context regardless: "Three rows of five... four equations... same three numbers." The MC structure with one clearly correct answer (all same numbers vs. broken sets) provides scaffolding even without M3 recall. Pipeline remediation handles the case where a student selects a non-family set.

### Interaction W.2: Which One Doesn't Belong? [JUDGMENT]

**Purpose:** Sharpen recognition of fact family membership by identifying a non-member. Requires checking whether all three numbers match — the same cognitive move as production but in a recognition frame.

**Visual: Arrays (dot array, display).** Horizontal. 3 rows × 5 columns = 15 dots (same array as W.1). Five equations displayed:
3 × 5 = 15 | 5 × 3 = 15 | 15 ÷ 3 = 5 | 15 ÷ 5 = 3 | 3 × 6 = 18

**Guide:** "Here are five equations. Four of them belong to the [vocab]fact family[/vocab] for 3, 5, and 15. But one doesn't belong. Can you spot the one that's NOT in the family?"

**Prompt:** "Which equation is NOT in the fact family for 3, 5, and 15?"

**Student Action:** Select (single)

**Options:**
A. 3 × 5 = 15
B. 15 ÷ 5 = 3
C. 3 × 6 = 18
D. 5 × 3 = 15

**Correct Answer:** C

* D: Member — uses 5, 3, 15 (commutative pair of A).

**On Correct:** "3 × 6 = 18 uses different numbers — 6 and 18 instead of 5 and 15. Not in this family."

**Remediation:** Pipeline

### Core Purpose

Build systematic fact family production from word problems, arrays, and equations — proving that multiplication and division are inverse operations related through a shared set of three numbers. Introduce think-multiplication as a named division strategy and check-by-inverse as verification. Extend unknown representation to □ and letters (per D7 revised).

### Pedagogical Flow

| Standard CRA Phase | Module M4 Implementation |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Concrete/Representational** | S1 Early: Word problem → student-specified array → dual equations (× and ÷). Construction IS understanding. |
| **Relational** | S2 Mid: Multiple fact families compared. Guide states pattern: "The product IS the dividend." Dedicated relational interaction (Pattern #10). |
| **Abstract** | S3 Late: Equations only — no array by default. Produce remaining family members from single equation. Letter unknowns. |
| **Application** | S3 Late: Think-multiplication (use known × to solve ÷) and check-by-inverse (verify ÷ with ×). |

### Misconception Prevention

**U4.3 — Cannot connect multiplication and division (PRIMARY):**

* 1.1–1.3: Construction requirement prevents number-shuffling. Students BUILD the array from word problems, then produce BOTH × and ÷ equations.
* 2.6: Sense-making check — student explains HOW the array shows the division equation.
* 3.2–3.4: Think-multiplication IS the application of the connection.
* Throughout: × and ÷ equations always appear together, never in isolation.

**A2 — Equals sign as "do something" (SECONDARY):**

* 1.2+: ≥30% nonstandard formats throughout (□ × 5 = 30, n × 7 = 35, 56 ÷ 8 = p).
* Fact families naturally produce varied formats. Guide uses "is the same as" language.

**A4 — Associative interference (MONITOR):**

* S1: Familiar facts only (factors 2–6, products ≤30).
* S2: Increasing complexity; familiar facts for unknown-factor tasks, harder facts where all numbers given.
* S3: Array-on-demand for verification when recall uncertain.
* No adjacent problems with confusable facts (6×7 and 6×8 never in same interaction set).

* **SK4 (Identify family triple):** 15% of problems — abstract reasoning without array. Given 3-4 equations, identify which set forms a fact family (or identify the intruder). Exercises the no-array skills from 3.1.
* **SK5 (Vocabulary identification):** 15% of problems — retrieval practice for the dividend/divisor/quotient triad. MC format. Appears interleaved with SK1/SK2 to maintain conceptual connection between vocabulary and procedure.

**Tier Classification:**

* **BASELINE:** SK1, SK2, SK4 — core inverse-relationship and strategy skills, counts toward mastery
* **STRETCH:** SK3 (check-by-inverse with incorrect answers — requires both strategy application and error detection) + SK5 (vocabulary in novel equation contexts), counts toward mastery
* **SUPPORT:** SK1 reduced — Guide provides × pair, student produces ÷ pair only (2.1-level task). Arrays fully visible. Products ≤ 40, factors ≤ 6. Does not count toward mastery.
* **CONFIDENCE:** Single ÷ equation displayed, student identifies the matching × equation from MC options. Familiar facts only (2s, 5s, 10s). Does not count toward mastery.

### Toy Constraints for Practice

Same as Lesson with the following notes:

* **Arrays:** Primary tool for SK1 problems. Full display mode. Array-to-equation overlay active. NOT used for SK2/SK3/SK4 problems (symbolic only) — available as remediation tap.
* **Equation Builder:** Full tile bank (numbers, ×, ÷, =, ?, □, letter tiles). Interactive mode for SK1/SK2/SK3. Display mode for given equations in SK4/SK5.
* **Data ranges:** Factors 2-10, products ≤ 100, all exact divisions. **Coverage note:** Lesson values lean toward factors 2-9 (S1 small, S2 mid, S3 larger). Practice should ensure factor 10 appears in SK2/SK3 for full range coverage. Near-square pairs (e.g., 6×7, 7×8, 8×9) should appear in SK2 to test precision where factors are close.
* **Equation format:** ≥30% nonstandard across Practice per D8. Formats include: result-on-left (7 = 42 ÷ 6), product-first (48 = 6 × 8), unknown-in-varied-positions (? × 8 = 56, 56 = ? × 8). All four nonstandard orientations should appear.
* **Unknown symbols:** ?, □, and letters (n, p, etc.) used interchangeably per D7 revised. System should rotate across problems — no more than 3 consecutive problems with the same symbol.

* **Division type balance (D2):** System tracks partitive/quotitive balance across Practice. For SK1 array problems, Guide's framing determines type. Target 50/50; flag at 45%; hard floor at 40%.

### Error-Pattern Monitoring

The following error patterns should trigger diagnostic flags in the Practice pipeline:

* **U4.3 — Cannot connect × and ÷ in family production:** Student produces × pair OR ÷ pair but not both, OR produces all four equations using incorrect numbers (e.g., writes 21 ÷ 7 = 3 but also writes 21 × 7 = 147). Intervention: route to SUPPORT tier with Guide providing × pair, student completes ÷ pair. Increase SK5 vocabulary problems to rebuild the conceptual connection.
* **A2 — Rejects nonstandard format:** Student solves standard-format think-multiplication problems but fails nonstandard (result-on-left). Intervention: increase nonstandard format frequency gradually, with Guide acknowledgment ("written differently, but means the same").
* **A1 — Dividend/divisor position errors in family production:** Student writes 3 ÷ 21 = 7 instead of 21 ÷ 3 = 7. Confuses which number goes first in ÷ equation. Intervention: return to array ("What's the total? That goes first in division."). Route to SUPPORT with array always visible.
* **Strategy confusion (think-multiplication vs. check-by-inverse):** Student uses think-multiplication when asked to check (or vice versa). Intervention: explicit framing distinction — "Think-multiplication SOLVES a ÷ problem. Check-by-inverse VERIFIES a ÷ answer." Increase SK3 frequency with explicit strategy labeling.
* **Procedural family production (U4.3 variant):** Student generates all four equations correctly but selects "you swap the numbers" on SK5 conceptual problems. Indicates number-shuffling without understanding. Intervention: route to array representation to rebuild visual connection, then increase SK5 frequency.

## 1.9 SYNTHESIS (6-8 minutes)

**[REQUIRED]**

### Purpose

Students step back from the procedural work of building fact families and applying strategies to see the BIGGER PICTURE: × and ÷ are two views of the same relationship, and fact families are the organizational structure that makes this visible. They connect across representations (array → equations → strategy), identify the invariant pattern ("three numbers, four equations"), reflect on which insight matters most, and preview M5's multiplication table exploration. Per TVP: "One array. Four equations. If you know one, you know the others."

### Parameters

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Duration** | 6-8 minutes |
| **Toys Used** | Arrays (dot array, display), Equation Builder (display + interactive) |
| **Interaction Count** | 3 student-action moments (S.2 representation transfer, S.3 pattern discovery, S.4 metacognitive reflection) + Opening Frame + Identity-Building Closure |
| **Task Types** | Type D: Representation Transfer (S.2 — same relationship across array/equations/strategy), Type A: Pattern Discovery (S.3 — invariant pattern in fact families), Metacognitive Reflection Type 4: Key Takeaway (S.4) |

### Constraints

| MUST | MUST NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Use a fresh value set not seen in Lesson, EC, or Warmup | Reuse Lesson/EC/Warmup factor-product triples |
| Connect across representations (array ↔ equations ↔ strategy) | Present more practice problems disguised as Synthesis |
| Include at least 2 different task types (Type D + Type A) | Use only one task type |
| Include metacognitive reflection before closure | Skip reflection |
| Identity-building closure references specific student achievements | Use generic praise ("Great job!") |
| Bridge to M5 mentions "multiplication table" and "patterns" per TVP | Bridge to content beyond M5 |
| Light remediation only (mastery assumed) | Extended reteaching |

### Opening Frame [No Student Action]

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from assessment to reflection. Connect backward to the three strategies learned (fact families, think-multiplication, check-by-inverse) and set up the consolidation work.
* **Visual: Arrays (dot array, display).** 2 rows × 9 columns = 18 dots. All dots visible. **Equation Builder (display).** Four equations displayed: 2 × 9 = 18, 9 × 2 = 18, 18 ÷ 2 = 9, 18 ÷ 9 = 2. Below: a think-multiplication callout — "□ × 9 = 18 → 18 ÷ 9 = ?" Numbers (2, 9, 18) color-coded across all representations.
* **Guide:** "You built [vocab]fact families[/vocab], used think-multiplication, and checked with the [vocab]inverse[/vocab]. Look at this: one array, four equations, one strategy. What ties them together?"
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** Opening displays all three representations simultaneously — the visual payoff of the module. The color-coding makes the "same three numbers" invariant visible before students are asked to articulate it. Fresh value (2 × 9 = 18) keeps cognitive load low for the connection task.

---

### Interaction S.2: Three Representations, One Relationship [REPRESENTATION TRANSFER — Type D]

* **Purpose:** Students identify what stays the same across three representations of the inverse relationship: array, fact family equations, and think-multiplication scenario. Type D task: same concept in different forms.
* **Visual:** Three panels side by side:
  - Panel A: Array — 2 rows × 9 columns = 18 dots
  - Panel B: Four equations — 2 × 9 = 18, 9 × 2 = 18, 18 ÷ 2 = 9, 18 ÷ 9 = 2
  - Panel C: Think-multiplication — "□ × 9 = 18 → □ = 2"
  - Numbers (2, 9, 18) color-coded identically across all three panels.
* **Guide:** "Look at all three. The array, the [vocab]fact family[/vocab], and think-multiplication. What stays the same?"
* **Prompt:** "What do the array, the fact family, and think-multiplication all have in common?"
* **Student Action:** Select (single)
* **Options:**
A. They all use the same three numbers: 2, 9, and 18
B. They all show division
C. They all need an array
D. They all have four equations
* **Correct Answer:** A
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - A (correct): The invariant — {2, 9, 18} appears in every representation. The array has 2 rows, 9 columns, 18 dots. The [vocab]fact family[/vocab] uses 2, 9, 18 in all four equations. Think-multiplication uses □ × 9 = 18 → □ = 2.
  - B: Panel C uses × to solve the ÷ problem, and Panel B includes both × and ÷. Not all show ÷ exclusively.
  - C: Only Panel A has the array. Think-multiplication (Panel C) works without one.
  - D: Only Panel B has four equations. The array and think-multiplication don't.
* **On Correct:** "Same three numbers everywhere. The array shows them, the [vocab]fact family[/vocab] organizes them, and think-multiplication uses them to solve."
* **Connection:** Students see that fact family production, think-multiplication, and check-by-inverse aren't three separate tricks — they're three applications of one relationship: the same three numbers connect × and ÷.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline (light)

---

### Interaction S.3: What's Always True? [PATTERN DISCOVERY — Type A]

* **Purpose:** Students generalize from specific fact families to the invariant pattern: any fact family uses three numbers to generate four equations (2× and 2÷). Type A task: identify the rule.
* **Visual: Equation Builder (display).** Two fact families displayed side by side:
  - Family 1 (from S.2): 2 × 9 = 18, 9 × 2 = 18, 18 ÷ 2 = 9, 18 ÷ 9 = 2
  - Family 2 (from Lesson 2.2): 5 × 8 = 40, 8 × 5 = 40, 40 ÷ 5 = 8, 40 ÷ 8 = 5
  - Numbers within each family color-coded (same scheme as S.2 for Family 1). Label: "Two different [vocab]fact families[/vocab]."
* **Guide:** "Two different [vocab]fact families[/vocab]. Different numbers. But something is ALWAYS true. What?"
* **Prompt:** "What's always true about any fact family?"
* **Student Action:** Select (single)
* **Options:**
A. Three numbers make four equations — two multiply and two divide
B. Fact families always have the number 18
C. You need an array to build a fact family
D. All four equations give the same answer
* **Correct Answer:** A
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - A (correct): The structural invariant — every [vocab]fact family[/vocab] has exactly three numbers, four equations (2× + 2÷). True for {2, 9, 18} and {5, 8, 40} and any other family.
  - B: Only true for Family 1. Family 2 uses 40, not 18.
  - C: 3.1 in Lesson showed families can be identified from equations alone, without an array.
  - D: Answers differ within each family (18 and 18 for × in Family 1, 9 and 2 for ÷).
* **On Correct:** "Three numbers, four equations, two operations. That's every [vocab]fact family[/vocab], no matter what the numbers are."
* **Connection:** This is the generalization moment — the student moves from "I can build a fact family" (procedure) to "I understand WHY fact families work this way" (structure). The pattern holds for ALL multiplication/division relationships within 100.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline (light)

### Interaction S.4: What's the One Thing to Remember? — Metacognitive Reflection (Type 4: Key Takeaway) [REFLECTION]

* **Purpose:** Students identify their key takeaway from the module. Type 4 (Key Takeaway) appropriate for mid-range module (M4). Produces a concrete, articulable output — the student names what matters most.
* **Visual: Equation Builder (display).** Callback equations: 6 × 8 = 48 and 48 ÷ 8 = □ (from Lesson 3.2's think-multiplication moment).
* **Guide:** "If you had to remember ONE thing from this lesson, what would it be?"
* **Prompt:** "What's the most important thing you learned in this lesson?"
* **Student Action:** Select (single)
* **Options:**
A. If I know a multiply fact, I can solve the matching divide problem
B. A fact family has four equations using three numbers
C. Check-by-inverse lets me verify my division answers
D. Arrays show both multiplication and division
* **Correct Answer:** Any (all valid metacognitive responses — all-correct MC)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - "If I know a multiply fact, I can solve the matching divide problem" = Core strategy insight (think-multiplication — THE primary strategy outcome)
  - "A fact family has four equations using three numbers" = Core structural insight (the organizational concept)
  - "Check-by-inverse lets me verify my division answers" = Core verification strategy
  - "Arrays show both multiplication and division" = Valid but M3 content (reinforcement, not new M4 learning)
* **On Correct:** (per selection)
  - A → "That's think-multiplication, the power tool. If you can multiply, you can divide."
  - B → "Three numbers, four equations. You can build the whole [vocab]fact family[/vocab] from any one piece."
  - C → "Multiply to check divide. You'll never be unsure about a division answer again."
  - D → "Arrays made it visible. That picture helps even when the array isn't there."
* **Connection:** Callbacks to the module's core learning trajectory. The ideal response names think-multiplication (the primary strategy outcome per §1.0); all responses are validated and connected to the broader arc.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline (light)

> **Design Note — All-Correct MC (Pipeline):** Intentionally all-correct MC. Every option is a valid metacognitive response — no wrong answers. Pipeline should NOT trigger remediation on any selection. All four On Correct responses are tailored. This interaction's purpose is identity-building reflection, not assessment.

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS

**[REQUIRED]**

### Purpose

KDDs document the non-obvious pedagogical design choices that make this module work. Each entry explains WHAT was decided, what ALTERNATIVE was considered, and WHY this path serves student learning. A reader seeing this module for the first time should understand why it works this way from the KDDs alone.

---

#### Representational Design

### KDD-1: M3 Callback Value (3 × 5 = 15) as Bookend

The Warmup opens with the 3 × 5 = 15 array — the same value M3's opening interaction used for dual-reading. This creates a narrative bookend: the array students first learned to read two ways now appears with its full four-equation fact family named. **Alternative:** Use a fresh value to avoid the M3 association. **Why this path wins:** The callback attaches the new label ("fact family") to a fully familiar pattern, so the naming competes with zero new content. Students who struggled in M3 get a second exposure; students who succeeded get a satisfying recognition moment.

### KDD-2: Array Fading Across Three Sections (Not Two)

S1 uses student-built arrays (construction mode), S2 uses given arrays (display mode), and S3 removes arrays entirely (equations only, array on demand). The three-step fade is slower than jumping from construction to symbolic. **Alternative:** Drop display-mode arrays and go directly from construction (S1) to equations-only (S3). **Why this path wins:** The intermediate step (S2: given array, no construction) isolates the family-production skill from the array-building skill. S1 tests "can you build the right array AND produce equations?" while S2 tests "given the array, can you produce ALL FOUR equations?" Conflating these makes it impossible to diagnose which skill is failing.

### KDD-3: 3.2 Strategy Modeling in Abstract Phase (ISF-1)

3.2 models think-multiplication as a worked example in the Abstract phase (no array). The Playbook places think-alouds in Concrete/Representational. **Alternative:** Move strategy introduction to S2 while arrays are still present. **Why this path wins:** Think-multiplication is specifically about solving division WITHOUT visual support — modeling it with an array would undermine the strategy's purpose. 3.2 is direct instruction (strategy demonstration), not a formal [Guide_Think_Aloud]; the formal think-aloud is in 1.1 (Concrete phase, per Playbook).

---

#### Vocabulary & Scope

### KDD-4: Vocabulary Retrieval Triad — Dividend (2.1b), Divisor (2.2b), Quotient (2.4b)

All three division-equation terms are explicitly identified by the student through MC sub-parts within S2. M3 introduced these terms for exposure and contextual use; M4 is the retrieval module per SME. **Alternative:** Embed vocabulary identification only in answer rationale and On Correct text (no student-facing MC). **Why this path wins:** Retrieval practice requires the student to actively select the correct term — passive exposure in narration doesn't test retrieval. Spacing across S2 (not all three in one interaction) distributes the retrieval load. Each MC has three options matching the three terms, so every MC reinforces the full triad even while testing one term.

### KDD-5: "Fact Family" Named in Warmup, Not Lesson (Exception to Vocabulary Rule)

The Warmup Playbook prohibits introducing new vocabulary. "Fact family" is treated as an exception because it was previewed in M3 Synthesis S.3 (On Correct: "Mathematicians call this set of equations a fact family"). **Alternative:** Defer naming to the Lesson Purpose Frame. **Why this path wins:** The Warmup's organizing concept IS the fact family. Deferring the label would require the Guide to say "this group of four equations" repeatedly for 2+ minutes before naming it, creating awkward circumlocution. The term attaches to a pre-existing pattern (four equations, one array) — the naming is retrieval of a familiar label, not introduction of a new concept.

### KDD-6: Think-Multiplication Named After Extensive Family Production (S3, Not S1)

Think-multiplication is named in 3.2 — after 6+ interactions of family production (2.1–2.6). **Alternative:** Name the strategy in S1 alongside first dual-equation production. **Why this path wins:** The strategy depends on understanding WHY × and ÷ share numbers; naming it before building families gives the label without the understanding.

---

#### Balance & Constraint

### KDD-7: Both Division Types Maintained Across Module (D2)

S1 includes both partitive (1.1: "12 crackers shared on 4 plates") and quotitive (1.2: "30 stickers, 6 per row") word problems. S2-S3 move to array-based and symbolic work where division type is implicit. Practice tracks partitive/quotitive balance at 50/50 with flag at 45% and hard floor at 40%. **Alternative:** Focus exclusively on partitive since it maps more naturally to "sharing equally." **Why this path wins:** Arrays support both readings naturally (same image, different question: "how many per row?" vs. "how many rows?"). Restricting to one type would create a gap that M5 would need to fill.

### KDD-8: Nonstandard Format Progression (D8)

Lesson runs below D8's 30% threshold (~4/15 interactions = 27% counting all sub-parts) to keep S1-S2 cognitive load on relationship understanding, not format novelty. EC adds 1/3 nonstandard (EC.2: p = 45 ÷ 5). Practice adds ≥30% nonstandard. Module-wide total meets D8. **Alternative:** Hit 30% within Lesson alone by adding more nonstandard formats in S1-S2. **Why this path wins:** Students in S1-S2 are learning to produce fact families — competing with unfamiliar equation layouts increases error without pedagogical benefit. Nonstandard formats test relational understanding of =, which is better assessed after the relationship is established (S3, EC, Practice).

---

#### Scaffolding Design

### KDD-9: Sub-Numbering for Multi-Step Interactions (Template Compliance)

Four interactions (2.1, 2.2, 2.4, 3.4) contain two distinct student actions and are sub-numbered (e.g., 2.1a/2.1b). Each sub-part has its own Correct Answer, On Correct, and Remediation: Pipeline. **Alternative:** Keep as single interactions with combined student actions. **Why this path wins:** Per Template §Multi-Step Interactions: "If a student submits more than one answer, there must be more than one sub-part." The two actions in each case test different skills (production vs. vocabulary identification for S2; computation vs. explanation for 3.4) and must be independently remediable.

### KDD-10: 2.6 Sense-Making Without Guide Pre-Answering

2.6's Guide asks "Which reading shows 42 ÷ 6 = 7?" without describing what the correct reading looks like. The student must independently determine that 6 columns with 7 dots each represents 42 ÷ 6 = 7. **Alternative:** Guide narrates the array structure ("Six columns with 7 dots in each — 42 dots split into 6 columns gives 7 per column") before posing the MC question. **Why this path wins:** Pre-narrating the correct reading allows pattern-matching without spatial reasoning — the student can match the Guide's description to the MC option without looking at the array. The sense-making check loses its diagnostic value. Distractors (B: row-reading, D: swapped rows/columns) test whether the student can independently distinguish row-reading from column-reading.

### KDD-11: Data Value Selection — M3 Collision Avoidance

Every Lesson product (12, 30, 8, 16, 40, 42, 54, 48, 35, 56, 27) was checked against M3's Lesson products (15, 24, 14, 20, 32, 54, 28, 48, 42, 63), M3's EC products (35, 27, 40), and M3's Synthesis products (36). Collisions: 42 (M3 S3, different demand — documented per Pattern #16), 54 (M3 2.3, different context), 15 (M3 S1 — deliberate Warmup callback). EC values (21, 45, 72) and Synthesis values (18) are fully fresh across both modules. **Alternative:** No cross-module checking — accept collisions. **Why this path wins:** Per Data Constraint Audit Pattern #16, repeated products across modules risk students confusing which module taught which concept, especially when the same fact appears in fundamentally different contexts.

### KDD-12: Check-by-Inverse as Core Teaching Beat (Not Optional Extension)

Module Mapping labels check-by-inverse as "EXTENSION" — meaning extended FROM the M3 discussion into M4 (where students actively find unknowns), not optional within M4. It appears in the TVP's Late activities and Exit Check as a standard teaching beat. **Alternative:** Treat check-by-inverse as optional, deferring to Practice if time runs short. **Why this path wins:** Removing 3.5–3.6 would leave students with no verification strategy — think-multiplication solves ÷ problems, but check-by-inverse confirms the answers are correct.
