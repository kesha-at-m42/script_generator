## 1.0 THE ONE THING

**[REQUIRED]**

Division IS finding a missing factor — the question "15 ÷ 3 = ?" is the SAME question as "? × 3 = 15" — and arrays make this visible because one arrangement simultaneously encodes both multiplication and division.

**CRA Stage:** Representational. Students transition from M1-M2's concrete Equal Groups to arrays as the primary representation. Equations with unknowns (?) appear alongside array visuals per D4 (simultaneous CRA connections) and research (Milton et al., 2019).

**Critical Misconception:** U4.3 — Cannot connect multiplication and division as inverse operations. If this connection doesn't click here, everything downstream (M4 fact families, M12 think-multiplication) suffers. Robinson & LeFevre (2012) found conceptual understanding of multiplicative inversion is significantly harder than additive inversion.

**Biggest Risk:** Students learn to produce the "matching" equation procedurally (swapping numbers between × and ÷ positions) without understanding WHY the relationship holds — reducing M3 to pattern-matching rather than genuine inverse relationship understanding. The array visual anchor and the interpretation push (student identifies what ? means in the array context) are the safeguards.

**Success Indicator:** Given a multiplication equation with a missing factor (e.g., ? × 4 = 28), the student identifies the missing factor, writes the corresponding division equation (28 ÷ 4 = 7), AND can explain what the ? represents in the array context ("? is the number of rows") — not just compute the answer. Per SME assessment criterion: if a student can produce both a multiplication AND division equation for one situation, the inverse relationship has clicked.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

**[REQUIRED]**

### 1.4.1 #U4.3: Cannot Connect Multiplication and Division (PRIMARY — CRITICAL)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student knows 7 × 8 = 56 but cannot quickly derive 56 ÷ 8 = 7. Uses counting or repeated subtraction for division even when the related multiplication fact is known. When asked "how does 3 × 5 = 15 help you solve 15 ÷ 3?", cannot explain the connection. May produce fact families procedurally (shuffling numbers) without understanding why four facts exist from one arrangement.

**Why It Happens:** Robinson & LeFevre (2012) found conceptual understanding of multiplicative inversion is significantly harder than additive inversion. Robinson et al. (2016) found repeated exposure alone does not reliably build this understanding — explicit instruction is required. Curricula that teach multiplication and division as separate, disconnected units produce students who see ÷ as an entirely unrelated operation. Weak multiplication fluency compounds the problem by consuming working memory needed for relational reasoning.

**Visual Cue:** One array with both equations displayed simultaneously. Row highlighting reads as multiplication (3 rows of 5 = 15 → 3 × 5 = 15); reframing the same image as "15 dots in 3 rows — how many per row?" reads as division (15 ÷ 3 = 5). The array IS the connection — same visual, different question.

**Prevention Strategy:** (1) Arrays as the bridge — every × equation is paired with a ÷ equation from the same visual, so the connection is visible, not abstract. (2) Unknown-factor framing makes the connection explicit: "? × 3 = 15 IS 15 ÷ 3 = ?" — the equations share structure, not just numbers. (3) Interpretation push — students identify what ? means in the array ("number of rows"), keeping the relationship grounded. (4) Synthesis four-equation construction shows the full pattern: one array, four facts, all connected.

### 1.4.2 #A2: Equals Sign as "Do Something" (SECONDARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student rejects 12 = 3 × 4 ("the answer should be on the right"). Insists 8 = 8 is "wrong" or "not a real equation." When writing fact families, always puts the product on the right. Struggles with ? × 5 = 40 format but can solve 5 × ? = 40. May reject n = 24 ÷ 6 and insist on writing 24 ÷ 6 = n instead. In true/false tasks, cannot evaluate equations without computing.

**Why It Happens:** Behr et al. (1980) and McNeil & Alibali (2005) found the majority of Grades 1–6 students treat = operationally ("the answer comes next"). Powell (2012) found curricula rarely present nonstandard equation formats. This misconception is especially critical in Unit 4 because students write fact families (4 equations from one array), unknown-factor equations (? × 5 = 40), and eventually two-step equations with letter unknowns — ALL require relational understanding of =. Students who treat = as "do something" will reject valid equations like 40 = 5 × 8 or n = 24 ÷ 6.

**Visual Cue:** Equations displayed in multiple formats simultaneously: ? × 5 = 40 alongside 40 = ? × 5. "Is the same as" language reinforces relational reading.

**Prevention Strategy:** Per D8 — nonstandard equation formats woven throughout at ≥30% of equations. Guide uses "is the same as" alongside = when presenting nonstandard formats. No true/false activities. Formats: ? × 5 = 40, 40 = ? × 5, 15 = 3 × ?. Consistent exposure normalizes relational understanding without requiring explicit instruction on the equals sign.

### 1.4.3 #U4.2: Partitive/Quotitive Confusion (TERTIARY — MONITORED)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student can solve "Share 24 candies among 4 friends" (partitive) but freezes on "24 candies, 6 per bag — how many bags?" (quotitive), or vice versa. May default to one division type exclusively. In array context, reads "3 rows of 5" (quotitive — how many per row) but cannot shift to "15 dots in 3 equal rows — how many rows?" (partitive).

**Why It Happens:** Students often learn division through a single model (usually sharing/partitive). Without exposure to both types, the quotitive interpretation — finding the number of groups — doesn't develop. M1–M2 taught both types with Equal Groups; M3 must sustain this balance in the new array representation.

**Visual Cue:** Arrays read two ways — row count vs. items-per-row — make both division types visible from one image. Guide frames both readings explicitly.

**Prevention Strategy:** D2 requires both division types with equal emphasis (target 50/50, hard floor 40/60). §1.5 Guardrails require "both partitive and quotitive readings from one array." S1 includes at least one quotitive reading per TVP. Both types present in S2 interpretation prompts. Data Constraints track division type balance by section.

## 1.6 WARMUP (~3 minutes)

**[REQUIRED]**

### Core Purpose

Reactivate the multiplication-division number connection previewed in M2's closure and bridge students from Equal Groups (M1-M2's tool) to Arrays (M3's primary tool) — establishing that one array simultaneously shows both operations.

**Key Function:** This Warmup delivers on M2's promise ("15 ÷ 3 = 5 and 3 × 5 = 15 use the same numbers — next time, you'll explore WHY"). It bridges from the Equal Groups representation students know to the Arrays representation they'll use throughout M3. Students see the same numbers (3, 5, 15) in both representations, confirming structural equivalence before the Lesson explores why × and ÷ are connected.

**Why this serves the concept:**

* Fulfills M2's curiosity gap — the student left M2 wondering WHY 15 ÷ 3 and 3 × 5 share the same numbers. The Warmup immediately activates that question.
* Establishes the EG → Arrays bridge that the TVP and SME require. Without this explicit visual bridging, students would encounter arrays as a "new tool" rather than recognizing them as a reorganization of the groups they already know.
* Activates both × and ÷ vocabulary from prior modules ([vocab]divide[/vocab], [vocab]equal groups[/vocab], [vocab]array[/vocab], [vocab]row[/vocab], [vocab]column[/vocab]) in a single observation — priming the dual-equation work in S1.

**Test:** If we removed this Warmup and went straight to the Lesson, students would encounter arrays as unfamiliar division tools. The EG → Array structural bridge would be missing, and the inverse relationship would lack its visual anchor. Students need to explicitly see that "3 bags of 5 apples" and "3 rows of 5 dots" encode the same structure before they can see that one array encodes both 3 × 5 = 15 AND 15 ÷ 3 = 5.

## 1.7 LESSON (~12-15 minutes)

**[REQUIRED]**

### Core Purpose

Students discover that division IS finding a missing factor — the question "15 ÷ 3 = ?" is the SAME question as "? × 3 = 15" — through arrays that simultaneously encode both operations. Formal vocabulary (quotient, dividend, divisor) is introduced AFTER the inverse insight lands. Students progress from guided observation (S1) through the key reframe with vocabulary (S2) to independent symbolic production (S3).

### Pedagogical Flow

S1 establishes that one array encodes both × and ÷ through guided observation with highlighted arrays. S2 delivers THE insight — unknown-factor framing makes the ×/÷ connection explicit — followed by vocabulary formalization after grounding (KP#11). S3 removes array support and asks students to produce equation pairs independently, testing whether the inverse relationship has transferred to symbolic reasoning. The CRA progression is: Representational with explicit instruction (S1) → Relational + Abstract vocabulary (S2) → Application (S3).

| S3: Independent Application | Produce equation pairs from symbolic input — arrays fade | Application | ~3-4 min |

* **S3 (Symbolic translation):** 30% of problems — independent equation translation without array support. Given × → produce ÷, and given ÷ → produce ×. Highest independence level. Include nonstandard formats per D8.
* **S4 (Interpretation):** 10% of problems — conceptual guardrail against procedural drift (U4.3 prevention). MC with meaning-based options. Appears after S1/S2 problems to confirm the student understands WHAT the unknown represents, not just WHAT the value is.
* **S5 (Conceptual confirmation):** 10% of problems — metacognitive/conceptual. MC or open-ended. "Why do these two equations use the same numbers?" Targets U4.3 detection — students who select procedural answers ("you flip the numbers") are flagged.

**Tier Classification:**

* **BASELINE:** S1, S2, and S3 problems — core inverse-relationship skills, counts toward mastery
* **STRETCH:** S5 (conceptual confirmation) — extended conceptual assessment, counts toward mastery
* **SUPPORT:** Reduce to S2 problems only (array → dual production), with arrays fully visible and Guide providing the × equation — student produces only the ÷ equation. Dividends ≤ 30, factors ≤ 6. Does not count toward mastery.
* **CONFIDENCE:** Single array → produce ÷ equation only, with pre-populated × equation displayed. Dividend ≤ 20, factors ≤ 5. Familiar facts only. Does not count toward mastery.

### Toy Constraints for Practice

Same as Lesson with the following notes:

* **Arrays:** Primary tool. Full display mode (S2 problems), partial concealment mode (S1 problems). Array-to-equation overlay active. No arrays for S3 problems (symbolic only) — available as remediation tap.
* **Equation Builder:** Full tile bank (numbers, ×, ÷, =, ?). Interactive mode for S1/S2/S3 production. Display mode for given equations. ? tile available for unknown-factor problems.
* **Equal Groups:** Remediation only — available if student needs to regress to concrete representation. Not in primary Practice flow.
* **Data ranges:** Factors 2-10, products within 100, all exact divisions. **Coverage note:** Lesson values lean toward factors 4-9 (Mid/Late range). Practice should include more factor 2, 3, and 10 problems to ensure full range coverage. Near-square pairs (e.g., 6×7, 7×8) should appear in S3 to test precision.
* **Equation format:** ≥30% nonstandard across Practice per D8. Formats include: ? × n = total, total = ? × n, total = n × ?, n × ? = total. All four formats should appear.
* **Division type balance (D2):** System tracks partitive/quotitive balance across Practice. For S2 problems, Guide's framing question determines which type the student practices first ("how many rows?" = quotitive, "how many per row?" = partitive). Target 50/50; flag at 45%; hard floor at 40%.

### Cross-Module Skill References

### Error-Pattern Monitoring

The following error patterns should trigger diagnostic flags in the Practice pipeline:

* **U4.3 — Cannot connect × and ÷:** Student can find unknown factors but cannot write the matching ÷ equation (or vice versa). The two operations remain disconnected. Intervention: route to SUPPORT tier with Guide providing explicit connection language ("You found ? = 7. So 7 × 5 = 35. Now: 35 divided by 5 is the same question — 35 ÷ 5 = ?"). Increase S4 interpretation problems.
* **A2 — Rejects nonstandard format:** Student solves standard-format problems correctly but fails or refuses nonstandard (result-on-left). Intervention: increase nonstandard format frequency gradually, with Guide acknowledgment ("written differently, but means the same").
* **A1 — Dividend/divisor position errors:** Student writes 7 ÷ 35 = 5 instead of 35 ÷ 7 = 5. Confuses which number goes where in the ÷ equation. Intervention: return to array ("What's the total? That goes first. What are you dividing by?"). Route to SUPPORT with array always visible.
* **Procedural pattern-matching (U4.3 variant):** Student produces matching equations correctly but selects "you flip the numbers" on S5 conceptual problems. Indicates procedural shortcut without understanding. Intervention: increase S4 interpretation problems to rebuild the array-meaning connection.

## 1.9 SYNTHESIS (6-8 minutes)

[REQUIRED]

### Purpose

Students build a complete four-equation family from a single array for the first time, discovering that one array generates two multiplication AND two division equations — a fact family preview. They consolidate the module's vocabulary (quotient, dividend, divisor connected to factor/product), reflect on which strategy helped them connect × and ÷, and preview M4's systematic fact family work. Per TVP: "One array. Four equations. If you know one, you know the others."

### Parameters

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Duration** | 6-8 minutes |
| **Toys Used** | Arrays (dot array, full display with guide-controlled row/column highlighting), Equation Builder (interactive + display) |
| **Interaction Count** | 4 student-action moments (S.2a Guide-led pair, S.2b student-led pair, S.3 pattern observation, S.4 metacognitive reflection) + Opening Frame + Identity-Building Closure |
| **Task Types** | Type B: Progressive Challenge (S.2a-S.2b four-equation construction), Type A: Pattern Discovery (S.3 vocabulary/number-role consolidation), Metacognitive Reflection Type 4: Key Takeaway (S.4) |

### Constraints

| MUST | MUST NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Use a fresh array value not seen in Lesson or EC | Reuse Lesson/EC factor-product triples |
| Build all four equations step-by-step with array highlighting support | Present four equations simultaneously without construction process |
| Include at least 2 different task types | Use only one task type |
| Include metacognitive reflection before closure | Skip reflection |
| Preview "fact family" vocabulary (exposure only) | Teach fact families as a strategy (M4) |
| Identity-building closure references specific student achievement | Use generic praise ("Great job!") |
| Bridge to M4 mentions "strategy" and "fact families" per TVP | Bridge to content beyond M4 |

### Opening Frame [No Student Action]

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from assessment to reflection/discovery. Connect backward to the Lesson's inverse relationship work and set up the four-equation construction.

* **Guide:** "You discovered something today: [vocab]multiply[/vocab] and [vocab]divide[/vocab] are connected. One [vocab]array[/vocab] gives you both. Now let's see how MANY [vocab]equations[/vocab] one [vocab]array[/vocab] can give you."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** The opening frame connects backward to 2.5's confirmation ("dividing IS finding the missing factor") and forward to the four-equation construction. "How MANY equations" creates anticipation — students expect two (they've been building pairs), but they'll discover four. Fresh value (4×9=36) not seen in Lesson or EC.

### Interaction S.2a: Guide Models Row-Reading Pair [PROGRESSIVE CHALLENGE — GUIDE-LED]

* **Purpose:** Guide demonstrates the row-reading of the array, producing the first × and ÷ pair. Students observe and confirm. This is the first half of the Type B Progressive Challenge — Guide does the row reading, student will do the column reading.
* **Visual: Arrays (dot array with guide-controlled row highlighting).** 4 rows × 9 columns = 36 dots. Row highlighting activates: rows glow one at a time. Equation Builder alongside.
* **Guide:** "I see 4 [vocab]rows[/vocab] of 9. That's 4 × 9 = 36." [Equation 4 × 9 = 36 appears in Equation Builder.] "Now the [vocab]divide[/vocab] question: 36 dots, 4 [vocab]rows[/vocab], how many per [vocab]row[/vocab]? That's 36 [vocab]÷[/vocab] 4 = 9." [Equation 36 ÷ 4 = 9 appears alongside.] "Two [vocab]equations[/vocab] from the rows. But there's another way to read this [vocab]array[/vocab]."
* **Prompt:** "Watch how the Guide reads the rows."
* **Student Action:** Observation confirmed via click/tap ("Got it" or equivalent)
* **Correct Answer:** N/A (observation + confirmation)
* **On Correct:** N/A
* **Connection:** Callbacks to S1's dual-reading pattern (one array, two equations) — the familiar pattern from Lesson. Sets up the column reading as the new cognitive step.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

### Interaction S.2b: Student Builds Column-Reading Pair [PROGRESSIVE CHALLENGE — STUDENT-LED]

* **Purpose:** Student shifts to the column reading of the SAME array and builds the second × and ÷ pair independently. This is the genuine cognitive step — the student must recognize that columns give a different (commutative) × reading AND a different ÷ reading. Per TVP: "Components developed in Mid activities — Synthesis assembles the pieces."
* **Visual: Arrays (dot array with guide-controlled column highlighting).** Same 4 × 9 array. Column highlighting activates: columns glow one at a time. Equation Builder: first pair (4 × 9 = 36 and 36 ÷ 4 = 9) displayed at top. Two new empty equation slots below. Tile bank: numbers (36, 9, 4), operators (×, ÷, =).
* **Guide:** "Now read the columns. 9 columns of 4. Build the multiply [vocab]equation[/vocab] AND the [vocab]divide[/vocab] [vocab]equation[/vocab]."
* **Prompt:** "Build both equations from the column reading."
* **Student Action:** Build two equations: 9 × 4 = 36 AND 36 ÷ 9 = 4
* **Correct Answer:** 9 × 4 = 36 and 36 ÷ 9 = 4
* **On Correct:** "9 × 4 = 36 and 36 [vocab]÷[/vocab] 9 = 4. Four [vocab]equations[/vocab] from one [vocab]array[/vocab]."
* **Connection:** The four equations are now visible together. Students see the complete pattern for the first time — one array, four equations, all using {4, 9, 36}. This is the Synthesis culmination.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Scaffolding Note:** S.2a is MEDIUM (Guide-led, student observes). S.2b is LOW (student produces independently with only column highlighting as scaffold). The asymmetry is intentional — the row-reading pair is familiar (done throughout Lesson), the column-reading pair is the new cognitive step requiring the perspective shift. Per TVP: "step-by-step submission with visual support (row vs column highlighting) lowers cognitive load."

### Interaction S.3: What Stays the Same? — Vocabulary Consolidation [PATTERN DISCOVERY]

* **Purpose:** Students observe that all four equations use the SAME three numbers in different roles, and connect to vocabulary: the numbers that are [vocab]factors[/vocab] in × become [vocab]dividend[/vocab] and [vocab]divisor[/vocab] in ÷. Type A task: "What do you notice across all four equations?"
* **Visual:** All four equations displayed together in a 2×2 grid:
  - 4 × 9 = 36 | 9 × 4 = 36
  - 36 ÷ 4 = 9 | 36 ÷ 9 = 4
  - Array (small, reference) displayed alongside. Numbers in equations color-coded: 36 (blue), 4 (red), 9 (green) — same colors across all four equations.
* **Guide:** "Four [vocab]equations[/vocab], one [vocab]array[/vocab]. Look at the numbers. What do all four [vocab]equations[/vocab] have in common?"
* **Prompt:** "What do you notice about the numbers in all four equations?"
* **Student Action:** MC (single)
  * **Options:** "They all use the same three numbers: 4, 9, and 36," "They all have the same answer," "They all use division," "They all show 4 rows"
* **Correct Answer:** "They all use the same three numbers: 4, 9, and 36"
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - "Same three numbers" = Correct (the invariant pattern — {4, 9, 36} in different positions/roles across all four equations)
  - "Same answer" = Incorrect (the answers differ: 36, 36, 9, 4)
  - "All use division" = Incorrect (two use × and two use ÷)
  - "All show 4 rows" = Partial (only the row-reading equations emphasize 4 rows; column-reading equations emphasize 9 columns)
* **On Correct:** "Same three numbers, four [vocab]equations[/vocab]. The [vocab]product[/vocab] becomes the [vocab]dividend[/vocab]. Each [vocab]factor[/vocab] takes a turn as the [vocab]divisor[/vocab]. That's a [vocab]fact family[/vocab]."

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS

[REQUIRED]

### Purpose

KDDs document the pedagogical design choices that make this module work. Each entry explains WHAT was decided and WHY it matters for student learning. A reader seeing this module for the first time should understand why it works this way from the KDDs alone.

---

#### Representational Design

### KDD-1: Groups-to-Arrays Bridge in Warmup (Not Cold Start)

M3 opens with an explicit Equal Groups → Arrays bridge (3 bags of 5 shown alongside a 3×5 dot array) rather than jumping directly to arrays. Students in M1-M2 worked exclusively with Equal Groups. Per SME: "Directly relate the groups visuals to the arrays visuals to activate prior knowledge and give a solid base for the inverse operations connections." The bridge ensures students see arrays as a new view of a familiar structure, not an unrelated tool.

### KDD-2: Array Partial Concealment Creates the Unknown-Factor Problem (S2)

Mid (S2) uses arrays with blurred rows or columns and a numerically displayed total to create unknown-factor problems. The concealment IS the unknown: "? rows × 8 = 32" corresponds to an array where the rows are hidden. This makes the unknown factor concrete (a physical part of the array that's hidden) rather than abstract (a letter or symbol in an equation). Engineering dependency: AF1 — the total must be displayed numerically since students can't count blurred dots.

### KDD-3: S3.1 No Array — Remediation Only (Engineering Constraint)

S3.1 omits the originally-designed faded array due to engineering feasibility constraints. The array is available as a remediation tap. This collapses the two-step scaffold fade (faded array → no array) into a single step (S2 concealment arrays → S3 purely symbolic). Pending SME confirmation that the single-step scaffolding drop is acceptable; if not, a proactive "show array" button may be needed. See AF3.1 in Working Notes for resolution tracking.

### KDD-4: Simultaneous CRA — Equations Alongside Arrays from S1 (D4)

Per D4 (Simultaneous CRA Connections), equations appear alongside array visuals from the earliest interactions. S1.1's think-aloud displays 3 × 5 = 15 in the Equation Builder AS the Guide reads the array. Not "all arrays first, then equations" — the symbolic representation is always present. Milton et al. (2019) supports simultaneous concrete/abstract pairing over strict sequential CRA.

---

#### Vocabulary & Scope

### KDD-5: Vocabulary After Grounding — Five Terms Introduced in S2, Not S1 (Known Pattern #11)

Formal vocabulary (quotient, dividend, divisor, unknown factor, missing factor) is introduced in S2's naming moment (2.2), AFTER students experience the inverse relationship concretely in S1 (dual array readings) and S2.1 (THE reframe worked example). The Module Mapping's "in context" language could imply vocabulary during S1, but KP#11 requires grounding before naming. Students hear "divide" and "equation" informally in S1; formal terms attach to the insight in S2.

### KDD-6: Interpretation Push — Meaning-Based Options, Not Vocabulary Labels

The S2.3b interpretation check uses meaning-based MC options ("Number of rows," "Number in each row," "Total dots") rather than vocabulary labels ("dividend," "divisor," "quotient"). This keeps interpretation conceptual and avoids drifting into M4's vocabulary identification scope. Per SME: M3 introduces terms for exposure and contextual use, not mastery. Vocabulary labeling assessment is explicitly deferred to M4.

### KDD-7: Fact Family Preview in Synthesis Only — Not Lesson (D13)

"Fact family" is named in Synthesis (S.3) after students build all four equations from one array. It is NOT taught as a strategy in the Lesson. Per D13 (M4/M5 Split): systematic fact family production is M4 content. M3's Lesson focuses on equation PAIRS (one × and one ÷ from the same array). The four-equation family appears only as a culminating discovery in Synthesis, creating a bridge to M4 without encroaching on M4's scope.

---

#### Balance & Constraint

### KDD-8: Both Division Types from Arrays (D2 Enforcement)

D2 requires partitive and quotitive with equal emphasis. S1 delivers both: 1.1-1.2 use partitive readings ("how many per row?"), 1.3 introduces quotitive ("how many rows?"). S2's interpretation check (2.3b) uses meaning-based options that include both readings. Practice tracks the balance; D2 system flag at 45%, hard floor at 40%. The array representation makes both readings natural — same image, different question.

### KDD-9: Nonstandard Format Distribution Across Module (D8)

D8 requires ≥30% nonstandard equation formats starting M3. The Lesson runs below target (~15%: 2.4 and 3.2) to keep S1 cognitive load on the relationship, not format novelty. EC adds 1/3 nonstandard (EC.3). Practice adds ≥30% nonstandard. Module-wide total meets D8 threshold. The progression is intentional: standard formats establish the relationship first (S1-S2), nonstandard formats test relational understanding of = (S2.4, S3.2, EC.3, Practice).

---

#### Scaffolding Design

### KDD-10: 2.3 Value Swap — Familiar Fact for First Guided Practice After Reframe

Interaction 2.3 (first student action after THE reframe and five new vocabulary terms) was changed from 7×8=56 (less-automatic) to 4×8=32 (highly familiar). The interpretive demand in 2.3 is the important cognitive load — "what does ? represent?" shouldn't compete with arithmetic recall. Less-automatic facts (7×8, 6×9, 7×9) are reserved for S2.4 and S3 where the inverse-relationship skill is more established.

### KDD-11: PE5.4 Confirmation Gate Before Scaffold Drop (2.5)

Interaction 2.5 is a metacognitive MC testing conceptual understanding of WHY × and ÷ share numbers. It serves as the scaffold-release gate: students who select the conceptual answer ("dividing IS finding the missing factor") demonstrate readiness for S3's purely symbolic work. Students who select "flip the numbers" reveal procedural pattern-matching (U4.3) and receive targeted remediation BEFORE scaffolding drops. This is not an interaction about doing math — it's an interaction about understanding math.

### KDD-12: 2.3 Sub-Numbered as 2.3a/2.3b (Template Multi-Step Compliance)

Interaction 2.3 contains two distinct student actions (drag tile to find unknown factor, then MC to interpret what ? represents). Per Template §Multi-Step Interactions, this is sub-numbered as 2.3a and 2.3b with per-sub-part fields. The two actions test different skills (computation vs. interpretation) and must be independently remediable.
