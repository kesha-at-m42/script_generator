# MODULE 1: WHAT IS DIVISION? (PARTITIVE & QUOTITIVE)

**Version:** 2026-04-07

# BACKBONE

---

## 1.0 THE ONE THING

[REQUIRED]

Division means finding unknown group information from a total — and there are two types: finding how many in each group (partitive/sharing) or finding how many groups (quotitive/grouping). Both are equally valid ways to divide.

**CRA Stage:** Concrete — M1 stays fully concrete per SME directive. Drawings and symbolic notation deferred to M2.

**Critical Misconception:** U4.1 — "Division always makes things smaller." Students overgeneralize from whole-number experience and form a rigid association between division and reducing. This belief persists into middle school if not addressed early.

**Success Indicator:** Given a word problem, the student can identify whether they know the number of groups or the number of items in each group, observe the appropriate Equal Groups animation, and state the quotient for both partitive and quotitive contexts.

**Biggest Risk:** Partitive dominance — if sharing problems feel more natural and quotitive problems feel "harder" or "different," students form a lopsided understanding of division that compromises later work with measurement contexts and fraction division.

**Component Balance:** CONC 70% / PROC 20% / TRANS 10% — Heavy conceptual/concrete per SME: "most of this module needs to stay concrete." Building meaning of division through hands-on experience with Equal Groups tool.

**Cognitive Focus:** IDENTIFY (recognizing both division types in context), COMPARE (distinguishing partitive from quotitive), CREATE (building equal groups representations), CONNECT (linking division to Unit 1 equal groups knowledge)

**Expected Struggles:** (1) Students who lack equal-groups understanding from Units 1–2 will struggle immediately — the warm-up reactivation is essential. (2) Quotitive problems ("how many groups of 4?") are less familiar than sharing — expect lower initial success rates on Mid activities. (3) Students may default to addition/subtraction strategies instead of grouping.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

[REQUIRED]

### 1.4.1 #U4.1: Division Always Makes Things Smaller (PRIMARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student refuses to accept that dividing can result in the same number (e.g., 6 ÷ 1 = 6). May choose subtraction instead of division in word problems because "division makes things smaller and subtraction also makes things smaller." May verbalize: "You can't divide something and get the same number."

**Why It Happens:** Students overgeneralize from whole-number experience where dividing by numbers >1 always yields smaller results. Fischbein's Intuitive Models Theory (1985): students form rigid associations between division and "making smaller." This becomes catastrophic when students encounter fraction division in later grades.

**Visual Cue:** The concrete dealing/measuring animations show division as *organizing into groups*, not *reducing*. The total number of items is always preserved — they just end up arranged differently. The Equal Groups display shows all items still present after division.

**Prevention Strategy:** Frame division as "organizing" and "finding out about groups," never as "making smaller." Use precise language: "Division tells us the size of groups or the number of groups." Avoid any phrasing that reinforces a "smaller" association. Include contexts where the quotient equals one factor of a familiar multiplication fact — the result feels natural, not "smaller." All Guide dialogue checked for "smaller/less/reduce" language — these words do not appear in division contexts.

> **Starter Pack Note (U4.1 Intervention):** If a student signals "division makes smaller" thinking (e.g., rejecting a correct answer, choosing subtraction), the Guide addresses it directly as a teaching moment — not via MC assessment. Per SME: the Guide acknowledges what the student observed and reframes: "You started with 15 items. After dividing, there are still 15 items — they're just in 3 groups now. Division organizes; it doesn't take any away." This is a callout, not a remediation script — the Pipeline handles follow-up.

---

### 1.4.2 #U4.2: Partitive/Quotitive Confusion (PRIMARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student solves "Share 12 among 3" correctly (4 each) but cannot solve "How many groups of 3 are in 12?" — or vice versa. Draws sharing diagrams for all problems regardless of type. When given a quotitive problem, tries to reinterpret it as sharing.

**Why It Happens:** Instruction overwhelmingly emphasizes "sharing" language (Burtscher & Gaidoschik, 2023: 77% partitive dominance by Grade 4). Students who can only interpret division as sharing struggle with measurement contexts, repeated subtraction, and fraction division.

**Visual Cue:** Distinct visual treatments for each type: partitive shows empty containers from the start (dealing into known containers); quotitive shows the group-size reference with containers forming during measurement (no containers at start). The process looks different even when the end state is identical.

**Prevention Strategy:** Both types taught from Day 1 with equal emphasis (D2). System tracks partitive vs. quotitive exposure balance — target 50-50, flag at 45%, hard floor at 40%. The structural framing question ("Do you know the number of groups, or the number of items in each group?") forces students to analyze what information they have rather than defaulting to a single pathway. Late activities use paired contrast with the same numbers to make both types feel equally natural.

---

### 1.4.3 #U4.4: Interpreting Remainder in Context (MONITOR)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student encounters a problem where their grouping doesn't come out evenly and becomes confused or reports an incorrect quotient. May discard leftover items or try to force even division.

**Why It Happens:** Lack of experience with non-even division. M1 problems all divide evenly, but students may make stepper errors or encounter near-miss situations.

**Visual Cue:** Not actively addressed in M1 visuals — all problems are designed to divide evenly.

**Prevention Strategy:** MONITOR only. If remainder situations surface naturally (e.g., student error creates an uneven grouping), address conversationally: "Sometimes numbers don't divide evenly — there are some left over. We'll learn more about that later." Do not formalize remainder notation or concepts. Remainder instruction is Grade 4 content.

### Partitive Animation (Mode 3) Specifications

**Setup:** Ungrouped items displayed alongside known number of empty containers.
**Animation — Round-robin (first exposure):** Items deal one-at-a-time into containers in rotation. Guide narrates count. Student observes the dealing process BEFORE being asked the result.
**Animation — Batched (subsequent):** System may deal items in batches once the round-robin pattern is established.
**Question timing:** Question comes AFTER the process completes: "How many ended up in each [container]?"
**Container types:** Early/Mid: bags, boxes. Late/EC: circles.
**Item types:** Early/Mid: pictures (apples, cookies, stars). Late/EC: dots.

### Quotitive Animation (Mode 4) Specifications

**Setup:** Ungrouped items displayed alongside group-size reference. NO containers visible at start — this is the key visual distinction from partitive.

**Stage 1 — Boundary Grouping (first quotitive exposure):**
Items remain in place. Student hits "Next" → boundary forms around first set of items matching group size. Guide narrates: "1 group of [size]." Student hits "Next" → remaining boundaries appear sequentially as Guide counts: "2 groups… 3 groups… [N] groups of [size]." Student answers MC: "How many groups of [size]?" Items never move — group structure is revealed within existing arrangement.

**Stage 2 — Measuring Out (subsequent quotitive problems):**
Items are in a collection. Student hits "Next" → first set of items slides out to form a new group. Guide narrates: "1 group of [size]." Student hits "Next" → remaining groups measure out sequentially. Same two-beat interaction rhythm, different animation — items move to new locations instead of boundaries forming in place.

**Scaffolding logic:** Stage 1 is perceptual (see the structure emerge without movement). Stage 2 is procedural (see the measuring-out action that creates groups). Stage 1 appears on first quotitive exposure; Stage 2 follows within the same lesson.

> **Starter Pack Note:** For the quotitive measuring interaction, consider a card-sort-style pattern where the student actively forms groups from the ungrouped collection rather than observing an animation. See UX card sorting work in progress. This is an engineering/UX consideration — the SP specifies the pedagogical intent; the implementation may vary.

## 1.5.2 Module-Specific: Structural Framing Question

This is not a toy but a pedagogical device that functions as a recurring interaction pattern throughout M1 and the unit.

**The Question:** "Do you know the number of groups, or the number of items in each group?"

**Role:** Primary structural organizer for distinguishing partitive from quotitive division. Introduced in Lesson S1 after first partitive experience. Reinforced in Lesson S2 after first quotitive experience. Applied independently in EC and Synthesis.

**Implementation:**
* Lesson S1: Guide introduces after first partitive problem. "For this problem, you KNEW the groups — 3 bags. You figured out what was IN each group."
* Lesson S2: Guide calls back after first quotitive problem. "THIS time, you knew what was in each group. You figured out HOW MANY groups."
* EC Problem 3: Student applies the question to a word problem via MC, then solves.
* Synthesis: Student applies to text-only word problems. MC options include specific values from the problem (not just "number of groups" / "number in each group") to prevent binary guessing and catch A1 errors.

> **Design Note:** The framing question replaces explicit type labeling (partitive/quotitive) as the assessment mechanism. Students are NEVER asked to categorize by division type name — they identify what information they have and what they need to find. This is per SME resolution in the TVP.

## 1.6 WARMUP (3 minutes)

[REQUIRED]

### Core Purpose

Reactivate equal groups from Unit 1 multiplication and pivot students toward division — the reversed question.

**Key Function:** This Warmup reactivates the equal groups structure students know from Unit 1 multiplication and then creates a conceptual pivot: same structure, reversed question. Instead of "how many total?", students will encounter "what if you START with the total?" This positions the Lesson's first partitive problem as an answer to a question the Warmup raised.

**Why this serves the concept:**

* Activates "groups of" language and equal groups visual structure — the exact representation M1 division builds on
* Establishes the multiplication → division reversal as a natural move, not a new topic disconnected from prior knowledge
* Creates a genuine question in the student's mind ("What happens when we start with the total?") that the Lesson immediately answers

**Test:** If we removed this Warmup and went straight to the Lesson, students would struggle with recognizing division situations as related to equal groups because the connection between "groups of" (multiplication) and "total into groups" (division) would not be activated. Students would encounter the dealing animation without the mental frame that division is the reverse of what they already know.

### Warmup Parameters

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Time** | ~3 minutes |
| **Interactions** | 3 (2 with student action + 1 bridge) |
| **Cognitive Load** | 20-30% |
| **Remediation** | Pipeline |
| **Vocabulary** | NEW terms: forbidden. ESTABLISHED terms from Unit 1: [vocab]equal groups[/vocab], [vocab]group[/vocab] activated for review. |

### Warmup Constraints

| MUST | MUST NOT |
| :---- | :---- |

The Activation type reestablishes the equal groups representation from Unit 1 and creates the pivot that makes the Lesson's first division problem feel like a natural next step. Per Warmup Playbook §4D: Binary Choice / Activation best for Modules 1–3 with concrete activation needs.

### Interaction W.1: Equal Groups Recall [ACTIVATION]

* **Purpose:** Reactivate recognition of equal groups from Unit 1. Students see a familiar multiplication display and identify the total — confirming the "groups of" structure is active.
* **Visual: Equal Groups with Pictures or Dots (Mode 1 — Structural Recognition).** Horizontal. 3 bags with 5 apples each, bags visible, items visible inside bags. No animation. Display-only arrangement.
* **Guide:** "Check this out! Here are some bags of apples. You've seen groups like this before. Let's see if you remember how they work. How many apples are there altogether?"
* **Prompt:** "How many apples altogether?"
* **Student Action:** MC (single)
  * **Options:** 15, 8, 5, 3
* **Correct Answer:** 15
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - 15 = Correct (3 groups of 5 = 15 total)
  - 8 = Adds 3 + 5 (confuses groups and group size with addition)
  - 5 = Reports items per group, not total (U4.2 precursor — focuses on one group)
  - 3 = Reports number of groups, not total
* **On Correct:** "Fifteen apples, 3 bags with 5 in each. You know your [vocab]equal groups[/vocab]."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Engagement Anchors:** (1) Narrative Setup — "bags of apples" connects to familiar sharing/grouping scenarios. (2) Personalization — "You've seen groups like this before" callbacks to Unit 1 experience.

---

### Interaction W.2: Reverse the Question [ACTIVATION]

* **Purpose:** Create the conceptual pivot. Students see the SAME display but the question reverses — instead of "how many total?" they're told the total and asked what information the groups give. This is the bridge from multiplication thinking to division thinking.
* **Visual: Equal Groups with Pictures or Dots (Mode 1 — Structural Recognition).** Horizontal. Same display: 3 bags with 5 apples each. Items visible. No animation.
* **Guide:** "OK, you figured out the total is 15. Now think about this, what if someone TOLD you there were 15 apples and 3 bags? What's left to figure out?"
* **Prompt:** "There are 15 apples and 3 bags. What's left to figure out?"
* **Student Action:** MC (single)
  * **Options:** How many in each bag, How many bags there are, How many apples there are, How many apples to add
* **Correct Answer:** How many in each bag
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - How many in each bag = Correct (you know the total and the number of bags — the unknown is items per bag)
  - How many bags there are = Incorrect (the number of bags is already given as 3)
  - How many apples there are = Incorrect (the total is already given as 15)
  - How many apples to add = Incorrect (addition framing — no apples are being added)
* **On Correct:** "You know the total and the number of bags, so you figure out how many go in each bag. That's a different kind of question."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Scaffolding Note:** W.2 reverses the question without naming it as division. The student experiences the structural shift (known → unknown changes) before any formal vocabulary enters. This is the core activation move — the Lesson will name this as "dividing."

---

### Interaction W.3: Bridge to Lesson [BRIDGE]

* **Purpose:** Create anticipation for the Lesson without teaching. Tease the dealing animation students will see next.
* **Visual: Equal Groups with Pictures or Dots (Mode 1 — Structural Recognition).** Same display: 3 bags with 5 apples each. Items visible.
* **Guide:** "You just figured out what you'd need to find, how many go in each bag. But what if you couldn't SEE inside the bags yet? What if the apples started out loose and you had to watch them get sorted? That's exactly what you're about to see."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** The bridge creates a question ("What does it look like when items get sorted into bags?") that the Lesson's first partitive dealing animation immediately answers. No division vocabulary is used.

## 1.7 LESSON (10-12 minutes)

[REQUIRED]

### Core Purpose

Build understanding that division means finding unknown group information from a total, and that there are two types: partitive (sharing into known groups) and quotitive (measuring into groups of known size). Students experience both concretely through Equal Groups animations, learn core vocabulary after visual grounding, and discover that the same numbers can be divided two different ways.

### Pedagogical Flow

Students progress through three sections, each introducing a distinct cognitive move while maintaining concrete visuals throughout:

**Section 1 (Partitive):** Students watch items deal into bags, identify how many per bag, and learn that this is called "dividing." Vocabulary attaches to what they just saw. The structural framing question is introduced: "Did you know the number of [vocab]groups[/vocab], or the number of items in each [vocab]group[/vocab]?"

**Section 2 (Quotitive):** Students watch items group by size, identify how many groups form, and learn the second division type. The framing question returns: same structure, different answer. "Partition" is introduced as a general term.

**Section 3 (Contrast + Construction):** Visuals shift to circles/dots. Students see the same total divided both ways in a paired contrast, then build their own division by choosing the setup. The relational insight — same numbers, two questions — is made explicit.

### Lesson Structure

| Section | Focus | Time |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1: Partitive Introduction | Dealing animation, identify quotient, vocabulary introduction | 3-4 min |
| Section 2: Quotitive Introduction | Boundary grouping/measuring animation, identify quotient, partition vocabulary | 3-4 min |
| Section 3: Abstraction + Contrast + Construction | Circles/dots, paired contrast, student construction | 4-5 min |

### 1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Partitive Introduction

### Interaction 1.1: Watch the Sharing [WORKED EXAMPLE]

* **Purpose:** First concrete experience of partitive division. Guide models think-aloud while the dealing animation plays, making visible what to attend to. Students observe the full process before being asked anything.
* **Visual: Equal Groups with Pictures or Dots (Mode 3 — Partitive).** Horizontal. 12 apples displayed loose. 4 empty bags visible. Round-robin dealing animation plays (~20-25 sec): apples deal one-at-a-time into bags in rotation until all 12 are distributed. Post-animation state: 4 bags with 3 apples each. Student observes during animation.
* **Guide (Think-Aloud):** "Let's see what happens. We've got 12 apples, and they need to go into 4 bags, the same number in each bag. Watch... [ATTENTION] I'm going to count how many end up in each bag as they go. One in the first bag, one in the second, one in the third, one in the fourth, that's one round. [SELF-CHECK] Let me keep watching... another round, that's 2 in each. One more round, that's 3 in each bag. [CONCLUSION] They're all sorted now. Every bag got 3 apples."
* **No student action.** Guide proceeds to 1.1b.

> **Design Note (Pattern 4 — Think-Aloud):** This is the first division experience. The Guide models the cognitive process of tracking the dealing animation: what to attend to (items per bag), how to count (per round), and how to state the result. Metacognitive tags are authoring-only annotations — strip before publishing. This think-aloud serves the SF2.3 pedagogy finding: Grade 3 students need explicit narration of what to observe during animations.

### Interaction 1.1b: Name the Result [GUIDED PRACTICE]

* **Purpose:** Student identifies the quotient from the animation they just watched. First assessed response in the Lesson.
* **Visual: Equal Groups with Pictures or Dots (Mode 3 — Partitive).** Post-animation state: 4 bags with 3 apples each visible.
* **Guide:** "You saw 12 apples go into 4 bags. How many ended up in each bag?"
* **Prompt:** "How many apples are in each bag?"
* **Student Action:** MC (single)
  * **Options:** 3, 4, 12, 8
* **Correct Answer:** 3
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - 3 = Correct (12 dealt into 4 bags = 3 per bag)
  - 4 = Reports number of bags, not items per bag (U4.2 precursor — confuses group count with group size)
  - 12 = Reports the total, not the per-group result
  - 8 = 12 - 4 (subtraction error — treats as take-away instead of grouping)
* **On Correct:** "Three apples in each bag. Twelve apples sorted into 4 [vocab]equal groups[/vocab] of 3."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

### 1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Quotitive Introduction

### Interaction 2.1: A Different Kind of Question [WORKED EXAMPLE]

* **Purpose:** First concrete experience of quotitive division. This is the key pedagogical moment — students discover a SECOND way to divide using the same equal groups structure. Guide models think-aloud for the boundary grouping animation (Stage 1), making the new process visible. Addresses PE3.1 finding: quotitive Problem 1 = Stage 1 with guide-narrated scaffolding.
* **Visual: Equal Groups with Pictures or Dots (Mode 4 — Quotitive).** Horizontal. 12 apples displayed in a loose collection. Group-size reference visible: "groups of 4." NO empty containers visible at start — this is the key visual distinction from partitive. Interactive boundary grouping animation (~25-30 sec): student hits "Next" → boundary forms around next 4 apples; Guide narrates; repeat until all groups bounded. Post-animation state: 3 bounded groups of 4 apples each.
* **Guide (Think-Aloud):** "Now here's something different. Same 12 apples, but this time, you know each [vocab]group[/vocab] has 4. The question is: how many [vocab]groups[/vocab] can you make? [ATTENTION] Watch. I'm going to look for groups of 4. Press Next to see the first [vocab]group[/vocab]."
* *(Student hits Next)*
* **Guide:** "[ATTENTION] There, 1 [vocab]group[/vocab] of 4. Let's find the next one."
* *(Student hits Next)*
* **Guide:** "2 [vocab]groups[/vocab] of 4. Any more?"
* *(Student hits Next)*
* **Guide:** "[CONCLUSION] 3 [vocab]groups[/vocab] of 4. All 12 apples are grouped."
* **No student action.** Guide proceeds to 2.1b.

> **Design Note (Quotitive Stage 1):** Boundary grouping is perceptual scaffolding — items don't move, groups are revealed within the existing arrangement. ### Interaction 3.2: Same Numbers, Two Ways [CONCEPTUAL CHECK]

* **Purpose:** Paired contrast — the relational bridge. Students see the SAME total divided both ways within a single interaction. This is where the "two types" insight becomes concrete and visible. Addresses PE5.2: both animations displayed sequentially in one interaction for direct comparison.
* **Visual: Equal Groups with Pictures or Dots (Mode 3 — Partitive).** Two-phase animation (~35-40 sec total). **Phase A** (~15 sec): 20 dots displayed loose with 4 empty circles visible; round-robin dealing plays until 4 circles with 5 dots each. Display holds. **Phase B** (~15 sec after ~5 sec transition): screen splits — left retains partitive end state; right shows 20 new loose dots with "groups of 5" reference; quotitive measuring-out animation plays → 4 groups of 5 dots. Both end states visible simultaneously on split screen. Student observes both animations, then answers MC.
* **Guide:** "Here's something interesting. We're [vocab]dividing[/vocab] 20 dots two different ways. First: 20 dots into 4 circles, [vocab]sharing[/vocab] equally."
* *(Partitive animation plays: 20 dealt into 4 circles = 5 each)*
* **Guide:** "5 in each circle. Now let's try the other way, same 20 dots, but this time [vocab]groups[/vocab] of 5."
* *(Quotitive animation plays on right: 20 measured into groups of 5 = 4 groups)*
* **Guide:** "Look at both sides. Same 20 dots. On the left, you [vocab]shared[/vocab] into 4 [vocab]groups[/vocab] and got 5 in each. On the right, you made [vocab]groups[/vocab] of 5 and got 4 [vocab]groups[/vocab]. Same numbers, different question, different process. Both are [vocab]division[/vocab]."
* **Prompt:** "Both divisions used 20 dots. What was different?"
* **Student Action:** MC (single)
  * **Options:** What you already knew and what you figured out, The number of dots, Whether the dots were divided, The color of the circles
* **Correct Answer:** What you already knew and what you figured out
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - What you already knew and what you figured out = Correct (partitive: knew groups, found group size; quotitive: knew group size, found group count)
  - The number of dots = Incorrect (both used 20)
  - Whether the dots were divided = Incorrect (both were divided)
  - The color of the circles = Incorrect (distractor — visual feature, not mathematical)
* **On Correct:** "That's the key, in both cases you [vocab]divided[/vocab] 20 dots into [vocab]equal groups[/vocab]. The difference is what information you started with and what you figured out. That's what makes them two kinds of [vocab]division[/vocab]."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note (Relational bridge):** This interaction addresses the pedagogy-eval PE5.2 finding. Both animations play within a single interaction, with both end states visible simultaneously on a split screen. This ensures the comparison is visual and immediate — not sequential across separate interactions where the first state would be lost from working memory. * ❌ "Division makes things smaller" — directly reinforces U4.1
* ❌ "When you divide, you get less" — U4.1 reinforcement

* ❌ "The answer is always smaller than what you started with" — U4.1
* ❌ "This is the sharing kind" / "This is the grouping kind" — students should NOT categorize by type label; use framing question instead
* ❌ "Partitive" / "quotitive" as student-facing terms — adult math terminology, not Grade 3
* ❌ "dividend" / "divisor" — formal terms deferred to M2/M3
* ❌ "How many are left over?" — implies remainders, which are out of scope for M1
* ❌ "You need to figure out..." without grounding in visual — instruction must follow observation

### Misconception Prevention

**#U4.1 (Division Always Makes Things Smaller):**

* All Guide dialogue frames division as "organizing into groups" and "finding out about groups," never as "making smaller" or "reducing"
* The Equal Groups animations show ALL items still present after division — they just end up in groups. Guide language reinforces: "All [N] items are sorted into groups" (Interactions 1.1, 1.3, 1.4)
* The On Correct pattern consistently states the full equation: "You divided [total] into [groups] equal groups of [size]" — the total is always named first, reinforcing that nothing was removed
* No "smaller/less/reduce" words appear in any division context throughout the Lesson

**#U4.2 (Partitive/Quotitive Confusion):**

* Both types taught within the same Lesson with equal emphasis (Section 1 = 3 partitive, Section 2 = 3 quotitive + vocabulary)
* Distinct visual setups: partitive has containers from the start (known groups); quotitive has NO containers (known group size, containers emerge)
* Structural framing question introduced in 2.3 as the connector: "Do you know the number of groups, or the number of items in each group?"
* Paired contrast (3.2, 3.3) makes both types visible simultaneously with the same total — the structural difference becomes undeniable
* MC distractors consistently include the U4.2 confusion pattern (reporting group count when asked for group size, or vice versa) so the Pipeline can detect and address it

**#U4.4 (Interpreting Remainder in Context):**

* MONITOR only. All problems divide evenly by design (Data Constraints). If remainder surfaces through student error, address conversationally per §1.4.3.

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS SUMMARY

[REQUIRED]

1. **Both division types from Day 1 with 50-50 balance target.** Partitive and quotitive are taught within the same module with equal emphasis, not sequentially across modules. The SME directive (D2) requires system tracking of partitive/quotitive balance — flag at 45%, hard floor at 40% for either type. This prevents the common curriculum error of treating partitive as "real division" and quotitive as secondary, which compromises later measurement and fraction division work.

2. **Structural framing question as the primary organizer.** "Do you know the number of groups, or the number of items in each group?" is introduced after the first partitive experience, reinforced after the first quotitive experience, and applied independently in EC and Synthesis. This question replaces type labels (students never hear "partitive" or "quotitive") and gives students a reusable thinking tool that transfers to text-only problems.

3. **Vocabulary introduced AFTER concrete experience (Known Pattern #11).** Students observe the dealing animation (1.1) and identify the result (1.1b) before hearing "divide" and "division" (1.2). The sequence is intentional: experience → identify → name. This ensures the formal term maps to a felt understanding rather than an abstract definition. *Flag for SME review.*

4. **Concrete-only CRA stage per SME directive (D4).** M1 stays fully concrete — objects dealt into containers (Early/Mid) and dots sorted into circles (Late). No drawings, no ÷ symbol, no formal equations. The visual abstraction step (bags → circles) happens within the concrete stage. Representational and symbolic work is deferred to M2.

5. **Quotitive two-stage introduction.** Quotitive division uses a two-stage animation sequence: Stage 1 (Boundary Grouping) is perceptual — group boundaries appear around stationary items, making structure visible. Stage 2 (Measuring Out) is procedural — items slide out to form new groups. Stage 1 appears once (2.1) as an introductory scaffold; all subsequent quotitive interactions use Stage 2. This mirrors the cognitive progression from "seeing" structure to "doing" measurement.

6. **Sequential paired-contrast animations, not simultaneous.** Section 3's paired contrast plays partitive and quotitive animations one after the other with the same total, then displays both end-states simultaneously for comparison. Sequential-then-compare was chosen over synchronized side-by-side to manage Grade 3 cognitive load — watching two animations simultaneously creates a split-attention problem. The learning happens in the comparison of completed states, not in watching two processes at once.

7. **S3 construction MC uses extended divisor range (2-10).** The all-correct MC in 3.4 (dividend 20) requires four options that all divide evenly. Only two divisors within {3, 4, 5} divide 20 evenly (4 and 5), so the range is extended to include 2 and 10 for this interaction only. This is a deliberate constraint relaxation documented in §1.5 to enable the all-correct design pattern while keeping the student experience of genuine choice.

8. **EC and Practice quotitive-weighted to correct Lesson balance.** The Lesson runs ~59% partitive / ~41% quotitive due to the structure of the three-section design (partitive introduction first, then quotitive, then paired contrast that includes both). EC is designed 33/67 partitive/quotitive, and Practice distribution targets 50-50 within S1/S2, to pull the overall module exposure toward the 50-50 target.

9. **Framing question MC distractors use specific problem values.** In EC.3 and Synthesis S.2, the MC options for the framing question include concrete values from the problem text (e.g., "4 cups," "6 in each cup") rather than abstract category labels ("number of groups," "number in each group"). This prevents binary guessing and catches U4.2 confusion — students must parse the problem text to answer, not just pick between two abstract categories. Per §1.5.2 and TVP Synthesis specification.

10. **No "reports total" distractor evolution planned for M1.** The "reports total" MC distractor pattern (offering the dividend as a wrong answer) is used consistently across Lesson and EC. In M1, this serves a diagnostic purpose: it catches students who watched the animation but didn't process the grouping result. In later modules, this distractor should be replaced with more targeted misconception options (see Working Notes — Author Observations).

11. **Warmup pivot is implicit by design.** The conceptual shift from W.1 ("what do you see?") to W.2 ("what's left to figure out?") uses the same visual with a different question type, without an explicit Guide bridging statement. This preserves the discovery flow — the shift in question type IS the pedagogical move at Grade 3. A writer adapting this module may add an explicit bridge if classroom observation suggests students miss the pivot.

12. **Think-aloud models in 1.1 and 2.1 use metacognitive tags for authoring clarity.** Tags ([PLANNING], [ATTENTION], [SELF-CHECK], [CONCLUSION]) structure the think-aloud narration during drafting but must be stripped before publishing. They are authoring-only — the published Guide speech reads as natural narration without visible tags.

13. **Animation specifications use Visual-field format, not Pattern 3 Specification Tables.** Template v3 defines a Pattern 3 Specification Table (with System Action, Duration, Student Role, Visual State Change columns) for system-driven activities. However, established SP convention folds animation details directly into the Visual field (animation type, duration, post-animation state) — this is consistent with EC interactions and all prior SPs. The spec table format was stripped from all 10 Lesson animations and replaced with enriched Visual fields. No information was lost; animation type, duration, student role, and state transitions are all captured inline.
