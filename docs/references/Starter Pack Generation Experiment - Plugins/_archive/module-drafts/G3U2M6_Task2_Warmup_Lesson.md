# §1.6 WARMUP (3-5 minutes)

### Core Purpose

Activate M5 knowledge of standard square units (square inches, square centimeters) and prime students for the idea that BIGGER areas need BIGGER units — without formally teaching square feet or square meters.

**Key Function:** The Warmup does three things: (1) confirms M5 prerequisite transfer — students recognize that different-sized squares measure area differently, (2) plants the seed that the two units they know might not be enough for everything, and (3) primes the inverse relationship from M5 ("bigger square covers more space, so you need fewer") without formally teaching it. The visual comparison of three 1×1 squares (sq cm, sq in, sq ft) and the text reference to sq m create a "there's more to discover" moment that positions the Lesson to formally introduce the new units with naming and measurement.

**Why this serves the concept:** M6's core challenge is unit-selection reasoning — knowing WHEN to use which unit. Students arriving from M5 know only two standard units. If they enter the Lesson without experiencing the size contrast between small and large units, the "too many" moment in Mid Lesson loses impact. The Warmup's side-by-side comparison builds intuitive size awareness (sq cm is tiny, sq ft is much bigger) so that when the Lesson shows a dense cm grid vs. a clean ft grid, students already have a visual reference for WHY the contrast is dramatic. The text note on sq m ("even BIGGER than a square foot") extends the comparison without requiring true-to-scale display, priming students for the full four-unit system. The inverse relationship nod in W.2 ("the bigger each square is, the fewer you'd need") connects to M5's core insight and directly prepares for L.8's "too many" payoff.

**Test:** "If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson?" **YES.** Without the side-by-side size comparison, students enter the Lesson with no visual reference for relative unit sizes. The "too many" moment in Mid Lesson depends on students already having an intuitive sense that square centimeters are MUCH smaller than square feet. Without this priming, the dense-vs-clean grid contrast feels arbitrary rather than revelatory. Additionally, without the M5 callback, there's no diagnostic check that students retained the "same shape + different unit = different number" principle from M5.

### Warmup Type Rationale

TVP specifies a three-way size comparison warmup: "Grid Rectangles (1×1, side-by-side — three unit types: sq cm, sq in, sq ft)" with a text note on sq m. This is closest to a **Notice & Wonder** warmup (§4D.4 — "Open exploration, question generation, pattern discovery") combined with a **Binary Choice** element (§4D.1 — "Quick comparison, attribute focus"). The Notice & Wonder structure fits because students observe three squares and notice size differences; the Binary Choice element appears in the judgment task (identifying which is biggest/smallest). Module 4-7 range aligns with the Playbook recommendation for Notice & Wonder and WODB types. The TVP's specified side-by-side comparison structure takes precedence over a standard named type.

### Interaction W.1: Three Squares [Type C] [ACTIVATION]

* **Purpose:** M5 callback + size comparison. Students see three 1×1 unit squares (sq cm, sq in, sq ft) side-by-side and identify which is the biggest. This activates M5's knowledge of sq in/cm and introduces sq ft visually without formal definition. Engagement Anchor #1: Personalization ("You've worked with two of these before...").
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Warmup config).** Three 1×1 squares displayed side-by-side, all on screen simultaneously. Left: 1 cm × 1 cm (labeled "square centimeter"). Center: 1 in × 1 in (labeled "square inch"). Right: 1 ft × 1 ft (labeled "square foot"). Screen limitation acknowledgment text visible: "These squares are shrunk to fit on our screen. A REAL square foot is about the size of a floor tile — much bigger than what you see here!"
* **Guide:** "You've worked with two of these squares before — the square centimeter and the square inch. But check out this third one. It's called a square foot. Look at all three. Which one takes up the most space?"
* **Prompt:** "Which square is the biggest? Tap it."
* **Student Action:** Tap to select
* **Correct Answer:** Square foot (right square)
* **On Correct:** "That's the square foot — it's a lot bigger than the other two. A real one is about the size of a floor tile."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** W.1 uses Personalization as Engagement Anchor #1 — the callback to M5 ("You've worked with two of these before") connects to the student's prior learning. The tap-to-select interaction is low-cognitive-load (20-30%) — students compare sizes visually, not mathematically. The screen limitation acknowledgment is load-bearing per TVP and §1.5: students must understand the display is not true-to-scale. The Guide does NOT define "square foot" formally (no "a square with sides of 1 foot") — that's Lesson S1 territory. The label and visual comparison are sufficient for Warmup.

> **Voice Note:** "Check out this third one" is invitational and curiosity-building (Medium-High energy per Warmup standard). "It's called a square foot" is a label, not a formal definition — the naming convention teaching ("same pattern as square inch") happens in Early Lesson. Observable acknowledgment: "That's the square foot" (references what system saw the student select).

### Interaction W.2: Even Bigger [Type C] [CONCEPTUAL CHECK]

* **Purpose:** Judgment/Noticing task. Extend size comparison to include sq m (via text only — not displayed to scale). Students think about size ordering of all four units. Engagement Anchor #2: Narrative Setup ("Imagine a square so big it wouldn't even fit on the screen...").
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Warmup config).** Same three squares from W.1 remain visible. NEW: Text annotation appears: "A square meter is even BIGGER than a square foot." Optional: arrow or indicator showing sq m is off-scale.
* **Guide:** "Here's something wild — there's actually a square that's even BIGGER than the square foot. It's called a square meter. It's so big it wouldn't even fit on our screen! If you lined up all four squares from smallest to biggest, which one would go first?"
* **Prompt:** "Which square unit is the smallest? Tap it."
* **Student Action:** Tap to select
* **Correct Answer:** Square centimeter (left square)
* **On Correct:** "The square centimeter — the tiniest one. Then square inch, then square foot, then square meter. Four sizes, smallest to biggest. And the bigger each square is, the fewer you'd need to cover the same area."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** W.2 is the required judgment/noticing task (WR-5). It requires actual thinking — the student must process the size ordering of four units, including one (sq m) that's only described in text, not shown. This is Engagement Anchor #2: Narrative Setup — "so big it wouldn't even fit on our screen" creates genuine wonder about the relative scale. The four-unit ordering (sq cm < sq in < sq ft < sq m) is primed here and assessed formally in Synthesis (drag-to-rank). The interaction type is MC/tap selection — low cognitive load because the visual scaffolding is present for three of the four units. The "smallest" question reverses the W.1 direction (W.1 asked "biggest") to ensure students are processing size in both directions. **Strengthen #4 (Inverse Relationship):** The On Correct's closing line ("And the bigger each square is, the fewer you'd need to cover the same area") is a brief, factual prime of M5's inverse relationship. It does not formally teach the principle — it states an observable implication of the size ordering the student just confirmed. This primes the "too many" moment in S2 (L.7-L.8): students arrive already expecting that bigger squares mean fewer needed.

> **Voice Note:** "Here's something wild" is Warmup-appropriate energy (curious, invitational). "It wouldn't even fit on our screen" is factual (sq m is ~10.8 sq ft) and creates genuine wonder. The four-unit listing in On Correct ("Then square inch, then square foot, then square meter") is a rapid anchor — not formal teaching, just a verbal ordering that students will encounter again in Lesson and Synthesis. The inverse-relationship line is stated matter-of-factly — no emphasis, no exclamation — consistent with a prime rather than a teach.

### Interaction W.3: Bridge to Lesson [Type A] [BRIDGE]

* **Purpose:** Create anticipation for the Lesson. Plant the question: "Which unit would you USE for different things?" — the core question M6's Lesson answers.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Warmup config).** Same display as W.2 (three squares + sq m text note).
* **Guide:** "So you know four sizes of square units now — from tiny to really big. But here's the question: if you wanted to measure a playground, would you use little square centimeters or big square feet? And what about a postcard? Let's figure out when to use which one."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** The bridge plants the unit-selection question without answering it. "Would you use little square centimeters or big square feet?" is a rhetorical framing that makes students curious about the answer — the Lesson's "too many" moment provides the payoff. The bridge does NOT teach unit-selection reasoning; it creates the motivating question. Per the Playbook's Bridge Quality Guide: this creates a question in the student's mind ("When do I use which unit?") that the Purpose Frame and Lesson answer.

> **Voice Note:** "Here's the question" is a natural curiosity marker. The playground/postcard examples are concrete, age-appropriate anchors that will recur in Mid Lesson MC items. Energy transitions from Medium-High (Warmup) toward Medium (Lesson entry).

## Core Purpose + Pedagogical Flow

M6 extends M5's standard-unit procedure (grid counting → multiply dimensions → state area) to two new units (square feet, square meters) and adds a new cognitive demand: unit-selection reasoning. The Lesson moves from familiar procedure (counting grids in new units) through a pivotal insight ("too many" — why larger units are needed) to independent application (choosing the right unit for a context).

> **KDD: M6 CRA Stage.** The Backbone (§1.0) identifies M6's CRA stage as Representational — the last full-grid module. M6 does not follow the standard Concrete → Relational → Abstract → Application sequence cleanly because students arrive with established grid-counting procedure from M5. The "Concrete" work is not building a new concept from physical objects; it's extending an existing Representational skill to new units. The Relational phase (the "too many" comparison) is the core conceptual move. The Abstract phase formalizes the unit-selection reasoning. Application is independent context-based selection. The mapping below reflects what M6 actually does.

| CRA Stage | What Students Actually Do | Interactions |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Representational (Extending)** | Students count grids in sq ft and sq m — same procedure as M5 with sq in/cm, new units. Grid counting is familiar; the new element is the unit label. Naming convention is explicitly stated: "Same pattern — a square with sides of 1 foot, so it's called a square foot." TVP specifies "2-3 problems sq ft, 1-2 sq m" — met with L.2-L.3 (sq ft practice) and L.5 (sq m practice). | PF, L.1-L.6 |
| **Relational** | Same rectangle shown in dense cm grid vs. clean ft grid. Students see dramatic visual contrast (thousands of tiny squares vs. 12 large squares). Guide explicitly states: "Same rectangle, same area — but WAY fewer squares when we use square feet." This is the pivotal "too many" insight. Easy/Hard MC precedes the ft-grid reveal. | L.7-L.8 |
| **Abstract** | Unit-selection reasoning modeled and formalized. Guide demonstrates reasoning for a specific context (L.9, partial worked example), then students answer "Which unit would you use for [context]?" with MC options that include both unit AND reasoning (L.10-L.11). Tier 1 (obvious context), Tier 2 (customary vs. metric). Vocabulary "appropriate unit" introduced via modeled reasoning. | L.9-L.11 |
| **Application** | Independent unit selection. Single unit per problem, context clues guide selection. No scaffolded options — student selects unit and calculates area. All four units in play. Includes both large contexts (sq ft, sq m) and small contexts (sq in). | L.12-L.14 |

## Interaction PF: Purpose Frame [Type A]

* **Purpose:** Orient students to what they'll learn (larger square units) and why it matters (some areas are too big for small units). Connects backward to M5 (sq in/cm) and forward to M6's core insight.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** No specific rectangle yet — blank or title card.
* **Guide:** "You've been measuring areas with square inches and square centimeters. Those work great for small things. But what about a classroom floor or a playground? You'd need SO many tiny squares. You're going to learn about two BIGGER square units that make measuring big areas way easier."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** The Purpose Frame uses only vocabulary students already know (square inches, square centimeters, area, measuring). "Two BIGGER square units" describes what they'll learn to DO without naming the formal terms yet. Connects backward (M5 units) and forward (bigger units for bigger areas). Satisfies §1A formula: [what they know] + [what they'll learn to DO] + [why it's useful].

> **Voice Note:** "SO many tiny squares" is emphatic without being overenthusiastic — it's factual foreshadowing of the "too many" moment. Contractions throughout ("You've been," "You'd need," "You're going to").

### Interaction L.1: First Square Foot Rectangle [Type C] [WORKED EXAMPLE]

* **Purpose:** Full worked example. Guide demonstrates grid counting with square feet using think-aloud protocol. Students observe the procedure (same as M5) applied to a new unit. Example-problem pair: L.1 (Guide demo) → L.2 (student tries similar).
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** 3 ft × 4 ft rectangle. Full grid visible (12 squares). Rows and columns clearly labeled. Unit label: "square feet."
* **Guide:** "[PLANNING] Let me show you how this works with square feet. First, I need to find the area — how much space this rectangle covers. [ATTENTION] I'm going to look at the rows and columns. I count 3 rows, and 4 in each row. [ACTION] 3 times 4 equals 12. [SELF-CHECK] Let me check — 3 rows of 4... 4, 8, 12. Yes, 12. So the area is 12 square feet. See the pattern? A square foot is a square with sides of 1 foot — same naming idea as square inch."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** This is the first formal introduction of "square foot" with the naming convention explicitly stated: "a square with sides of 1 foot — same naming idea as square inch." This references M5's naming convention for sq in, making the extension feel natural rather than arbitrary. The think-aloud follows the required [PLANNING], [ATTENTION], [SELF-CHECK] structure. The Guide does both skip-counting (4, 8, 12) and multiplication (3 × 4 = 12) to reinforce both strategies. The 3×4=12 rectangle is chosen for manageable grid size and familiar factors.

> **Voice Note:** "Let me show you how this works" is warm and instructional (Type C energy). "See the pattern?" creates the naming-convention connection without being didactic. Contractions: "Let me," "I'm going to."

### Interaction L.2: Your Turn — Square Feet (1) [Type B] [PRACTICE]

* **Purpose:** Example-problem pair follow-up to L.1. Student applies grid counting with square feet to a similar rectangle. Fading stage: student acts independently after full worked example.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** 5 ft × 3 ft rectangle. Full grid visible (15 squares). Unit label: "square feet."
* **Guide:** "Your turn. Here's another rectangle measured in square feet. Count the rows and columns, then multiply. What's the area in square feet?"
* **Prompt:** "Find the area. How many square feet?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** 8, 12, 15, 18
* **Correct Answer:** 15
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 15 = Correct (5 rows × 3 columns = 15 square feet)
  * 8 = Added dimensions (5 + 3) instead of multiplying
  * 12 = Confused with L.1's answer (carryover error)
  * 18 = Miscounted rows as 6 (off-by-one)
* **On Correct:** "15 square feet. 5 rows of 3 — you multiplied to find the area."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** L.2's MC distractors are diagnostic: 8 (additive error), 12 (carryover from L.1), 18 (counting error). The rectangle 5×3 is intentionally different from L.1's 3×4 — different factors, different product, different orientation — to prevent pattern-matching without reading the grid. Observable acknowledgment: "5 rows of 3 — you multiplied" references both the visual structure and the strategy the student used.

### Interaction L.3: Your Turn — Square Feet (2) [Type B] [PRACTICE]

* **Purpose:** Additional sq ft practice. TVP specifies "2-3 problems sq ft" — this is the second independent sq ft attempt. Ensures procedural fluency with the new unit before transitioning to sq m. Different dimensions from L.1 and L.2 to confirm generalization.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** 4 ft × 5 ft rectangle. Full grid visible (20 squares). Unit label: "square feet."
* **Guide:** "One more with square feet. Count and multiply — what's the area?"
* **Prompt:** "Find the area. How many square feet?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** 9, 15, 20, 25
* **Correct Answer:** 20
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 20 = Correct (4 rows × 5 columns = 20 square feet)
  * 9 = Added dimensions (4 + 5) instead of multiplying
  * 15 = Carryover from L.2
  * 25 = Miscounted as 5×5 (squared one dimension)
* **On Correct:** "20 square feet. 4 rows of 5."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note (Strengthen #2):** L.3 was added to meet TVP's "2-3 problems sq ft" specification. The original draft had only one independent sq ft attempt (L.2) before transitioning to sq m. With L.3, students have two independent sq ft attempts (L.2 and L.3) before encountering sq m in L.4, building procedural confidence with the first new unit. The 4×5=20 rectangle uses factors not yet seen together (4 and 5 haven't appeared as a pair), with a product (20) distinct from L.1 (12) and L.2 (15). The Guide's "One more with square feet" is deliberately brief — Type B minimal teaching, since the procedure is established.

### Interaction L.4: First Square Meter Rectangle [Type C] [WORKED EXAMPLE]

* **Purpose:** Partial worked example. Guide starts the count, student completes. Introduces square meter with the same naming convention. Example-problem pair: L.4 (partial demo) → L.5 (student tries).
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** 2 m × 5 m rectangle. Full grid visible (10 squares). Unit label: "square meters."
* **Guide:** "Now let's try a different unit — the square meter. Same idea: a square with sides of 1 meter. That's even bigger than a square foot! I can see 2 rows. How many are in each row? Count and multiply to find the area in square meters."
* **Prompt:** "Find the area. How many square meters?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** 7, 10, 12, 15

### Interaction L.10: Which Unit — Playground? [Type C] [APPLICATION] (Tier 1)

## Module-Specific Lesson Guidance

### Required Phrases

* "square foot" / "square feet" — formal unit name (S1, first introduced L.1)
* "square meter" / "square meters" — formal unit name (S1, first introduced L.4)
* "a square with sides of 1 foot" — naming convention (L.1 think-aloud)
* "a square with sides of 1 meter" — naming convention (L.4)
* "same naming pattern" / "same naming idea" — connects M6 to M5 convention (L.1, L.6)
* "square inch" / "square centimeter" — reinforced from M5 (Warmup, L.6, L.7, S2-S3)
* "standard unit" — reinforced from M5 (L.6)
* "area" / "dimensions" — reinforced from M4-M5 (throughout)
* "rows" / "columns" / "multiply" — reinforced from M3-M5 (throughout)
* "appropriate unit" — assessment vocabulary (L.9, first use; L.10-L.14)
* "too many" — key reasoning phrase (L.7-L.8)
* "same rectangle, same area" — inverse relationship language (L.8)
* "bigger unit, fewer squares" — inverse relationship language (L.8 On Correct)
* "Would you use square inches or square feet for a classroom floor? Why?" — assessment stem from §1.1
* "What is the area in square feet?" — assessment stem from §1.1
* "Would you use square centimeters, square inches, square feet, or square meters for [context]? Explain your choice." — extended assessment stem from §1.1

### Forbidden Phrases

* ❌ "feet squared" / "meters squared" — ambiguous; confuses with exponent notation. Use "square feet" / "square meters"
* ❌ "count the squares" (without specifying unit) — ambiguous; unit must always be named. Use "count the square feet" or "count the square meters"
* ❌ "bigger number means bigger area" — false when units differ. Use "same area, different unit → different number"
* ❌ "using the grid" (vague) — doesn't clarify what grid means. Use "counting the grid squares" or "multiplying the rows and columns"
* ❌ "unit size" (without context) — too abstract. Use "the size of each square" or "bigger/smaller unit"
* ❌ "convert" / "conversion" / "how many ___ in a ___" — unit conversion is M10+, not M6
* ❌ "perimeter" / "distance around" — perimeter is excluded (Decision #5)
* ❌ "partial grid" / "tick marks" / "dimensions only" — grid fading is M7, not M6
* ❌ "Perfect!" / "Excellent!" / "Amazing!" — overpraise; use specific observable acknowledgments
* ❌ "You understood that" / "You're thinking about..." — assumed internal states; reference observable behavior
* ❌ "Yesterday..." / "Today we will learn..." — use session-relative language
* ❌ "carefully" / "thoroughly" / "systematically" — Red Flag Words per Voice Script Prompt
* ❌ "You're a mathematician!" — aspirational identity label; use behavioral praise

### Misconception Prevention

**M8: Square Unit Confusion (PRIMARY):** Prevention is embedded across all three sections. S1 (L.1-L.6) builds correct unit naming through explicit naming convention — "a square with sides of 1 foot" — preventing confusion between linear and square units. S2 (L.7-L.8) addresses the "all units are interchangeable" manifestation through the dramatic dense-vs-clean grid contrast — students see viscerally that sq cm and sq ft produce wildly different counts for the same area. S2 MC items (L.10-L.11) address the "always pick [X] unit" manifestation by requiring context-based reasoning with scaffolded options. S3 (L.12-L.14) tests whether unit selection has internalized by removing reasoning scaffolds and presenting large contexts (sq ft in L.12, sq m in L.13) AND small contexts (sq in in L.14).

**"More Squares = More Area" (SECONDARY):** L.8 directly targets this by showing the same rectangle in two units — "Same rectangle, same area, but thousands of square centimeters or just 12 square feet." The Guide explicitly states the inverse relationship: "Bigger unit, fewer squares." L.8's On Correct reinforces: "That's why we use square feet for big areas." The misconception is further prevented in L.14 where a small-context problem ensures students don't default to "big unit = more area." The inverse relationship is primed in Warmup W.2 ("the bigger each square is, the fewer you'd need") before formal treatment in L.8.

**M3: Confusing Area and Perimeter (LOW priority):** Per Misconceptions sheet, M3 surfaces M5-M10 "only if student-introduced." M6 does NOT teach or reference perimeter (Decision #5). If a student adds dimensions instead of multiplying (e.g., 5+3=8 instead of 5×3=15 in L.2), Pipeline handles this as a computational error, not a perimeter misconception. No explicit perimeter prevention needed in M6 — it's simply excluded from scope.

### Incomplete Script Flags (§1.7.4)

* **ISF-1: Dense CM grid rendering (L.7)** — Requires engineering to display ~11,000 squares visibly packed at screen resolution. Fallback: stylized representation showing "many many" tiny squares without rendering each individually.
* **ISF-2: Dual-grid display (L.8)** — Requires two-display capability: dense cm grid and clean ft grid for the same rectangle, side-by-side or toggled. Fallback: sequential display with Guide narration bridging.
* **ISF-3: Two-part interaction (L.12, L.13, L.14)** — Requires unit-selection MC followed by area-calculation MC in a single interaction. Engineering must confirm this flow. Fallback: split into two separate interactions.
* **ISF-4: Dynamic grid relabeling (L.12)** — If student selects sq m for garden instead of sq ft, grid should relabel. Fallback: pre-label grid as sq ft and accept only sq ft.
* **ISF-5: Context images (L.10, L.11, L.12, L.13, L.14)** — Real-world images needed: playground, classroom floor, garden plot, school gym floor, book cover. Content Author must source or create.

### Success Criteria (§1.7.5)

* Student correctly names and defines square foot and square meter (L.1-L.5)
* Student articulates "too many" reasoning — bigger areas need bigger units (L.7-L.8)
* Student selects appropriate unit for a given context with reasoning (L.10-L.11)
* Student independently selects unit AND calculates area for large contexts in both sq ft (L.12) and sq m (L.13)
* Student independently selects unit AND calculates area for small contexts (L.14)
* Vocabulary used correctly: "square feet," "square meters," "appropriate unit" (not abbreviations in assessment)
* All four units recognized as part of a size-ordered system (sq cm < sq in < sq ft < sq m)
