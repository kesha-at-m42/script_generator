# §1.6 WARMUP (2-4 minutes)

### Core Purpose

Activate students' M6 grid-counting fluency with a familiar rectangle, then create a dramatic curiosity moment by removing the grid entirely — showing that the rectangle and its area haven't changed even though the squares are gone. This primes the central question of M7: how do you find area when you can't see the grid?

**Key Function:** The Warmup does two things: (1) confirms M6 prerequisite transfer (students can calculate area of a full-grid rectangle by multiplying), and (2) creates the M7 driving question through a Mystery Reveal — students watch a familiar grid disappear and confront the fact that the area is still determinable from the dimension labels alone. This positions the Lesson to teach the systematic progression from partial grids to dimensions-only reasoning.

**Why this serves the concept:** M7's core risk is that students will treat grid removal as "losing information" rather than recognizing that dimensions encode the same information the grid provided. By showing the grid disappear on a rectangle students already know (4×6 = 24), the Warmup makes two things simultaneously true: (a) the area hasn't changed, and (b) the only remaining information is the two dimension numbers. This creates the productive tension that drives the entire Lesson — "If I can't see the squares, but the numbers are still there, maybe the numbers are enough." Without this moment, the Lesson's partial grids would feel like degraded tools rather than scaffolded steps toward independence.

**Test:** "If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson?" **YES.** Without the full-grid → dimensions-only reveal, students enter the partial grid phase without experiencing the core insight: that dimensions alone carry the area information. They would encounter partial grids as a novel tool rather than as a step in a progression they've already previewed. The Mystery Reveal makes grid fading feel purposeful rather than arbitrary.

### Warmup Type Rationale

TVP specifies: "Full grid, familiar rectangle (e.g., 4×6), quick area calculation, then SAME rectangle with grid removed — only outline + dimension labels." This maps directly to **Mystery Reveal** (Playbook §4D): show partial information → students predict/observe → reveal full picture → connect prediction to reality. The "mystery" is the grid disappearing; the "reveal" is that the numbers (dimensions) remain and the area is unchanged. The dramatic visual transition (grid fading to nothing while dimension labels persist) is the hook. Personalization comes from the M6 callback ("You've been finding area with grids"), and the Narrative anchor is the transformation itself. This Warmup type also appears in the Playbook's Module 4-7 recommended range and the "counterintuitive concept" selection criteria — the counterintuitive element is that removing visual information doesn't remove mathematical information.

### Interaction W.1: The Familiar Grid [Type B] [ACTIVATION]

* **Purpose:** Quick callback to M6 grid-counting. Student sees a familiar full-grid rectangle and calculates area by multiplying — confirming prerequisite fluency before the grid disappears.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Full Grid).** 4×6 rectangle (4 rows, 6 columns). All 24 squares visible. Grid lines clear. Labeled "square tiles."
* **Guide:** "Here's a rectangle you've seen before. 4 rows, 6 in each row. What's the area?"
* **Prompt:** "What is the area of this rectangle in square tiles?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** A) 10 square tiles, B) 20 square tiles, C) 24 square tiles, D) 46 square tiles
* **Correct Answer:** C) 24 square tiles
* **Answer Rationale:** A) 10 — adds 4+6 (misconception #5 preview: adding dimensions instead of multiplying); B) 20 — common multiplication error or rounding; C) 24 — correct (4 × 6 = 24); D) 46 — concatenates digits "4" and "6"
* **On Correct:** "24 square tiles. 4 rows of 6 — you multiplied to find the area."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** The 4×6 rectangle is the TVP-specified Warmup rectangle. MC distractor A (10) tests whether the addition misconception (#5) is active before the Lesson addresses it. This serves as a lightweight diagnostic: if a student selects A, the Pipeline can flag this for the Lesson's targeted remediation in Early Phase. The On Correct uses "multiplied" — vocabulary from M4, not new for M7.

### Interaction W.2: The Grid Disappears [Type C] [MYSTERY REVEAL — JUDGMENT]

* **Purpose:** The core Mystery Reveal moment. The SAME 4×6 rectangle from W.1 transitions via animation: grid squares fade, grid lines disappear, until only the rectangle outline and dimension labels ("4" on the left side, "6" on the top) remain. Student makes a judgment about whether the area changed — the key insight is that it did NOT.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Full Grid → Dimensions Only, animated transition).** Same 4×6 rectangle. Animation: grid interior fades over 3-4 seconds. Grid lines dissolve. Outline remains. Dimension labels "4" and "6" appear (or persist) on the left and top edges. Final state: bare outline with two numbers.
* **Guide:** "Watch what happens. [ANIMATED GRID FADE.] The squares are gone. But look — those two numbers are still there: 4 and 6. Here's my question: did the area change?"
* **Prompt:** "Did the area of the rectangle change? Tap Yes or No."
* **Student Action:** Binary tap (Yes / No)
* **Correct Answer:** No
* **On Correct:** "The area didn't change. It's still 24 square tiles — the rectangle is the same rectangle. We just can't see all the squares anymore."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** This is the hook AND the judgment task (satisfying WR-3 and WR-5 simultaneously). The animated transition must feel like the SAME rectangle losing its grid, not a new rectangle appearing. The dimension labels (4 and 6) are the only visual residue — this plants the seed that dimensions carry the area information. The binary Yes/No format is low-stakes (WR-12) but requires genuine mathematical judgment. The On Correct deliberately echoes the TVP's key message verbatim: "The rectangle didn't change. The area didn't change. We just can't see all the squares anymore."

> **Voice Note:** "We just can't see all the squares anymore" — the word "anymore" implies loss, creating productive tension. The Guide names what happened (grid disappeared) and what DIDN'T happen (area didn't change) without explaining HOW to solve it without the grid. That's the Lesson's job.

### Interaction W.3: Bridge to Lesson [Type A]

* **Purpose:** Create anticipation for the Lesson without teaching. The question "How do you find the area when you can't see the grid?" is left deliberately unanswered.
* **Visual:** Remains on the dimensions-only 4×6 rectangle from W.2.
* **Guide:** "You've always had a grid to count. Every time you found area, you could see the squares. But what if there's no grid — just those two numbers? Let's figure out how to find area when the squares aren't there."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** Bridge per Warmup Playbook §4G. Creates anticipation ("Let's figure out how...") without explaining the method. The phrase "just those two numbers" points to dimensions as the key information source — this is the thread the Purpose Frame will pick up. "Let's figure out" is invitational per Voice Script Prompt §3.

# §1.7 LESSON (8-12 minutes)

## Lesson Requirements Checklist [REQUIRED]

*(Carried forward from extraction above — tracked through drafting)*

## Core Purpose + Pedagogical Flow

Students learn to determine the area of a rectangle by multiplying its dimensions — without counting grid squares. The grid fading progression (partial grid → tick marks → dimensions only) systematically removes visual scaffolding while proving at each step that the mathematical relationship (dimensions × dimensions = area) holds regardless of what's visible. By the end, students solve text-only area problems using multiplication of side lengths alone.

### Pedagogical Flow Table

| CRA Stage | M7 Implementation | Section | Key Mechanism |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|---------------|
| **Concrete** | Partial grid — first row and first column visible, interior empty. Student can still COUNT one row and COUNT the number of rows. | Section 1 (Early) | "I see 7 in a row and 4 rows. 4 × 7 = 28." Grid structure is partially visible. |
| **Relational** | SAME rectangle shown in partial grid AND tick marks (animated transition). Student confirms: same area, less visual information. | Section 1→2 Transition (1.5) | Side-by-side comparison proves equivalence across representations. |
| **Abstract** | Tick marks only — no interior squares. Vocabulary introduction: "side length," "dimension." Equation Builder with unit labels. | Section 2 (Mid) | Dimensions are named and formalized. Student reads numbers from marks. |
| **Application** | Dimensions only — outline + labels. Then text-only problems (no visual). Full independence. | Section 3 (Late) | "The number already tells you what the tick marks showed." |

### Lesson Structure Table

| Section | Focus | CRA Stage | Interactions | Scaffolding Level |
|---------|-------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Purpose Frame** | Orient to grid fading goal | — | 1 (PF, no student action) | — |
| **Section 1: Early Phase** | Partial grid → multiplication | Concrete | 4 (1.1-1.4) + 1 Relational (1.5) | High (partial grid visible) |
| **Section 2: Mid Phase** | Tick marks + vocabulary | Abstract | 1 vocab bridge (2.0) + 3 problems (2.1-2.3) | Medium (tick marks only, EB with labels) |
| **Section 3: Late Phase** | Dimensions only + text-only | Application | 1 transition (3.0) + 4 problems (3.1-3.4) + 1 text intro (3.5) + 1 text practice (3.6) + EC Bridge | Low (no visual scaffolding) |

## Purpose Frame [Type A]

### Interaction PF: What You'll Learn [Type A]

* **Purpose:** Orient students to the Lesson's goal: finding area when the grid isn't there. Connects backward to Warmup (grid disappeared) and forward (you'll learn to find area anyway).
* **Visual:** Same dimensions-only 4×6 rectangle from Warmup W.2-W.3.
* **Guide:** "You just saw the grid disappear — but the area stayed the same. Here's what you're going to learn: how to find the area of any rectangle, even when you can't see the squares. You'll start with a little bit of the grid showing, then less, then none at all. By the end, you'll look at two numbers and know the area."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** Purpose Frame per Lesson Playbook §1A. Connects backward ("You just saw the grid disappear") and forward ("how to find the area... even when you can't see the squares"). Previews the three-phase progression ("a little bit... then less... then none") without teaching the method. Uses only Warmup-level vocabulary — no "side length," "dimension," or new terms. "Two numbers" is informal and accessible; "dimensions" comes later in Section 2.

## Section 1: Early Phase — Partial Grid (Concrete)

### Interaction 1.1: Watch How I Read It [Worked Example #1 — Full] [Type C] [CONCRETE]

* **Purpose:** First exposure to partial grid mode. Guide demonstrates reading the visible row and column to determine dimensions, then building a multiplication expression. Students observe the complete visual-to-multiplication mapping before attempting it themselves.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Partial Grid).** 4×7 rectangle. First row fully tiled (7 squares visible across top). First column fully tiled (4 squares visible down left side). Interior empty (white). **Equation Builder (unlabeled slots):** `[___] × [___] = [___]`. **Tile palette: {3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8}.**
* **Guide:** [THINK-ALOUD] "This looks different from the full grid — most of the squares are gone. But I can still figure it out. [PLANNING] I need to find the area, so I need two numbers to multiply. [ATTENTION] First, I look at the top row. I count the squares: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7. Seven in each row. Now I look down the left side and count the rows: 1, 2, 3, 4. Four rows. [ACTION: Guide drags 4 into first slot, 7 into second slot. Product 28 auto-computes.] [SELF-CHECK] 4 rows of 7. 4 × 7 = 28 square tiles. Even without seeing every square, I know there are 28."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** Full worked example with think-aloud per Lesson Playbook §3A. The Guide models the complete partial-grid reading process: identify the visible row → count it → identify the visible column → count it → map to multiplication. The think-aloud uses [PLANNING], [ATTENTION], [SELF-CHECK] tags (1 of 1-2 per Lesson per Playbook). The 4×7 rectangle is within Early data constraints (one factor ≤5: 4 rows; other 3-9: 7 across; product 28 ≤ 45). "Square tiles" is the generic unit for Early per §1.5 configuration. Tile palette {3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8} includes correct factors plus distractors.

### Interaction 1.2: Now You Try [Type B] [CONCRETE]

* **Purpose:** Example-problem pair — student applies what 1.1 demonstrated. First student use of partial grid + Equation Builder. Same scaffold level, similar difficulty.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Partial Grid).** 3×8 rectangle. First row: 8 squares across. First column: 3 squares down. Interior empty. **Equation Builder (unlabeled slots):** `[___] × [___] = [___]`. **Tile palette: {3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9}.** Product auto-computes.
* **Guide:** "Your turn. Count the top row and the rows going down. Build the multiplication."
* **Prompt:** "Drag the right number tiles into the slots."
* **Student Action:** Drag-to-place (Equation Builder) — select 2 tiles from palette
  * **Expected:** 3 into first slot, 8 into second (or 8, 3 — both accepted). Product 24 auto-computes.
* **Correct Answer:** 3 × 8 = 24 (or 8 × 3 = 24)
* **On Correct:** "3 × 8 = 24 square tiles. You counted the row, counted the rows, and multiplied. 3 rows of 8."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** Type B — minimal teaching, the worked example already demonstrated the process. 3×8 is within Early constraints (one factor ≤5: 3; other 3-9: 8; product 24 ≤ 45). Tile palette {3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9}: distractors include 4, 5 (adjacent to 3), 6 (not a factor), 9 (adjacent to 8). On Correct restates the process concisely. Dual-orientation validation accepts both factor orders per §1.5 guardrails.

* **Purpose:** Second worked example with fading — Guide identifies one dimension, student completes. Introduces a 5-row rectangle (the ≤5 upper bound for Early).

### Interaction 1.5: Same Rectangle, Less Information [Type C] [RELATIONAL]

* **Purpose:** RELATIONAL phase. The SAME 5×6 rectangle from 1.3 transitions via animation from partial grid to tick marks. Student observes the transformation and confirms that the area is unchanged. Key insight: less visual information, same mathematical content. This is the dedicated Relational interaction — the comparison happens through the animated transition and the student's judgment.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Partial Grid → Tick Marks, animated transition).** The 5×6 rectangle from 1.3. Animation: partial grid tiles fade. Evenly spaced tick marks appear along the top edge (6 marks) and left edge (5 marks). Rectangle outline remains. No interior. Duration: 3-4 seconds.
* **Guide:** "Watch this rectangle — it's the same one from before, 5 rows of 6. [ANIMATED TRANSITION.] The squares are gone now. But see those little marks along the edges? They show where the grid lines used to be. The area is still 30 square tiles. Count the marks across the top."
* **Prompt:** "How many tick marks are across the top? Tap the number."
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** A) 5, B) 6, C) 11, D) 30
* **Correct Answer:** B) 6
* **On Correct:** "6 marks across the top. And 5 marks down the side. 5 × 6 = 30. Same rectangle, same area — the tick marks show you what the grid showed you, just without the squares."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** This is the sole Relational interaction (LR-2 compliance). D (30) tests whether the student confuses the dimension count with the area. Intentional reuse of 5×6=30 from 1.3 is documented — the pedagogical purpose is proving that the SAME rectangle yields the SAME area across representations.

> **Voice Note:** "Same rectangle, same area" echoes the Warmup's "The area didn't change" language, building continuity. The Guide states the relationship explicitly ("the tick marks show you what the grid showed you") per Lesson Playbook §4B — direct instruction, not discovery.

## Section 2: Mid Phase — Tick Marks + Vocabulary (Abstract)

### Interaction 2.0: Naming the Parts [Type A] [VOCABULARY BRIDGE]

* **Purpose:** Vocabulary introduction — "side length" and "dimension" are introduced while the 1.5 tick-mark rectangle (5×6) is still visible. Both terms are grounded in the concrete and relational experience students just completed, following the vocabulary-after-experience principle (Playbook §4A).
* **Visual:** Same as 1.5 — 5×6 rectangle with tick marks. No new visual elements.
* **Guide:** "Those marks along the edges show you how long each side is. We call that the side length — it's how long one side of the rectangle is. This rectangle has a side length of 6 across the top and 5 down the side. Together, those two numbers are the dimensions. The dimensions tell you everything you need to find the area. Dimensions are the factors in your multiplication."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** Type A — vocabulary bridge per §1.3 staging. "Side length" and "dimension" introduced HERE, after Early phase concrete experience (per §1.3: "Introduce after Early phase, not before"). Both terms are grounded in the 5×6 rectangle the student just worked with — the visual anchor IS the vocabulary introduction. The key connection "Dimensions are the factors in your multiplication" links geometric language to algebraic language from M4. Script writers must NOT use "side length" or "dimension" before this interaction.

## Module-Specific Lesson Guidance

### Required Phrases

These vocabulary words and key phrases MUST appear in the Lesson script. Writers use this as a checklist.

* **"side length"** (Section 2, interaction 2.0: "We call that the side length")
* **"dimension" / "dimensions"** (Section 2, interaction 2.0: "Together, those two numbers are the dimensions")
* **"Dimensions are the factors in your multiplication"** (Section 2, interaction 2.0 — the key vocabulary connection)
* **"area"** (throughout — reinforced from M4)
* **"multiply"** (throughout — reinforced from M4)
* **"square feet," "square meters," "square inches," "square centimeters"** (Section 2 onward — all four unit types)
* **"expression"** (available for use; reinforced from M4)
* **"factor"** and **"product"** (Section 2 onward — reinforced from M4)
* **"rows" / "columns"** (Section 1 — concrete language before vocabulary)
* **"___ rows of ___"** (Section 1, connecting to M3-M4 language)
* **Assessment stems:** "What is the area?" / "What is the area of this rectangle in square [unit]?" / "A [object] is [number] [unit] long and [number] [unit] wide. What is its area?"

### Forbidden Phrases

* ❌ **"formula"** (standalone) — Avoid: "Use the formula length × width." Instead: "Multiply the dimensions to find the area." Reasoning: "Formula" suggests rote memorization. M7 is about reasoning why multiplication works from dimensions. Per §1.3 Terms to Avoid.
* ❌ **"perimeter"** — Do not use, even for contrast or clarification. If student adds dimensions, address via multiplication reasoning only. Per Important Decision #5.
* ❌ **"decompose"** — Not in M7 scope. Composite figures appear in later units. Per §1.3 Terms to Avoid.
* ❌ **"You have to..." / "You need to..."** — Controlling language. Use "Try..." or "You can..." Per Voice Script Prompt anti-patterns.
* ❌ **"carefully" / "thoroughly" / "systematically"** — Red flag words that assume internal states. Per Voice Script Prompt §1.
* ❌ **"You understood that" / "You're thinking about..."** — Assumed internal states. Reference observable behavior only.
* ❌ **"Perfect!" / "Excellent!" / "Amazing!"** — Generic overpraise. Use specific observable acknowledgments.
* ❌ **"Yesterday..." / "Today we will learn..."** — Calendar language. Use session-relative: "last time," "this time."
* ❌ **"feet squared" / "meters squared"** — Ambiguous; students confuse with exponent notation. Use "square feet," "square meters." Per §1.3 Terms to Avoid.
* ❌ **"Count the squares"** (in Mid/Late) — Students cannot count squares from tick marks or dimensions only. Use "Multiply the dimensions."
* ❌ **"Bigger number means bigger area"** — False without qualifying unit. Per §1.3 (from M6).

### Misconception Prevention

**#5: Not Multiplying Side Lengths (PRIMARY, HIGH)**
* **Where targeted:** Throughout all three Lesson sections.
* **Section 1 (Early — Partial Grid):** Interactions 1.1-1.4. The partial grid makes the row/column structure visible. Student sees "I count 7 in the row, 4 rows down. 4 groups of 7. Multiply: 4 × 7." The Equation Builder FORCES the multiplication action — student cannot submit without building a multiplication expression. If student adds dimensions instead (e.g., enters 11 for a 4×7), Pipeline redirects to row-counting from the partial grid.
* **Section 2 (Mid — Tick Marks):** Interactions 2.1-2.4. Tick marks remove interior squares but preserve dimensional information. Equation Builder with unit labels makes the multiplication explicit: "6 × 3 = 18 square feet." The unit label reinforces that the product IS the area.
* **Section 3 (Late — Dimensions Only):** Interactions 3.1-3.5. No visual scaffolding. If student adds dimensions instead of multiplying, guide reveal (§1.5) can temporarily show the grid. 2+ reveals flag M3 reinforcement.
* **Warmup diagnostic:** W.1 distractor A (10 = 4+6) pre-screens for this misconception.

**#8: Square Unit Confusion (SECONDARY, MEDIUM)**
* **Where targeted:** Sections 2-3 (Mid and Late phases).
* **Section 2 (Mid):** Equation Builder requires unit labels in all answers. Student must produce "18 square feet," not "18" or "18 feet." All four unit types appear across 2.1-2.4, requiring correct unit selection.
* **Section 3 (Late):** Text-only problems (3.4-3.5) require the student to state the answer with the full square unit. On Correct always includes the complete unit: "27 square inches," not "27."
* **Vocabulary anchor from 2.0:** "Dimensions are the factors in multiplication" — connecting dimensions to area language helps students remember that multiplying two lengths produces a SQUARE unit.

### Incomplete Script Flags (§1.7.4)

* **ISF-1: Animated grid transitions (W.2, 1.5, 3.0)** — Three animated transitions require engineering: full grid → dimensions only (W.2), partial grid → tick marks (1.5), tick marks → dimensions only (3.0). Fallback: Guide narration + showing second representation as a new visual (per §1.5 UX fallback). ⚠️ See Author Flag A2.
* **ISF-2: Equation Builder unstructured mode (S3: 3.1-3.5)** — Per TVP: "No scaffolding" in Late phase. Requires engineering to support free-form expression building vs. slot-based input. Fallback: Equation Builder with unlabeled slots (reduced scaffolding, not zero).
* **ISF-3: Guide reveal overlay (S3 remediation)** — Guide can activate grid overlay on dimensions-only rectangles for remediation. Requires engineering capability for on-demand overlay. ⚠️ See Author Flags A3, A7.
* **ISF-4: Text-only problem format (3.4-3.5)** — No Grid Rectangles displayed. Problem presented as text. Requires engineering support for text-only problem rendering within the activity framework.

### Success Criteria (§1.7.5)

* Student reads partial grid and builds correct multiplication (Interactions 1.2, 1.4)
* Student reads tick marks and builds correct multiplication with unit labels (Interactions 2.1-2.4)
* Student reads dimensions-only and builds correct equation independently (Interactions 3.1-3.3)
* Student extracts dimensions from text and calculates area with correct unit (Interactions 3.4-3.5)
* All four unit types used correctly in Mid and Late phases
* Both factor orders accepted as correct (dual-orientation validation)
* "Side length" and "dimension" used correctly in context (Guide models; assessed in EC)
