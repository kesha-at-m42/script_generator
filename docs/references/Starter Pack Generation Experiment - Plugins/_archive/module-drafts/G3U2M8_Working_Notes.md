## Cross-Reference Table B — TVP Extraction

```
TVP: MODULE 8
===============
OUR Lesson 9 (adapted) | Standard: 3.MD.C.7.b (multiply side lengths for area)

Learning Goal: Find the area of rectangles from dimensions. Given an area, determine possible side lengths.

Key Teaching Points:
1. Forward: given dimensions → multiply → area (consolidation of M7's abstraction)
2. Reverse: given area → find dimensions that produce it (new cognitive direction)
3. THE BIG INSIGHT: Multiplication works both ways. If 4 × 6 = 24, then a rectangle with area 24 could be 4 by 6. But it could ALSO be 3 by 8, or 2 by 12. An area doesn't have just ONE rectangle — it has MULTIPLE possible rectangles. First time students encounter one-to-many mathematical relationships in this unit.
4. Forward problems extend M7 into richer contexts and all four unit types with full independence — no grids, no tick marks, dimensions only
5. Reverse problems preview the factor-pair thinking that M10 (multiplication table) will build on
6. CRA Stage: Abstract — fully at dimensions-only level. No grid support.

Cognitive Focus:
- APPLY (Transfer) — fluent multiplication for area across varied contexts (forward)
- CONNECT (Transfer) — linking area back to factor pairs; seeing multiplication bidirectionally (reverse)
- CREATE (Procedural) — generating multiple valid rectangles for a given area (reverse)

Misconceptions Targeted:
- M5: Not Multiplying Side Lengths (PRIMARY, HIGH) — Extended practice at dimensions-only level provides additional consolidation.
- Picking numbers without understanding (SECONDARY) — In reverse problems, students may guess dimensions randomly rather than using multiplication reasoning. Addressed by requiring verification.

What Students DO:

WARM-UP:
- Grid Rectangles (outline + dimensions) → Two rectangles with DIFFERENT dimensions but the SAME area
- Example: 3 × 8 and 4 × 6 — both have area 24
- Students calculate both areas, notice they match
- "Two different rectangles, same area. How is that possible?"
- Purpose: Surface the one-to-many relationship before formal introduction.

LESSON:
Early activities (Forward consolidation — dimensions-only fluency):
- Grid Rectangles (outline + dimensions, varied units) → Students multiply and state area with correct units
- Full independence — no grid, no tick marks, no Equation Builder scaffold
- All four unit types: sq cm, sq in, sq ft, sq m
- Contexts for some problems: "A garden bed is 7 feet by 5 feet. What is its area?"
- 4 problems — sufficient consolidation at M7 abstraction level
- Text-only problems mixed in (rigid sentence template from M7)
- Error patterns caught: adding instead of multiplying, missing units, wrong unit type

Mid activities (Reverse problem introduction — given area, find dimensions):
- Grid Rectangles (outline, no dimensions) + Equation Builder (multiplication with blanks) → Reverse reasoning
- Given: "The area of a rectangle is 12 square centimeters. What could the side lengths be?"
- Equation Builder displays: ___ × ___ = 12
- Interaction: Multi-select MC — "Select ALL sets of dimensions that give an area of [target]."
- Options show unique pairs only (2×6, 3×4 — not 4×3, 6×2 as separate options)
- Options include all valid unique factor pairs plus 2-3 distractors
- Number of correct answers varies by target area — do NOT tell students how many to find
- Scoring: All correct = full credit. Some correct, no incorrect = partial credit. Any incorrect = incorrect.
- Distractors must have products ≠ target area and must not share factors with correct answers (per Edit 83)
- Commutativity check (1 problem, explicit): "Kiran found two rectangles with area 12: 3×4 and 4×3. How many DIFFERENT rectangles did Kiran find?" MC with 4 options. Correct: B ("One — turning the same rectangle doesn't make a new one")
- ⚠️ No dichotomous (Yes/No/Agree/Disagree) answer frames
- On selection: rectangle with those dimensions appears as visual confirmation
- Then: "Is there ANOTHER way?" — student selects different unique factor pair
- Key beat: After 2-3 rectangles found with same area, display side by side. "One area, MANY rectangles. The side lengths tell you the SHAPE. The area tells you the SPACE."
- 3-4 reverse problems with areas having multiple clean factor pairs
- After students find 2+ pairs, guide asks: "Are there any others? How could you check?" (plants seed for M10)

Late activities (Mixed forward and reverse, independent):
- ⚠️ All problems text-only. Both types use same format so visual doesn't signal problem type.
- Forward example: "A rug is 7 feet long and 4 feet wide. What is its area?"
- Reverse example: "A rug has an area of 28 square feet. It is 7 feet long. How wide is it?"
- 5-6 problems, mixed types
- Reverse problems without Equation Builder scaffold
- Some reverse with context: "Tyler has 20 square tiles. He wants to arrange them in a rectangle. What are two different rectangles he could make?"

EXIT CHECK:
3 problems:
1. Forward: Dimensions-only rectangle (7 ft × 4 ft) → calculate area with correct unit
2. Reverse: "A rectangle has area 18 sq cm. Select TWO possible sets of side lengths." MC with valid pairs (2×9, 3×6) and distractors with no shared factors. Better distractors: (4×5, 3×7), (2×8, 3×5), (2×7, 4×6).
3. Forward, text-only: "A rug is 9 feet long and 3 feet wide. What is the area of the rug?" No visual.
- Assessment ratio: 2 forward : 1 reverse (deliberate — forward fluency prioritized for M9)

PRACTICE:
- Mixed forward and reverse across all four unit types
- Forward: dimensions-only + text-only
- Reverse: given area, select valid factor pairs from MC; "Find at least two" for areas with multiple pairs
- STRETCH (not BASELINE): 1-2 problems asking "Which rectangle would be longer and skinnier? Which closer to a square?"
- Mastery tracking tags: Missing-dimension problems = TRANSFER (APPLY verb). Forward application = BASELINE. Shape comparison = STRETCH. Design problems = STRETCH. Multi-step = STRETCH. Constraint = CHALLENGE.

SYNTHESIS:
- Display: several rectangles of different dimensions all sharing same area (e.g., 1×24, 2×12, 3×8, 4×6 for area 24)
- ⚠️ All rectangles must be drawn to scale relative to each other
- "One area, MANY rectangles. The side lengths tell you the SHAPE. The area tells you the SPACE."
- Consolidation: "You can go forward — dimensions to area. And you can go reverse — area to dimensions."
- Vocabulary review: length, width
- Bridge to M9: "Now you're going to use everything you know about area to solve real problems — like designing a garden or figuring out how much paint to buy."

Scaffolding Progression:
[No explicit scaffolding stages listed — M8 is fully abstract from the start]

Tool Requirements:
- Reverse problem confirmation: When student selects a factor pair, rectangle with those dimensions appears on screen
- Side-by-side rectangle display: Multiple rectangles of different dimensions but equal area simultaneously
- No new tools. Grid Rectangles and Equation Builder — both familiar from M4-M7.

Data Constraints:
Forward problems: Factors 2–10, products within 100, all four unit types
  Examples: 7×4=28 sq ft, 8×5=40 sq cm, 9×3=27 sq m, 6×6=36 sq in
Reverse problems — target areas with multiple clean factor pairs (unique pairs only):
  Factor cap: Both factors ≤ 12. 1×n always included (trivial). No factors 13+.
  12: 1×12, 2×6, 3×4
  18: 1×18, 2×9, 3×6
  20: 1×20, 2×10, 4×5
  24: 1×24, 2×12, 3×8, 4×6
  30: 1×30, 3×10, 5×6
  36: 1×36, 3×12, 4×9, 6×6
  Note: 2×15 (for 30) and 2×18 (for 36) excluded — factors beyond useful 3rd grade range.
Reverse MC distractors: Close but wrong products. Same factor must not appear in correct AND distractor.
Avoid: Prime-number areas, areas >50, 1×n as prompted answer (but accept if offered; included in MC per Edit 91)
Text-only: Rigid template: "A [object] is [number] [unit] long and [number] [unit] wide. What is its area?"

Vocabulary:
  New: length, width (formalized — used casually since M4 but now central to text-only problem structure)
  Reinforced: area, side length, dimension, factor, product, square inch, square centimeter, square foot, square meter
  Language pattern:
    Forward: "The area is ___ square [unit]."
    Reverse: "___ × ___ = [area], so the side lengths could be ___ and ___."

TRANSITION IN (from M7):
  Pedagogical Shift: Grid fading progression → Fluency at abstract level + reverse reasoning
  What carries forward: Dimensions-only area calculation, all four standard units, text-only problem format, no grid support
  What's new: Reverse problems, factor-pair reasoning, multiple valid answers, side-by-side rectangle comparison
  Why: M7 achieved the abstraction. M8 consolidates through volume of practice AND extends in new cognitive direction.

TRANSITION OUT (to M9):
  What M8 establishes: Fluent dimensions-to-area calculation; one area maps to multiple rectangles; factor-pair reasoning
  What M9 builds: Real-world application — using area to solve problems with context, multiple solution strategies, problems with multiple correct answers
  Bridge: "You know how to find area AND how to find rectangles that have a given area. Now let's use those skills to solve real problems."
```

## Design Constraints — Important Decisions Extraction

```
DESIGN CONSTRAINTS (from Important Decisions)
=============================================

Decision 1: Unified Path with Adaptive Pacing
  Rule: Use a single instructional path following CRA progression. Differentiate through pacing and representation duration, not different approaches.
  Applies to M8? YES
  Constraint: M8 follows the single CRA path. All students get forward consolidation → reverse introduction. Differentiation is in pacing (some students need more forward practice before reverse), not in alternative approaches.

Decision 2: Explicit Spatial Structuring as Central Focus
  Rule: Make row-by-column "seeing" an explicit instructional target.
  Applies to M8? PARTIAL
  What applies: Spatial structuring is ASSUMED knowledge by M8 (mastered in M3-M5). M8 doesn't teach it but depends on it — students who can't "see" rows/columns in a dimension-labeled rectangle will struggle with both forward and reverse problems.
  What doesn't: No dedicated spatial structuring activities in M8. That's M3-M4 territory.

Decision 3: Progressive Grid Removal (Concreteness Fading)
  Rule: Follow fading sequence: Full grid → Partial grid → Tick marks → Dimensions only.
  Applies to M8? YES — HARD CONSTRAINT
  Constraint: M8-M9 are "Dimensions only / text-only (abstract problem-solving)." M8 CANNOT use grids, partial grids, or tick marks as instructional tools. The only exception is visual confirmation of reverse problem answers (displaying a rectangle with selected dimensions), which is a verification display, not a scaffold.

Decision 4: Array Knowledge is Helpful but Not Required
  Rule: Design Unit 2 to be self-contained. Don't assume Unit 1 completion.
  Applies to M8? YES (inherited constraint)
  Constraint: By M8, array knowledge has been taught within Unit 2 (M3-M4). This decision means M8 doesn't need to worry about Unit 1 prerequisites — but it still assumes M1-M7 within Unit 2.

Decision 5: Perimeter Kept Separate — Do Not Introduce
  Rule: Do NOT introduce perimeter. Do not proactively address perimeter-area confusion.
  Applies to M8? YES — HARD CONSTRAINT
  Constraint: No perimeter language, formula, or concept in M8. If students add dimensions instead of multiplying (Misconception #5), address through multiplication reasoning, NOT by contrasting with perimeter.

Decision 6: Dragging Over Tapping for Manipulatives
  Rule: Prioritize drag-to-place interactions for virtual manipulatives over tap-to-place.
  Applies to M8? PARTIAL
  Constraint: M8 has no manipulatives (tiles, arrays). Most interactions are MC selection or text-based. Equation Builder in Mid uses drag-to-place for factor slots — Decision 6 applies there. MC selection (tapping) is acceptable per the exception for "simple selection tasks."

Decision 7: Standard Units Get Full Scaffolding
  Rule: Keep sq in/cm (L6) and sq ft/m (L7) as separate modules.
  Applies to M8? NO (already completed in M5-M6)
  All four unit types are established. M8 uses all four freely.

Decision 8: Include Multiplication Table Connection (L11) as Module
  Rule: Include L11 as synthesis/connection module.
  Applies to M8? NO (applies to M10)
  M8's factor-pair reasoning previews M10's multiplication table connection but doesn't depend on this decision.

Decision 9: Keep Application Module (L15) Standalone
  Rule: Include L15 as standalone application module.
  Applies to M8? NO (applies to M14)

  Rule: Ruler dropped from OUR L9. Not in engineering specs, poor digital translation.
  Applies to M8? YES — DEFINES MODULE IDENTITY
  Constraint: M8 has NO ruler measurement activities. The module's identity shifts from "Measure to Find Area" to "Area Fluency — Forward and Reverse." This is the biggest OUR departure in Unit 2, confirmed by SME (Andrea, Feb 17, 2025).
```

## Section Plan

**Warmup:** Activate M7 dimensions-only fluency + surface one-to-many relationship. Type: Discovery / Anticipation — two rectangles with different dimensions, same area. Bridge: "How is that possible? That's what we'll explore." Warm-up type: Comparison Discovery (§4D).

**Lesson sections:**
- **Section 1 (Early — Forward consolidation):** 4 forward problems, dimensions-only rectangles with all four unit types. Some with real-world contexts, some text-only. CRA: Abstract (Application). Establishes fluency at M7's abstraction level before introducing reverse.
- **Section 2 (Mid — Reverse introduction):** Equation Builder scaffolded. Multi-select MC for factor pairs. Commutativity check. Key beat: side-by-side display of multiple rectangles with same area. Major pedagogical move: reverse reasoning introduced. CRA: Abstract (Connect/Create). Vocabulary formalization: length, width, factor pair.
- **Section 3 (Late — Mixed practice):** Text-only format for BOTH forward and reverse. No visual signal of problem type. 5-6 mixed problems. CRA: Abstract (Application/Transfer). Independent performance.

**Exit Check:** 3 problems — 2 forward (1 dimensions-only, 1 text-only), 1 reverse (multi-select MC). Tests forward fluency + factor-pair identification.

**Synthesis:** Side-by-side rectangles with same area (drawn to scale). Consolidation of both directions. Vocabulary review. Bridge to M9 real-world application.
