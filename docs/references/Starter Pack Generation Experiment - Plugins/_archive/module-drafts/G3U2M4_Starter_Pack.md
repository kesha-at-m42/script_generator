## 1.0 THE ONE THING

**[REQUIRED]**

The area of a rectangle can be found by multiplying its side lengths — because "4 rows of 6" IS "4 × 6," and the multiplication expression describes the row-by-column structure students already know how to see.

**CRA Stage:** Representational → Abstract transition. Students work with fully-gridded rectangles (representational) but the cognitive work is connecting visual structure to symbolic multiplication notation (abstract). Grid support remains throughout — grid abstraction is M7.

**Critical Misconception:** #4.0 — Wrong Rectangle for Expression. Students create or identify a rectangle that doesn't match a multiplication expression, revealing a fragile connection between the visual structure and symbolic notation. Prevention: every expression is built FROM the rectangle's visible structure, not matched to an abstract equation.

**Success Indicator:** Given a fully-gridded rectangle, the student independently identifies the dimensions, builds a correct multiplication expression (either orientation accepted), calculates the product, and states the area in square units — without Equation Builder scaffolding or Guide prompting.

**Biggest Risk:** Students learn to mechanically fill in the Equation Builder without connecting the symbols to the rectangle's structure. If "4 × 6" becomes a rote procedure rather than a description of "4 rows of 6 tiles each," the multiplication-area connection is hollow and breaks down when grids are removed in M7. The SME insight is critical: motivate multiplication through RECOGNITION ("you already know this — the × symbol just names what you see"), not through EFFICIENCY ("this is faster than skip-counting"). Students who skip-count by 2s, 5s, 10s may not feel the "faster" motivation — but they WILL feel the recognition that structure they already see has a mathematical name.

### **1.1.2 Module Bridges**

**From M3 (Structured Counting — Rows and Columns):** Students can identify the row/column structure of a tiled rectangle, describe it using "___ rows of ___" or "___ columns of ___" language, and skip-count to find area. They understand that rows and columns give the same area total. They know "row," "column," and "array" vocabulary. M3 Synthesis closure: "At the start, you counted 24 tiles one by one. They build multiplication expressions from rectangles using the Equation Builder, learn that both orientations describe the same area (commutativity), and by late activities write expressions independently as "length × width = area." **To M5 (Standard Units):** Students leave M4 able to write multiplication expressions for any gridded rectangle's area and state results in generic "square units." M5 introduces the question: "But what SIZE are those square units?" — moving from generic to standard units (square inches, square centimeters). Same formula, but now the unit matters.

**[REQUIRED]**

* Grid fading, partial grids, tick marks, or dimensions-only rectangles — M7+ (Decision #3: M1-M4 full grids)

* Perimeter or "distance around" — NOT in Unit 2 (Decision #5)

* Multiplication fact memorization as a goal — M4 teaches the NOTATION CONNECTION, not fact fluency
* "Faster than skip-counting" as the primary motivation — per SME insight, motivate through RECOGNITION, not EFFICIENCY
* Area without visible grid — grid abstraction is M7's assessment, not M4's

Students must understand that multiplication EMERGES FROM structure, not that it's an arbitrary rule. Every interaction should reinforce that the × symbol DESCRIBES what they can already SEE.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

**[REQUIRED]**

**AF#2 (RESOLVED):** TVP misconceptions mapped to global IDs: #4.0 (Wrong Rectangle for Expression) as PRIMARY, #2.0 (Counting One-by-One) and #9.0 (Array Structure Not Seen) as SECONDARY persistence from M3. TVP's "dimension confusion" treated as prevention target within commutativity teaching. Author confirmed.

### **1.4.1 #4.0: Wrong Rectangle for Expression (PRIMARY)**

**Trigger Behavior:** Given a multiplication expression like 5 × 7, student identifies or constructs a rectangle with different dimensions (e.g., 5×8 or 4×7). When building an expression from a rectangle, student writes the wrong factors — entering dimensions that don't match the visible rows and columns. Note: 5×7 and 7×5 are BOTH correct (commutativity) — only truly mismatched dimensions are errors.

**Why It Happens:** The connection between the visual structure (rows and columns) and the symbolic notation (factors in an expression) is fragile. Students may mechanically place numbers in Equation Builder slots without verifying against the rectangle. Or they may count rows correctly but lose the count-per-row when shifting attention to the Equation Builder. The abstraction leap from "I see 4 rows of 6" to "I write 4 × 6" requires coordinating visual, verbal, and symbolic representations simultaneously.

**Visual Cue:** Full grid with visible rows and columns. Row/column highlighting available in early activities helps students verify their expression matches the structure. The Equation Builder's pre-labeled slots ("Rows" × "In each row" = "Area") in early activities scaffold the visual-to-symbolic mapping.

**Prevention Strategy:** M4's scaffolding progression directly addresses this: (1) Early activities with pre-labeled Equation Builder slots explicitly connect "Rows" to the first factor and "In each row" to the second factor — the mapping is structurally enforced. (2) Mid activities remove labels but keep the grid fully visible so students can always check their expression against the rectangle. (3) Late activities remove all scaffolding but the grid remains — students can verify by counting. The key pedagogical move is building expressions FROM rectangles (not matching to expressions), ensuring the visual is always the source of truth. The rotation key beat (Early) prevents rigid factor-order thinking by showing that the SAME rectangle yields two valid expressions.

**Anti-Pattern Detection (for Practice):** Expression errors where the product is correct but factors don't match the rectangle's dimensions (e.g., writing 3 × 8 = 24 for a 4×6 rectangle) — student knows the area but isn't connecting the expression to the structure. Also: students who consistently enter the same factor order regardless of rectangle orientation (always rows-first or always larger-number-first) may be applying a memorized rule rather than reading from the rectangle. Both triggers should return to Mid-level scaffolding (unlabeled EB slots with grid visible) rather than Early (pre-labeled), since the issue is attention to structure, not inability to use the tool.

### **1.4.2 #2.0: Counting One-by-One (SECONDARY — persistence from M3)**

**Trigger Behavior:** Student bypasses multiplication entirely and counts every tile individually (1, 2, 3... 24) instead of using the row-column structure to multiply. May produce correct area totals through brute-force counting but cannot write a matching expression.

**Why It Happens:** One-by-one counting was the correct strategy in M1-M2 and was only recently replaced by skip-counting in M3. Some students may revert to their most secure strategy when facing the additional cognitive demand of expression-building. If M3's structure-seeing didn't fully transfer, the student may not have the prerequisite for M4's multiplication connection.

**Visual Cue:** Grid Rectangles with full grid — the structure is visible whether or not the student uses it. Row highlighting in Warmup callback re-activates the structural view.

**Prevention Strategy:** M4's Warmup opens with an M3 callback that REQUIRES structure description before introducing ×: "How many rows? How many in each row?" This verifies structure-seeing is active. If a student shows counting-one-by-one behavior in M4, the issue is M3 prerequisite — Pipeline remediation should return to structure identification before continuing with expression building.

### **1.4.3 #9.0: Array Structure Not Seen (SECONDARY — persistence from M3)**

**Trigger Behavior:** Student looks at a gridded rectangle but doesn't see row/column organization. Cannot answer "How many rows?" or answers incorrectly. Counts tiles in random order.

**Why It Happens:** Spatial structuring is a developmental milestone (only 31% of 3rd graders achieve it without instruction per Decision #2). If M3's three-step highlighting scaffold didn't fully transfer, the prerequisite for M4's multiplication connection is missing.

**Visual Cue:** Full grid with row/column highlighting available. Warmup callback uses row highlighting to re-activate structural seeing from M3.

**Prevention Strategy:** Warmup callback serves as a diagnostic — if students can correctly identify "4 rows of 6" and skip-count, M3's structure-seeing transferred. Row highlighting in Warmup provides visual scaffolding for students who need a reminder. If persistent, Pipeline remediation returns to M3-level structure identification activities.

## 1.6 WARMUP (2-4 minutes)

### Core Purpose

Activate M3 structure-seeing, validate skip-counting fluency, and introduce the × symbol as a LABEL for what students already know — bridging from "4 rows of 6" to "4 × 6" without teaching expression-building.

**Key Function:** The Warmup does two things: (1) confirms M3 prerequisite transfer (students can identify row structure and skip-count), and (2) introduces the × symbol as a recognition moment — students see that the structure they describe as "rows of" has a mathematical name. This positions the Lesson to formalize expression-building with the Equation Builder.

**Why this serves the concept:** M4's core risk is students treating multiplication as an arbitrary procedure disconnected from rectangle structure. By introducing × through recognition ("you already do this — here's what mathematicians call it"), the Warmup inoculates against the "formula as arbitrary rule" misconception (#4.0) before the Lesson begins. The M3 callback also serves as a diagnostic — if students cannot identify "4 rows of 6" and skip-count, the M3 prerequisite hasn't transferred.

**Test:** "If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson?" **YES.** Without the callback, students enter expression-building without verified structure-seeing. Without the × introduction as recognition, students encounter × for the first time inside the Equation Builder — where it risks feeling procedural rather than meaningful. The Warmup's recognition framing is the SME-specified pedagogical entry point.

### Warmup Type Rationale

### Interaction W.1: Remember This? [Type B, with Type C On Correct]

* **Purpose:** M3 callback. Student sees a familiar rectangle with row highlighting and skip-counts to find area (Type B — familiar task, minimal teaching). Confirms structure-seeing has transferred from M3. The Guide then writes the × expression as a label for what they just did (Type C teaching delivered in On Correct — the × reveal is designed as a RECOGNITION moment AFTER the familiar action, not front-loaded teaching).
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** 4×6 rectangle (4 rows, 6 columns). All 24 squares shaded. Full grid visible. System row highlighting: rows highlighted in distinct colors, synced with Guide narration.
* **Guide:** "You've worked with rectangles like this before. Look — the rows are highlighted. How many rows? And how many in each row? Skip-count to find the area."
* **Prompt:** "Skip-count the rows. What is the area?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** 18, 20, 24, 28
* **Correct Answer:** 24
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 24 = Correct (4 rows of 6: 6, 12, 18, 24)
  * 18 = Stopped at 3 rows (skip-counted 6, 12, 18 and stopped early)
  * 20 = Skip-counted by 5 instead of 6 (5, 10, 15, 20)
  * 28 = Skip-counted by 7 instead of 6, or added an extra row
* **On Correct:** "24 square units. 4 rows of 6 — you skip-counted that fast. Here's something cool: mathematicians write '4 rows of 6' as 4 × 6 = 24. The × means 'rows of.' You already knew how to do this — now you know what it's called."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** W.1's interaction structure (MC skip-count) is Type B — a familiar M3 task with minimal teaching. The On Correct delivers Type C teaching (the × introduction). This split is intentional: the student's action is comfortable and familiar, and the substantial teaching arrives as a REVEAL after success, not as front-loaded instruction. A downstream script Claude should NOT front-load teaching into this interaction — the whole point is that the × symbol arrives as recognition of what the student already did. The expression "4 × 6 = 24" appears as a Guide-written label alongside the rectangle, not in the Equation Builder. The system row highlighting re-activates M3's structural seeing.

> **Voice Note:** "You've worked with rectangles like this before" is session-relative personalization (Engagement Anchor #1). "Here's something cool" is invitational and creates the recognition moment. Energy is Medium-High per Warmup standard.

### Interaction W.2: Same Rectangle, Different View [Type C]

* **Purpose:** Show the same rectangle in a different orientation (6×4). Student skip-counts again, and the Guide writes the second × expression. This plants the commutativity seed — same area, two expressions — without formally teaching it.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** 6×4 rectangle (6 rows, 4 columns). All 24 squares shaded. Full grid visible. System row highlighting: 6 rows highlighted in distinct colors. This is the SAME rectangle viewed from the other orientation (per TVP: "same rectangle orientation as M3 synthesis callback").
* **Guide:** "Now look at this rectangle. Same tiles, but the rows go the other direction. How many rows this time? Skip-count to find the area."
* **Prompt:** "Skip-count the rows. What is the area?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** 18, 20, 24, 30
* **Correct Answer:** 24
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 24 = Correct (6 rows of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24)
  * 18 = Stopped at row 4 or 5 (miscounted the number of rows)
  * 20 = Stopped at row 5 (4, 8, 12, 16, 20)
  * 30 = Skip-counted by 5 instead of 4
* **On Correct:** "24 again. 6 rows of 4. That's 6 × 4 = 24. Same rectangle, same area — just described two ways. 4 × 6 and 6 × 4 both equal 24."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** W.2 uses the same rectangle in the other orientation per TVP. The On Correct delivers the second key beat: "6 × 4 = 24." The "same rectangle, same area — just described two ways" plants commutativity informally without using the word or formally teaching it. Both expressions (4 × 6 and 6 × 4) are now visible as Guide-written labels. This is Engagement Anchor #2 — the Narrative anchor of "same area, two names" creates a genuine mathematical surprise.

> **Voice Note:** "Same rectangle, same area — just described two ways" is the curiosity moment. The parallel structure (W.1 showed "4 × 6 = 24," W.2 showed "6 × 4 = 24") lets the student experience the pattern rather than being told about it.

### Interaction W.3: Bridge to Lesson [Type A]

* **Purpose:** Create anticipation for the Lesson without teaching expression-building. The bridge introduces the Equation Builder conceptually as a "tool for writing these math sentences" and frames the Lesson as building this skill independently.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** Both expressions (4 × 6 = 24 and 6 × 4 = 24) still visible alongside the rectangle.
* **Guide:** "You just described rectangles using multiplication — and you already knew how. In the Lesson, you'll use a tool to build these multiplication sentences yourself. Let's see how that works."

> **Design Note:** W.3 is the bridge to Lesson. The bridge makes the student curious about the tool; the Purpose Frame at the Lesson opening orients them to what they'll learn. Per Warmup Playbook: bridge creates a question in the student's mind.

> **Voice Note:** "You just described rectangles using multiplication — and you already knew how" reinforces the recognition framing one final time before the Lesson begins. Energy is invitational.

## Core Purpose + Pedagogical Flow

> **KDD: M4 CRA Stage Position.** The Backbone (§1.0) identifies M4's CRA stage as "Representational → Abstract transition." M4 does NOT follow the standard four-stage CRA sequence (Concrete → Relational → Abstract → Application). Instead, the entire Lesson IS a single representational-to-abstract transition, with **scaffolding fading** as the progression mechanism rather than CRA stage shifts. Students work with grid representations (Representational) and build symbolic expressions (Abstract) from the first interaction — there are no physical manipulatives and no concrete stage. The four-label mapping below reflects what M4 actually does, not a forced fit to standard CRA.

| M4 CRA Stage | What Students Actually Do | Interactions |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Representational → Abstract (Scaffolded)** | Pre-labeled EB enforces the visual-to-symbolic mapping. Students read rectangle structure and place numbers into labeled slots ("Rows" × "In each row" = "Area"). The scaffold makes the mapping explicit — this is not concrete, it's representational-to-abstract with training wheels. | 1.1-1.2, 1.5 |
| **Relational** | Same rectangle, two orientations, two expressions. Student builds both and then makes a comparison judgment about the relationship. Commutativity experienced through action, not named. | 1.3-1.4 |
| **Representational → Abstract (Independent)** | EB labels removed, then EB template removed. Students perform the same visual-to-symbolic mapping independently, with vocabulary formalized along the way. The cognitive task is identical to S1 minus scaffolding. | 2.1-3.3 |
| **Application** | Tested in EC + Practice (not in Lesson proper). Students apply expression-building to novel rectangles with no scaffolding and no Guide support. | EC, Practice |

## Equation Builder Palette Configuration

> **Design Principle (from U1 M8 precedent):** The tile palette includes both correct factors PLUS diagnostic distractors. A palette with only the correct numbers makes the EB a trivial drag exercise. Distractors ensure students must READ the rectangle to select the right factors, not just drag the only tiles available.

### Product Slot Behavior

The product **auto-computes** after both factors are correctly placed. Students do NOT drag a product tile.

| Scaffold Level | Template | Product Behavior | Rationale |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Pre-labeled (S1)** | `[Rows] × [In each row] = [auto]` | Auto-computes when factors placed | Factor mapping is the M4 skill; product is confirmatory (8, 15, 24 all known from skip-counting). Keeps cognitive focus on visual-to-symbolic mapping. |
| **Unlabeled (S2)** | `[___] × [___] = [auto]` | Auto-computes when factors placed | Products go up to 32; requiring students to produce 4×8=32 mentally shifts the task from expression-building to computation. Auto-compute keeps focus on factor mapping. |
| **Unstructured (S3)** | Student builds `[n] × [n] = [auto]` from tiles | Auto-computes after valid `n × n` formed | Products go up to 64. Tile set is 2-10; there are no 2-digit product tiles. The challenge is building the expression structure independently, not mental arithmetic. |

> **Engineering Note:** The "Area" slot (or auto-computed product position) animates the result appearing after both factors are placed — a brief computed-result animation (e.g., number materializes with a subtle glow). This provides visual feedback that the expression is complete. The student then states the area as part of the On Correct acknowledgment.

### Tile Palette Design

**Palette source:** Number tiles 2-10 per Backbone §1.5.2.

**Progressive expansion (adapted from M8):** 5-6 tiles in S1, 7 tiles in S2, 6 number tiles + {×, =} in S3.

**Distractor types for M4:**
- **Off-by-one:** Factor ±1 (e.g., for a 3-row rectangle, include 2 and 4). Catches row/column miscounting.
- **Sum of factors:** When sum ≤ 10 (e.g., 3+4=7, 3+7=10, 2+4=6). Catches addition-instead-of-multiplication.
- **Carryover factor:** A factor from the previous interaction. Catches recall-instead-of-reading.
- **Between-factors:** A number between the two correct factors (e.g., 4 for a 3×5 rectangle). Catches imprecise structure reading.

> **Design Note:** This is the sole Relational interaction. The comparison judgment is the genuinely relational student action — the student must evaluate the RELATIONSHIP between two expressions rather than just building one. The binary tap format (not MC with distractors) follows M3's tap-to-highlight pattern: student-initiated, system-confirmed. The animated rotation per §1.5.1 UX requirements must feel like the SAME rectangle viewed differently, not a new rectangle. This is NOT formally teaching commutativity — it's letting students experience it through action and then make a judgment about what they experienced. The commutativity key beat is from TVP Early activities.

> **Voice Note:** "Same rectangle, same area — 24 square units either way" echoes the Warmup's "same area, two names" language, building continuity across the module. "Two expressions, one rectangle" is the relational summary — concise enough for a student to internalize.

## Module-Specific Lesson Guidance

### Required Phrases
* × / times (Warmup — recognition framing)
* "expression" (S1, after first EB build)
* "factor" (S2: "The numbers you multiply are called factors")
* "product" (S2: "The answer is called the product")
* "length" (S2: "The number of rows is also called the length")
* "width" (S2: "The number in each row is the width")
* "dimensions" (S3: "The length and width together are the dimensions")
* "Area = length × width" (SYNTHESIS §1.9 only — NOT in Lesson)
* "___ rows of ___" / "___ × ___" (core connection)
* "Same rectangle, same area" (commutativity key beat)
* "You already knew how to do this" (recognition framing per SME)

### Forbidden Phrases
* "This is faster" / "easier way" / "quicker method" / "shortcut" — never motivate × through efficiency; motivate through recognition
* "You have to..." / "You need to..." — controlling language; use "Try..." or "You can..."
* "formula" as a term (M7+ — use "pattern" or "what we've been doing")
* "square centimeters" / "square inches" / etc. (M5-M6; M4 uses generic "square units")
* "perimeter" / "distance around" (not in Unit 2)
* "decompose" / "partition" (M11)
* "Perfect!" / "Excellent!" / "Amazing!" — overpraise; use specific observable acknowledgments
* "You understood that" / "You're thinking about..." — assumed internal states; reference observable behavior
* "Yesterday..." / "Today we will learn..." — use session-relative language
* "Create a rectangle from this expression" — TVP specifies expression-FROM-rectangle only

### Misconception Prevention

**#4.0 Wrong Rectangle for Expression (PRIMARY):** Pre-labeled EB slots (S1) enforce the mapping. Rotation key beat (1.3-1.4) prevents rigid factor-order thinking. Labels removed (S2) but grid visible for self-check. No scaffolding (S3) with grid present — diagnostic: structure-reading failure (M3) vs. notation-mapping failure (M4).

**#2.0 Counting One-by-One (SECONDARY):** Warmup callback requires skip-counting. Skip-counting is NOT #2.0 — it's appropriate M3 skill use. #2.0 only triggers if student counts tiles individually without using row structure.

**#9.0 Array Structure Not Seen (SECONDARY):** Warmup highlighting re-activates M3. S1 highlighting on tap. S2-S3 no highlighting — if student can't identify rows/columns, Pipeline returns to M3.

### Incomplete Script Flags (§1.7.4)
* **ISF-1: Animated rotation (S1, 1.4)** — Engineering feasibility. Fallback: Guide narration + showing second rectangle with swapped dimensions (per §1.5.1 UX fallback).
* **ISF-2: EB unstructured mode (S1-S3)** — Requires engineering to support free-form expression building vs. slot-based input.
* **ISF-3: Dimension labels on Grid Rectangles (S2-S3)** — New capability for M4, requires engineering build.
* **ISF-4: EB auto-compute product (S1-S3)** — Product slot displays computed result after both factors placed. If not feasible, fallback: pre-populated product after correct factor validation.
* **ISF-5: EB tile reuse (S3, 3.3)** — For 8×8, student must place 8 twice. Requires duplicate tiles or tiles returning to palette. Option (b) preferred (more general).

### Success Criteria (§1.7.5)
* Student builds correct expression from labeled EB (Interactions 1.2, 1.5)
* Student builds correct expression from unlabeled EB (Interactions 2.2, 2.3, 2.4)
* Student builds correct expression from unstructured EB (Interactions 3.1, 3.2, 3.3)
* Both orientations accepted as correct (dual-orientation validation)
* Vocabulary used correctly in context (Guide models in On Correct; assessed in EC)
* Skip-counting accepted as computation strategy (never marked incorrect)

### EC.1: Build Expression — Unlabeled Slots [CREATE] [Type B]

* **Purpose:** Can the student build a correct multiplication expression using unlabeled EB slots? Tests the Section 2 skill: reading dimensions from the rectangle and placing factors without label guidance.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display) + Equation Builder (unlabeled slots).** 4×7 rectangle (4 rows, 7 columns). 28 squares shaded. Full grid. NO highlighting. NO dimension labels. EB: `[___] × [___] = [auto]`. **Tile palette: {3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8}.** Product auto-computes.
* **Guide:** "Here's a rectangle. Look at the rows and columns, and build the multiplication expression."
* **Prompt:** "Build the multiplication expression for this rectangle."
* **Student Action:** Drag-to-place (Equation Builder) — select 2 tiles from palette of 6
  * **Expected:** 4 into first slot, 7 into second slot (or 7, 4 — both accepted). Product 28 auto-computes.
* **Correct Answer:** 4 × 7 = 28 (or 7 × 4 = 28)
* **On Correct:** "4 × 7 = 28 square units."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** Type B — brief Guide transition + complete instruction; Prompt restates complete instruction. Tile palette includes off-by-one distractors (3, 5 for 4; 6, 8 for 7) following the Lesson's palette design pattern. 4×7 is a new rectangle not used in Lesson. Unlabeled slots match Lesson S2 — this is the "can they do it with some structure?" check. Dimension labels are OFF — student must count rows and columns from the grid alone.

### EC.2: Build Expression — No Scaffolding [CREATE] [Type B]

* **Purpose:** Can the student independently construct a complete multiplication expression with no template? Tests the Section 3 skill: building the full notation structure from scratch.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display) + Equation Builder (no scaffolding — tiles and symbols available, no pre-structured template).** 5×7 rectangle (5 rows, 7 columns). 35 squares shaded. Full grid. NO highlighting. NO dimension labels. **Tile palette: {4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9} + {×, =}.** Product auto-computes after valid `n × n` expression formed.
* **Guide:** "Now this one — no slots to guide you. Build the complete multiplication expression for this rectangle."
* **Prompt:** "Build the complete multiplication expression for this rectangle."
* **Student Action:** Drag-to-place (Equation Builder — unstructured) — arrange tiles to form expression
  * **Expected:** 5, ×, 7, = (or 7, ×, 5, =). Product 35 auto-computes.
* **Correct Answer:** 5 × 7 = 35 (or 7 × 5 = 35)
* **On Correct:** "5 × 7 = 35 square units."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** Type B — brief context about scaffold removal + complete instruction in Guide; Prompt restates complete instruction. Full independence test — no template. Student must construct the expression structure AND select the correct factors. Tile palette follows S3 pattern: number tiles + symbol tiles. 5×7 is a genuinely new rectangle — no overlap with any Lesson interaction. The unstructured EB is the hardest interaction type in M4's toolkit. Distractors: 4 (off-by-one↓ from 5), 6 (off-by-one↑ from 5 / off-by-one↓ from 7), 8 (off-by-one↑ from 7), 9 (nearby).

### EC.3: Area Without Equation Builder [IDENTIFY] [Type B]

* **Purpose:** Can the student determine the area of a gridded rectangle using multiplication WITHOUT any Equation Builder support? Tests whether multiplication fluency for area has transferred beyond the notation tool.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** 3×9 rectangle (3 rows, 9 columns). 27 squares shaded. Full grid. NO highlighting. NO dimension labels. NO Equation Builder.
* **Guide:** "Last one. No equation builder this time. What is the area of this rectangle?"
* **Prompt:** "What is the area of this rectangle?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** 12 / 21 / 27 / 36
* **Correct Answer:** 27
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 27 = Correct (3 × 9 = 27, or skip-count: 9, 18, 27)
  * 12 = Added 3 + 9 (sum of factors — #4.0 variant: dimension-operation confusion)
  * 21 = 3 × 7 — a familiar multiplication fact students may confuse with 3 × 9, or may result from miscounting columns
  * 36 = 4 × 9 (off-by-one on rows — miscounted 3 as 4)
* **On Correct:** "27 square units."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** Type B — brief context about tool removal + complete instruction in Guide; Prompt restates complete instruction. MC selection, no EB. This is the ultimate independence test: can the student go from rectangle → area using multiplication (or skip-counting) without any notation scaffolding? The MC distractors target #4.0 (wrong rectangle for expression) in its most basic form: getting the wrong area because of a dimension error. The sum-of-factors distractor (12) catches students who add instead of multiply. 3×9 is new to the module. Skip-counting by 9 (9, 18, 27) is only 3 steps — manageable for students who don't know 3×9. Skip-counting by 3 (3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18, 21, 24, 27) is longer but uses Grade 2 prior knowledge.

> **Design Note (No SUPPORT tier):** TVP does not specify a SUPPORT tier for M4 EC. The 3-problem structure (unlabeled → no scaffolding → no EB) itself serves as a built-in difficulty gradient. Students who struggle on EC.2 or EC.3 but succeed on EC.1 provide useful diagnostic data for the Pipeline without needing an additional support interaction.

### Connection Task S.1: How You Got Here [Type B — Progressive]

* **Purpose:** M1→M4 progression callback. Show the same rectangle being approached four different ways — one per module. Students see that multiplication is the latest (and most efficient) tool for finding area, building on everything they've learned since M1. This is the TVP's "M1 (tiled and counted) → M2 (tiled correctly) → M3 (skip-counted rows) → M4 (multiplied)" beat.
* **Task Type:** Type B (Progressive Challenge) — four steps building from recognition to generalization.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** Four panels showing the SAME 3×7 rectangle, each styled to represent one module's approach:

**Panel 1 — M1 (Tiling):**
* **Visual:** 3×7 rectangle with tiles numbered 1-21 (sequential count visible).
* **Guide:** "Remember when you first learned about area? You counted every single tile. 1, 2, 3... all the way to 21."

**Panel 2 — M2 (Accurate Tiling):**
* **Visual:** 3×7 rectangle with neat, gap-free tiles (clean grid, no numbering).
* **Guide:** "Then you learned to tile carefully — no gaps, no overlaps. The tiles fill the rectangle perfectly."

**Panel 3 — M3 (Skip-Counting):**
* **Visual:** 3×7 rectangle with row highlighting: "7... 14... 21."
* **Guide:** "Then you saw the rows: 3 rows of 7. Skip-count: 7, 14, 21. Way faster than counting every tile."

**Panel 4 — M4 (Multiplication):**
* **Visual:** 3×7 rectangle with dimension labels (3 and 7) + the expression "3 × 7 = 21" displayed beside it.
* **Guide:** "And now? You write 3 × 7 = 21. The multiplication expression says exactly what the rectangle shows — 3 rows of 7. Which way of finding area is the newest thing you learned?"

* **Prompt:** "Which way of finding area is the newest thing you learned?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** "Counting every tile" / "Tiling carefully" / "Skip-counting by rows" / "Writing a multiplication expression"
* **Correct Answer:** "Writing a multiplication expression"
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * "Writing a multiplication expression" = Correct — M4's new skill
  * "Counting every tile" = M1 (foundational, not new)
  * "Tiling carefully" = M2 (accurate tiling, not new)
  * "Skip-counting by rows" = M3 (structure-counting, not new)
* **On Correct:** "Exactly. Multiplication is the newest tool — and it came from everything you already knew. Rows of tiles became skip-counts, and skip-counts became multiplication."
* **Connection:** Multiplication expression-building (M4) is the latest step in a progression from counting (M1) → tiling (M2) → skip-counting (M3) → symbolic notation (M4). Each module's approach builds on the previous one.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline (light — brief redirect only)

> **Design Note:** The four-panel layout shows the M1→M4 progression visually on the SAME rectangle (3×7). This makes the efficiency gain concrete: same 21 tiles, but the approach keeps getting more powerful. The MC question is deliberately easy — the point isn't assessment, it's narration. The On Correct response makes the progression explicit: tiles → skip-counts → multiplication. M2's panel is included for completeness but doesn't get much air — accurate tiling is a means, not a strategy. The system animation of M3's row highlighting can be brief (1-2 seconds).

### Connection Task S.2: The Formula Has a Name [Type A — Pattern Discovery]

* **Purpose:** "Area = length × width" formula emergence. This is the culminating insight reserved from Lesson 3.3 — the formula that NAMES what students have been doing across M1-M4. Students look at three completed expressions and discover the pattern.
* **Task Type:** Type A (Pattern Discovery) — present multiple examples, student identifies the commonality.
* **Visual:** Three completed rectangle-expression pairs displayed simultaneously:

| Rectangle | Dimension Labels | Expression |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 3×7 (from S.1) | length 3, width 7 | 3 × 7 = 21 |
| 5×6 (from Lesson 2.2) | length 5, width 6 | 5 × 6 = 30 |
| 8×8 (from Lesson 3.3) | length 8, width 8 | 8 × 8 = 64 |

* **Guide:** "Look at all three. The length, the width, and the expression. What's the pattern?"
* **Prompt:** "What's the pattern?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** "The length times the width equals the area" / "The bigger numbers always come first" / "All the rectangles are the same size" / "The area is always the length plus the width"
* **Correct Answer:** "The length times the width equals the area"
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * "The length times the width equals the area" = Correct — the formula pattern
  * "The bigger numbers always come first" = Incorrect — factor order varies (3×7, 5×6, 8×8)
  * "All the rectangles are the same size" = Incorrect — three different sizes
  * "The area is always the length plus the width" = #4.0 variant — addition instead of multiplication
* **On Correct:** "That's it. Length times width equals the area. Every single time. Mathematicians write it like this: **Area = length × width.** You've been using this formula the whole time — now it has a name!"
* **Connection:** The formula "Area = length × width" names the pattern students have been executing since Lesson S1 — reading dimensions from rectangles and building multiplication expressions. This is the culminating recognition moment that unifies the entire module.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline (light — mastery assumed)

> **Design Note:** The formula reveal is deliberately underplayed: "You've been using this formula the whole time — now it has a name!" This is the RECOGNITION framing from the Warmup (§1.6) applied to the formula level. Just as × was introduced as a label for what students already did ("4 rows of 6 IS 4 × 6"), the formula is introduced as a label for what students have been doing all session. The three rectangles span Early (3×7 Mid, actually — but first introduced in S.1), Mid (5×6), and Late (8×8) to show the formula works across all sizes. The addition distractor (length + width) catches the #4.0 sum-of-factors misconception one final time. Dimension labels are VISIBLE on all three rectangles — this is the first time students see "length," "width," and the expression together on multiple rectangles simultaneously. The visual pattern (length on one side, width on another, expression beside) makes the formula self-evident.

> **Voice Note:** "You've been using this formula the whole time — now it has a name" — this is the emotional payoff of the entire module. The recognition framing ("you already knew this") is M4's consistent pedagogical move, from × in the Warmup to the formula in Synthesis. Avoid "I'm going to teach you a formula" framing — the formula was always there, students just didn't have a name for it.

### Connection Task S.3: Every Multiplication Fact Is an Area [Type A — Generalization]

* **Purpose:** Bridge from rectangle area to multiplication broadly. Per TVP: "Every multiplication fact is an area!" Students see that ANY multiplication they know describes a rectangle — connecting M4 back to Unit 1 multiplication and forward to all future area work.
* **Task Type:** Type A (Generalization) — extending a specific insight to the general case.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** A blank grid space where the system builds a rectangle based on the student's selection.
* **Guide:** "Here's something cool. Pick any multiplication fact you know."
* **Prompt:** "Pick a multiplication fact."
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** "2 × 5 = 10" / "4 × 3 = 12" / "6 × 6 = 36" / "3 × 8 = 24"
* **Correct Answer:** Any selection (no wrong answer — all options demonstrate the principle)
* **System Response (on any selection):** The corresponding rectangle appears on screen, fully gridded, with dimension labels matching the factors.
* **On Correct (per selection):**
  * "2 × 5 = 10" → "Look — 2 rows of 5. A rectangle with area 10 square units. Your multiplication fact IS a rectangle!"
  * "4 × 3 = 12" → "There it is — 4 rows of 3. Area: 12 square units. The multiplication fact built the rectangle!"
  * "6 × 6 = 36" → "A square! 6 rows of 6. Area: 36 square units. Even squares are rectangles with multiplication expressions."
  * "3 × 8 = 24" → "3 rows of 8. Area: 24 square units. Every multiplication fact you know is already an area."
* **Guide (after response):** "Every multiplication fact is an area. 2 × 5 is a rectangle. 6 × 6 is a square. Any two numbers you multiply — that's a rectangle waiting to be drawn."
* **Connection:** Any multiplication fact describes a rectangle — this bridges M4's area work back to Unit 1 multiplication and forward to all future area applications. The visual-to-symbolic connection runs BOTH directions: rectangle → expression AND expression → rectangle.
* **Remediation:** N/A (no wrong answer)

> **Design Note:** This task makes the TVP's "Every multiplication fact is an area!" beat interactive. The student CHOOSES the fact, the system BUILDS the rectangle — making the connection visceral rather than abstract. All four options produce valid rectangles within the data constraints (factors 2-10, products ≤ 100). The 6×6 option includes the square case (echoing Lesson 3.3's 8×8). The Guide's final statement generalizes beyond the four options. No wrong answer — any selection demonstrates the principle.

### Metacognitive Reflection S.4: Grid or Expression? [Type B]

* **Purpose:** Students reflect on which representation helped them most — the grid or the expression. Planning-focused reflection (strongest for age 8-9). M4 used two primary tools (Grid Rectangles + Equation Builder), so Type 3 (Tool/Approach Preference) is the natural fit.
* **Reflection Type:** Type 3 (Tool/Approach Preference) — "Which tool made more sense to you?"
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** A 4×5 rectangle displayed with both the grid AND the expression "4 × 5 = 20" beside it.
* **Guide:** "You used the grid and the multiplication expression today. Which one makes it easier to find the area?"
* **Prompt:** "Which makes it easier to find the area?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:**
    * "The grid — I can see all the tiles"
    * "The expression — it's quick and clear"
    * "Both — they show the same thing different ways"
    * "I'm not sure yet"
* **Correct Answer:** Any selection (no wrong answer — reflection has no incorrect response)
* **On Correct (per selection):**
  * "The grid" → "The grid makes every tile visible. You can always check your answer by counting. That's a solid foundation."
  * "The expression" → "The expression captures the whole rectangle in one line. Length times width — done. That's efficient thinking."
  * "Both" → "That's the big idea — the grid and the expression are two ways of saying the same thing. Having both makes you flexible."
  * "I'm not sure yet" → "That's fine — you're still building your sense of which tools fit where. You'll figure it out as you keep going."
* **Connection:** Students identify which representation (visual or symbolic) supports their thinking — building metacognitive awareness of tool preferences that will serve them in M5+ as representations shift from grid to labeled diagrams.
* **Remediation:** N/A (no wrong answer — reflection)

> **Design Note:** All four options are valid — no wrong answer in reflection (per Playbook §3E). The "Both" option captures the module's deepest insight: the grid and the expression are equivalent representations. Guide responses are ONE sentence each per Playbook. "I'm not sure yet" is explicitly supported — it's an honest answer that doesn't need correction. The 4×5 rectangle is a fresh, simple example not used in Lesson or EC — chosen for clarity, not assessment.

## Identity-Building Closure S.5 + M5 Bridge [Type A]

* **Purpose:** Specific, growth-oriented affirmation referencing what the student actually did. Bridge to M5 per TVP: "You can find area of ANY rectangle now. But we've been using 'square units' — tomorrow we'll learn about SPECIFIC square units that everyone uses."
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** The 3×7 rectangle from the Opening Frame. Dimension labels visible. Expression "3 × 7 = 21 square units" displayed.
* **Guide:** "When we started, multiplication was just a symbol. Now you see it everywhere — in the rows, in the dimensions, in the formula. You connected what you could SEE to what you could WRITE. That's Area = length × width, and you built that yourself."
* **Guide (M5 bridge):** "You can find the area of any rectangle now. But we've been calling everything 'square units.' Next time, you'll learn about specific square units that everyone uses — like square inches and square centimeters. Same formula, but the units will matter."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** Closure references the specific behavioral arc: symbol → visual connection → formula. "You built that yourself" attributes the growth to the student (autonomy support). The M5 bridge follows TVP exactly: "But what SIZE are those square units?" — reframed as "specific square units that everyone uses." This creates curiosity about M5 without teaching M5 content. "Square inches" and "square centimeters" are MENTIONED as a preview, not introduced as vocabulary — they're M5's responsibility. The 3×7 rectangle returns as the visual thread from Opening through Closure, creating a narrative arc within Synthesis.

> **Voice Note:** "You connected what you could SEE to what you could WRITE" — this is M4's behavioral punchline. It references the visual-to-symbolic connection that IS the module's One Thing. The formula statement ("That's Area = length × width") lands here as a callback to S.2's reveal, not a new teaching moment.

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS (KDD)

## Purpose

KDD documents the pedagogical design choices that make this module work. Each entry explains WHAT was decided and WHY it matters for student learning. A writer seeing this module for the first time should understand why it works this way from the KDDs alone.

---

### KDD-1: Recognition Framing Over Efficiency Framing

The × symbol and the "Area = length × width" formula are both introduced through RECOGNITION ("you already knew this — here's what mathematicians call it"), never through EFFICIENCY ("this is faster than skip-counting"). Students who skip-count by 2s, 5s, 10s may not feel the "faster" motivation — but they will feel the recognition that structure they already see has a mathematical name. This framing runs from the Warmup (× as label) through the Synthesis (formula as name), and it directly prevents misconception #4.0 (treating multiplication as an arbitrary procedure disconnected from structure).

---

### KDD-2: "Area = length × width" Reserved for Synthesis

The formula does NOT appear in the Lesson — not even casually in an On Correct response. It is reserved for Synthesis §1.9 S.2, where it serves as the culminating insight that names what students have been doing across M1-M4. Introducing it in a Lesson interaction would undercut its weight: the formula deserves a moment where students discover it as a PATTERN across examples, not encounter it as a throwaway line. Every Lesson On Correct carefully avoids stating the formula. The Synthesis reveal uses recognition framing: "You've been using this formula the whole time — now it has a name!"

---

### KDD-3: Product Auto-Computes in Equation Builder

Students do NOT drag product tiles. The product appears automatically after both factors are placed. M4's cognitive focus is factor mapping (reading dimensions from the rectangle → placing them in the expression), not computation. Products range up to 64 (8×8) — requiring mental multiplication of 8×8 would shift the task from expression-building to arithmetic, which is not M4's learning goal. Auto-compute also solves an engineering constraint: the tile set is 2-10, but products go up to 64. There are no 2-digit product tiles.

---

### KDD-4: EB Palette Uses Diagnostic Distractors

Per Unit 1 M8 precedent, tile palettes include both correct factors PLUS diagnostic distractors (off-by-one, sum-of-factors, carryover, between-factors). A palette with only the correct numbers makes the EB trivially easy. Palettes are specified per-interaction in the EB Palette Configuration section (§1.7). Palette size progresses: 5-6 tiles in S1, 7 tiles in S2, 6 number tiles + {×, =} in S3. EC palettes follow the same pattern.

---

### KDD-5: CRA Mapping Is R→A Transition, Not Standard 4-Stage

M4 sits at the Representational → Abstract boundary. Students work with grid representations (R) from their first interaction, and the cognitive work is connecting visual structure to symbolic notation (A). There are no manipulatives and no concrete stage within M4 — Concrete was M1-M2. The Lesson uses: R→A Scaffolded (1.1-1.2, 1.5), Relational (1.4 — dedicated comparison), R→A Independent (2.1-3.3), with Application deferred to EC+Practice. This is honest labeling of what M4 actually does, not forced mapping to a 4-stage template.

---

### KDD-6: Both Factor Orientations Always Accepted

Every EB interaction accepts both R×C and C×R (e.g., 4×7 and 7×4 are both correct). M4 explicitly teaches commutativity through the rotation key beat (Lesson 1.3→1.4, 2.1→2.4). Marking one orientation as incorrect would contradict the module's own teaching. The system validates that BOTH factors are present in the expression, not their order. The only module that enforces factor order is Unit 1 M8, which has a different pedagogical purpose.

---

### KDD-7: Dimension Labels as Coupled Scaffold

Dimension labels (numeric annotations on two adjacent sides) appear in Mid (when "length" and "width" vocabulary lands in Lesson 2.2) and remain through Late and Synthesis. They are OFF in Early (Warmup, S1) because students haven't learned "length/width" yet, and OFF in EC primary flow (independence test) but available On Correct/Remediation. This coupling — vocabulary and visual appearing together, disappearing together — ensures students always have the visual support when they need the vocabulary, and always face the independence test when being assessed. Labels supplement the grid in M4; they replace the grid starting M7.

---

### KDD-8: EC Tests EB Proficiency by Design

Known Pattern #13 says "EC tests internalized criteria, not tool proficiency." In M4, the Equation Builder IS the notation tool that bridges visual-to-symbolic understanding. Testing at different scaffold levels (unlabeled → no scaffolding → no EB) IS testing whether the student has internalized the connection. EC.1 tests with slot structure. EC.2 tests without structure. EC.3 tests without the tool entirely. This directly matches the TVP's three assessed skills.

---

### KDD-9: No Equation Builder in Synthesis

Synthesis uses Grid Rectangles (Display) only — no Equation Builder. The EB is a learning scaffold; Synthesis is post-mastery reflection. Using the EB in Synthesis would make it feel like more Practice. The formula "Area = length × width" appears as text alongside rectangles, not as an EB interaction. Students see the formula as a CONCLUSION, not a task.

---

### KDD-10: Warmup × Introduction Serves Dual Population

Per Decision #4, students MAY have encountered × in Unit 1 M7-M8. The Warmup introduces × as a LABEL for "rows of" — meaningful through the structure connection regardless of prior exposure. For Unit 1 students: callback and deepening. For non-Unit-1 students: genuine introduction through recognition. The same framing works for both populations because it grounds × in the visual structure students HAVE seen (M3), not in prior arithmetic.

---

### KDD-11: 3×4 as Supplementary Early Rectangle

Interaction 1.5 uses 3×4 (area 12), which is not on the TVP's Early example list (2×4, 3×5, 4×6). TVP Early examples are exhausted by interactions 1.1-1.3. 3×4 is within Early data constraints (factors 2-6, area 8-24) and serves as the independent practice + "expression" vocabulary moment. Documented because the dimension is not TVP-specified.

---

### KDD-12: M5 Bridge Mentions Named Units as Preview

The Synthesis closure mentions "square inches and square centimeters" as a PREVIEW of M5, not as vocabulary introduction. These terms are M5's responsibility. The mention creates curiosity ("But what SIZE are those square units?") without teaching — students hear the words but aren't expected to use them. This follows the TVP's bridge specification exactly.

---

### KDD-13: EC Dimensions Exclude Lesson Rotations

EC rectangles must differ from ALL Lesson values AND their rotations. The module teaches that R×C and C×R are the SAME rectangle (commutativity), so using a rotation of a Lesson rectangle in EC would allow recall-based answering rather than genuine grid-reading. EC.1 (4×7), EC.2 (5×7), and EC.3 (3×9) have no rotation overlap with any Lesson or Warmup rectangle.

---

### KDD-14: Practice MC Shows Single Correct Orientation per Problem

Expression Selection MC problems (Practice Type 2a) include only ONE correct orientation per option set. Both R×C and C×R are valid expressions, but showing both in a single-select MC creates a 50% guess rate and confuses the interaction type. The system varies which orientation appears across problems. For commutativity reinforcement, a separate Multi-Select variant (Type 2b) explicitly asks students to "Select ALL" valid expressions — used in ~25-30% of Expression Selection problems.

---

### KDD-15: Synthesis Omits Dedicated Type D (Representation Transfer) Task

The Synthesis Phase Playbook recommends at least one Type D (Representation Transfer) task for Middle Modules. M4's Synthesis intentionally omits a dedicated Type D because visual-to-symbolic transfer is the module's entire cognitive arc — every Lesson interaction is already a representation transfer task (grid → expression). Adding a labeled Type D in Synthesis would repackage the Lesson's core activity as a reflection task, making Synthesis feel like more Practice rather than consolidation. S.2 (formula emergence) and S.3 ("every multiplication fact is an area") both involve simultaneous visual and symbolic representations, giving Synthesis natural transfer qualities without a dedicated task. Modules where representation transfer is a COMPONENT of learning (not the whole thing) are better candidates for explicit Type D tasks.

---

### KDD-16: EC.3 Removes Equation Builder Entirely (Computation Required)

EC.3 is the only EC problem with no Equation Builder at all — the student must determine area via MC selection, computing 3 × 9 mentally or by skip-counting. KDD-3 explains why the EB auto-computes products (cognitive focus is factor mapping, not computation), but EC.3 flips that rationale: with the EB removed, the student MUST compute. This tests whether the multiplication-area connection holds without notation support and whether students can apply the "rows × per-row = area" principle independently. Skip-counting (9, 18, 27) remains a valid path — the test is conceptual understanding of how to find area from structure, not multiplication fact recall.
