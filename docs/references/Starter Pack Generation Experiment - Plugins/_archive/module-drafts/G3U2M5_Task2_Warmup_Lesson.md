# §1.6 WARMUP (2-4 minutes)

### Core Purpose

Activate students' area-finding confidence from M4, create a visual mystery (same rectangle, different tile counts), and build curiosity about WHY the numbers differ — positioning the Lesson to name and explain standard square units.

**Key Function:** The Warmup creates the conceptual NEED for standard units. By showing the same rectangle tiled two ways (with visibly different-sized squares) and withholding unit labels, it generates a genuine question: "Why does the same rectangle have different numbers of tiles?" This question becomes the Lesson's driving force. Secondarily, the Warmup validates M4 transfer — students still see rectangles as "rows × columns = area."

**Why this serves the concept:** M5's core risk is students treating square inches and square centimeters as arbitrary labels with no conceptual weight. If students encounter "square inch" and "square centimeter" as vocabulary-first, the terms feel like trivia. By first experiencing the PUZZLE (same shape, different counts), students have a felt need for the vocabulary — the unit names EXPLAIN what they're seeing. The Notice & Wonder structure creates student-owned questions that the Lesson answers. Additionally, this structure directly prevents M8 (square unit confusion) by making the visual contrast salient BEFORE names are attached.

**Test:** "If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson?" **YES.** Without the side-by-side unlabeled comparison, students enter the Lesson without having noticed the core phenomenon (same shape, different counts). The Lesson's unit naming would arrive without a context to anchor it. The "Why are the numbers different?" question wouldn't be the student's question — it would be the Guide's question imposed on them. The Warmup makes the mystery the student's own.

**Conscious Tradeoff — Multiplication Activation:** This Warmup activates area VOCABULARY and VISUAL REASONING (rectangles, tiles, same-shape observation) but does NOT include a multiplication calculation check. The rationale: (1) adding a calculation step pushes the Warmup toward assessment, violating WR-12 (no assessment framing); (2) multiplication is an M4 prerequisite skill that students are expected to have — it is activated in the Lesson at 1.1b (first student area calculation) before any interaction depends on it; (3) the Warmup's purpose is conceptual priming (the mystery), not skill verification. If playtesting reveals that students struggle with multiplication in Section 1, consider inserting a brief "How many tiles on the left?" count-and-multiply check into W.1 as a future revision.

### Interaction W.1: Same Rectangle, Different Tiles [ACTIVATION] [Type C]

* **Purpose:** Hook + Notice. Students see the same rectangle displayed with two different grid overlays side by side. The grids are visibly different sizes (inch-scale squares are larger than cm-scale squares), but NO unit labels appear. Students notice that the tiles are different sizes — the observation that drives M5's entire teaching arc. M4 callback: "You know how to find area."
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display, side-by-side mode).** Left panel: A 4×3 rectangle tiled with large squares (inch-scale). Right panel: The SAME rectangle tiled with small squares (cm-scale, approximately 10×8). Both panels show the same rectangle outline. Full grids visible. NO dimension labels. NO unit labels. Grid squares are rendered at correct physical size ratio (inch squares visibly larger than cm squares).
* **Guide:** "You know how to find the area of a rectangle — you've been doing it. Check this out. Here are two pictures of the SAME rectangle. Look at the tiles in both pictures. What's different?"
* **Prompt:** "Look at the tiles in both pictures. What's different?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** "The tiles on the left are bigger" / "The tiles are the same size" / "The rectangles are different sizes"
* **Correct Answer:** "The tiles on the left are bigger"
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * "The tiles on the left are bigger" = Correct — the core observation that tile SIZE differs, which drives M5's entire teaching
  * "The tiles are the same size" = Incorrect — visual clearly shows different-sized squares
  * "The rectangles are different sizes" = Incorrect — the rectangle outline is identical in both panels; only the grid overlay differs
* **On Correct:** "You spotted it — the tiles on the left are bigger than the tiles on the right. Same rectangle, different-sized tiles. That changes things."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** W.1 opens with an M4 callback ("You know how to find the area of a rectangle — you've been doing it") serving as Engagement Anchor #1 (Personalization). The MC targets the single most pedagogically important observation: tile SIZE difference. Unlike an "All of these" aggregation, this format validates the specific noticing that drives the Lesson. "The tiles are the same size" and "The rectangles are different sizes" are both visually falsifiable — students who look at the display can rule them out. The 4×3 inch rectangle (12 squares) vs. approximately 10×8 cm rectangle (80 squares) is the TVP's first example pair. The grid size ratio must be physically accurate — the inch squares are visibly larger. NO unit labels on the grids.

> **Voice Note:** "Check this out" is invitational, Medium-High energy per Warmup standard. "That changes things" is a curiosity hook — the Guide signals that this observation matters without explaining HOW it matters. The observation acknowledgment ("You spotted it") is behavioral — references what the student selected.

### Interaction W.2: Why Are the Numbers Different? [CONCEPTUAL CHECK] [Type C]

* **Purpose:** Wonder + Judgment. Students consider WHY the same rectangle has different tile counts. This is the judgment/noticing task that requires thinking. The Guide does NOT answer — just validates the question as worth investigating.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display, side-by-side mode).** Same display as W.1. System adds tile count labels to each panel: Left panel shows "12 tiles," Right panel shows "80 tiles." Still NO unit labels on the grid squares themselves.
* **Guide:** "The left picture has 12 tiles. The right has 80 tiles. Same rectangle — so why are the numbers different? What do you think is going on?"
* **Prompt:** "Why does the same rectangle have different numbers of tiles?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** "The tiles are different sizes" / "Someone counted wrong" / "They're actually different rectangles" / "I'm not sure yet"
* **Correct Answer:** "The tiles are different sizes"
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * "The tiles are different sizes" = Correct — the core observation that drives M5's entire teaching
  * "Someone counted wrong" = Misconception precursor — assumes same tiles must give same count (M8 seed)
  * "They're actually different rectangles" = Incorrect — visual clearly shows same outline
  * "I'm not sure yet" = Valid uncertainty — redirected gently
* **On Correct:** "That's it. The tiles are different sizes. Smaller tiles, more of them to fill the same space. That's a big idea — and it has a name. Let's find out what it is."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** W.2 is the judgment task (WR-5). The MC format provides scaffolding for the conceptual leap — students don't need to generate the insight independently, but they do need to select it from options. "Someone counted wrong" is a deliberate M8-precursor distractor — it captures the intuition that "same shape should give same number," which is exactly the misconception M5 addresses. The Guide's On Correct acknowledges the observation ("tiles are different sizes") and creates anticipation ("it has a name") without teaching. Engagement Anchor #2 is the Curiosity Gap created by "it has a name — let's find out what it is." **W.1/W.2 Observation Overlap (acknowledged):** W.1 targets tile SIZE as a physical observation ("The tiles on the left are bigger"); W.2 targets tile COUNT as a causal consequence ("The tiles are different sizes" explains WHY 12 ≠ 80). The progression is observation → explanation, not redundancy. Both interactions are needed because the COUNT mystery (12 vs. 80) depends on students having first noticed the SIZE difference.

> **Voice Note:** "That's a big idea — and it has a name" creates the curiosity gap that the bridge and Lesson will fulfill. The Guide does NOT say "square inch" or "square centimeter" — those words are reserved for the Lesson.

---

### Interaction W.3: Bridge to Lesson [Type A]

* **Purpose:** Create anticipation for the Lesson without teaching. Frame the Lesson as answering the question the student just identified ("Why different numbers?") and naming the different tile sizes.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display, side-by-side mode).** Same display as W.2 with tile counts visible. Still NO unit labels.
* **Guide:** "You've been calling all tiles 'square units.' But not all square units are the same size. Those different-sized tiles have specific names — and once you learn them, you'll understand exactly why 12 and 80 are both correct for the same rectangle. Let's find out."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** W.3 bridges from the Warmup mystery to the Lesson answer. "You've been calling all tiles 'square units'" is the M4 callback — students have used "square units" generically since M1. "Not all square units are the same size" is the conceptual bridge that the Lesson will elaborate. "Those different-sized tiles have specific names" creates anticipation for vocabulary without introducing it. The phrase "12 and 80 are both correct" preemptively addresses M8 (the misconception that one must be wrong). Per Warmup Playbook: the bridge creates a question in the student's mind; the Purpose Frame at the Lesson opening answers it.

> **Voice Note:** "Let's find out" is invitational and forward-looking. Energy transitions from Medium-High (Warmup) to Medium (Lesson).

## Core Purpose + Pedagogical Flow

> **M5 CRA Stage Position.** The Backbone (§1.0) identifies M5's CRA stage as "Representational — full grids return because GRID SIZE is the new concept." M5 uses full grids throughout all activities. The progression is NOT a standard grid-fading CRA — instead, M5 introduces NEW CONTENT (standard square units) on a FAMILIAR representation (full grids). The CRA labels below reflect what M5 actually does: students first observe unit squares (Concrete/Representational), then compare the same rectangle in two units (Relational), then formalize vocabulary and the inverse relationship (Abstract), then practice independently with correct unit labels (Application).

| M5 CRA Stage | What Students Actually Do | Interactions |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Concrete/Representational** | Students see 1×1 unit squares with dimension labels ("1 in" / "1 cm"). Guide names them ("square inch," "square centimeter"). Students identify unit squares and distinguish linear from square units. Full grids visible. | 1.1-1.5 |
| **Relational** | SAME rectangle displayed with inch grid AND cm grid. Students calculate area in both units, then see the two results side by side. Guide explicitly states the pattern: "Smaller unit → bigger number. Same rectangle, same area, different numbers." Student confirms through comparison judgment. | 2.1-2.4 |
| **Abstract** | Vocabulary fully formalized — "square inch," "square centimeter," "standard unit" — and applied to the comparison. The inverse relationship ("It takes more of a smaller unit to cover the same area") is stated explicitly as the module's key insight. | 2.3-2.4 (formalization within Relational context) |
| **Application** | Single-unit problems. Students find area, include unit labels in answers. No side-by-side support. MC comparison problem tests whether the insight transferred. | 3.1-3.4 |

## Forbidden Phrases

* ❌ **"sq in" / "sq cm" / "in²" / "cm²"** — Abbreviations not introduced in M5 per TVP SME Decision. Full names only. Using abbreviations would skip a vocabulary step students haven't reached.
* ❌ **"two-dimensional" / "2D"** — Jargon for Grade 3. Use "area" or "flat shape" instead.
* ❌ **"linear inch"** — Awkward phrasing that adds confusion. Use "inch" for length, "square inch" for area.
* ❌ **"metric" / "standard" as system names** — Creates false dichotomy. Both inches and centimeters are standard units.
* ❌ **"square feet" / "square meters"** — M6 content. Mentioning these units teaches beyond M5 scope.
* ❌ **"convert" / "conversion" / "How many square centimeters in a square inch?"** — Unit conversion is M6+ content.
* ❌ **"perimeter" / "distance around"** — Not in Unit 2 scope (Decision #5).
* ❌ **"The bigger number means bigger area"** — Directly reinforces the secondary misconception. Never state this even to refute it in the Warmup or early Lesson — address it only through comparison in Section 2.
* ❌ **"The area is 12" (without unit)** — Every area answer must include the unit label. Guide and Prompt must model this consistently.
* ❌ **"carefully" / "thoroughly" / "systematically"** — Red Flag Words per Voice Script Prompt. Reference observable behavior only.
* ❌ **"You understand" / "You're thinking" / "confused"** — Assumed internal states per Voice conventions.

## Misconception Prevention

### M8: Square Unit Confusion (PRIMARY)

**Prevention embedded in these interactions:**
* **1.1-1.2 (Unit Naming):** Explicit definitions ("A square inch is a square with sides of 1 inch") anchor each unit to its defining characteristic. Students see the 1×1 grid with dimension labels — the visual IS the definition.
* **1.4-1.5 (Linear vs. Square):** 2 Lesson MC problems (1.4 inch-specific binary, 1.5 generalization across both units) plus 1-2 Practice problems (including the cm-specific binary moved from old 1.5). This targets the specific behavioral indicator: conflating linear and square units. The TVP's 3-4 MC requirement is met across Lesson + Practice combined.
* **2.1-2.2 (Same Rectangle, Both Units):** Students calculate area of the SAME rectangle in both units and write the full unit label. The side-by-side display makes the count difference salient.
* **2.3 (Comparison):** Guide explicitly states: "Same rectangle, different numbers. The rectangle didn't change — it takes MORE of a smaller unit to cover the same space." The explicit statement prevents the misconception from forming.
* **3.1-3.4 (Independent Practice):** Every answer requires the full unit label. Unit label omission is caught and corrected via Pipeline remediation.

### Bigger Number = Bigger Shape (SECONDARY)

**Prevention embedded in these interactions:**
* **2.3 (Comparison Judgment):** MC options include "80 is bigger, so that's the bigger area" as a distractor. Students must reject this and select the correct interpretation.
* **2.4 (Consolidation):** Guide's explicit statement: "12 square inches and 80 square centimeters measure the SAME rectangle. The numbers are different because the units are different sizes." This directly addresses the magnitude confusion.
* **W.2 (Warmup precursor):** "Someone counted wrong" distractor in Warmup plants the seed that both numbers can be correct — addressed fully in Section 2.

## Section 1: Naming the Units — Early (Concrete/Representational)

### Interaction 1.1: This Is a Square Inch [WORKED EXAMPLE — Full] [Type A → Type C]

* **Purpose:** First worked example. Guide demonstrates what a square inch IS by showing a 1×1 inch grid square with dimension labels. Think-aloud models how to connect the linear unit (inch) to the area unit (square inch). Students observe, then identify a square inch on a small rectangle.

### Interaction 2.3: Same Rectangle, Different Numbers [CONCEPTUAL CHECK] [Type C] [RELATIONAL]

* **Purpose:** THE RELATIONAL INTERACTION. Both measurements appear side by side. Guide explicitly states the pattern. Student makes a comparison judgment confirming the key insight. This is the module's conceptual climax — "Same shape + different unit = different number."
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display, side-by-side mode).** Left panel: The 4×3 inch-grid rectangle from 2.1, labeled "Area = 12 square inches." Right panel: The same rectangle with cm grid from 2.2, labeled "Area = 80 square centimeters." Both panels show the SAME rectangle outline. The inch grid has fewer, larger tiles. The cm grid has more, smaller tiles.
* **Guide:** "Look — you measured the SAME rectangle two different ways. 12 square inches, and 80 square centimeters. The rectangle didn't change. The rectangle didn't grow. So why are the numbers different?"
* **Prompt:** "Why does the same rectangle show 12 square inches AND 80 square centimeters?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** "The square centimeters are smaller, so more of them fit" / "80 is bigger, so that's the bigger area" / "Someone made a mistake — the numbers should match" / "The rectangles are actually different sizes"
* **Correct Answer:** "The square centimeters are smaller, so more of them fit"
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * "The square centimeters are smaller, so more of them fit" = Correct — the inverse relationship between unit size and count
  * "80 is bigger, so that's the bigger area" = SECONDARY MISCONCEPTION — bigger number ≠ bigger area (the rectangle is the same)
  * "Someone made a mistake — the numbers should match" = M8 — assumes same shape must give same count regardless of unit
  * "The rectangles are actually different sizes" = Visual misreading — the rectangle outline is identical
* **On Correct:** "That's it. The square centimeters are smaller, so it takes MORE of them to cover the same space. 12 square inches and 80 square centimeters — same rectangle, same area, different numbers. It takes more of a smaller unit to cover the same area."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** This is the sole Relational interaction (LR-2). It displays two representations simultaneously (inch grid and cm grid of the SAME rectangle), the Guide explicitly states the pattern ("It takes more of a smaller unit to cover the same area"), and the student confirms through a comparison judgment (MC). The "80 is bigger, so that's the bigger area" distractor directly targets the secondary misconception. The "Someone made a mistake" distractor targets M8. The On Correct delivers the module mantra: "It takes more of a smaller unit to cover the same area." This phrase must appear exactly — it's from the TVP and Module Mapping notes.

> **Voice Note:** "The rectangle didn't change. The rectangle didn't grow." — repetition for emphasis at a key conceptual moment. The phrasing is concrete and behavioral, not abstract. "Same rectangle, same area, different numbers" echoes the Warmup language, creating a through-line.

> **Scaffolding Note:** The side-by-side display must render the SAME rectangle outline in both panels. The grid overlay is the only difference. If the rectangles appear to be different sizes on screen, the visual contradicts the teaching. Per §1.5 guardrails: "Grid rendering must be proportionally accurate."

## Section 3: Independent Practice with Equation Builder — Late (Application)

### Equation Builder Configuration for M5

> **Design Principle:** The Equation Builder in M5 serves a DIFFERENT purpose than in M4. In M4, the EB taught expression-building (visual → symbolic mapping). In M5, the multiplication is assumed — the EB's role is to enforce the **unit label as part of the answer**. The M5 EB template adds a unit-label slot that students must fill correctly. This is the pedagogically distinct skill the TVP identifies: "Students identify dimensions from grid, multiply, state area with correct unit name."

**M5 EB Template:** `[___] × [___] = [auto-product] [unit label slot]`

**Number tiles:** Correct factors + diagnostic distractors (per M4 palette design pattern)

**Unit label tiles:** `square inches` / `square centimeters` / `inches` / `centimeters`

The unit label distractors (`inches`, `centimeters`) directly test M8 — students who select the linear unit instead of the square unit reveal conceptual confusion. The product auto-computes after both factors are placed (same as M4). The student's final action is selecting the correct unit label tile.

## Module-Specific Lesson Guidance

### Unit Label Convention

Every Lesson interaction that involves an area answer MUST include the full unit name in:
- The **Correct Answer** field
- The **On Correct** response
- At least one **MC option** that has the correct number but wrong unit (M8 diagnostic)

Never display an area answer as just a number. "The area is 12" is ALWAYS wrong — it must be "The area is 12 square inches" or "The area is 12 square centimeters."

### Equation Builder in M5

Per Decision #8 and the Backbone, multiplication in M5 is a TOOL, not content. Students use "rows × per row = area" from M4 without relearning expression building. However, the Equation Builder IS used in Late Lesson (Section 3) per TVP specification, with a critical M5-specific addition: a **unit-label slot**. The EB template is `[___] × [___] = [auto] [unit label]`, and the unit-label tile palette includes both square units (correct) and linear units (M8 distractors). This means the EB serves a different purpose in M5 than in M4: in M4, the EB taught visual-to-symbolic mapping; in M5, the EB enforces unit-label production as part of the mathematical expression. The factor-identification skill is assumed from M4; the new skill is attaching the correct unit.

**EB is NOT used in Sections 1-2.** The Warmup and early/mid Lesson use MC selection for area answers. The EB appears only in Section 3 (Application phase), after students have learned the unit names and the inverse relationship. This sequencing ensures the EB introduces one new element (unit-label slot) on a familiar tool, not two new concepts simultaneously.

### Grid Permanence

Per Decision #3, full grids remain visible throughout ALL M5 activities. The grid is not a fading scaffold — it IS the representation. Grid SIZE (unit type) is the new concept being learned. Grid fading begins M7.

## Incomplete Script Flags (§1.7.4)

* **ISF-1: Side-by-side grid rendering.** Sections 2 and 3 require the SAME rectangle displayed with two different grid overlays simultaneously. The rectangle outline must be identical in both panels; only the grid size differs. Engineering must confirm that Grid Rectangles supports dual-panel display with different grid scales. Fallback: sequential display (show inch, then switch to cm) — but this degrades the comparison because students can't see both at once.
* **ISF-2: Proportionally accurate grid rendering.** The 1-inch grid squares must render visibly larger than the 1-cm grid squares. If both are rendered at the same pixel size, the visual contrast disappears and the entire module's teaching fails. Per §1.5 guardrails: "Grid rendering must be proportionally accurate."
* **ISF-3: Unit labels on grid cells.** Individual grid cells need small labels ("1 square inch" or "1 square centimeter") visible in at least one cell per display. Must not clutter the display for larger rectangles.
* **ISF-4: Linear vs. square visual display (1.4-1.5).** Interactions 1.4-1.5 show a line segment alongside a filled square. This may require a visual format outside Grid Rectangles (e.g., a comparison card or annotation layer). Engineering must confirm display capability.
* **ISF-5: Equation Builder unit-label slot (3.1, 3.2, 3.4).** M5 EB template adds a unit-label tile slot after the auto-computed product: `[___] × [___] = [auto] [unit label]`. The unit-label palette must present 4 tiles: "square inches," "square centimeters," "inches," "centimeters." Engineering must confirm that the EB can support a non-numeric tile slot for the unit label. Fallback: unit label selected via a separate MC prompt after EB expression is built (degraded — loses the single-expression production experience but preserves the unit-selection test).

## Success Criteria (§1.7.5)

A student exits the Lesson ready for EC when they can:
1. Name a square inch and a square centimeter from visual displays (with dimension labels)
2. Distinguish "inch" (length) from "square inch" (area)
3. Find the area of a gridded rectangle and state the answer with the correct unit label
4. Explain (via MC selection) why the same rectangle gives different area numbers in different units
5. Apply the inverse relationship: smaller unit → bigger number
6. Build a multiplication expression with the correct unit label using the Equation Builder (e.g., 5 × 4 = 20 square inches)
