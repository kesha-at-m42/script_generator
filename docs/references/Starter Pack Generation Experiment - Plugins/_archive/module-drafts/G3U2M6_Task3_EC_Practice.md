### EC.1: Area in Square Feet [IDENTIFY] [Type B]

* **Purpose:** Can the student calculate area by counting/multiplying a sq ft grid? Tests S1 procedural skill with square feet — the first of M6's two new units.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** 5 ft × 7 ft rectangle. Full grid visible (35 squares). Rows and columns clearly countable. Unit label: "square feet."
* **Guide:** "Here's a rectangle measured in square feet. Find the area."
* **Prompt:** "What is the area in square feet?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** 12, 28, 35, 42
* **Correct Answer:** 35
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 35 = Correct (5 rows × 7 columns = 35 square feet)
  * 12 = Added dimensions (5 + 7) instead of multiplying
  * 28 = Miscounted rows as 4 (4 × 7 = 28, off-by-one)
  * 42 = Miscounted rows as 6 (6 × 7 = 42, off-by-one)
* **On Correct:** "35 square feet."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** Type B — brief instruction in Guide + complete instruction in Prompt. The Guide's brevity is deliberate — EC is assessment, not teaching. "Here's a rectangle measured in square feet" provides context without hinting at strategy. MC distractors target the two most common errors: additive (12) and off-by-one counting (28, 42). Product 35 is mid-range — harder than L.1 (12) but simpler than L.12 (24 with unit-selection overhead). Feedback is minimal per Playbook §3E: just the answer, no re-teaching.

### EC.3: Unit Selection — Three Contexts [COMPARE] [Type B]

* **Purpose:** Can the student select the appropriate unit for real-world contexts? Tests S2 unit-selection reasoning across Tier 1 (small), Tier 2 (large-unit distinction), and Tier 3 (boundary). This is a single interaction with three sub-parts, per TVP: "Three real-world contexts → select most appropriate unit each."
* **Visual:** Three context images displayed sequentially or side-by-side: (a) phone screen, (b) doormat, (c) notebook cover. No grids — this is a reasoning interaction.
* **Guide:** "For each thing below, pick the unit that makes the most sense to measure its area."
* **Prompt:** "Which unit would you use for each one?"

#### EC.3a: Phone Screen

* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** Square centimeters, Square inches, Square feet, Square meters
* **Correct Answer:** Square centimeters or Square inches (both accepted — phone screen is small; both small units are reasonable)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * Sq cm = Correct — a phone screen is small; sq cm is appropriate (Tier 1 small)
  * Sq in = Also correct — sq in is also appropriate for a small area
  * Sq ft = Incorrect — far too large for a phone screen (M8 trigger)
  * Sq m = Incorrect — far too large for a phone screen (M8 trigger)
* **On Correct:** "A phone screen is small — a small unit fits."

#### EC.3b: Doormat

* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** Square centimeters, Square inches, Square feet, Square meters
* **Correct Answer:** Square feet (single-correct — a doormat is roughly 1.5 ft × 2.5 ft ≈ 3.75 sq ft, well under 1 square meter)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * Sq ft = Correct — a doormat is a small-to-medium area; sq ft produces a practical count (3-6 square feet)
  * Sq m = Incorrect — a standard doormat is less than 1 square meter; you'd get a fraction, not a whole number (Tier 2 diagnostic: can the student distinguish WITHIN the large-unit family?)
  * Sq in = Incorrect — while technically possible, you'd need hundreds of squares for a doormat (~400-500 sq in); impractical
  * Sq cm = Incorrect — far too small; thousands of squares
* **On Correct:** "A doormat is small for square meters — square feet fit."

#### EC.3c: Notebook Cover

* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** Square centimeters, Square inches, Square feet, Square meters
* **Correct Answer:** Square inches or Square centimeters (both accepted — notebook cover is a Tier 3 boundary context; both small units are reasonable)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * Sq in = Correct — a notebook cover is a small-to-medium area; sq in is appropriate
  * Sq cm = Also correct — sq cm is also reasonable for a notebook-sized area
  * Sq ft = Incorrect — too large for a notebook cover
  * Sq m = Incorrect — far too large for a notebook cover
* **On Correct:** "A notebook cover is small — small units work."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline (applied to overall EC.3 interaction)

> **Design Note:** EC.3 is the TVP's "Three real-world contexts → select most appropriate unit each" problem. The three contexts were chosen to span all three Tiers of the unit-selection reasoning taught in the Lesson: (a) phone screen is Tier 1 small (obvious small unit — tests "small thing → small unit" heuristic), (b) doormat is Tier 2 (distinguishes WITHIN the large-unit family — a doormat is too small for sq m but right for sq ft), (c) notebook cover is Tier 3 boundary (sq in and sq cm both reasonable — the curriculum's own misconception note flags "notecard or book cover" as a boundary case). This Tier 1 → Tier 2 → Tier 3 progression creates a genuine difficulty ramp within the compound interaction. All three contexts are DIFFERENT from Lesson contexts (parking lot, playground, classroom floor, garden, gym floor, book cover) to ensure transfer, not recall.

> **Design Note (Dual-Correct Convention — Intentional Exception at EC.3b):** EC.3a and EC.3c maintain the dual-correct convention from the Lesson (both small units accepted for small contexts). EC.3b deliberately breaks this convention: sq ft is the only correct large unit because a doormat is under 1 sq m. This is the module's sole single-correct unit-selection item, and it exists because Tier 2 reasoning CANNOT be assessed under dual-correct — the whole point of Tier 2 is that there IS a better answer between sq ft and sq m for a given context. Without this item, a student could pass all unit-selection problems using only Tier 1 reasoning ("small thing → small unit, big thing → big unit"), which is a strictly simpler skill than what the Lesson taught. The Lesson explored Tier 2 in L.10 (classroom floor); the EC must verify that learning transferred.

### EC.4: Context + Grid — Picture Frame [IDENTIFY + CREATE] [Type B]

* **Purpose:** Can the student identify the appropriate unit from a context AND calculate the area? Tests S3 integration skill — the combined two-part interaction practiced in L.12-L.14. Uses sq in to ensure all four units are represented in EC.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display) + context image.** Image of a picture frame. Grid Rectangle: 7 in × 4 in, full grid (28 squares). Unit label initially hidden — revealed after unit selection.
* **Guide:** "One more. Here's a picture frame with a grid. First, pick the right unit. Then find the area."
* **Prompt:** "What unit would you use for a picture frame, and what is the area?"
* **Student Action:** Two-part response — (1) Unit selection: MC, then (2) Area calculation: MC
  * **Unit Options:** Square centimeters, Square inches, Square feet, Square meters
  * **Area Options (after unit selected):** 11, 21, 28, 35
* **Correct Answer:** (1) Square inches or Square centimeters (both accepted for small area), (2) 28 (if sq in: 7 × 4 = 28 square inches)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * Sq in or sq cm = Correct — a picture frame is a small area; both small units are appropriate
  * Sq ft or sq m = Incorrect — far too large for a picture frame (M8 trigger)
  * 28 = Correct (7 × 4 = 28)
  * 11 = Added dimensions (7 + 4)
  * 21 = Miscounted (7 × 3, off-by-one column)
  * 35 = Miscounted (7 × 5, off-by-one column) — also EC.1's answer, tests whether student is reading THIS grid
* **On Correct:** "28 square inches. Small area, small unit."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** EC.4 mirrors L.12-L.14's two-part structure (unit selection → area calculation). The picture frame context is fresh (not used in Lesson). Using sq in for EC.4 completes the four-unit coverage: EC.1 (sq ft), EC.2 (sq m), EC.3 (all four via selection), EC.4 (sq in). The 7×4=28 dimensions are new to the module. The distractor 35 = EC.1's answer — a student who carries over the previous answer rather than reading this grid would select it, providing useful diagnostic data. Two-part interaction requires engineering confirmation per ISF-3 (same as Lesson L.12-L.14).

> **Design Note (EC.4 tests small-unit selection):** A deliberate design choice: EC.4 uses a SMALL context (picture frame) after EC.3b tested a medium-sized context (doormat → sq ft). This confirms the student hasn't developed a "big units are always right" heuristic — the same error L.14 (book cover) was designed to catch in the Lesson. Using sq in rather than sq cm here differentiates from EC.3a (phone screen, where sq cm was more natural).

## Connection Task S.1: Rank the Units [Drag-to-Rank] [Type A — Pattern Discovery]

* **Purpose:** Students rank all four unit squares by actual size, using reasoning from labels and prior learning — not from visual cues on screen. This consolidates the unit-size hierarchy that was built across Warmup (visual comparison), Lesson (inverse relationship), and EC (unit-selection reasoning). The same-size visual display forces retrieval of learned size relationships.
* **Task Type:** Custom Drag-to-Rank (closest to Type A — Pattern Discovery: identifying the ordering pattern across four examples).
* **Visual:** Four squares displayed in a **scrambled** arrangement, ALL THE SAME SIZE on screen. Each square is labeled with its full unit name:

| Display Position | Label |
| :---- | :---- |
| Position 1 | square inch |
| Position 2 | square meter |
| Position 3 | square centimeter |
| Position 4 | square foot |

Target zone: a horizontal ranking bar with four slots labeled "Smallest → Largest" (left to right).

* **Guide:** "Here are four unit squares. They look the same size on screen, but you know they're NOT the same size in real life. Drag them into order — smallest to largest."
* **Prompt:** "Drag from smallest to largest."
* **Student Action:** Drag-to-rank (drag each square from scrambled area to the correct slot in the ranking bar)
* **Correct Order:** square centimeter → square inch → square foot → square meter (left to right)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * Correct order = Student has internalized the unit-size hierarchy from Lesson and EC
  * sq cm and sq in swapped = May confuse which small unit is smaller — review M5 visual comparison
  * sq ft and sq m swapped = May confuse which large unit is larger — this is exactly what M6 added to M5's knowledge
  * Random order = Student is guessing — may not have internalized unit sizes at all
* **On Correct:** "You got it — square centimeter, square inch, square foot, square meter. A square centimeter is like your fingertip. A square inch is like a sticky note. A square foot is like a floor tile. And a square meter is like a big rug. Smallest to biggest."
* **Connection:** The ranking consolidates the FULL four-unit system. M5 taught the first two (sq cm, sq in) and their size relationship. M6 added the second two (sq ft, sq m). This is the first time students have placed all four in a single ordered sequence — connecting M5's knowledge with M6's addition. The real-world anchors in the feedback ground each abstract unit name in a concrete, memorable image.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline (light — if student makes an error, briefly highlight the two swapped units: "Think about which one is bigger in real life — a [unit A] or a [unit B]?" Allow retry.)

> **Design Note:** The same-size visual display is the critical design choice (per Backbone §1.5). In the Warmup, students SAW that a square foot tile was bigger than a square inch tile. Here, that visual support is gone — all squares are identical on screen. This forces retrieval and reasoning, not perception. It's a deliberate scaffold removal that mirrors M7's broader theme (M7 removes grids entirely). If students can rank without seeing real sizes, they've internalized the hierarchy. The scrambled starting order prevents students from simply confirming a pre-arranged sequence. Feedback uses the TVP's real-world anchors verbatim (fingertip, sticky note, floor tile, big rug) to cement each unit in a concrete, memorable image. Per Decision #6, this MUST be a drag interaction, not tap-to-select.

> **ISF-S1: Drag-to-Rank interaction layout.** Engineering must confirm: (1) four same-size draggable squares with text labels, (2) horizontal target zone with four ordered slots, (3) snap-to-slot behavior, (4) visual feedback on correct placement, (5) ability to rearrange before confirming. Simpler alternative: vertical ranking (top = smallest, bottom = largest).

## Connection Task S.2: Match Units to Contexts [Drag-to-Match] [Type C — Real-World Bridge]

* **Purpose:** Students match each of the four units to a real-world context by dragging unit labels to context images. This consolidates the unit-selection reasoning practiced across Lesson S2 (L.9-L.11) and EC.3. It also completes Author Flag #11: both drag-to-rank AND drag-to-match appear in Synthesis.
* **Task Type:** Type C (Real-World Bridge) — linking abstract unit names to concrete, familiar contexts via drag-to-match.
* **Visual:** Two columns:
  * **Left column (draggable labels):** square centimeter, square inch, square foot, square meter (scrambled order)
  * **Right column (target images):** Four context images with brief captions:

| Image | Caption | Target Unit |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Postage stamp | "a postage stamp" | square centimeter |
| Sticky note | "a sticky note" | square inch |
| Floor tile | "a floor tile" | square foot |
| Classroom rug | "a big classroom rug" | square meter |

* **Guide:** "Now match each unit to the thing it's closest to in size. Drag the unit name to the picture."
* **Prompt:** "Which unit goes with which thing?"
* **Student Action:** Drag-to-match (drag each unit label from left column to its matching context image on right)
* **Correct Matches:**
  * square centimeter → postage stamp
  * square inch → sticky note
  * square foot → floor tile
  * square meter → classroom rug
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * All four correct = Student connects unit sizes to real-world objects — strong M6 mastery
  * sq cm / sq in swapped = Student confuses the two small units — same diagnostic as S.1
  * sq ft / sq m swapped = Student confuses the two large units — the exact distinction M6 was designed to teach
  * Small units matched to large contexts (or vice versa) = M8 (Square Unit Confusion) — student doesn't connect unit size to context size
* **On Correct:** "A square centimeter is about the size of your fingertip — like a stamp. A square inch is about the size of a sticky note. A square foot is about the size of a floor tile. And a square meter is about the size of a big rug. Now you can picture every unit."
* **Connection:** Drag-to-match anchors each unit in a CONCRETE mental image. When students encounter these units in M7+ (without grids), they'll retrieve these anchors to reason about scale. The matching task also reinforces the Lesson's core insight: unit selection is about MATCHING the unit's size to the object's size — not arbitrary, not random, but a practical decision. This is the real-world bridge that makes abstract unit names into useful measurement tools.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline (light — if a match is incorrect, highlight the mismatched pair: "Think about the size of [context] — is it closer to [assigned unit] or [correct unit]?" Allow retry.)

> **Design Note:** The four contexts are chosen as TVP-specified anchors (fingertip/stamp, sticky note, floor tile, rug) rather than Lesson contexts (parking lot, playground, classroom floor, garden). This is intentional: Lesson contexts were large-scale areas where the student selected WHICH unit to use. Synthesis contexts are 1:1 scale anchors — each context is approximately the SIZE of one unit square. This is a different cognitive task: Lesson asked "which unit for this big area?" while Synthesis asks "which unit is THIS SIZE?" The 1:1 scale anchors are more portable for future reasoning — a student who can picture "a square foot is about a floor tile" can estimate ANY area in square feet by imagining floor tiles. Context images must be clearly identifiable and age-appropriate. "Postage stamp" replaces the Backbone's "fingertip" suggestion because it's a more concrete, measurable AREA (a fingertip is a surface, not a flat area a student would think to measure). The feedback text uses "fingertip" as a secondary anchor alongside the primary context ("about the size of your fingertip — like a stamp") to maintain TVP alignment.

> **Design Note (Tier 3 tolerance):** The Backbone §1.5 specifies "accept reasonable alternative matches at Tier 3 level." In this interaction, "sticky note → sq cm" is a Tier 3 alternative (a sticky note is roughly 3in × 3in = 9 sq in, but it could be perceived as "small enough for square centimeters" by some students). If engineering can support partial-credit feedback, the ideal response for this swap is: "A sticky note is actually closer to a square inch — but you're right that it's small! Square centimeters are even tinier, like a stamp." If partial credit isn't feasible, accept only the primary matches.

> **ISF-S2: Drag-to-Match interaction layout.** Engineering must confirm: (1) four draggable text labels in left column, (2) four target image zones in right column with captions, (3) snap-to-target behavior, (4) visual feedback on correct placement, (5) ability to rearrange before confirming. Simpler alternative: MC for each context (select the matching unit from dropdown), but this loses the drag requirement per Decision #6.

## Metacognitive Reflection S.3: What Helped You Choose the Right Unit? [Type 1 — Strategy Identification]

* **Purpose:** Students reflect on what strategy helped them with unit selection — M6's primary cognitive addition. Planning-focused reflection (Type 1), strongest for age 8-9 per Playbook §3E. The reflection targets the unit-SELECTION reasoning skill (not the grid-counting procedural skill) because that's what distinguishes M6 from M5.
* **Reflection Type:** Type 1 (Strategy Identification) — "What helped you most?"
* **Visual:** The four unit-to-context matches from S.2 remain visible (postage stamp, sticky note, floor tile, classroom rug with their unit labels). This anchors the reflection in a concrete, just-completed experience.
* **Guide:** "You've been choosing the right unit for different situations all lesson. What helped you most when deciding which unit to use?"
* **Prompt:** "What helped you most?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:**
    * "Thinking about how big the area is"
    * "Picturing the unit square next to the thing"
    * "Remembering which unit goes with big things and which goes with small things"
    * "Something else"
* **Correct Answer:** Any selection (no wrong answer — reflection has no incorrect response)
* **On Correct (per selection):**
  * "Thinking about how big the area is" → "Size is the key — big area, big unit. That reasoning works every time."
  * "Picturing the unit square next to the thing" → "You're visualizing — putting the unit next to the object in your mind. That's a powerful way to estimate."
  * "Remembering which unit goes with big things and which goes with small things" → "You built a system — small units for small things, big units for big things. That's the pattern."
  * "Something else" → "Everyone finds their own way in. What matters is you can pick the right unit — and you can."
* **Connection:** Students identify which cognitive strategy supported their unit-selection reasoning. The three named options correspond to genuine strategies: (1) absolute size assessment (how big is this area?), (2) mental visualization (picture the unit square next to the object), (3) categorical reasoning (big/small classification). Building metacognitive awareness of these strategies will serve students in M7 when grids disappear and unit reasoning must happen without visual support.
* **Remediation:** N/A (no wrong answer — reflection)

> **Design Note:** Type 1 (Strategy Identification) is preferred for M6 per Playbook §3E module-level guidance (Types 1 and 3 for Modules 1-6). The three named options map to three real strategies students may have used, ordered from most abstract (size assessment) to most concrete (categorical reasoning). "Something else" allows for unexpected strategies. Guide responses are ONE sentence plus a brief validation per Playbook §3E. The S.2 visual remains visible to anchor reflection in a concrete experience — students look at the matches they just made while reflecting on HOW they made them.

# §1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS (KDD)

## Purpose

KDD documents the pedagogical design choices that make this module work. Each entry explains WHAT was decided and WHY it matters for student learning. A writer seeing this module for the first time should understand why it works this way from the KDDs alone.

---

### KDD-1: Full Grids Throughout — Last Module Before Fading

Full grids are visible in every interaction from Warmup through EC (Decision #3: M1-M6). In M6, students must SEE unit squares to understand why different units produce different numbers for the same rectangle. Grid fading begins in M7; M6 is the final module where students can rely on countable grids, making thorough grid practice here load-bearing for the transition.

---

### KDD-2: Dual-Correct Convention for Unit Selection — With One Intentional Exception

Most unit-selection items accept both reasonable units (sq ft OR sq m for large areas, sq in OR sq cm for small areas) because M6 teaches SIZE FAMILY reasoning (small vs. large), not customary-vs-metric distinction. The one exception is EC.3b (doormat), which is single-correct (sq ft only) because a doormat is under 1 sq m — testing whether students can distinguish WITHIN the large-unit family. Without this exception, Tier 2 reasoning goes untested and students can pass all unit-selection items using only Tier 1 heuristics.

---

### KDD-3: Worked Example Fading — Full → Partial → Independent

The Lesson's WE sequence progresses from full teacher modeling (L.1, sq ft grid counting with think-aloud) through partial scaffolding (L.4, sq m with student completing the final step) to independent application (L.12-L.14, two-part unit-selection + calculation). This mirrors the CRA progression within the Representational stage and ensures students aren't asked to work independently before the procedure has been modeled in each unit type.

---

### KDD-4: "Too Many" Visual Contrast Is the Core Relational Insight

L.6-L.7's dense cm grid versus clean ft grid on the SAME rectangle is M6's pivotal moment. Students SEE that covering a 4ft×3ft rectangle with square centimeters produces ~11,102 tiny tiles, while square feet produce 12 clean tiles. This visual contrast makes the inverse relationship visceral — not a rule to memorize but a pattern to observe. The W.2 inverse-relationship prime ("the bigger each square is, the fewer you'd need") is deliberately brief because the "Too Many" moment will deliver the full insight through experience.

---

### KDD-5: Inverse Relationship Emerges from Comparison, Not Direct Instruction

The inverse relationship is never stated before students have seen it. L.6-L.7 present the visual contrast; L.7's On Correct response explicitly names the pattern only AFTER the student confirms it. This follows M5's precedent (KDD-4 in M5) and supports constructivist learning — students who discover a pattern retain it more durably than students who are told a pattern and then verify it.

---

### KDD-6: Same Dimensions, Different Units — L.3 and L.13 Share 4×5/5×4

L.3 (4ft×5ft = 20 sq ft) and L.13 (5m×4m = 20 sq m) use reversed factors producing the same count (20) but in different units. This is pedagogically intentional: it reinforces M6's core message that the NUMBER alone doesn't define the measurement — 20 square feet and 20 square meters are vastly different areas. The shared count invites the insight; the different units deliver it.

---

### KDD-7: Unit-Selection Tier Scaffolding — Tier 1 → 2 → 3

The Lesson progresses unit-selection difficulty from Tier 1 (L.10, playground — obvious large area), through Tier 2 (L.11, classroom floor — sq ft or sq m both reasonable but context suggests customary), to Tier 3 (L.14, book cover — sq in or sq cm, genuine boundary). The EC mirrors this arc: EC.3a (Tier 1 small), EC.3b (Tier 2, single-correct), EC.3c (Tier 3 boundary). This ensures both teaching and assessment represent the full difficulty range of unit-selection reasoning.

---

### KDD-8: No Equation Builder in M6

M6 uses Grid Rectangles (Display) exclusively — no Equation Builder. The EB was introduced in M5 for unit-label production (entering "12 square inches" as a typed answer). M6's focus is unit SELECTION (choosing between four units), which is a reasoning task best served by MC and context images, not equation entry. This also avoids overloading a module that already introduces two new units and a complex reasoning framework.

---

### KDD-9: Drag-to-Rank Uses Same-Size Squares — Visual Scaffolding Removed

In the Warmup, students SAW that a square foot tile was bigger than a square inch tile (visual comparison). In Synthesis, all four squares appear the same size on screen, forcing pure reasoning from labels and learned knowledge. This deliberate scaffold removal prepares students for M7, where grids disappear entirely and students must reason about area from dimensions and unit understanding alone.

---

### KDD-10: Practice Weighted 40/60 Toward Unit Selection

Grid counting (40% of Practice) is procedurally identical across M4-M6 — the same row × column multiplication with different unit labels. Unit selection (60%, including combined problems) is M6's primary cognitive addition and the skill most likely to be fragile after a single lesson. The 40/60 split allocates Practice time where it adds the most learning value.

---

### KDD-11: Unit-Label Error Distractors Catch M8 at the Label Level

Practice includes "correct number, wrong unit" MC distractors in at least 2 grid-counting problems per session (e.g., "35 square meters" as a distractor when the grid shows square feet). This catches M8 (Square Unit Confusion) at the label-reading level — a student who can multiply but doesn't attend to the unit label on the grid. The pattern is drawn from M5's TVP diagnostic distractor specification and is M6's extension of that principle to four units.

### KDD-12: Inverse Relationship Assessed in Practice, Not EC — Flagged for SME

The TVP's 4-problem EC structure tests procedural fluency (EC.1-2), unit-selection reasoning (EC.3), and integration (EC.4) but does not include an inverse-relationship item. The inverse relationship is instead included in Practice at approximately 10% frequency. This gap is documented and flagged for SME (Andrea) confirmation. The risk is that a student passes M6 without demonstrating inverse-relationship understanding; M7's grid-fading then exposes the gap. If SME determines EC inclusion is necessary, the cleanest restructure is replacing EC.2 with an inverse-relationship comparison.

### KDD-13: Full Unit Names Only — No Abbreviations

Per TVP and M5 precedent (M5 KDD-13), all interactions use "square feet," "square meters," etc. — never "sq ft" or "sq m." Students need to hear the SQUARE prefix enough times that it becomes automatic. Abbreviations compress the term and may let students skip connecting "square" to the two-dimensional unit. Abbreviations may be introduced in M7+ once full terms are internalized.

---

### KDD-14: M7 Bridge Uses Curiosity, Not Anxiety

The Synthesis closure raises the question "What if the grid wasn't there?" as an intriguing challenge, not a threatening removal. This framing matters: M7's grid fading is a significant scaffold removal, and students who leave M6 anxious about losing grids will approach M7 defensively. Students who leave M6 curious about "what's next" will approach M7 as a challenge they're equipped for — which, after M6's comprehensive grid practice, they are.

---

### KDD-15: Synthesis Contexts Are 1:1 Scale Anchors, Not Area-Measurement Contexts

Lesson and EC contexts are large-scale areas (parking lot, playground, garden, gym floor) where students selected WHICH unit to use. Synthesis contexts are 1:1 scale anchors — each context is approximately the size of ONE unit square (postage stamp ≈ 1 sq cm, sticky note ≈ 1 sq in, floor tile ≈ 1 sq ft, classroom rug ≈ 1 sq m). This is a different cognitive task: the Lesson asked "which unit for this area?" while Synthesis asks "which object IS this unit?" The 1:1 anchors are more portable for future reasoning — a student who pictures "a square foot is about a floor tile" can estimate any area in square feet.
