## 1.0 THE ONE THING

[REQUIRED]

Area is a tool for solving real problems — by the end of M9, students can read a real-world situation, identify that area is relevant, choose and execute an appropriate strategy (forward calculation, missing-dimension reasoning, or design), and justify their answer in context. This module doesn't teach a new skill. It teaches students to USE the skills they built in M1–M8 to answer questions that matter: "Will this fit?" "How much space do we have?" "Is this enough?"

**CRA Stage:** Application (Abstract). Dimensions-only throughout — no grids, no partial grids, no tick marks (per TVP; Decision #3 permits dimensions-only at this stage). Students work at full independence with the multiplication-area connection established through M7's grid fading and M8's forward/reverse fluency.

**Critical Misconception:** Students pick numbers from the word problem and multiply them without reasoning about what the numbers represent. Example: "The garden is 6 feet from the fence and the area is 24 square feet" → student multiplies 6 × 24 = 144. The shift from abstract calculation to contextual application introduces new cognitive load; students who were fluent in M8 may grab numbers reflexively when the problem format changes.

**Success Indicator:** Student reads a word problem, identifies the relevant dimensions (ignoring irrelevant numbers), multiplies to find area, and states the answer with correct square units — AND can explain why area is the right measurement for the question being asked.

**Biggest Risk:** The module becomes "more practice with multiplication" instead of genuine application. M9's identity is the cognitive shift from "find the area" (procedure) to "use area to solve this" (judgment). If every problem reduces to "read the two numbers and multiply," students never make the application leap. Problem variety (forward, missing-dimension, design, comparison, multi-step) is the mechanism that prevents this — each format requires a different entry point into the same underlying skill.

## 1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES

[REQUIRED]

### ✅ Must Teach

* **Forward area application in word problems:** Students read real-world problems with dimensions stated, identify the dimensions, multiply, and answer contextual questions ("How many square feet of sod does Jada need?"). This consolidates M7–M8 calculation into genuine application. Community garden context for Early/Mid; varied contexts for Late.
* **Missing-dimension reasoning (area + one dimension → other dimension):** Students find a missing side length when given the area and one dimension. Framed as multiplication with a missing factor (___ × 5 = 30), NOT as division. Guide models the first problem; students practice independently with Equation Builder support. Builds directly on M8's reverse reasoning in a constrained format.
* **Design problems with area constraints:** Students design rectangles that meet exact area requirements ("Design a garden with exactly 24 square feet"). Problems may have multiple correct answers — builds directly on M8's factor-pair discovery. Scaffolded from MC selection (recognition) to Equation Builder free production.
* **Comparison problems:** Students calculate areas of two rectangles and compare — "Who covers more space?" Two rectangles displayed side by side with clear visual separation. Students calculate each area, compare, find the difference.
* **Multi-step problems (two separate rectangles):** Students find individual areas of two separate rectangles and add to find total area. Key beat: explicit "separate vs. connected" distinction — two objects in different places, not one composite shape. Previews M11 composite figures.
* **Shape reasoning from factor pairs (STRETCH tier, Practice only):** 1-2 problems asking students to reason about rectangle shape from dimensions ("Which garden would be longer and skinnier? Which would be closer to a square?"). Connects factor-pair arithmetic to geometric intuition. Tagged STRETCH per Edit 84 — counts toward mastery but does not penalize students still solidifying core skills.
* **Constraint problems (CHALLENGE tier):** "At least / at most" inequality reasoning. Practice only — 1-2 MC problems. Tagged CHALLENGE (bonus only) — above-grade exposure, not a Grade 3 expectation. Deferred from Lesson to keep the Lesson block within the queue's ~15 min target.
* **"Whole number" constraint language in all design/reverse stems:** Per Edit 91, all design and reverse problem prompts must include "whole number dimensions" or "whole number side lengths" to close the fractional answer loophole.
* **Multiple solution strategies validated (not taught):** Multiplication is the primary path in the digital app. If a student uses an alternative strategy (skip-counting, grouping), the system accepts the correct result. Guide validates different approaches if offered but does not actively teach alternatives.
* **All four standard unit types used freely:** Square centimeters, square inches, square feet, square meters — distributed across problem types. No unit-specific scaffolding (established M5–M7).
* **Age-appropriate real-world contexts:** Community garden (Early/Mid), rugs, forts, posters, bookshelves, playgrounds (Late/Practice). Every context must pass the area test: "Is this genuinely measured in square units?" Avoid volume-ambiguous contexts (soil, mulch, gravel) unless treated with product-label specificity.

### ❌ Must Not Include

* **Grids, partial grids, or tick marks as instructional tools** — M9 is fully abstract per Decision #3. Rectangle outlines with dimensions only. Exception: remediation pipeline may activate grid overlays for students who need visual support (M7 remediation tool), but this is pipeline behavior, not authored content.
* **Perimeter** — Per Decision #5. If student adds dimensions instead of multiplying, address through multiplication reasoning, not perimeter contrast.
* **Division language** — Missing-dimension problems use multiplication framing (___ × 5 = 30), not division (30 ÷ 5 = ___). Division is not introduced in Unit 2.
* **Composite figures** — Multi-step problems involve two SEPARATE rectangles in different places, NOT one shape made of connected rectangles. Composite figures begin at M11. The "separate vs. connected" key beat explicitly prevents conflation.
* **New vocabulary** — M9 is a consolidation module. No new terms. All vocabulary is review from M1–M8.
* **Open-ended text input** — All responses via click/tap/drag, MC selection, or Equation Builder number selection. No typed answers.
* **Volume-ambiguous contexts without treatment** — "How much soil?" is volume, not area. If a volume context is used, it must include a product label specifying coverage area at a stated depth. Prefer area-native contexts: sod, landscape fabric, paving stones, wallpaper.
* **New units** — All four unit types established M5–M7.

⚠️ **SEPARATE VS. CONNECTED:** M9's multi-step problems add areas of separate objects. M11's composite figure problems add areas of connected rectangles that form one shape. The visual and conceptual distinction is load-bearing. Multi-step problems in M9 must display rectangles with clear space between them — never adjacent or touching.

## 1.3 VOCABULARY ARCHITECTURE

[REQUIRED]

M9 is a consolidation module — no new vocabulary is introduced. All terms are review from M1–M8. The instructional focus shifts from vocabulary acquisition to vocabulary APPLICATION: students encounter familiar terms embedded in word-problem contexts where the challenge is comprehending the situation, not learning the language.

**Assessment Vocabulary (appears on state tests and Exit Check):** area, side length, dimensions, length, width, multiply, factor, product, square inch, square centimeter, square foot, square meter

**Note on vocabulary staging:** Because no terms are new, vocabulary staging in M9 is about REINFORCEMENT CONTEXT rather than introduction timing. Terms appear naturally in problem stems, Guide dialogue, and answer feedback. The Warmup uses all familiar terms immediately; the Lesson uses them in word-problem contexts; the EC tests comprehension through contextual application.

### Vocabulary Staging by Phase

| Phase | Terms | Reinforcement Approach |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Warm-Up** | area, dimensions, length, width, multiply, square feet | Natural use in paired problems. No formal review — students are expected to use these terms fluently from M8. |
| **Lesson Section 1 (Forward + Missing-Dim Intro)** | area, length, width, multiply, square feet/cm/m/in | Embedded in word-problem stems. "Jada's garden is 8 feet long and 3 feet wide. What is the area?" Guide uses dimensional language naturally. Equation Builder reinforces multiplication framing for missing-dimension. |
| **Lesson Section 2 (Design + Independent Missing-Dim)** | area, dimensions, side lengths, factor pair (review), square feet | Design problems use "side lengths" and "dimensions" interchangeably. Factor pair reasoning is implicit in design (finding all pairs for a target area) but the term "factor pair" is used naturally, not re-taught. |
| **Lesson Section 3 (Comparison, Multi-Step)** | area, length, width, multiply, square feet/cm/m/in, "covers more space," "total area" | Context shift to varied real-world scenarios introduces new phrasing ("covers more space," "total area of both") while using established vocabulary. |
| **EC** | All assessment vocabulary | Terms appear in problem stems and answer options. No new phrasing beyond what Lesson introduced. |
| **Synthesis** | All terms — toolkit review | "Area isn't just counting squares. It's a tool for solving real problems." Vocabulary appears in the toolkit review: forward (dimensions → area), reverse (area → dimensions), design (area → possible dimensions), compare, combine. |

### Vocabulary Notes

**"Covers more space" (comparison phrasing):** M9 introduces this as the standard comparison phrasing for area. Preferred over "has more space on" (ambiguous — could mean total area or unused space) and "is bigger" (imprecise — could refer to perimeter, visual size, or area). This phrasing directly equates the object's area with the answer. Apply consistently across all comparison problems.

**"Whole number side lengths" / "whole number dimensions" (Edit 91 language):** Required in all design and reverse problem stems. This is constraint language, not vocabulary — students don't need to define "whole number," but the stem must include it to close the fractional answer loophole.

**Factor pair (review from M8):** Used naturally in design problem reasoning but not re-taught. Students who completed M8 know that one area maps to multiple factor pairs. M9's design problems put this knowledge to work in context.

**Language pattern for comparison answers:** "[Object A] covers [X] more square [unit] than [Object B]." Or: "[Object A] has an area of [X] square [unit]. [Object B] has an area of [Y] square [unit]. [Object A/B] covers [difference] more square [unit]."

### Terms to Avoid (Save for Later Modules)

* **"Formula"** (standalone) — Suggests rote memorization. Use: "Multiply the dimensions to find area." Same constraint as M7–M8.
* **"Perimeter"** — Do not use, even for contrast. Per Decision #5.
* **"Decompose"** — Not applicable to M9. Composite figures and decomposition begin at M11.
* **"Feet squared," "Meters squared"** — Ambiguous; use "Square feet," "Square meters." Same constraint as M7–M8.
* **"Equation"** — Students build multiplication expressions via Equation Builder, not equations. The tool name is not student vocabulary.
* **"Divide" / "Division"** — Missing-dimension problems use multiplication framing. Division language is not introduced in Unit 2.
* **"Has more space on"** — Ambiguous; could mean total area or unused space. Use "covers more space" for all comparison problems.
* **"Is bigger"** — Imprecise; could refer to perimeter, visual size, or area. Use "covers more space" to tie the comparison directly to area.
* **"At least" / "At most"** (as math vocabulary) — Used in Practice constraint problems but treated as English comprehension, not mathematical vocabulary. Practice feedback makes the meaning explicit ("at least 18 means 18 or MORE") rather than teaching it as a term. Tagged CHALLENGE.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

[REQUIRED]

### 1.4.1 M9-LOCAL-1: Picking Numbers Without Understanding (PRIMARY)

**ID:** M9-LOCAL-1 (module-specific — not globally relevant to Unit 2; this behavior surfaces specifically in M9's word-problem application context)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student extracts numbers from the word problem and multiplies them without reasoning about what each number represents. Example: "The garden is 6 feet from the fence and the area is 24 square feet" → student multiplies 6 × 24 = 144. Or: "Jada needs sod for a garden that is 8 feet long and 3 feet wide. She has 15 square feet of sod." → student multiplies 8 × 3 × 15 or 8 × 15.

**Why It Happens:** In M7–M8, every number in a problem was a relevant dimension or area — there were no distractors or irrelevant values. M9 introduces real-world contexts where problems contain extra information (distances, quantities, costs) alongside the relevant dimensions. Students who developed a "find two numbers and multiply" heuristic in M8 apply it indiscriminately when the problem format changes.

**Visual Cue:** Grid Rectangles displays the rectangle with labeled dimensions, making the relevant quantities visually prominent. For missing-dimension problems, the "?" on the unknown side helps students identify which number is missing. For multi-step problems, separate rectangle displays prevent students from conflating numbers across objects.

**Prevention Strategy:** Problem structure requires students to identify relevant dimensions before calculating. Guide models the thinking on early problems: "What are the dimensions of this garden? Which numbers tell us the length and width?" Equation Builder's blank-slot format (___ × ___ = ___) forces students to deliberately place each number. Remediation pipeline catches number-grabbing by verifying the student's intermediate steps (requiring individual areas before totals in multi-step problems).

---

### 1.4.2 #5: Not Multiplying Side Lengths (SECONDARY)

**Global ID:** #5

**Trigger Behavior:** Student identifies the correct dimensions but adds them instead of multiplying (e.g., 8 + 3 = 11 instead of 8 × 3 = 24), or identifies dimensions correctly but doesn't calculate at all.

**Why It Happens:** This misconception was primary in M5–M7 (students hadn't yet automated the multiplication-area connection) and should be largely resolved by M9. However, the increased cognitive load of word-problem parsing can cause students to revert to less sophisticated strategies. When attention is consumed by reading comprehension, the calculation step may regress.

**Visual Cue:** Equation Builder's multiplication template (___ × ___ = ___) makes the operation explicit. The × symbol in the template reminds students that area requires multiplication.

**Prevention Strategy:** Consistent problem structure across all formats reinforces the multiplication pattern. Equation Builder is available (guide-activated) as a scaffold for students who struggle. If a student adds instead of multiplying, the remediation pipeline addresses through multiplication reasoning — never through perimeter contrast (Decision #5).

---

### 1.4.3 #10: Partial Unit Handling (LOW PRIORITY)

**Global ID:** #10

**Trigger Behavior:** When a figure doesn't tile perfectly with whole squares, student either rounds or leaves the answer incomplete.

**Why It Happens:** Students expect clean whole-number answers from their experience with unit squares.

**Prevention Strategy:** LOW priority in M9 — all problems use whole-number dimensions with whole-number products. This misconception is unlikely to surface. Listed for completeness because the Misconceptions database shows it surfacing through M9, but M9's data constraints (factors 2–10, products ≤100, clean factor pairs) eliminate the conditions that trigger it.

### Data Constraints

**Forward problems:**
* Factors: 2–10 each
* Products: ≤ 100
* All four unit types represented across the module
* Examples: 8×3=24 sq ft, 7×4=28 sq cm, 9×5=45 sq m, 6×6=36 sq ft

**Missing-dimension problems:**
* Target area given, one dimension given, missing dimension is a whole number ≤ 10
* Keep target areas to products with clean factor pairs — no remainders
* Frame as multiplication with a missing factor, NOT division
* Examples: area=30, width=5, length=6; area=28, width=4, length=7; area=42, width=7, length=6

**Design problems:**
* Same target areas as M8 reverse problems: 12, 18, 20, 24, 30, 36
* All valid unique factor pairs included (both factors ≤12 per Edit 92, plus 1×n always valid per Edit 91)
* All stems include "whole number" constraint language per Edit 91
* Design problem progression: MC scaffolding (first 1-2) → Equation Builder number palette 0-12 (subsequent)
* For MC design: "Select ALL sets of whole number side lengths that give an area of [target]." (Edit 88) — students not told how many correct answers exist
* MC distractors: products ≠ target area AND no shared factors with any correct answer (Edit 83)
* For Equation Builder design: neutral feedback for incorrect products; rectangle confirmation for correct products; "Can you find a DIFFERENT one?" prompt; guide hint after 2-3 failed attempts

**Factor pair reference for design problems (Edit 91 + Edit 92 applied):**

| Target Area | Valid Factor Pairs (unique, 1×n always included, non-1 factors ≤12) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 12 | (1,12), (2,6), (3,4) | 3 pairs |
| 18 | (1,18), (2,9), (3,6) | 3 pairs |
| 20 | (1,20), (2,10), (4,5) | 3 pairs |
| 24 | (1,24), (2,12), (3,8), (4,6) | 4 pairs — best for multi-solution beats |
| 30 | (1,30), (3,10), (5,6) | 3 pairs — 2×15 excluded (factor 15 >12 and not 1×n) |
| 36 | (1,36), (3,12), (4,9), (6,6) | 4 pairs — 2×18 excluded; includes perfect square |

**Comparison problems:**
* Two rectangles with DIFFERENT areas (same-area pairs are M8's domain)
* Areas close enough to require calculation — not obviously different by visual inspection
* Examples: 7×4=28 vs 5×6=30 (difference 2); 9×3=27 vs 7×4=28 (difference 1)
* **Design Note:** Comparison problem pairs should NOT share a dimension — shared dimensions allow visual length comparison without area calculation, defeating the purpose. Both examples above use no shared dimensions.
* All factors 2–10

**Multi-step problems:**
* Two SEPARATE rectangles — displayed with clear visual separation
* Individual areas ≤ 50, total area ≤ 100
* Examples: 8×3=24 and 5×4=20, total=44; 6×6=36 and 3×5=15, total=51
* App requires intermediate answers (each rectangle's area) before final total

**Constraint problems (CHALLENGE):**
* "At least / at most" framing — inequality language made explicit in English-comprehension framing
* Practice only: 1-2 MC-only problems. No constraint problems in Lesson — deferred to keep Lesson within the ~15 min queue target.
* Tagged CHALLENGE (bonus only) — above-grade exposure, not Grade 3 expectation.

**Word problem contexts:**
* Community garden: Early and Mid (reduces parsing overhead while learning new problem formats)
* Varied real-world: Late and Practice (rugs, forts, posters, bookshelves, playgrounds, tablecloths)
* Context test (load-bearing): "Is this genuinely measured in square units?" Avoid soil, mulch, gravel, sand unless product label specifies coverage area at stated depth.
* Recommended area-native contexts: sod/grass, landscape fabric, paving stones, wallpaper, peel-and-stick tiles
* Comparison phrasing: "covers more space" (not "has more space on" or "is bigger")
* Multi-step contexts must be meaningful to 8-year-olds. Test: "Would a third grader understand WHY someone in this story wants to know the total area?"

**Avoid:**
* Prime-number areas for design problems (only one non-trivial factor pair)
* Areas > 50 for individual problems (keeps calculations manageable)
* Same-area comparison pairs (that's M8's concept)

**Dimension Variety Note:** Track which specific dimension values (3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10) appear across the module and in which problem types. Avoid over-relying on any single dimension value, especially as the "known" dimension in missing-dimension problems. Script writers at Task 2 should distribute dimensions across the full 2–10 range. Example concern: if dimension 4 appears as the known side in multiple missing-dimension problems AND as a constraint width AND in a comparison rectangle, students may develop an unintended association. Vary known dimensions across missing-dim problems — aim to use at least 4 different values (e.g., 3, 5, 6, 7, 8) across the module's missing-dimension set.

### Interaction Constraints

* Student cannot drag/manipulate rectangles (display only)
* Student can tap/click to enlarge for clarity (accessibility)
* Student responds via MC selection, Equation Builder, or multi-step intermediate answer entry
* Side-by-side rectangle comparisons are view-only — no student action on the comparison display itself
* "Separate vs. connected" animation is system-driven, not student-initiated

### 1.5.2 Equation Builder

### 1.5.3 Secondary Tools

**Multiple Choice (MC) — Multiple Phases**
* **Forward problems (Early/Late/EC):** Standard single-select MC. Student selects area value with correct unit from 4 options. * **Missing-dimension problems (Mid/EC):** Standard single-select MC. Student selects the missing dimension from 4 options. Distractors include: the area value, the given dimension, the sum of dimensions, a near-miss multiplication fact.
* **Design problems (Mid, first 1-2 only):** Multi-select MC. "Select ALL sets of whole number side lengths that give an area of [target]." All valid unique factor pairs included (1×n included per Edit 91, non-1 factors ≤12 per Edit 92). 2-3 distractors with products ≠ target area and no shared factors with correct answers (Edit 83). * **Comparison problems (Late/EC):** MC for "Which has more area?" question + numerical entry or MC for "How much more?" question. OR: Multi-step MC with intermediate answer entry.
* **Constraint problems (Practice only):** Standard single-select MC. Student selects correct dimension value. Tagged CHALLENGE. Example template: "A rug must cover at least 18 square feet. Distractors include too-small dimensions, and the constraint number itself (number-grabbing trap). ### Core Purpose

**Purpose:** Activate M7-M8 forward area fluency and M8 reverse reasoning, and surface the insight that the SAME context (a garden) can generate DIFFERENT kinds of area questions — priming M9's five-format problem variety.

**Key Function:** This Warmup positions the Lesson by presenting two garden problems that use the same math (area) but ask different questions — one forward (dimensions → area) and one reverse (area + one dimension → missing dimension). Students succeed quickly at both, using familiar skills from M8. The FORMAT contrast — not the computational difficulty — is the priming mechanism. When the Lesson opens with "area problems come in different forms," students already have a concrete experience of what that means.

**Why this serves the concept:**

* **Activates forward fluency from M7-M8:** W.1 is a straightforward dimensions-to-area calculation using the dimensions-only display students mastered in M7 and consolidated in M8. Quick success confirms readiness for the Lesson's extended forward application in word-problem contexts.
* **Activates reverse reasoning from M8:** W.2 asks "what's the missing side?" — the reverse direction M8 introduced. Students who completed M8 can solve this, confirming that the skill transferred. This primes the Lesson's missing-dimension word problems (a new FORMAT for the same SKILL).
* **Surfaces problem-type variety without formalizing it:** The paired-problem structure (same garden context, different questions) is the seed of M9's central insight — area is a TOOL that answers many different kinds of questions. The bridge names this observation and creates anticipation for the Lesson.

**Test:** If we removed this Warmup and went straight to the Lesson, students would encounter the word-problem formats cold — no activation of either forward or reverse fluency, and no preparation for the idea that one mathematical tool (area) serves many different question types. The Lesson's Purpose Frame assumes students arrive having already experienced the forward-reverse contrast; without it, the Purpose Frame's "you'll use area to solve all kinds of real problems" lacks grounding.

### Warmup Type Rationale

**Selected Type:** Activation (custom) — two paired problems with the same garden context but different question types (forward and missing-dimension).

**Why not Number Talk?** A Number Talk (e.g., "What's 5 × 4?") would activate multiplication fluency but miss M9's defining characteristic: area as an APPLICATION tool across problem TYPES. M9's Warmup needs to prime format variety, not mental math strategies. The visual rectangle display is load-bearing — students need to see that the same garden can generate different questions.

**Why not Visual Pattern?** Visual Patterns (growing sequences) prime pattern recognition and algebraic thinking. M9's Lesson doesn't involve pattern discovery — it involves applying known skills to varied contextual problems. The paired-problem activation is a better fit.

**Why not Binary Choice?** Binary Choice ("Which rectangle has area 20?") tests recognition but doesn't activate both computational directions (forward AND reverse). M9 needs students to have DONE both before the Lesson opens.

**Custom rationale:** The paired-problem structure is unique to M9's application-module identity. Two problems, same context, different question types — this is the smallest possible demonstration of M9's core idea (area problems come in different forms) within a low-stakes Warmup frame. It satisfies the Playbook's requirements (2+ interactions, hook, judgment task, bridge) while specifically priming M9's problem-variety Lesson.

### Core Purpose

### Module-Specific Lesson Guidance

**Context Progression:** Community garden context is used exclusively in Sections 1 and 2 to reduce parsing overhead while students learn new problem formats. Section 3 shifts to varied real-world contexts (rugs, forts) to demonstrate that area applies broadly. This context progression maps to the scaffolding principle: hold context constant when format varies, then vary context after format is established.

**Equation Builder Activation Logic:** EB is visible-by-default in Section 1 missing-dimension problems (1.5) and completely absent in Section 3. The fade progression:

- 2.2: No EB (MC design)
- 2.3: EB number palette (design production — different EB mode)

**Multi-Step Intermediate Answers:** Interactions 3.1 and 3.2 require intermediate answers (individual areas) before the final answer (comparison/total). ### Misconception Prevention

**M9-LOCAL-1 (Picking Numbers Without Understanding) — PRIMARY:**

* **Prevention in 1.1:** Think-aloud explicitly models "Which numbers are the dimensions?" — Guide identifies relevant vs. irrelevant information before calculating. [PLANNING] and [ATTENTION] tags make the reading strategy visible.
* **Prevention in 1.3:** Word problem includes irrelevant number (3 tomato plants). All three number-grabbing products (3×5=15, 3×9=27, 3×9×5=135) appear as MC distractors. On Correct feedback explicitly states why the irrelevant number doesn't matter.
* **Prevention in 1.4:** Missing-dimension WE think-aloud models "What do I know? What am I looking for?" — forces identification of knowns vs. unknowns.
* **Structural prevention:** Equation Builder's blank-slot format (___ × [known] = [area]) forces students to deliberately assign each number to a role. The physical act of placing numbers in slots prevents reflexive calculation.
* **Ongoing prevention:** All word problems include clear dimensional labels in the visual display. Students who struggle with text parsing can cross-reference the rectangle's labeled dimensions against the word problem.

**#5 (Not Multiplying Side Lengths) — SECONDARY:**

* **Prevention in 1.2, 1.3, 2.1:** All MC interactions include an addition-error distractor (dimensions added instead of multiplied). If student selects the addition option, the remediation pipeline addresses through multiplication reasoning.
* **Structural prevention:** Equation Builder displays × symbol explicitly. The multiplication template (___ × ___ = ___) makes the required operation visible.

**#10 (Partial Unit Handling) — LOW PRIORITY:**

* Not directly addressed. All M9 problems use whole-number dimensions and whole-number products. Conditions that trigger this misconception are absent from M9's data constraints.

### Connection Task S.1: Area Toolkit Review — Progressive Challenge [CONSOLIDATION]

* **Purpose:** Explicit consolidation of all five problem formats. Guide names each format with a concrete example, building the "area toolkit" that students carry forward. This is the Critical Consolidation Moment — multiple problem types reviewed side by side.
* **Task Type:** B (Progressive Challenge — builds from listing to understanding connections)
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Dimensions-only mode).** Five small rectangles displayed in a row, each with a one-word label underneath: "Find" (forward), "Missing?" (missing-dim), "Design" (design), "Compare" (comparison), "Total" (multi-step). Each rectangle is visually distinct (different proportions).
* **Guide:** "Here's everything you can do with area now. You can find the area when you know both sides. You can figure out a missing side when you know the area. You can design a rectangle that fits a target area. You can compare two rectangles to see which covers more space. And you can find the total area when there are two separate rectangles. That's five different kinds of area problems, and you can do all of them."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** This is a Type A/B hybrid — the guide explicitly names all five formats (Pattern Discovery) and builds to the "five tools in your kit" generalization (Progressive Challenge). No student action because the consolidation is observational — the student sees the full range summarized. The next interactions test whether they can apply the toolkit.

---

### Connection Task S.2: Which Strategy? — Real-World Bridge [REAL-WORLD BRIDGE]

* **Purpose:** Test whether the student can identify the correct problem type for a given scenario. Presents a real-world situation and asks which area "tool" applies. This is the transfer test — can the student select the right approach, not just execute it?
* **Task Type:** C (Real-World Bridge)
* **Visual:** No rectangle display initially — scenario text only. Rectangle appears after selection as confirmation.
* **Guide:** "Here's a real problem. A school wants to know if a new bookshelf will fit against a wall. The wall is 8 feet long and 3 feet tall. What kind of area problem is this?"
* **Prompt:** "The school wants to know if a bookshelf fits. The wall is 8 ft × 3 ft. What are you looking for?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** A) Find the area, B) Find a missing side, C) Compare two areas, D) Find the total area
* **Correct Answer:** A) Find the area
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - Find the area = Correct (both dimensions given, question asks about space coverage)
  - Find a missing side = Both dimensions stated — nothing is missing
  - Compare two areas = Only one rectangle described
  - Find the total area = Only one rectangle — no separate areas to combine
* **On Correct:** "Right. You know both dimensions, so you find the area."
* **Connection:** "When you know both sides and need to figure out how much space something covers, that's a forward area problem."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** S.1 introduces the five-category taxonomy ("Find," "Missing?," "Design," "Compare," "Total") immediately before S.2 asks students to apply it. The taxonomy is new in Synthesis — it was implicit throughout the Lesson but never formalized. S.2 tests whether students can map a novel scenario to the taxonomy they just learned in S.1, not whether they already knew the categories. The wall/bookshelf context is new (not from Lesson), testing transfer. The Connection statement explicitly names the decision rule.

### Connection Task S.3: Which Strategy? (Part 2) — Real-World Bridge [REAL-WORLD BRIDGE]

* **Purpose:** Second strategy-identification task with a different problem type. Tests whether the student can distinguish missing-dimension from forward.
* **Task Type:** C (Real-World Bridge)
* **Visual:** Scenario text only. Rectangle appears after selection.
* **Guide:** "One more. A playground needs a sandbox with an area of 50 square feet. One side has to be 5 feet. How long should the other side be? What kind of problem is that?"
* **Prompt:** "A sandbox has an area of 50 sq ft. One side is 5 ft. What are you looking for?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** A) Find the area, B) Find a missing side, C) Design a rectangle, D) Compare two areas
* **Correct Answer:** B) Find a missing side
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - Find a missing side = Correct (area and one dimension given, other dimension unknown)
  - Find the area = Area is already given (50 sq ft) — nothing to calculate
  - Design a rectangle = Dimensions are constrained by the area and known side — this is finding, not designing
  - Compare two areas = Only one rectangle described
* **On Correct:** "That's right. You know the area and one side, so you find the missing side."
* **Connection:** "When you know the area and one dimension, that's a missing-side problem. You think: what times 5 gives me 50?"
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

### Connection Task S.4: Metacognitive Reflection — Key Takeaway [METACOGNITIVE REFLECTION]

* **Purpose:** Student reflects on what they've learned. Type 4 (Key Takeaway) — "If you had to remember one thing about area problems, what would it be?" Builds metacognitive awareness.
* **Task Type:** Metacognitive Reflection (Type 4)
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Dimensions-only mode).** The five-rectangle "toolkit" display from S.1 visible again.
* **Guide:** "You've solved a lot of area problems. If you had to remember one thing about area, what would it be?"
* **Prompt:** "What's the most important thing you learned about area?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** A) Area means multiply the two sides, B) Area can answer many different kinds of questions, C) You always need to know both sides to find area, D) Area is measured in square units
* **Correct Answer:** Any selection accepted (all are valid takeaways)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  - All four options are mathematically valid takeaways. No incorrect answer exists. Per-option On Correct feedback validates each choice genuinely.
* **On Correct:** Response varies by selection:
  * Option A: "Multiplying the sides is the key skill. And now you know you can use it in lots of different ways."
  * Option B: "Exactly. Area isn't just one kind of problem. It's a tool for all kinds of questions."
  * Option C: "That's important. And when you don't know both sides, you can figure out the missing one."
  * Option D: "Square units tell you how much space something covers. That's what makes area useful."
* **Connection:** "Area isn't just counting squares. It's a tool for solving real problems."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline (activates only on extended inactivity — all selections are correct)

> **Design Note:** All four options are valid mathematical insights. The Connection statement delivers the TVP's signature Synthesis line verbatim: "Area isn't just counting squares. It's a tool for solving real problems." The MC format makes the reflection actionable in the digital context (no open-ended typing), and the per-option On Correct validates each takeaway genuinely.

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS (KDD)

[REQUIRED]

### Purpose

KDD documents the pedagogical design choices that make this module work. Each entry explains WHAT was decided and WHY it matters for student learning. A writer seeing this module for the first time should understand why it works this way from the KDDs alone.

---

### KDD-1: Application Module Identity — No New Skills, Five Problem Formats

M9 doesn't teach a new mathematical skill. It teaches students to USE the forward calculation and reverse reasoning they built in M7-M8 to solve real-world problems across five formats: forward application, missing-dimension, design, comparison, and multi-step. The problem variety IS the instruction — each format requires a different entry point into the same underlying multiplication-area connection. If any format is removed, the module collapses into "more practice with multiplication" and the application leap never happens.

---

### KDD-2: Dimensions-Only Throughout — No Grids at Application Stage

M9 operates entirely at the Abstract CRA stage per Decision #3. No grids, no partial grids, no tick marks appear in any authored content from Warmup through Synthesis. Grid fading completed in M7; M8 consolidated dimensions-only independence. The sole exception: the remediation pipeline may activate grid overlays for students who need visual support, but this is adaptive behavior, not authored content. Reintroducing grids in M9 would undermine the independence students developed.

---

### KDD-3: Community Garden Context Strategy — Hold Context Constant When Format Varies

Sections 1 and 2 use community garden context exclusively to reduce word-problem parsing overhead while students encounter new problem formats (missing-dimension, design). Section 3 shifts to varied contexts (rugs, forts) to demonstrate that area applies broadly. This mirrors the scaffolding principle: hold one variable constant (context) when another varies (problem format), then release both in the transfer phase.

---

### KDD-4: M9-LOCAL-1 Prevention Through Problem Structure

The primary misconception — students grabbing numbers from word problems without reasoning about what they represent — is prevented structurally, not just through explicit instruction. Three mechanisms: (1) Think-aloud in 1.1 models "Which numbers are the dimensions?" before calculating; (2) Interaction 1.3 includes an irrelevant number with all three number-grabbing products as distractors; (3) Equation Builder's blank-slot format forces deliberate number placement. The structural prevention means even students who miss the think-aloud modeling encounter the safeguard.

---

### KDD-5: Equation Builder Fade — Visible to Guide-Activated to Absent

EB scaffolds the missing-dimension format: visible for all in Section 1 (1.5, guided), guide-activated only in Section 2 (2.1, independent), completely absent in Section 3 and EC. Design problems use a different EB mode (number palette) in 2.3 that tests production, not recognition. By EC, students solve missing-dimension problems from pure multiplication reasoning — the scaffold has been fully withdrawn.

---

### KDD-6: "Separate vs. Connected" as Load-Bearing Beat

Multi-step problems in M9 explicitly display rectangles with clear space between them and include the 3.3 animation/static contrast showing what happens when rectangles are pushed together. This distinction prevents conflation with M11's composite figures. The Guide's language ("separate rectangles" vs. "stuck together in one piece") plants vocabulary that M11 will formalize. Removing or softening this beat risks students treating M11 composite figures as "just more multi-step problems."

---

### KDD-7: EC.3 Values Changed from TVP for Independence

TVP specifies EC.3 as "Garden A: 6×5=30, Garden B: 8×3=24." Both rectangles appear verbatim in the Lesson (3.1b: 5×6=30; 1.1/3.2a: 8×3=24). Per Guidance, EC values should differ from Lesson values to ensure the EC tests skill transfer, not recognition of familiar problems. Changed to 9×3=27 vs 8×4=32 — same comparison skill tested, no Lesson dimension overlap. The TVP's specific rectangles are exemplars of the problem type, not mandated values.

---

### KDD-8: Warmup Uses Different Garden, Not TVP's Same-Garden Pairing

TVP specifies both Warmup problems use the SAME garden (5×4=20 forward, then area 20/side 5→4 reverse). The SP uses a different garden for W.2 (area 18/side 3→6) because the same-garden version allows memory-based answering — the student just computed 5×4=20 and can answer "area 20, side 5, what's the other side?" from recall rather than activating reverse reasoning. The different-garden version forces genuine skill activation. The format contrast (forward vs. reverse) remains visible through the question-type difference; identical values are not required.

---

### KDD-9: Constraint Problems Deferred to Practice — CHALLENGE Tier, Bonus Only

"At least / at most" inequality reasoning is above Grade 3 expectations. Constraint problems appear exclusively in Practice (1-2 MC problems, CHALLENGE tag, bonus only). Originally, Lesson Section 3 included a guide-modeled constraint worked example (3.4), but this was deferred to Practice to keep the Lesson block within the Activity Queue's ~15 min target. The constraint worked example was genuinely Practice-level work — it requires fluency with core area calculation plus an additional inequality constraint. Deferring it gives the queue a natural breakpoint after the multi-step/separate-vs-connected beat. Practice provides brief Guide reasoning on correct answers ("'At least 18' means 18 or MORE. 4 times 5 is 20, and 20 is at least 18.") to compensate for the absence of a Lesson WE.

---

### KDD-10: Independent Missing-Dimension Count Reduced from TVP

TVP specifies 2-3 independent missing-dimension problems. The SP includes 1 (interaction 2.1) to accommodate design problems within the 12-17 minute Lesson budget. The single independent problem is sufficient to verify transfer from guided practice — if a student succeeds at 2.1 without EB, the skill has transferred. Additional missing-dimension practice occurs in EC.2 and Practice.

---

### KDD-11: Unit Distribution Across Lesson

Lesson unit distribution: sq ft (10 problems), sq cm (1, interaction 1.2 tile sample), sq m (1, interaction 1.3), sq in (1, interaction 2.1 card). The sq ft dominance reflects that most real-world area problems at this scale use feet. Unit variety is distributed across the module rather than balanced within the Lesson — EC and Practice provide additional sq in, sq cm, and sq m exposure. The tile sample (cm) and card (in) contexts were chosen specifically because centimeters and inches are realistic for those objects.

---

### KDD-12: Practice Design Problems Use Single-Select, Not Multi-Select

Lesson design problem 2.2 uses multi-select MC ("Select ALL sets of whole number side lengths that give an area of 24 square feet") to teach exhaustive factor-pair reasoning. Practice design problems downshift to single-select MC ("Select a valid pair") because Practice assesses whether a student can identify ANY valid pair — the production skill — not whether they can enumerate all of them. The exhaustive-search moment was the Lesson 2.2 learning event; Practice tests transfer of the underlying factor-pair reasoning to new target areas.
