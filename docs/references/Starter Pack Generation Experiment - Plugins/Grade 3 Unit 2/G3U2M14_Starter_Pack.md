# MODULE 14: Room Design Application

# BACKBONE

---

## 1.0 THE ONE THING

**[REQUIRED]**

Students use area to answer a real question — "Will this furniture fit in this room?" — by orchestrating skills from across the unit: find missing side lengths on a composite room shape (M13), decompose into rectangles and calculate each area (M11–M12), add component areas (M12), calculate furniture areas and compare to available space (M9), determine fit, and compute usable floor space. No new mathematical skill is introduced; the challenge is coordinating every skill in service of a genuine purpose.

**CRA Stage:** Application (full abstraction). M14 operates entirely at the dimensions-only level (Decision #3: M13–M14 = "dimensions only with missing sides, full abstraction"). No grids appear in any phase. Students have demonstrated each component skill independently across M11–M13; M14 asks them to orchestrate all components in a sustained real-world context. Highlight Related Sides is the only scaffold, available as remediation only.

**Critical Misconception:** #7 — Wrong Missing Side Length (PRIMARY). Room shapes have more visible dimensions than the abstract figures in M13, and the real-world context adds parsing overhead — students may grab wrong dimensions from the room diagram, especially when furniture dimensions are also visible. **Secondary:** #12 — Multiplying All Given Numbers. Room diagrams with furniture nearby create many visible numbers from different objects; students may cross-contaminate dimensions (e.g., room width × desk length). **Secondary:** #11 — Completing the Rectangle. L-shaped rooms invite bounding-box thinking ("The room is 10 by 9") when the room is composite.

**Success Indicator:** Given a composite room with missing side labels and separate furniture dimensions, the student independently finds missing sides, decomposes the room, calculates total room area, calculates furniture areas, compares them, determines fit, and computes usable floor space — without scaffolding and without confusing room dimensions with furniture dimensions.

**Biggest Risk:** Cognitive overload from the sustained multi-phase problem. Unlike M11–M13 where each problem was self-contained, M14 uses one room across the entire Lesson. Students who lose track of where they are in the problem chain (found missing sides → calculated room area → now calculating furniture → now comparing) may restart or freeze. The TVP's explicit step-naming scaffold ("We figured out the room's area. Now let's figure out what the furniture needs — that's Step 2.") is essential metacognitive support, not optional decoration.

## 1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES

**[REQUIRED]**

### ✅ Must Teach

* **Complete area workflow in room design context:** Find missing sides → Decompose into rectangles → Calculate each rectangle's area → Add → Compare to furniture area → Decide on fit → Calculate usable floor space. This is the full orchestration of M11–M13 skills in a sustained real-world problem. No single step is new; the orchestration IS the learning.
* **Furniture area calculation and comparison:** Students calculate simple rectangular areas for furniture items (bed, desk) and compare total furniture area to total room area. This is M9's comparison skill applied to composite figures with multiple objects.
* **"Will it fit?" decision-making:** The culminating question. Students determine whether furniture fits by comparing area AND considering dimensional constraints (does the furniture fit within a specific decomposed region?). The guide surfaces the "enough area ≠ it fits" insight through discussion.
* **Usable floor space = room area − furniture area:** First subtraction in area context in this unit. Must be explicitly framed as subtracting object areas from total space — NOT as subtraction decomposition of a shape.
* **Sustained multi-phase problem:** One room used across the entire Lesson. Missing sides found in Early carry forward to Mid and Late. This continuity is a deliberate pedagogical choice — students experience the full problem-solving arc, not disconnected exercises.
* **Vocabulary reinforcement:** area, square feet, dimensions, composite figure, decompose, missing side, related sides, multiply, add. All reinforced in room design context, no new terms.
* **Receptive context vocabulary:** room, floor plan, furniture, floor space, "usable space." These are real-world words students already know — they connect to the math vocabulary, not replace it.

### ❌ Must Not Include

* **Perimeter** — Per Decision #5. Not mentioned, even for contrast. "Usable floor space" is subtraction of AREAS, not edge measurements. The "related sides add up" principle from M13 may superficially resemble perimeter reasoning; the SP must not draw this connection.
* **Grids** — Per Decision #3. M14 is full abstraction (dimensions only). No full grids, partial grids, or ghost grids in any phase.
* **New mathematical skills or vocabulary** — M14 is an application module. Every mathematical skill used was taught in M1–M13. No new interaction types, no new cognitive demands beyond orchestration.
* **Dependency chains between missing sides** — Per TVP data constraints: "Missing sides follow M13 constraints" (independently solvable). See Author Flag AF-1 for discussion.
* **U-shapes or complex figures** — Per TVP: rooms are L-shaped or T-shaped only. L-shapes are the core figure type used in Warmup, Lesson Early/Mid, and EC. T-shapes appear only as optional Late extension or in Practice — never in the primary teaching sequence. Realistic room layouts, not abstract geometric puzzles.
* **More than 2 missing sides per room** — Per TVP: 1–2 missing sides. Cognitive load ceiling in a multi-phase problem.
* **Furniture that doesn't fit (main Lesson)** — Per TVP: room dimensions designed so furniture FITS. Positive resolution for third graders. "Enough area ≠ it fits" is surfaced through guided discussion, not through a problem where furniture doesn't fit. Practice STRETCH includes selective exposure.
* **More than 2 furniture items in the Lesson** — Per TVP. A third item may appear in Practice as stretch.
* **Problems without room/furniture context** — Per TVP: every M14 problem has a real-world framing. No abstract figures.
* **"Formula" language** — Same constraint as M7–M13. Decomposition is a strategy, multiplication is a tool.
* **Division language** — Not introduced in Unit 2.
* **Subtraction decomposition strategy** — The "complete rectangle minus missing piece" approach. All decomposition in M14 is additive (same as M11–M13). *Source: TVP M12 establishes additive-only decomposition; M14 inherits this constraint. SP extends it to M14's usable-floor-space context to prevent conflation with the area-subtraction operation (room − furniture).*

## 1.3 VOCABULARY ARCHITECTURE

**[REQUIRED]**

M14 introduces no new vocabulary. This is a consolidation and application module — all mathematical terms were taught in M1–M13. The module's vocabulary work is purely reinforcement: terms students already know are used in a new context (room design). The primary vocabulary challenge is the interface between mathematical terms and real-world context vocabulary: "area" becomes "floor space," "composite figure" becomes "room shape," "dimensions" become "measurements." The Guide maintains mathematical precision while using context vocabulary naturally — never replacing one with the other, but connecting them: "The room has an area of 70 square feet — that's 70 square feet of floor space."

**Assessment Vocabulary (appears on state tests and Exit Check):** area, square units (square feet), dimensions, multiply, add, composite figure, decompose, rectangle, missing side, related sides

**Note on context vocabulary:** "Room," "furniture," "floor space," and "usable space" are not math vocabulary — they are real-world words that connect to the math students have learned. The Guide uses them naturally but always ties them to the mathematical term: "floor space" = area. Students should hear both and understand they mean the same thing in this context.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

**[REQUIRED]**

M14 is a culminating application module — any gap from M11–M13 can surface under the cognitive load of a multi-step contextual problem. The TVP identifies six misconception risks in priority order.

### 1.4.1 #7: Wrong Missing Side Length (PRIMARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** Given an L-shaped room with some sides labeled and others marked "?", the student subtracts a vertical dimension from a horizontal total (or vice versa), producing a plausible-looking value that cascades through the rest of the workflow.

**Why It Happens:** Room shapes have more visible dimensions than the abstract figures in M13, and the real-world context (room labels, furniture dimensions nearby) adds visual noise. Students may grab the nearest visible number rather than identifying which sides are directionally related. The sustained multi-phase problem increases working memory load, making careful dimension selection harder.

**Visual Cue:** Highlight Related Sides (remediation only in M14) — color-matches the missing side with its related known sides on the same directional axis and displays the constraint equation with actual values. Identical to M13's remediation-level HRS.

**Prevention Strategy:** M13 established directional reasoning ("horizontal with horizontal, vertical with vertical"). M14 reinforces through consistent Guide language when referencing dimensions: always name the direction ("Look at the horizontal sides of the room") and always name the object ("the room's left side," not "that side"). Clear visual separation between room and furniture prevents cross-object dimension confusion.

---

### 1.4.2 #12: Multiplying All Given Numbers (SECONDARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** Given a room diagram with dimension labels AND separate furniture with dimension labels, the student multiplies dimensions from DIFFERENT objects (e.g., room width × desk length) or multiplies more than two dimensions of the same figure indiscriminately.

**Why It Happens:** M14 has more visible numbers on screen than any previous module — room dimensions, resolved missing sides, AND furniture dimensions. The multi-object display creates new confusion opportunities that didn't exist in M11–M13's single-figure problems.

**Visual Cue:** Clear visual separation between room and furniture (furniture displayed separately, labeled by name). Per-rectangle dimension highlighting after decomposition (from M12) — tapping a component rectangle shows only ITS dimensions.

**Prevention Strategy:** The Guide always names the object when referencing dimensions: "Look at the bed's dimensions" not "look at the dimensions." Furniture items are displayed separately from the room diagram with distinct visual treatment. The decompose-first workflow naturally constrains which dimensions students work with — they calculate one rectangle at a time.

---

### 1.4.3 #11: Completing the Rectangle (SECONDARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student treats the L-shaped room as a complete rectangle, calculating area as total width × total height (e.g., "The room is 10 × 9 = 90 square feet" when the actual L-shaped room is 70 square feet).

**Why It Happens:** L-shaped rooms invite bounding-box thinking, especially when the "missing" notch is small relative to the overall shape. The real-world context may reinforce this — students may think of rooms as rectangular by default.

**Visual Cue:** The decomposition step itself — placing a decomposition line makes the two component rectangles visually distinct and prevents bounding-box calculation.

**Prevention Strategy:** The decompose-first workflow is the primary guard. Additionally, the furniture-fitting question provides a natural error catch: if the room area is calculated too large (bounding box), the "Will it fit?" question produces an answer that's too easy ("Obviously yes, there's tons of space") — this can prompt the Guide to redirect. The key defense is that students have been decomposing composite figures since M11; by M14, the habit should be automatic.

---

### 1.4.4 #6: Overlapping Decomposition (TERTIARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student decomposes the room but counts the corner/junction region in both rectangles, inflating the total area.

**Why It Happens:** Careless placement of the decomposition line, especially if the student rushes through the familiar decomposition step to get to the novel furniture-fitting question.

**Visual Cue:** Color-coding after decomposition — each rectangle highlights in a distinct color, making overlap visually obvious.

**Prevention Strategy:** Low risk by M14. Students have practiced clean decomposition across M11–M13 with validated decomposition lines. The Composite Figures tool validates all decomposition lines — invalid attempts receive specific feedback.

---

### 1.4.5 #5: Not Multiplying Side Lengths (TERTIARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student has the correct dimensions for a rectangle (room component or furniture item) but adds the side lengths instead of multiplying — e.g., calculating a 7 × 4 bed as 7 + 4 = 11 sq ft instead of 28 sq ft.

**Why It Happens:** By M14, this misconception is largely resolved — students have been multiplying side lengths since M4. However, M14's cognitive load is higher than prior modules (sustained multi-step problem, multiple objects), and under load, students may revert to earlier habits. The risk is highest when calculating simple furniture areas, where the multiplication feels "too easy" relative to the room decomposition work.

**Visual Cue:** Equation Builder displays the multiplication expression with color-coded terms. If the student's selected value is implausibly small relative to the rectangle shown, the Guide redirects.

**Prevention Strategy:** Low risk at this stage. The decompose-calculate-add workflow from M11–M13 has reinforced multiplication as the area operation. Monitor but do not over-scaffold.

---

### 1.4.6 Context-Specific: Confusing Room Area with Furniture Area

**Trigger Behavior:** Student calculates room area correctly but then uses room dimensions when calculating furniture area (e.g., multiplies the room's 10 ft side by the desk's 3 ft side).

**Why It Happens:** When room and furniture are visible simultaneously, dimension cross-contamination can occur — especially if the Guide says "look at the dimensions" without specifying which object.

**Visual Cue:** Furniture displayed SEPARATELY from the room diagram. Each furniture item is a simple labeled rectangle below or beside the room, clearly labeled with its name and dimensions ("Bed: 7 ft × 4 ft").

**Prevention Strategy:** The Guide ALWAYS names the object when referencing dimensions: "How much floor space does the bed need? Look at the bed's dimensions — 7 feet by 4 feet." The pronoun ambiguity constraint (from TVP) is a hard rule: the Guide must NEVER say "look at the dimensions" without specifying WHICH object.

### Core Purpose

**One-line summary:** Activate composite-figure recognition and missing-side awareness in a room design context, building curiosity about the sustained problem ("Can furniture fit in this room?") that drives the entire Lesson.

**Key Function:** The Warmup transitions students from M13's abstract composite figures to M14's real-world room context. Students see "Ana's New Room" — an L-shaped floor plan with missing measurements — and generate their own observations and questions. This positions the Lesson as answering the student's own question, not presenting a teacher-assigned task. The Notice & Wonder format also activates the specific prior knowledge M14 needs: composite shape recognition, awareness that some measurements are missing (M13 skill), and curiosity about what the room can be used for (M14's driving question).

**Why this serves the concept:**

* M14 is an APPLICATION module — no new skills are taught. The Warmup's job is to reactivate skills from M11–M13 in a new context, not to preview new learning.
* The room floor plan makes the familiar shape (ungridded L-shape with missing sides) feel different from M13's abstract figures — students must recognize it as the SAME mathematical structure they already know.
* Notice & Wonder lets students discover the connection between "composite figure with missing sides" and "room that needs to be measured" on their own, reducing teacher-directed framing.
* Furniture is deliberately NOT shown in the Warmup — this creates a natural curiosity gap ("What goes in this room?") that the Lesson's Mid section answers.

**Test:** If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson? **Yes.** Without it, students would encounter the room floor plan cold — without having recognized it as a familiar composite figure. The context shift from abstract figures (M13) to real-world rooms (M14) is significant enough that it needs an explicit bridge. Students who skip the Warmup may be thrown by the visual change and not realize they already know how to handle this shape.

### Warmup Type Rationale

**Type selected:** Notice & Wonder

**Why this type fits M14:** M14's room floor plan is a visually rich image that invites open observation — students notice the shape, the missing measurements, the room-like features. Notice & Wonder is the recommended type for "complex concept needing deep priming" and "visual-rich content" (Playbook §4D). The TVP explicitly specifies a Notice & Wonder format with MC notice (multi-select) and MC wonder (single-select).

**Why not other types:**

* Activation — Close, but M14's warmup isn't reactivating a specific skill step-by-step (like M13 did). It's orienting to a new context. Notice & Wonder serves this better.
* Binary Choice — Too narrow. The room floor plan has multiple features worth noticing; a binary comparison would lose the open-exploration quality the TVP specifies.
* Mystery Reveal — The "mystery" in M14 is the furniture-fitting question, which doesn't emerge until the Lesson. The Warmup's mystery is simpler: "What IS this shape, and can we figure out its missing measurements?"

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

### Interaction W.2: What Do You Wonder? [NOTICE & WONDER — WONDER + BRIDGE]

* **Purpose:** Channel student curiosity into the driving questions that the Lesson will answer. M14's hook is "Ana just moved into a new room. Here's the floor plan" — a narrative anchor. ### Interaction 3.1: Calculate Usable Floor Space [APPLICATION]

* **Purpose:** Student calculates room area minus furniture area. This is the first SUBTRACTION in an area context in Unit 2. The Guide explicitly frames the operation contrast: adding areas (decomposition) vs. subtracting areas (usable floor space).
* **Visual: Composite Figures (room mode, furniture placed).** Ana's room with both furniture items visually placed in their regions. Room area label (70 sq ft). Furniture area labels (bed 28 sq ft, desk 15 sq ft, total 43 sq ft).
* **Guide:** [ATTENTION] "Here's something important. To find the room's area, we ADDED rectangle areas together. Now we're doing something different: we're SUBTRACTING to find what's left. The room is 70 square feet. The furniture takes up 43. How much floor space is left?"
* **Prompt:** "How much usable floor space is left? Room: 70 sq ft. Furniture: 43 sq ft."
* **Student Action:** MC selection.
  * **Options:** A) 27 sq ft, B) 113 sq ft, C) 33 sq ft, D) 70 sq ft
* **Correct Answer:** A (27 sq ft — 70 − 43 = 27)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A = Correct — 70 − 43 = 27
  * B = Addition error — 70 + 43 = 113 (added instead of subtracted — confused the operation)
  * C = Arithmetic error — 70 − 43 ≠ 33
  * D = Ignored subtraction — reported room area without subtracting furniture
* **On Correct:** "You subtracted 43 from 70. 27 square feet of floor space left. Ana's room has space for the bed, the desk, and still 27 square feet to walk around, open drawers, maybe even put down a rug later."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note (Operation Contrast):** The Guide's [ATTENTION] tag marks the explicit contrast between ADDING areas (decomposition, which students have done since M11) and SUBTRACTING areas (usable floor space, new in M14). This distinction is pedagogically critical — without it, students may conflate the two operations. The contrast is stated directly: "we ADDED... now we're SUBTRACTING." This is not a new mathematical skill (students know subtraction), but a new APPLICATION of subtraction in the area context. Per §1.2: subtraction decomposition (bounding box minus notch) is NOT used; this is subtraction of object areas from total space.

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS

**[REQUIRED]**

### KDD-1: CRA Departure — Pure Application Module

M14 does not follow the standard Concrete → Relational → Abstract → Application sequence. Every mathematical skill used in M14 was taught in M1–M13. The Lesson structure follows the room design problem (understand room → determine furniture fit → calculate remaining space) rather than the CRA arc. This is the defining characteristic of M14: orchestration of known skills, not acquisition of new ones.

### KDD-2: Worked Examples Departure — Orchestration Model

Traditional worked examples with skill fading don't apply because no new skill is taught. M14 replaces worked examples with an orchestration model: the Guide's think-aloud (1.1) models PLANNING — knowing what to do in what order — and subsequent interactions are guided practice of known skills with decreasing narration. The fading is in Guide verbosity, not in scaffolding removal.

### KDD-3: Step-Naming Scaffold as Metacognitive Architecture

The TVP's step-naming pattern ("Step 1: figure out the room's area") is implemented as Guide language only, not as a visual element. This keeps the scaffold lightweight while providing essential metacognitive support for the sustained multi-phase problem. Step names appear at 1.1, 1.5 (closure), 2.1 (opening), 2.5 (closure), 3.1 (implicit in operation contrast), and 3.2 (full recap). Removing or weakening the step names would increase the risk of students losing track of where they are in the problem chain.

### KDD-4: Vertical Decomposition — Accept Both, Adapt Placement

The lesson room supports two valid decompositions (horizontal: 10 × 4 + 6 × 5; vertical: 6 × 9 + 4 × 4). Both yield 70 sq ft. The horizontal decomposition is pedagogically cleaner for furniture placement because each region accommodates one furniture item. The vertical decomposition creates a 4 × 4 region where the desk doesn't fit, forcing both items into the larger region. Rather than steering students toward horizontal, the system accepts both and the Guide adapts placement guidance. This is a richer learning experience: decomposition choice affects practical outcomes, reinforcing that mathematical decisions have real-world consequences.

### KDD-5: "Enough Area ≠ It Fits" — Guided Discussion, Not Gotcha

The TVP's key mathematical modeling insight is surfaced through guided discussion in 2.3, not through a problem where furniture doesn't fit. Furniture fits in the main Lesson (positive resolution for third graders). The "doesn't fit" scenario appears only in Practice STRETCH. This maintains emotional safety while still building conceptual awareness. The 2.3 interaction offers "It depends — the area is enough, but we need to check if they actually fit" as the best answer, with the Guide explaining the principle regardless of which option the student selects.

### KDD-6: Operation Contrast — Explicit Framing at Single Point

The first subtraction in an area context (usable floor space = room − furniture) is explicitly framed against additive decomposition in 3.1. The contrast is stated directly: "We ADDED rectangle areas together. Now we're SUBTRACTING to find what's left." This is placed at a single, prominent moment rather than repeated, to maximize impact without belaboring the point. The EC tests the subtraction skill (EC.3) but not the conceptual contrast — the Lesson plants it, and future units can build on it.

### KDD-7: T-Shape Extension Deferred to Practice

The TVP specifies an optional T-shaped second room in Lesson Late. This is deferred to Practice STRETCH (ISF-3) because Late is already tight at ~2–3 minutes and the module runs slightly long as an optional capstone. The core Late experience (usable floor space + reflection) is pedagogically complete without it. Students who encounter T-shapes in Practice STRETCH will apply the same decomposition skills to a 3-rectangle figure.

### KDD-8: EC Error Isolation Design

EC problems share a room context but must test skills independently. If a student miscalculates room area in EC.1, their EC.2 and EC.3 scores should reflect furniture-calculation and comparison skills, not cascade from an earlier error. The system provides the correct room area before EC.2 if EC.1 is wrong: "Ben's room is 44 square feet." This is a deliberate engineering specification, not a pedagogical hedge. The trade-off: the student loses the opportunity to use their own area result downstream, but gains the ability to demonstrate furniture-placement reasoning and comparison skills — which are the core M14 orchestration competencies. Without isolation, a single early error would mask whether the student can do the rest of the workflow.

### KDD-9: Unit Capstone Synthesis

M14 Synthesis serves double duty: M14-specific orchestration reflection (S.3) and unit-wide toolkit celebration (S.2, S.4, S.5). The toolkit walkthrough (M1 tiling → M14 room design) is the TVP's explicit closing arc. Mathematical modeling (MP4) is named but not taught — students hear that their room design work has a professional name, connecting student actions to adult mathematical practices. No M15 bridge exists; the closure connects forward to real life.

### KDD-10: Pronoun-Ambiguity Constraint

M14 is the first module with room AND furniture dimensions visible simultaneously. The Guide never says "the dimensions" or "look at the dimensions" without specifying which object (room vs. furniture). This is a hard rule from the TVP's pronoun-ambiguity constraint, implemented consistently across all interactions where both room and furniture are visible (2.1–2.5, 3.1). Removing object names from Guide language would reintroduce Misconception #12 (cross-contamination) risk.
