# MODULE 2: Tiling Rectangles — No Gaps, No Overlaps

**Version:** 03.13.26

---

# BACKBONE

---

## 1.0 THE ONE THING


Students must understand that unit squares must fit together with no gaps and no overlaps to accurately measure area — gaps mean uncounted space (area too low), and overlaps mean double-counted space (area too high).

**Critical Misconception:** #1.0 — Gaps/Overlaps Acceptable. Students believe tiling with gaps or overlaps still produces a valid area measurement. They count tiles without evaluating coverage quality, leading to incorrect counts.

**Success Indicator:** Given a rectangle and unit square tiles, the student tiles the shape with no gaps and no overlaps, counts the tiles, and states the area in square units. Given a pre-made tiling with an error, the student identifies whether the error is a gap or an overlap and explains why it produces a wrong count.

**Biggest Risk:** Students learn the PROCEDURE of tiling neatly without understanding WHY gaps and overlaps are problems. If the connection between coverage quality and count accuracy isn't made explicit (gaps → too few, overlaps → too many), students may tile correctly through mechanical compliance rather than conceptual understanding. The sequential error presentation (gaps first, then overlaps) is designed to make each error type's numerical consequence visible before combining them into a single rule.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS


### 1.4.1 #1.0: Gaps/Overlaps Acceptable (PRIMARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** When asked to find area, student counts tiles even when they overlap or leave gaps. Student may place tiles carelessly and report the tile count without checking coverage quality. When shown a pre-made tiling with errors, student says the area measurement is correct.

**Why It Happens:** In M1, overlap detection was mechanically active (visual + audio feedback prevented overlaps), and gap feedback was not active. Students may have learned "just place tiles and count" without developing the concept that coverage quality affects count accuracy. The mechanical feedback did the quality checking — the student didn't.

**Visual Cue:** Grid Rectangles display showing (a) a rectangle with gaps — visible empty grid squares between tiles, with a count showing "10 tiles" when the real area is 12; (b) the same rectangle with overlaps — stacked tiles visible, with a count showing "14 tiles" when the real area is 12. The numerical consequences (10 vs. 12 vs. 14) make the error concrete.

**Prevention Strategy:** Sequential error presentation (Lesson Section 1) surfaces each error type independently with explicit numerical consequences before students tile. Students DISCOVER the errors and their effects before being told the rules. The self-check routine ("Any gaps? Any overlaps?") is modeled and practiced through Sections 2-3.

### 1.4.2 #9.0: Array Structure Not Seen (PREVIEW)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student sees a rectangle of tiles but doesn't recognize the row/column structure — counts randomly instead of by rows. With a 3×4 rectangle, student counts 1, 2, 3... 12 instead of recognizing "3 rows of 4."

**Why It Happens:** Students are still in the one-by-one counting phase (appropriate for M1-M2). Array/row-column structuring is an explicit instructional target in M3-M4 (Decision #2: Explicit Spatial Structuring). Including as PREVIEW because M2 students tile rectangles that inherently have row/column structure — some students may begin noticing it organically.

**Visual Cue:** Not actively addressed in M2. Students tile and count one-by-one. If a student spontaneously counts by rows, the Guide can acknowledge it ("You found a pattern!") without formally teaching structured counting.

**Prevention Strategy:** Not a prevention target in M2 — this is a PREVIEW. M2 establishes accurate tiling; M3 builds structure on top of it. The Synthesis bridge (highlighting one row of a completed tiling) is the only intentional connection to M3's structuring.

> **Design Note:** TVP also notes "Rectangle properties unclear (MODERATE)" as a pedagogical concern. This does not warrant a formal Misconception ID — it is addressed through Synthesis vocabulary activation (rectangle defined as 4 sides, 4 right angles, from Grade 2 knowledge). Not tracked in the Misconceptions database.

### Interaction W.1: What Do You Notice?

* **Purpose:** Activate M1's tiling experience. Students observe a correctly tiled rectangle and describe (via MC) what they notice. Hook: callback to M1 Synthesis.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** Full grid. 3×4 rectangle, all 12 squares shaded (complete, correct tiling). Horizontal orientation.
* **Guide:** "Last time, you ended by looking at some tiles that didn't fit quite right. But first, check this one out. What do you notice about how these tiles fit together?"
* **Prompt:** "What do you notice about the tiles? Pick one."
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** The tiles fit together with no space between them; Some tiles are on top of each other; There are empty spaces inside the shape; The tiles are different sizes
* **Correct Answer:** The tiles fit together with no space between them
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * The tiles fit together with no space between them = Correct (accurate description of complete, correct tiling — "good tiling" from M1)
  * Some tiles are on top of each other = Overlap concept — not visible in this display; selected if student projects overlap errors onto a correct tiling
  * There are empty spaces inside the shape = Gap concept — not visible in this display; selected if student projects gap errors onto a correct tiling
  * The tiles are different sizes = Unit squares are uniform; selected if student has misconception about tile size variation
* **On Correct:** "Every tile fits right next to the others. No space left over."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Voice Note:** Hook uses Personalization anchor (callback to M1) + Curiosity anchor ("check this one out"). Energy is Medium-High per Warmup voice guidelines. "Last time" uses session-relative language.

> **Design Note:** Each distractor maps to a concept taught in the Lesson (gaps, overlaps, non-uniform tiles), surfacing pre-existing confusion without correcting it.

> **Script Writer Note:** Three of four options describe things not visible in the image. For 8-year-olds, evaluating text descriptions against a visual is a subtler cognitive task than it appears — weaker readers may select a plausible-sounding statement. The Guide's "check this one out" must genuinely direct attention to the visual BEFORE the MC options appear. Consider a brief pause or visual focus cue.

### Interaction W.2: Same Area, Different Look

* **Purpose:** Judgment/noticing task. Students see two correctly tiled rectangles with the SAME area (12 square units) but different dimensions (3×4 and 2×6). Students recognize they're equal.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** Two rectangles side by side. Left: 3×4 (horizontal). Right: 2×6 (vertical). Both fully shaded. Full grid visible on both.
* **Guide:** "Here are two rectangles. They look pretty different. Which one covers more space?"
* **Prompt:** "Which rectangle has more area? Select A, B, or Same."
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** A (3×4), B (2×6), Same Area
* **Correct Answer:** Same Area (both are 12 square units)
* **Answer Rationale:**

  * B (2×6) = Selected based on taller/longer appearance ("taller = larger" reasoning)
* **On Correct:** "They look different, but they both have 12 tiles. Same number of tiles, same area."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** Primes the Module Mapping Question/Test Language stem: "These rectangles have the same area — how do you know?" The concept is SHOWN here but not formally taught.

## Section 1: Understanding the Problem

**CRA Stage: Concrete (1.1–1.4) → Relational (1.5) → Abstract (1.6–1.8)**

**Scaffolding Pattern:** Symmetrical observe→act for each error type. Gaps: 1.1 (observe) → 1.2 (act). Overlaps: 1.3 (observe) → 1.4 (act). Equal pacing and landing time for each error type.

---

### Interaction 1.1: The Gap Problem

* **Purpose:** First error presentation — students observe a rectangle tiled WITH GAPS and discover the numerical consequence (area count too low).
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** Full grid. 3×4 rectangle (12 squares total). 10 squares shaded, 2 squares unshaded (gaps).
* **Guide:** "Here's a rectangle with empty spaces inside. The tiles cover 10 squares, but the whole rectangle is 12 squares. The empty spaces made the count too low."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** Display-only — student sees the error and its consequence before being asked to identify it. The word "gap" is NOT used yet.

---

### Interaction 1.2: What Went Wrong Here? (Gaps)

* **Purpose:** Student identifies what's wrong. Formal term "gap" introduced immediately after concrete observation — vocabulary grounded in experience.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** Same 3×4 rectangle with gaps from 1.1.
* **Guide:** "Those empty spaces have a name — they're called GAPS. A gap is space left uncovered. Gaps made the count too low because some space wasn't counted. How many gaps are there?"
* **Prompt:** "How many gaps are there? Select 1, 2, or 3."
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** 1, 2, 3
* **Correct Answer:** 2
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 2 = Correct (2 unshaded squares visible within the 3×4 rectangle boundary)
  * 1 = Undercount — missed one gap; may have focused on only part of the rectangle
  * 3 = Overcount — may have misidentified a shaded square's edge as a gap
* **On Correct:** "Two gaps. Two squares that didn't get counted! That's why the count was off — 10 instead of 12."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

---

### Interaction 1.3: The Overlap Problem

* **Purpose:** Second error presentation — students observe a rectangle tiled WITH OVERLAPS. Display-only to mirror the gap observation pattern.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** Full grid. 3×4 rectangle. All 12 squares shaded PLUS 2 tiles visually stacked/overlapping. Authored pre-tiled state using overlap visual treatment (same color change appearance as runtime overlap detection, for visual consistency). Display mode — no student interaction. Tile count displayed: 14.
* **Guide:** "Now look at this one. See where tiles are stacked on top of each other? The tiles cover 14 squares, but the whole rectangle is only 12 squares. The stacked tiles made the count too high."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** Mirrors the gap pattern: 1.1 observe → 1.2 act; 1.3 observe → 1.4 act. The word "overlap" is NOT used yet.

> **Engineering Note:** The visual is an authored pre-tiled state, NOT runtime overlap detection. The overlap detection system does not need to be active in display mode. The visual treatment should match runtime appearance for consistency.

### Interaction 1.4: What Went Wrong Here? (Overlaps)

**[RELATIONAL PHASE — DEDICATED INTERACTION]**

### Module-Specific Lesson Guidance

**Sequential Error Presentation with Symmetrical Scaffolding:** Present gaps FIRST (1.1-1.2), then overlaps (1.3-1.4), then correct tiling (1.5). Each error type gets the same observe→act pacing.

**Ternary Error Classification:** Error identification interactions (1.7, 1.8) use three options: Gap, Overlap, Correct — no errors. Trains genuine evaluation.

**Self-Check Routine Fading:**
1. 2.1a — Guide MODELS
2. 2.2 — Guide PROMPTS
3. 3.1+ — Student PERFORMS independently

**Overlap Detection vs. Gap Awareness:**
* Overlap detection is MECHANICAL (always-on visual + audio). Motor-level correction.
* Gap awareness is CONCEPTUAL (taught in Section 1, practiced via self-check). Cognitive-level prevention.
* This asymmetry is intentional.

---

### Misconception Prevention

**#1.0: Gaps/Overlaps Acceptable (PRIMARY)** — Section 1 makes numerical consequences visible. Self-check routine ("Any gaps? Any overlaps?") is the internalized prevention strategy. Pipeline handles remediation.

**#9.0: Array Structure Not Seen (PREVIEW)** — NOT a prevention target in M2. One-by-one counting is expected. If student spontaneously counts by rows, Guide acknowledges without teaching.

### Interaction S.1: Which Ones Follow the Rule? — Pattern Discovery

* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** Four rectangles in a 2×2 grid:
  * Top-left: 2×3, correctly tiled (6 tiles)

  * Bottom-left: 4×3, correctly tiled (12 tiles)
  * Bottom-right: 2×5, correctly tiled (10 tiles)
* **Guide:** "Look at these four rectangles. Three of them are tiled the right way. One has a problem. Which one doesn't follow the rule?"
* **Prompt:** "Which tiling has a problem? Select A, B, C, or D."
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** A (2×3 correct), B (3×4 gap), C (4×3 correct), D (2×5 correct)
* **Correct Answer:** B (3×4 with gap)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * B (3×4 gap) = Correct (gap visible — the only tiling that breaks "no gaps, no overlaps")

  * C (4×3 correct) = Follows the rule (complete coverage, count matches)

### Interaction S.2: Where Does This Rule Matter? — Real-World Bridge

* **Purpose:** Connect the rule to real-world contexts through language framing. Uses Grid Rectangles capabilities.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** Three 3×4 rectangles:
  * A: Correctly tiled (12 squares, all shaded)
  * B: Tiled with 2 gaps (10 shaded, 2 empty)
  * C: Tiled with 2 overlaps (14 tiles shown, 2 stacked — authored pre-tiled state)
* **Guide:** "No gaps and no overlaps isn't just a math rule. Imagine you're putting tiles on a kitchen floor. Which of these would work?"
* **Prompt:** "Which tiling would work for a real floor? Select A, B, or C."
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** A (complete tiling), B (gaps), C (overlaps)
* **Correct Answer:** A (complete tiling)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A (complete tiling) = Correct (no gaps, no overlaps — every square covered exactly once; would work for a real floor)
  * B (gaps) = Incorrect (bare floor would show through the gaps — incomplete coverage)
  * C (overlaps) = Incorrect (tiles stacked on each other would create bumps — uneven surface)
* **On Correct:** "A! No gaps means no bare floor. No overlaps means no bumps. The tiling rule works the same way in the real world."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** Real-world connection comes from Guide language, not custom illustrations. Grid Rectangles capabilities confirmed for displaying three simultaneous patterns.

### Interaction S.3: What Helped You Most? — Metacognitive Reflection

* **Purpose:** Strategy identification. Students reflect on what helped them tile accurately.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** The correctly tiled 3×4 from the Opening Frame.

* **On Correct (per selection):**
  * "Checking for gaps" → "Checking for gaps is exactly what strong mathematicians do — they don't just finish, they verify."

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS SUMMARY


### KDD 1: Observe Before Act

Students observe each error type (1.1, 1.3) before being asked to identify it (1.2, 1.4). Vocabulary attaches to concrete experience, not description — "gap" is named in 1.2 only after the student has seen gaps in 1.1. Change the order and vocabulary becomes arbitrary labels.

### KDD 2: Numerical Consequences Are the Teaching Mechanism

M2 doesn't teach "gaps are bad" as a rule to memorize. It shows 10 vs. 12 vs. 14 on the same 3×4 rectangle and lets the arithmetic make the case. Area is held constant at 12 across all of Section 1 so the ONLY variable is tiling quality. The rule emerges from evidence, not instruction.

### KDD 3: Symmetrical Scaffolding for Parallel Error Types

Each error type gets identical pacing: observe → identify. Gaps: 1.1 → 1.2. Overlaps: 1.3 → 1.4. If one error type gets more time than the other, students build a stronger mental model for one and a weaker model for the other. Symmetry prevents this.

### KDD 4: Ternary Classification, Not Binary

Error identification (1.7, 1.8, EC.2) uses three options: Gap, Overlap, Correct — no errors. Binary forces elimination ("it's not a gap, so it must be an overlap"). Ternary forces genuine evaluation. Interaction 1.8 shows a correct tiling so students practice all three outcomes before EC.

### KDD 5: Display Mode Before Target Mode

Section 1 is entirely observation (Grid Rectangles in display). Section 2 transitions to action (Grid Rectangles as target). Students understand the rules before they have to follow them. This prevents the risk in §1.0: mechanical compliance without conceptual understanding.

### KDD 6: Vocabulary Timing Is Load-Bearing

"Gap" introduced in 1.2, immediately after observing gaps in 1.1. "Overlap" in 1.4, immediately after 1.3. "Tiling" in 1.6, after all three states compared. Each term lands at maximum concrete grounding. Moving any term earlier makes it a label students memorize before they understand.

### KDD 7: Overlap Detection Is Permanent; Cognitive Scaffolding Fades

Overlap detection (visual color change + audio) never turns off — it's a motor-level correction. But completion feedback fades: immediate (2.1) → submit-only (2.2) → self-check before submit (3.1+). The asymmetry is deliberate: the tool handles motor errors permanently; the student takes ownership of cognitive errors progressively.

### KDD 8: Self-Check as Cognitive Routine, Not Assessable Skill

The self-check ("Any gaps? Any overlaps?") is modeled (2.1a), prompted (2.2), then expected independently (3.1+). Pipeline cannot detect whether a student actually self-checks — there's no telemetry for it. The decision to invest lesson time in a non-assessable routine is a bet that the habit matters for learning even when you can't measure it.

### KDD 9: Real-World Connection Through Language, Not Illustration

S.2 uses Grid Rectangles to show three tiling patterns and Guide language to make the real-world bridge ("Imagine you're putting tiles on a kitchen floor"). When a pedagogical goal can be achieved through language, don't request custom visuals. The toy constrains the display; language extends the conceptual frame.

### KDD 10: Practice Distribution and Dimension Strategy
