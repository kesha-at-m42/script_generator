# MODULE 11: Area is Additive

# BACKBONE

---

## 1.0 THE ONE THING

[REQUIRED]

A composite figure is NOT a rectangle, so you can't just multiply two sides — but it's MADE of rectangles. The only new skill is decomposition: seeing where to draw a line that splits the shape into rectangles students already know how to measure. Find each rectangle's area, add them together, and the total IS the area of the whole shape. This works because area is additive — the total area of a figure equals the sum of its non-overlapping parts. Students don't need new calculation skills; they need a new way of SEEING shapes. The grid makes every square visible, providing a safety net: decomposition is new, but the area-finding inside each rectangle is M3–M4 territory. Multiple valid decompositions of the same figure always produce the same total — this flexibility is mathematically powerful, not confusing.

**CRA Stage:** Concrete/Representational — full grids throughout. This is a deliberate CRA RESET. After M7's grid fading and M8–M10's fully abstract work, M11 returns to full grids because composite figures are a NEW concept requiring concrete support (Decision #3: "M11: Full grids return for composite figures"). Students who fluently multiply dimensions for single rectangles still need to SEE the unit squares when learning to decompose a shape into parts. The CRA progression resets for the new concept, not because students regressed. M12 begins grid fading for composite figures.

**Critical Misconception:** #11 — Completing the Rectangle. Students treat L-shapes and composite figures as complete rectangles by multiplying the longest horizontal side by the longest vertical side. This produces the area of the bounding rectangle, not the composite figure. The full grid is the primary defense — students can SEE that the "missing corner" has no squares. A proactive bounding-rectangle demonstration in Early addresses this before students encounter it independently.

**Success Indicator:** Given a composite figure with a full grid, the student independently draws a valid decomposition line, calculates each component rectangle's area by multiplying its dimensions, adds the areas, and arrives at the correct total — demonstrating they understand that area is additive and that decomposition is a strategy, not a formula.

**Biggest Risk:** Students learn the decompose-calculate-add PROCEDURE without understanding WHY it works (the additive property of area). If decomposition is taught as "follow these steps" rather than "the parts add up to the whole because area works that way," students will struggle when figures become more complex in M12–M14. The procedure must be grounded in the conceptual insight that non-overlapping parts account for every square exactly once.

## 1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES

[REQUIRED]

### ✅ Must Teach

* **Decomposition of composite figures into non-overlapping rectangles:** The core skill. Students draw decomposition lines (straight, horizontal or vertical, edge-to-edge) to partition a composite figure into rectangles. This is the only new procedural skill in M11 — all other skills (multiplication for area, addition for totals) are established.
* **Area as additive property:** The total area of a composite figure equals the sum of the areas of its non-overlapping rectangular parts. This is the conceptual insight, not just a procedure. Students should understand WHY decompose-calculate-add works, not just HOW.
* **Multiple valid decompositions:** Different decomposition lines produce different rectangles but always the same total area. Students see at least two decompositions of the same figure and verify they produce equal totals. In Late, a light-touch efficiency observation (1 problem only) notes that some decompositions produce fewer/simpler pieces — but efficiency is an observation, NOT an assessment target. **Validate-first safeguard:** A correct decomposition is NEVER wrong, even if inefficient. The Guide must ALWAYS affirm a valid decomposition before optionally mentioning efficiency.
* **Parenthesized expressions for composite area:** (a × b) + (c × d) = product₁ + product₂ = total. Parentheses are descriptive notation showing each rectangle's area calculation. Students INTERPRET parenthesized expressions displayed by the Equation Builder; they do NOT write or construct them independently.
* **Inside-corner identification as decomposition heuristic:** "Find a corner that goes INWARD — that's where you can draw a straight line to make two rectangles." A transferable strategy for where to place decomposition lines.
* **Proactive "completing the rectangle" misconception prevention:** Explicit demonstration that multiplying the longest sides gives the bounding rectangle's area, not the composite figure's area. All students receive this — it costs 30 seconds and prevents the most common error in composite figure work.
* **Figure types for decomposition and calculation:** L-shapes (Early through Late), T-shapes and U-shapes (Late only, 1–2 problems). All 2–3 component rectangles. **Exception:** the inside-corner identification beat in Early uses display-only figures including one T-shape — students identify corners via MC but do not decompose or calculate area. This recognition-only exposure prevents the false generalization that all composite figures have exactly one inside corner.
* **Vocabulary:** "composite figure" and "decompose" as active vocabulary. "Rectilinear" as receptive vocabulary (guide uses, students hear, not assessed).
* **Module Mapping vocabulary teaching note:** Decompose = "break into parts." Composite = "made of multiple parts." Rectilinear = "made of rectangles, all right angles."

### ❌ Must Not Include

* **Perimeter** — Per Decision #5. Not mentioned, even for contrast. The "completing the rectangle" misconception check references "bounding rectangle" and "missing corner," not perimeter.
* **Missing dimensions ("?" sides)** — Finding missing side lengths on composite figures begins at M13. M11 labels all dimensions explicitly.
* **Subtraction decomposition** — The strategy of "bounding rectangle minus missing piece" is taught in M12 Late. M11's "by itself" language deliberately foreshadows this but does not teach it. Students only use additive decomposition (break into parts, add areas).
* **Ungridded figures** — All M11 figures have full grids (Decision #3). Dimensions-only composite figures begin at M12.
* **Figures with more than 3 components** — M13–M14 handle complex multi-component figures. M11 introduces the concept with 2–3 components only.
* **Real-world contexts** — M11 uses generic "square units." Real-world contexts (patios, rooms, gardens) come in M12. The OUR curriculum's garden context is adapted: the decomposition concept transfers, but the context is deferred to maintain M11's focus on the mathematical structure.
* **Order of operations language** — Parentheses are descriptive notation ("the parentheses show each rectangle's area"), not prescriptive rules. "Order of operations" is beyond Grade 3 scope.
* **Grid Rectangles as primary tool** — M11's primary tool is Composite Figures. Grid Rectangles appear briefly in Warmup only (2 activation problems). All other phases use Composite Figures exclusively.
* **Equation Builder in student-construction mode** — The Equation Builder DISPLAYS parenthesized expressions based on the student's decomposition. Students do not construct or type expressions. They confirm values via MC.
* **Division language** — Factor-pair reasoning uses multiplication framing only. Division not introduced in Unit 2.
* **Formal "square number" vocabulary** — Not relevant to M11 (that was M10's diagonal observation).

## 1.3 VOCABULARY ARCHITECTURE

[REQUIRED]

M11 introduces two new active terms — "composite figure" and "decompose" — and one receptive term — "rectilinear." The instructional focus is on applying existing area vocabulary (multiply, dimensions, area, square units) in a new context: shapes made of multiple rectangles. Parenthesized expression notation is introduced through the Equation Builder display, not as vocabulary instruction.

**Assessment Vocabulary (appears on state tests and Exit Check):** area, square units, dimensions, multiply, add, composite figure, decompose, rectangle, expression

**Note on vocabulary staging:** "Composite figure" and "decompose" are introduced AFTER concrete experience, per the vocabulary-after-grounding rule (Known Pattern #11). Students first COUNT the L-shape's squares in the Warmup, then SEE the system decompose it, then PRACTICE the workflow in Early — only then does the Guide name the strategy and the shapes. "Rectilinear" is introduced later as a receptive label: the Guide uses it naturally ("these are called rectilinear figures — all straight edges, all right angles") but students are not expected to produce or be assessed on it.

### Vocabulary Notes

**"Composite figure" (new active):** Introduced after concrete experience. Teaching note from Module Mapping: "Composite = made of multiple parts. Like a composite sandwich — multiple layers." The figure is composite because it's made of multiple rectangles. Introduced AFTER the student has seen the system decompose at least 2 figures.

**"Decompose" (new active):** Teaching note from Module Mapping: "Decompose = break into parts. The opposite of compose." Introduced as a label for what the system and student are already DOING. Not definition-first.

**"Rectilinear" (new receptive):** Teaching note from Module Mapping: "Rectilinear = made of rectangles, all right angles." Every edge is straight (horizontal or vertical), every corner is a right angle. Any rectilinear figure can be decomposed into rectangles. Guide introduces and uses the term naturally; students are not expected to produce or be assessed on it. "Composite figure" is the active term students use; "rectilinear" describes the TYPE of composite figure.

**Parenthesized expression notation:** "(4 × 3) + (5 × 2) = 12 + 10 = 22." The Equation Builder generates this automatically based on the student's decomposition. Parentheses are DESCRIPTIVE notation — they show the structure of what students are already doing (find each rectangle's area, then add), not a formula rule to follow. Guide language: "The parentheses show each rectangle's area — one set of parentheses for each rectangle you made." Do NOT introduce "order of operations" — this is beyond Grade 3 scope.

**Language patterns for M11:**

* "This figure is made of __ rectangles."
* "Rectangle A is __ by __. Its area is __ square units."
* "Rectangle B is __ by __. Its area is __ square units."
* "(__ × __) + (__ × __) = __ + __ = __ square units."
* "The total area of the figure is __ square units."

### Terms to Avoid (Save for Later Modules)

* **"Perimeter"** — Do not use, even for contrast. Per Decision #5.
* **"Formula"** (standalone) — Suggests rote memorization. Use: "Multiply the dimensions to find the area of each rectangle, then add." Same constraint as M7–M10.
* **"Divide" / "Division"** — Not introduced in Unit 2. Decomposition uses spatial language ("break into parts"), not arithmetic language ("divide").
* **"Order of operations"** — Parentheses are descriptive notation. Grade 3 does not teach formal operation ordering.
* **"Subtract"** (in decomposition context) — Subtraction decomposition (bounding rectangle minus missing piece) is M12 Late. M11 uses additive decomposition only. Students may naturally add within the workflow, but the Guide should not frame decomposition as subtraction.
* **"Missing side"** — No "?" dimensions in M11. All sides are labeled. Missing side-length problems begin at M13.
* **"Memorize"** — Decomposition is a strategy, not a fact to memorize.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

[REQUIRED]

### 1.4.1 #11: Completing the Rectangle (PRIMARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student treats an L-shape or composite figure as a complete rectangle by multiplying the longest horizontal side by the longest vertical side. For an L-shape with bounding box 6 × 8, the student answers "48 square units" (the bounding rectangle area) instead of the correct area (e.g., 38). The student mentally "fills in" the missing corner, treating the composite figure as the smallest rectangle that could contain it.

**Why It's a Barrier:** This misconception undermines the entire module — if students think they can just multiply the longest sides, they see no need for decomposition. The error is also self-reinforcing: the bounding rectangle answer is always LARGER than the correct answer but still plausible, so students who don't verify don't catch it.

**Prevention Strategy (M11-specific):**

* **Primary defense — full grid:** The grid makes the "missing corner" visually undeniable. Students can see that those squares aren't there.
* **Proactive demonstration (Early, after 3rd problem):** System shows the bounding rectangle as a dotted outline, highlights the empty space, and explicitly states: "The bounding rectangle is too big — it includes squares that don't belong to our shape." This costs ~30 seconds and prevents the most common error. All students receive it.
* **EC problem 3:** MC options include the bounding-rectangle error (48 for a 6×8 bounding box when correct is 38). Tests whether the misconception prevention held.
* **"By itself" foreshadowing:** Guide says "Just multiplying the longest sides doesn't work BY ITSELF." This is deliberate — in M12 Late, students learn that the bounding rectangle IS useful if you subtract the missing piece. M11 establishes the naive approach is wrong; M12 completes the idea.

**Where It Surfaces:** EC problem 3 (targeted), Late independent work (may emerge when students face T/U-shapes with more complex bounding boxes).

### 1.4.2 #6: Overlapping Decomposition (SECONDARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** When decomposing, student draws a line that creates overlapping regions and counts some squares twice. For example, if a student draws two lines through an L-shape that cross or overlap, squares in the overlap zone would be counted in both rectangles, inflating the total.

**Why It's a Barrier:** Overlapping decomposition violates the "non-overlapping" requirement of 3.MD.C.7.d. If students don't understand that each square belongs to exactly one rectangle, the additive property doesn't hold — the "parts" no longer add up to the "whole."

**Prevention Strategy (M11-specific):**

* **Primary defense — system validation:** The Composite Figures tool validates decomposition lines. Invalid lines (diagonal, same-edge, non-edge, creating overlap) receive specific feedback explaining why. The tool structurally prevents overlap in practice.
* **Conceptual address (Early):** Even though the tool prevents overlap, the Guide addresses the concept: "Each square can only be in ONE rectangle. If a square is in BOTH rectangles, you'd count it twice." This builds conceptual understanding for M12–M14 where the tool validation may not be available.
* **Color-coding reinforcement:** After decomposition, each rectangle highlights in a distinct color. Visually, every square is one color only — the absence of color mixing reinforces non-overlapping partitioning.

**Where It Surfaces:** Mid (when students first draw their own lines — the most likely moment for invalid attempts), though the tool validation catches it mechanically.

**New Capabilities Required (flagged for engineering)**

If the grid is discontinuous, students may perceive the rectangles as separate shapes rather than parts of one shape. This undermines the core concept.

### Data Constraints

**Early (system-decomposed):**

* 2-component L-shapes with clean dimensions
* Side lengths: 1–10 each
* Component rectangle areas: ≤ 50 each, and must be DIFFERENT (avoid two identical rectangles)
* Total area: ≤ 40 (grid counting should be feasible in ~30 sec per problem — students may count to verify their multiplication)
* Total area should be easily verifiable by counting the grid
* TVP examples: L-shape → 4×3 + 5×2 = 22; L-shape → 3×6 + 2×4 = 26

**Mid (student-decomposed):**

* 2–3 component L-shapes and simple rectilinear figures
* At least 2 valid decomposition paths per figure
* Both decompositions should produce friendly multiplication (avoid decompositions creating 1×n strips)
* 3-component figures (T-shapes, U-shapes): one cut creates 2 pieces, a second cut creates 3
* Total area: ≤ 40

**Late (independent):**

* L-shapes, T-shapes, U-shapes with 2–3 components
* 3-component figures limited to 1–2 problems
* T-shapes and U-shapes introduced HERE, not earlier
* Total area: ≤ 50 (students trust multiplication by Late; grid-count verification not expected — the higher ceiling reflects this)
* Component areas require genuine multiplication (not 1×n)

**Equation Builder values:**

* Products within single-digit multiplication (≤ 81)
* Sums ≤ 100
* TVP examples: (4 × 5) + (3 × 6) = 20 + 18 = 38; (2 × 7) + (3 × 4) + (2 × 3) = 14 + 12 + 6 = 32

**EC values (from TVP):**

* EC.1 (guided decomposition): L-shape, system-placed line, 2 rectangles color-coded with dimensions. Student calculates each area (MC), adds (MC).
* EC.2 (student decomposition): L-shape, no line provided. Student draws decomposition line, calculates total.
* EC.3 (misconception check): L-shape, bounding box 6 × 8 = 48. Correct area = 38. MC options: 48 (bounding error), 38 (correct), 28 (partial calculation), 34 (addition/dimension error).

**Avoid in M11:**

* Missing dimensions (no "?" sides) — M13
* Subtraction decomposition (bounding minus missing) — M12 Late
* Ungridded figures — all M11 figures have full grids
* Figures with more than 3 components — M13–M14
* Real-world contexts — M12
* 1×n component rectangles in Late (not genuine multiplication)

### Core Purpose

**One-line purpose:** Activate multiplication-area fluency and surface the decomposition need through an L-shape that resists single-rectangle strategies.

**Key Function:** The Warmup does two things in sequence: (1) confirms that students can still find the area of a single rectangle instantly (2 rapid forward problems — if any student hesitates, M11 can adapt pacing), and (2) presents an L-shape that can't be solved by multiplying two sides, creating the conceptual need for a new strategy. The system's decomposition demonstration at the end shows THAT the strategy works without explaining HOW — the Lesson picks up the "how."

**Why this serves the concept:** M11's core challenge is that students arrive with a well-rehearsed "multiply two sides" approach that works for rectangles but fails for composite figures. The Warmup must activate that fluency (so it's available for use inside component rectangles) AND reveal its limitation (it doesn't work for the whole L-shape). The counting task (W.3) is the pivot: counting works but is slow and error-prone for larger figures. The system demonstration (W.4) shows a faster, structured alternative — this is the curiosity hook that drives students into the Lesson wanting to learn the workflow.

**Test:** "If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson?" **YES.** Without the forward problems, the Lesson would need to verify multiplication fluency before introducing decomposition, adding time and losing focus. Without the L-shape counting → demonstration sequence, the Lesson would need to motivate WHY decomposition is useful before teaching it. The Warmup handles both prerequisites so the Lesson can begin with the first system-decomposed problem immediately.

### Anti-Patterns for Practice Problem Writers

* Do NOT include ungridded figures — all M11 figures have full grids

### Opening Frame

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from Practice to reflection/connection.
* **Visual:** Composite Figures — display-only L-shape, T-shape, and U-shape side by side (no grids, just silhouettes — these are reflection props, not problems to solve).
* **Guide:** "You've been decomposing all kinds of shapes. L-shapes, T-shapes, U-shapes. Different shapes, but the same idea every time. Let's see what you noticed."
* **No student action.**

*Design Note: Opening uses figure silhouettes as visual anchors for the reflection. "Same idea every time" frames the synthesis around the invariant strategy (decompose-calculate-add) rather than the varying figure types. Energy: Medium (reflective, discovering together).*

### Interaction SY.1: "Different Shapes, Same Strategy" — Figure Comparison [TYPE A: PATTERN DISCOVERY]

* **Purpose:** Surface the invariant strategy across figure types. Student decomposes a novel figure to prove the strategy transfers.
* **Visual:** Composite Figures — three panels displayed:
  * Panel 1: L-shape (from Early) with decomposition line and expression: (4 × 3) + (5 × 2) = 22
  * Panel 2: T-shape (from Late) with decomposition and expression
  * Panel 3: New L-shape, full grid, NO decomposition line — student's problem
* **Guide:** "Look at the first two. Both different shapes, both decomposed into rectangles, both add up to a total. What about this new one?"
* **Prompt:** "Decompose this figure and find the total area."
* **Student Action:** Decomposition line drawing → MC for total area
* **Correct Answer:** Any valid decomposition (horizontal or vertical, edge-to-edge, creates complete rectangles) + correct total area via MC.
* **On Correct:** "Same strategy. Break it into rectangles, find each area, add. The parentheses show each rectangle's area before you add: (__ × __) + (__ × __) = total."
* **Connection:** The decompose-calculate-add strategy is not shape-specific. It works for any rectilinear figure because area is additive.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

*Design Note: Type A (Pattern Discovery) — the "pattern" is the invariance of the strategy across figure types. The student's active decomposition (Panel 3) proves the strategy transfers to a figure they haven't seen before. This is synthesis, not practice: the panels create a comparison context that reveals the structural pattern. The figure in Panel 3 uses values not appearing in Lesson or EC.*

---

### Interaction SY.2: Vocabulary Consolidation + Key Insight [TYPE B: PROGRESSIVE CHALLENGE]

* **Purpose:** Consolidate vocabulary and the additive property insight through a progressive sequence.
* **Visual:** Composite Figures — L-shape from SY.1 still displayed with color-coded rectangles and Equation Builder expression.
* **Guide:** "You've learned some important words for what you've been doing."
* **Prompt:** "Three quick vocabulary checks. See below."
* **Student Action:** MC selection (3 steps)
  * **Step 1:** "What's it called when you break a shape into parts? A) Decompose, B) Multiply, C) Count"
  * **Step 2:** "A shape made of multiple rectangles is called a... A) Composite figure, B) Big rectangle, C) Square"
  * **Step 3:** "Why does adding the parts always give you the total area? A) Because area is additive — the parts add up to the whole, B) Because all the rectangles are the same size, C) Because you always get the same number"
* **Correct Answer:** Step 1: A) Decompose. Step 2: A) Composite figure. Step 3: A) Because area is additive — the parts add up to the whole.
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * Step 1: "Decompose" = correct label for breaking into parts. "Multiply" and "Count" are actions within the workflow, not the strategy name.
  * Step 2: "Composite figure" = correct term for shapes made of multiple rectangles. "Big rectangle" is the misconception (completing the rectangle). "Square" is a specific shape type.
  * Step 3: Additive property = correct conceptual explanation. "Same size" confuses additive property with equal partitioning. "Same number" is a tautology that doesn't explain the mechanism.
* **On Correct:** Step 1: "Decompose. Break it into parts." Step 2: "Composite figure. Made of multiple parts." Step 3: "Area is additive. Every square belongs to exactly one rectangle."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

*Design Note: Type B (Progressive Challenge) — moves from vocabulary recall (decompose, composite figure) to conceptual understanding (WHY the additive property works). Step 3 is the conceptual check that the Lesson's procedural reps set up but didn't test directly. This addresses the Gate 2 Finding 2 gap (conceptual understanding of WHY area is additive only gets one teaching moment in the Lesson). The MC format constrains depth, but the three distractors target specific misunderstandings: "same size" confuses additive property with equal partitioning; "same number" is a tautology that doesn't explain the mechanism.*

### Interaction SY.3: Metacognitive Reflection — Key Takeaway [TYPE 4: KEY TAKEAWAY]

* **Purpose:** Surface what the student considers most important from M11.
* **Visual:** Composite Figures — display-only composite figure (neutral, no decomposition).
* **Guide:** "If you had to remember ONE thing about finding the area of shapes like these, what would it be?"
* **Prompt:** "What's the most important thing you learned? A) Break the shape into rectangles, B) The parts add up to the whole, C) There's more than one way to break a shape apart, D) Something else I noticed"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** A) Break the shape into rectangles, B) The parts add up to the whole, C) There's more than one way to break a shape apart, D) Something else I noticed
* **Correct Answer:** All options accepted (metacognitive reflection — no wrong answers)
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A = Valid (decomposition as strategy — the procedural insight)
  * B = Valid (additive property — the conceptual insight)
  * C = Valid (multiple valid decompositions — the flexibility insight)
  * D = Valid (student-directed observation — respects unexpected takeaways)
* **On Correct:** A: "That's the key move. Find the rectangles inside any shape." B: "That's the big idea. Area is additive." C: "You proved it. Different rectangles, same total." D: "Good. Noticing things on your own is what mathematicians do."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

*Design Note: Type 4 (Key Takeaway) per Synthesis Playbook §3E. The four options map to M11's three core insights: (A) decomposition as strategy, (B) additive property as principle, (C) multiple valid decompositions as flexibility. Option D respects unexpected observations. All Guide responses are one sentence per Playbook guidance.*

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS (KDD)

[REQUIRED]

### KDD-1: Activation + Discovery hybrid warmup type instead of Number Talk or Visual Pattern The Warmup Playbook §7 recommends Number Talk or Visual Pattern for M8–12. M11 uses a custom Activation + Discovery hybrid because the CRA reset and new-concept introduction require confirming prior skill (single-rectangle area) AND revealing its limitation (doesn't work for L-shapes). Number Talk assumes the concept is established; Visual Pattern assumes students can generate patterns from what they see. M11 needs both activation AND the surprise of encountering a non-rectangular shape for the first time.

### KDD-2: CRA resets to Concrete/Representational with full grids throughout — no Abstract phase M11 returns to full grids after M7's grid fading and M8–M10's abstract work because composite figures are a NEW concept requiring concrete support (Decision #3). The CRA progression resets for the new concept, not because students regressed. There is no Abstract phase in M11 because there is no abstraction away from the grid — M12 handles grid removal. The "Abstract equivalent" is the parenthesized expression notation introduced through the Equation Builder display.

### KDD-3: 1.1 uses the same L-shape from W.4 as an intentional I-do-then-you-do continuation The student already knows the answer (22) from watching the Warmup demonstration. This is a feature: the think-aloud in 1.1 models the PROCESS (planning, attention, self-check), not the answer. The student's cognitive load is on the strategic frame, not the arithmetic. Using a fresh figure would split attention between learning the workflow and computing new values simultaneously.

### KDD-4: 1.2 collapses to a single MC for total area as genuine fading from 1.1's three-step MC The critical fading dimension is what the student does, not what the teacher says (Renkl, 2002). In 1.1, the student answers three separate MCs (blue area, pink area, total). In 1.2, the student receives both dimensions and must mentally compute both products and add — one MC for total. This is real fading: the student's task increased while the Guide's narration decreased.

### KDD-5: Third system-decomposed problem (1.4) placed after additive property naming, before misconception check The TVP specifies "after 3rd problem" for the completing-the-rectangle misconception check. The third problem is pedagogically load-bearing: after the additive property is named in 1.3, students need one more rep applying the named concept before the bounding-rectangle test. Without this rep, the misconception check tests an emerging skill rather than an established one.

### KDD-6: Late section sequences independence before figure-type novelty 3.1 uses a familiar L-shape at Late complexity (larger values, Equation Builder not pre-loaded) to establish the independence expectation. Only 3.2 introduces T-shapes with rectilinear vocabulary. This separates two cognitive variables: scaffold removal (independence) and figure-type novelty (T/U-shapes). The 1.6 corner ID beat previews T-shapes, but separating the variables in Late is cleaner cognitive sequencing per Sweller's cognitive load research.

### KDD-7: Efficiency observation dropped from Late to allocate the interaction to the 3rd Early problem The TVP's efficiency observation ("Could you have done it with just 2 rectangles?") was conditional (fires only if student uses 3 when 2 suffice), not an assessment target, and light-touch by design. The 3rd system-decomposed problem in Early is unconditionally load-bearing for TVP alignment. With a 13-interaction count and 10–14 min timing, the trade favors the Early practice rep.

### KDD-8: EC uses L-shapes only, not T/U-shapes T-shapes and U-shapes were introduced in Late (3.2–3.3). The EC Playbook requires "representative middle" difficulty — not edge cases, not trivial. L-shapes represent the core of the module (Early through Late); T/U-shapes represent Late extension. Testing T/U in EC would assess a Late-introduced skill at the same weight as the core workflow, which overstates its importance in the module's learning arc.

### KDD-9: EC.3 misconception values from TVP (bounding box 6×8=48, correct area 38) The TVP specifies these exact values for the misconception check. The 48 distractor is the completing-the-rectangle error (6×8); the correct answer (38) requires decomposition. EC.3 does not require the student to draw a decomposition line — it's an IDENTIFY problem testing whether the student resists the bounding-rectangle trap. This is a different cognitive demand than EC.2 (which tests the full CREATE workflow).

### KDD-10: Synthesis addresses the conceptual check gap from the Lesson The Lesson's "Biggest Risk" flag (§1.0) warns that students may learn the decompose-calculate-add PROCEDURE without understanding WHY it works. The Lesson has more procedure reps than conceptual checks — the additive property is stated in 1.3 and reinforced through 2.2/2.4 comparisons, but never directly tested. SY.2 Step 3 ("Why does adding the parts always give you the total area?") is the first direct test of the conceptual principle, placed in Synthesis where mastery is assumed and the student can reflect on the WHY rather than executing the HOW.

### KDD-11: M12 bridge uses ungridded figure teaser without solving The Synthesis closure displays an L-shaped figure without a grid — just the outline and dimension labels. This previews M12's central challenge (grid fading) without teaching or explaining it. "You already know the strategy" affirms that decompose-calculate-add transfers; "you just won't be able to count squares to check" names exactly what changes. The teaser creates anticipation without front-loading M12's content.

### KDD-12: "Composite figure" and "decompose" are active vocabulary; "rectilinear" is receptive only "Composite figure" and "decompose" are introduced in Early after concrete experience (vocabulary-after-grounding), used actively by the Guide and in prompts through Mid and Late, and consolidated in Synthesis. "Rectilinear" is introduced receptively in Late (3.2) when T/U-shapes appear — the Guide uses it naturally, students hear it, but it's not assessed. This three-tier staging (established vocabulary → new active vocabulary → new receptive vocabulary) matches the Lesson Playbook's vocabulary progression guidelines.

### KDD-13: "By itself" foreshadowing in the bounding-rectangle misconception check (1.5) "Just multiplying the longest sides doesn't work BY ITSELF" is deliberate M12 foreshadowing. In M12 Late, students learn that the bounding rectangle IS useful if you subtract the missing piece (subtraction decomposition). M11 establishes the naive approach is wrong; M12 completes the idea. The "by itself" qualifier is the pedagogical hinge — it's honest (the approach isn't completely wrong) without teaching the M12 strategy prematurely.
