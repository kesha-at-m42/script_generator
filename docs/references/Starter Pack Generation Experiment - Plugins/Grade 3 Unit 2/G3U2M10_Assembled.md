# MODULE 10: Area and the Multiplication Table

# BACKBONE

---

## 1.0 THE ONE THING

[REQUIRED]

The multiplication table is a map of every rectangle with whole-number dimensions — every cell IS an area, the row number and column number ARE the dimensions, and the product at the corner of a shaded region IS the area of that rectangle. Students don't learn new math here; they learn to SEE everything they've built since M4 organized in one place. The rectangle region marking interaction makes this spatial and physical: shading from cell (1,1) to (4,6) creates a visible 4-by-6 rectangle inside the table, and the corner product (24) is the area. This is a synthesis module — it connects the bidirectional area-multiplication relationship (forward from M4–M7, reverse from M8, applied from M9) into a single visual representation.

**CRA Stage:** Abstract — the multiplication table is a purely symbolic representation, the endpoint of the unit's concreteness fading sequence (Decision #3: Full grid → Partial grid → Tick marks → Dimensions only → Multiplication table). The multiplication table extends Decision #3's original fading sequence beyond its stated endpoint of "measurement with ruler" and abstract problem-solving — per TVP, the table is a unique abstract representation that serves as the final stop for the single-rectangle area CRA progression before M11 resets to concrete for composite figures. The rectangle region marking CONNECTS it back to the concrete/representational work students did with grids in M1–M6. A shaded 4×6 region in the table visually resembles the 4×6 grid rectangle from M3. That resemblance is the pedagogical bridge.

**Critical Misconception:** #2 — Counting One-by-One. Students who haven't internalized multiplicative structure may try to count cells in the shaded table region rather than reading the corner product directly. The table itself is the antidote — it makes organized structure undeniable — but students who still count cells need M3 reinforcement.

**Success Indicator:** Given a rectangle with labeled dimensions, the student can predict the area, mark the correct region in the multiplication table, and confirm the corner product matches their prediction — demonstrating that they understand the table AS an area representation, not just a fact-lookup tool.

**Biggest Risk:** Students treat the multiplication table as a memorization aid rather than a meaningful representation of area relationships. If the connection between the shaded table region and the physical rectangle isn't made explicit through the Connection Animation and co-display, students will interact with the table mechanically without the conceptual "aha" that makes M10 pedagogically valuable.

## 1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES

[REQUIRED]

### ✅ Must Teach

* **Rectangle region marking on the multiplication table:** Students shade rectangular regions from (1,1) to (R,C) and read the corner product as the area. This is the core interaction — it makes the "table = collection of rectangles" insight spatial and physical.
* **Bidirectional table-area connection:** Forward (given dimensions → mark region → read area) AND reverse (given product location → identify dimensions → visualize rectangle). Both directions must be explicitly practiced.
* **Factor-pair reasoning within the table:** "Where is 24 in the table?" leads to multiple locations, each representing a different rectangle with area 24. Extends M8's factor-pair discovery by giving it a visual home.
* **Commutative pair distinction:** 3×8 and 8×3 are "the same rectangle turned sideways — same area, same shape." 3×8 and 4×6 are "DIFFERENT rectangles, same area — different shapes." The table inherently shows both orientations; the guide makes the distinction explicit.
* **Pattern discovery (light-touch):** Row patterns (multiples), square numbers on diagonal, using known products to find unknown products. Conceptual observation, not formal instruction.
* **Vocabulary:** "multiplication table" (new). Reinforce: factor, product, area, dimensions, multiply, row, column.
* **Module Mapping vocabulary teaching note:** "The multiplication table is like a collection of all the rectangles we could make!" — this framing must appear in guide dialogue.

### ❌ Must Not Include

* **Perimeter** — Per Decision #5. Not mentioned, even for contrast.
* **Division language** — Factor-pair reasoning uses multiplication framing only. Division not introduced in Unit 2.
* **Composite figures** — Shapes made of multiple rectangles begin at M11. M10 works with single rectangles only.
* **12×12 table** — M10 uses a 10×10 table (factors 1–10).
* **Equation Builder** — Not used in M10. The table IS the representation. No equation construction.
* **1×n facts as primary teaching examples** — They're trivially on the table edge and don't demonstrate the spatial insight of rectangle region marking. May appear in pattern observations or factor-pair completeness, but not as core examples.
* **Formal square number vocabulary** — The diagonal observation ("both sides the same — it's a square") is light-touch. "Square number" is not a formal vocabulary target.
* **New unit types or unit-specific scaffolding** — M10 works with abstract products, not specific square units. Unit types established M5–M7 are not the focus.
* **Memorization drills** — The table supports fact-finding; it is not a memorization tool in this module. The conceptual connection (table = area representation) is the goal.

⚠️ **SYNTHESIS MODULE IDENTITY:** M10 does not teach a new mathematical skill. It teaches students to SEE their existing skills inside a new representation. The multiplication table IS a collection of rectangle areas — every cell is a product, every product IS an area, and the row/column coordinates ARE the dimensions. Students have been building this understanding piece by piece since M4. M10 makes the connection explicit and complete.

## 1.3 VOCABULARY ARCHITECTURE

[REQUIRED]

M10 introduces one new term — "multiplication table" — and reinforces the area-multiplication vocabulary students have built across M1–M9. The instructional focus is not vocabulary acquisition but vocabulary CONNECTION: students see familiar terms (factor, product, area, dimensions) operating inside a new representation. The table makes the relationship between these terms visible and spatial.

**Assessment Vocabulary (appears on state tests and Exit Check):** area, dimensions, multiply, factor, product, multiplication table, row, column

**Note on vocabulary staging:** "Multiplication table" is the only new term. It's introduced through demonstration (Warmup), not definition-first. Students see what the table DOES (organize rectangle areas) before they hear what it's called. All other terms are used naturally — students arrive at M10 fluent with area vocabulary from nine prior modules.

### Vocabulary Staging by Phase

| Phase | Terms | Introduction Approach |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Warm-Up** | multiplication table, area, dimensions, row, column | "Multiplication table" introduced via demonstration — Guide names the object as students see it. "You know this rectangle is 3 by 5, and its area is 15. Watch what happens in the table." The term is grounded in the visual before it becomes a label. |
| **Lesson Section 1 (Early)** | multiplication table, area, dimensions, row, column, product | "Row number = one dimension, column number = other dimension, product = area." Guide explicitly maps table vocabulary to area vocabulary. Key framing: "The multiplication table is like a collection of all the rectangles you could make." |
| **Lesson Section 2 (Mid)** | factor pair (reinforced from M8), area, product, dimensions | Factor-pair language returns naturally: "24 shows up more than once. Each location is a different rectangle with area 24." Students connect M8's factor-pair reasoning to table positions. |
| **Lesson Section 3 (Late)** | pattern, multiples, square (descriptive only) | Pattern vocabulary used naturally. "These are special — look at their row and column numbers. They match! A rectangle where both sides are the same is a SQUARE." Light-touch — square number is not a formal target. |
| **EC** | All assessment vocabulary | Terms appear in problem stems. No new phrasing beyond Lesson. |
| **Synthesis** | multiplication table, area, dimensions, factor, product | "Area atlas" framing: "Every rectangle, every area — organized right here in the multiplication table." Vocabulary consolidation through the unit arc summary. |

### Vocabulary Notes

**"Multiplication table" (new):** Introduced through demonstration, not definition. The Guide shows the table, demonstrates rectangle region marking, and THEN names it. The conceptual anchor: "The multiplication table is like a collection of all the rectangles we could make!" This framing should appear early and be reinforced.

**"Row" and "column" (reinforced):** Not new to this unit — students have encountered rows and columns since M3 (row-by-column structure). In M10, these terms acquire a new meaning layer: row number = one dimension, column number = other dimension. Guide makes the mapping explicit.

**"Factor pair" (reinforced from M8):** Returns in Mid activities when students find multiple table locations for the same product. Not re-taught — used naturally. "Remember finding multiple rectangles for one area back when we worked with factor pairs? The table shows you exactly where they all live."

**Language pattern for M10:** "Mark the rectangle: ___ rows, ___ columns. The corner product is ___. That's the area of a ___ by ___ rectangle."

### Terms to Avoid (Save for Later Modules)

* **"Formula"** (standalone) — Suggests rote memorization. Use: "Multiply the dimensions to find area." Same constraint as M7–M9.
* **"Perimeter"** — Do not use, even for contrast. Per Decision #5.
* **"Decompose"** — Not applicable to M10. Composite figures and decomposition begin at M11.
* **"Divide" / "Division"** — Factor-pair reasoning uses multiplication framing. Division is not introduced in Unit 2.
* **"Square number"** (as formal vocabulary) — The diagonal observation uses descriptive language ("both sides the same — it's a square"), not formal "square number" terminology. Save for later math.
* **"Memorize"** — The table is a representation, not a memorization tool. Avoid framing that reduces the table to fact storage.

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

[REQUIRED]

### 1.4.1 #2: Counting One-by-One (PRIMARY)

**Trigger Behavior:** With a shaded 4×6 region in the multiplication table, student counts individual cells (1, 2, 3... 24) instead of reading the corner product directly. May manifest as extended response time on problems where the product is already visible, or off-by-one errors (23, 25) that suggest counting rather than reading.

**Why It Happens:** Students who haven't internalized multiplicative structure from M3–M4 default to their most reliable strategy: counting every square. The multiplication table's grid-like appearance may actually trigger this — the cells look like unit squares, and the student's prior experience with unit squares involved counting them.

**Visual Cue:** The rectangle region marking and Connection Animation address this directly. The shaded region IS a rectangle — students see the shape. The emphasized corner product IS the area — students see the number. The Connection Animation links the table region to the Grid Rectangle, reinforcing that both represent the same multiplication fact.

**Prevention Strategy:** The prediction-first pattern in Early activities forces multiplicative reasoning BEFORE the table interaction. Student calculates area via MC (e.g., 4×7 = 28) and THEN marks the region to confirm. If the student already has 28 in mind, they read the corner product rather than counting cells. The table serves as confirmation, not primary computation.

**Detection in digital context:** Direct observation of counting is not possible. Practical proxies: (1) Extended response time when the corner product is already visible; (2) Off-by-one errors (23 or 25 when the product is 24); (3) Explicit check interaction: "The product in the corner is 24. What is the area?" — if the student answers anything other than 24, the core M10 insight is missing.

### 1.4.2 Table as Memorization Tool Only (SECONDARY, M10-specific)

**Trigger Behavior:** Student interacts with the multiplication table mechanically — looking up products correctly but never connecting the table positions to rectangle dimensions or areas. If asked "What rectangle does this cell represent?" student cannot answer, even though they can read the product.

**Why It Happens:** Many students have prior experience with multiplication tables as fact-lookup tools in other contexts. They bring an existing mental model: "the table tells me what 4 times 6 equals." The AREA connection — that 4×6=24 means "a 4-by-6 rectangle has area 24" — is the new insight M10 must build.

**Visual Cue:** The Connection Animation is the primary visual antidote. It shows that the shaded table region LOOKS like a miniature version of the Grid Rectangle. Students literally see rectangles inside the table, not just isolated facts.

**Prevention Strategy:** Every Early activity pairs the table interaction with a co-displayed Grid Rectangle. The student sees both representations simultaneously. The Guide's language consistently maps between them: "Row 4, column 6 — just like the rectangle beside it. And the corner number? 24. That's the area." The rectangle region marking (shading the whole region, not just highlighting a cell) makes the shape connection physical.

### 1.4.3 Note on #3: Confusing Area and Perimeter

Misconception #3 (Area-Perimeter Confusion) has M10 within its surface range per the Misconceptions sheet (M5–M10). Priority is LOW (only if student-introduced) and M10 does not introduce perimeter per Decision #5. No dedicated M10 treatment required. If a student spontaneously references perimeter, address through the pipeline — do not proactively contrast area and perimeter in any authored content.

**Data Constraints**

**Early (rectangle → table):**

* Factors: 2–9 each, products ≤100
* Avoid 1×n facts — trivially on edge, don't demonstrate spatial insight
* Choose dimensions that land in different table regions (not all clustered in one area)
* Both orientations accepted for region marking
* TVP examples: 2×4=8, 3×7=21, 5×6=30, 4×9=36, 6×8=48

**Mid (table → rectangle):**

* Products with at least 2 unique factor pairs within 1–10 range
* Good targets: 12 (2×6, 3×4), 18 (2×9, 3×6), 24 (3×8, 4×6), 36 (4×9, 6×6), 20 (2×10, 4×5)
* Avoid prime products or products with only one factor pair in range
* MC options for dimensions: unique pairs only — do NOT include commutative mirrors

**Commutative pairs in the table:** Unlike M8's "unique factor pairs only" convention, M10 shows all table positions because the table inherently displays both orientations (row 3/col 4 AND row 4/col 3 are separate cells). The Guide distinguishes:

* 3×8 and 8×3 → "same rectangle turned sideways — same area, same shape"
* 3×8 and 4×6 → "DIFFERENT rectangles, same area — different shapes"

**Pattern problems:** Focus on rows 2, 5, 10 (skip-counting families), the diagonal (square numbers: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100), and products that appear multiple times. Avoid patterns requiring knowledge beyond Grade 3.

**EC values (from TVP):**

* EC.1: 7×4=28 (mark region, read corner product)
* EC.2: Product 40 at row 5, col 8 → dimensions "5 by 8" (row/column anchored)
* EC.3: Area 18 at row 2, col 9 → find different rectangle "3 by 6" (factor-pair reasoning)

**EC distractor design:**

* EC.2 distractors: "4 by 10" (different factor pair for 40 — student finds valid dimensions but from a different table position, not the specified cell), "5 by 10" (row correct, column wrong — student reads row 5 correctly but misreads or miscounts the column), "8 by 4" (column-first reading error — student grabs column 8 as first dimension, then selects wrong second dimension 4 instead of 5)
* EC.3 distractors: "2 by 8" (grabs factor 2, product 16≠18), "3 by 9" (grabs column 9, product 27≠18), "4 by 5" (nearby product 20≠18)

**Assessment ratio:** 1 forward (mark rectangle) : 1 reverse (read table) : 1 synthesis (factor pairs). This module is about the bidirectional connection and pattern recognition, not procedural calculation. All three problem types test whether students understand the table AS an area representation.

### Core Purpose

**One-line purpose:** Activate the "rectangles are hiding in the multiplication table" insight through guided observation.

**Key Function:** The Warmup demonstrates the rectangle region marking interaction and the core visual connection (table region ↔ Grid Rectangle) so that students enter the Lesson knowing WHAT they'll be doing — but not yet having done it themselves. ### Misconception Prevention

**Misconception #2: Counting One-by-One (PRIMARY)**

* **Prevention embedded in:** S1.1–S1.5 (Early) — the prediction-first pattern. Students calculate area via MC BEFORE touching the table. Having the answer (e.g., 28) in mind before marking the region means they READ the corner product as confirmation, rather than counting cells.
* **Specific interactions:** S1.1 (worked example models prediction → mark → confirm cycle explicitly). S1.2–S1.5 (student repeats the cycle — MC prediction forces multiplicative reasoning first).
* **Detection:** If a student selects the wrong MC answer for a simple product (e.g., chooses 27 for 4×7), the remediation pipeline addresses the multiplication fact gap — but this is M3-level, not M10's responsibility. M10's structure prevents counting by making it unnecessary.
* **Key beat:** S1.3 — "Every time you marked a rectangle, the corner number was the area." This explicitly names the read-the-product strategy.

**Misconception: Table as Memorization Tool (SECONDARY)**

* **Prevention embedded in:** S1 (Early) — the Connection Animation. Every forward problem co-displays the Grid Rectangle alongside the table region. Students literally SEE rectangles inside the table, not just isolated products.
* **Specific interactions:** W.1–W.2 (Warmup demos establish the visual link), S1.1–S1.5 (Connection Animation fires on every correct marking), S1.3 Key Beat ("The multiplication table is like a collection of ALL the rectangles you could make!").
* **Key beat:** S2.3 — Multiple rectangles for the same area. This is the moment where the table stops feeling like "just a chart" — the same number appearing in multiple places, each representing a DIFFERENT rectangle, is inherently meaningful and structural, not rote.

### Module-Specific Lesson Guidance

**Synthesis Module Identity:** M10 does not teach new mathematics. The Lesson's job is to make the table-area CONNECTION as visible, physical, and undeniable as possible through the rectangle region marking interaction and Connection Animation. Script writers should resist the temptation to add procedural teaching (e.g., "how to read a multiplication table" or "how to multiply") — students arrive with these skills from M1–M9. The Lesson teaches SEEING, not DOING.

**Co-display Priority (AF-2 resolved):** The simultaneous display of Grid Rectangle + Multiplication Table + Guide + MC is confirmed feasible by UX mock. This co-display is not optional decoration — it IS the pedagogical mechanism. If any implementation constraint forces sequential display, the Connection Animation loses its impact. Flag for engineering review.

**State Persistence:** Cell reveals from S1 persist through S2 and S3. This accumulation is intentional — watching the table fill up as they mark regions is part of the learning experience. It creates a sense of progress ("I'm mapping the whole table") and makes Mid's reverse problems easier (more visible products to reference). Table resets between Warmup → Lesson and between Lesson → EC.

**Interaction SY.2: "Same Area, Different Shapes" — Pattern Discovery [TYPE A: PATTERN DISCOVERY]**

* **Type:** A (Pattern Discovery — per Synthesis Playbook §3B)
* **Purpose role:** Consolidation of factor-pair insight across multiple products
* **Purpose:** Student actively discovers that multiple products share the "appears in more than one place" property, then articulates why. Two-phase: active cell discovery + conceptual generalization.

* **Visual:** Multiplication Tables Grid (fully revealed). System highlights product 24 at all four locations: (3,8), (4,6), (6,4), (8,3). The four cells pulse briefly.
* **Guide:** "You discovered that 24 shows up in four places. That means there are four rectangles with area 24. But 24 isn't the only number that does this. Can you find another number that shows up more than once?"
* **Prompt:** "Tap a number in the table that appears in more than one spot."
* **Student Action (Phase 1):** Targeted cell click — student taps any cell whose product appears at multiple table locations. System validates by firing highlight-by-value on the tapped product, revealing all its locations. Valid targets include: 12, 16, 18, 20, 24, 30, 36, 40, and many others. Most taps will succeed (most non-prime products appear multiple times in a 10×10 table).
* **On Correct Click:** System highlights all cells containing the student's selected product. Guide response adapts to the product chosen:
  * General pattern: "[Product] shows up in [N] places. That's [N] different rectangles, all with area [product]."
  * Example if student taps 36: "36 shows up in three places. That's three different rectangles, all with area 36."
* **Guide (Phase 2):** "You found that [product] appears in multiple spots, just like 24 did. Here's the big pattern."
* **Prompt (Phase 2):** "Why do some numbers appear in more than one spot in the table? A) Each one is the area of more than one rectangle, B) They are the biggest numbers in the table, C) They are all even numbers"
* **Student Action (Phase 2):** MC selection
  * **Options:** A) Each one is the area of more than one rectangle, B) They are the biggest numbers in the table, C) They are all even numbers
* **Correct Answer:** A) Each one is the area of more than one rectangle
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A = Correct (multiple factor pairs → multiple table locations → multiple rectangles with same area)
  * B = Misconception: confusing frequency of appearance with magnitude (students may assume "shows up a lot" = "big number," but 12 appears four times while 97 wouldn't appear at all in a 10×10 table)
  * C = Surface-level pattern (many repeated products are even, but odd products like 9 also repeat at (1,9), (3,3), (9,1); evenness is incidental, not causal)
* **On Correct:** "Different rectangles, same area. The table shows you all the ways to make each area."
* **Connection:** This generalizes the S2.3 factor-pair discovery — it's not just product 24 that has multiple locations. The student's own discovery (Phase 1) proves this is a system-wide pattern. The table is a map of ALL the rectangles.
* **Remediation:** Pipeline. Phase 1 edge case: if tap hits a product that appears only once — this is rare and limited to products whose only factor pair in the 1–10 range is (n,n): specifically 49 (only at 7,7), 81 (only at 9,9), or 100 (only at 10,10) — system says "That one only appears once. Try another number — look for one you see in more than one place." Every non-diagonal product appears at minimum at (r,c) and (c,r), so most taps will succeed.

> **Design Note:** The two-phase structure makes this a genuine discovery task, not just an observation. In Phase 1, the student ACTS — scanning the table, selecting a product, and seeing the highlight-by-value confirmation. This is the same interaction pattern as S2.3 in the Lesson (student finds → system confirms), now applied to any product the student chooses. Phase 2 asks the student to generalize from their own discovery plus the shown 24 example. The distractor rationales target specific misconceptions: B targets the frequency-magnitude confusion (common in children's number sense), C targets the surface-pattern trap (noticing a numerical property that correlates but doesn't cause).

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS (KDD)

[REQUIRED]

**KDD-1: Mystery Reveal warmup type for a synthesis module.** M10's core insight is counterintuitive — students don't expect to find rectangles inside a number table. Mystery Reveal (Warmup Playbook §4D Type 7) turns this surprise into the hook: the system reveals, the student watches, and the curiosity gap ("the table has rectangles hiding everywhere") drives engagement into the Lesson. Binary Choice and Number Talk were rejected because they require student judgment before the core concept is established.

**KDD-2: Outline-only Grid Rectangles in Lesson Early (no unit squares).** M10 is the Abstract CRA endpoint of the unit's progression. The Warmup uses full-grid Grid Rectangles (unit squares visible) to establish the visual resemblance between the grid rectangle and the table region. Once that connection is made, the Lesson uses outline + dimensions only. This prevents CRA regression — showing unit squares in the Lesson would pull students back toward Concrete counting when they should be reading the corner product abstractly. The outline provides a familiar shape reference without the visual detail students should be past by Module 10.

**KDD-3: Prediction-before-marking interaction pattern to prevent counting misconception.** Every Early forward problem asks students to predict the area via MC BEFORE marking the table region. This forces the student to have the answer (e.g., "28 for 4×7") in mind before seeing the table, which encourages reading the corner product rather than counting cells one by one. If students marked first and then read the product, the spatial array of shaded cells would invite counting. The prediction-first pattern is the primary defense against Misconception #2 (Counting One-by-One).

**KDD-4: Two-phase S2.3 factor-pair discovery (student finds → system confirms).** The restructured S2.3 has the student independently locate a second instance of product 24 before the system fires highlight-by-value showing all four locations. This preserves the "Wait, 24 is HERE too?" discovery moment that makes the table feel structural rather than mechanical. Showing all 24s upfront would turn a discovery into a mere observation and weaken the misconception prevention (Table as Memorization Tool).

**KDD-5: Product 24 used in both Warmup (4×6) and Mid (3×8, 4×6 factor pairs) intentionally.** The Warmup demonstrates 24 at position (4,6). In Mid, the student discovers that 24 also appears at (3,8), (8,3), and (6,4). The repetition is the pedagogical point — seeing the same product in the warmup's "magic trick" context reappear in the lesson's analytical context reinforces the connection. The student's reaction shifts from "Cool, 24 is in the table" to "Wait, 24 is in FOUR places in the table."

**KDD-6: Product 30 at cell (5,6) used in both S1.3 (forward) and S2.2 (reverse) as intentional bidirectional reinforcement.** The student marks the same cell going forward ("5×6 rectangle, what's the area?") and reads it backward ("product 30 at row 5, column 6 — what rectangle?"). This deliberate reuse demonstrates the bidirectional table-area connection with the same spatial location, making the "same rectangle, two directions" insight visceral rather than theoretical.

**KDD-7: Product 36 appears in three pedagogically distinct contexts across the module.** S1.4 (4×9 forward), S2.4 (4×9 reverse), and S3.3 (6×6 on the diagonal). The 4×9 pair provides directional reinforcement. The 6×6=36 appearance on the diagonal connects to square numbers — a genuinely different rectangle for the same area. This triple use demonstrates that the table reveals multiple layers of structure around a single product.

**KDD-8: Column patterns from TVP Late omitted in favor of diagonal + known→unknown.** The TVP's Late section includes column patterns with a commutativity connection. This was omitted because commutativity is adequately addressed in S2.3 (commutative pairs vs. genuinely different pairs), and a column-pattern interaction would repeat the same observation type as the row pattern (S3.1) with less novelty. The three selected Late interactions (row → diagonal → known→unknown) offer increasing abstraction rather than parallel observations.

**KDD-9: CRA labeled "Abstract with Representational bridge" throughout, not "Concrete."** M10 operates at the Abstract level — the multiplication table is a symbolic representation. The outline Grid Rectangle in Early provides a Representational bridge: it connects the new table representation to the familiar rectangle shapes from M1–M9 without regressing to Concrete (which would require unit squares and physical counting). This labeling aligns with M10's position as the CRA endpoint of the unit.

**KDD-10: EC uses exclusively MC + Region Marking — no Grid Rectangles, no Connection Animation.** EC assesses the table-area connection in its abstract form. Grid Rectangles and Connection Animation are scaffolds that bridge the gap between concrete and abstract — appropriate for Lesson (where the bridge is the teaching tool) but inappropriate for assessment (where the student should demonstrate they've internalized the connection). SUPPORT-tier Practice problems can restore Connection Animation for students who need it.

**KDD-11: Required Phrases assessment stem adapted from Module Mapping values to TVP values.** The Module Mapping stem "Lin knows 8 × 5 = 40. How can she use this to find 8 × 4?" was adapted in S3.3 to "You know 6 × 5 = 30. How can you find 6 × 6?" to match the TVP's specific Late activity design. Same reasoning pattern (add/subtract one group), different numbers. The TVP is downstream of the Module Mapping and provides the authoritative values for interaction design.

**KDD-12: "Area atlas" framing as the module's conceptual anchor.** The phrase "area atlas" (first appearing in §1.1 Module Goal, reprised in Synthesis closure) frames the multiplication table as a map of every rectangle. This is more than a metaphor — it operationalizes the module's pedagogical identity. An atlas organizes known territory; the multiplication table organizes known areas. The framing prevents reductive interpretations (table as memorization tool, table as calculator) by centering the structural insight: every product is an area, every position is a rectangle.

**KDD-13: Transfer gate satisfied by BASELINE CONNECT problems.** M10's core skill IS transfer — connecting the rectangle representation to the table representation. The forward region-marking task (BASELINE tier, CONNECT verb) is itself a transfer action, not procedural repetition. A student who passes BASELINE at 70% has demonstrated the representational connection that defines M10. The transfer gate does not require STRETCH-tier problems because the gate's purpose (confirming the student can transfer, not just execute) is inherently satisfied by the module's dominant cognitive verb.

**KDD-14: Product 40 at commutative table position in Practice BASELINE.** Practice includes product 40 at (8,5), the commutative position of EC.2's (5,8). This tests whether students read specific row/column coordinates rather than recognizing a familiar product value. A student who solved EC.2 ("40 at row 5, column 8 → 5 by 8") and then encounters "40 at row 8, column 5" must independently read the new position to get "8 by 5" — same product, different coordinates, different dimensions. This is the same caliber of bidirectional reinforcement as KDD-6 (product 30 reuse).
