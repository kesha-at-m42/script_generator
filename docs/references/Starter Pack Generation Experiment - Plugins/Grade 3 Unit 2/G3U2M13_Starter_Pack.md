# **MODULE 13: Finding Missing Side Lengths**

# BACKBONE

---

## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**[REQUIRED]**

Students find missing side lengths on composite figures by applying the principle that related sides add up — horizontal pieces add up to the total horizontal length, vertical pieces to the total vertical length. Once all sides are known, they decompose and calculate area using the same workflow from M12. All missing sides in M13 are independently solvable (no dependency chains — that's M14).

**CRA Stage:** Abstract (full). M13 operates entirely at the dimensions-only abstraction level established in M12 (Decision #3: M13–M14 = "dimensions only with missing sides, full abstraction"). No grids appear in any phase. The Highlight Related Sides teaching action (color-matched edges with constraint equation) is the primary scaffold, fading from automatic (Early) to guide-controlled (Mid) to remediation-only (Late).

**Critical Misconception:** #7 — Wrong Missing Side Length. The signature error is directional: students subtract a vertical dimension from a horizontal total (or vice versa), producing plausible-looking values that cascade through the rest of the workflow undetected. The "add up" relationship requires attending to the WHOLE figure's edge structure — students must hold the global shape in mind while reasoning about local dimensions. **Secondary:** #12 — Multiplying All Given Numbers (carried from M12, risk increased because finding missing sides adds MORE visible dimension labels to the figure).

**Success Indicator:** Given a composite figure with 1–3 missing dimension labels, the student independently decomposes, identifies which missing sides are needed for their decomposition, correctly applies the "sides add up" principle to find them (choosing addition or subtraction as appropriate, on the correct directional axis), calculates each rectangle's area, and adds — arriving at the correct total without scaffolding support.

**Biggest Risk:** Students learn the "sides add up" principle as a mechanical subtraction step without spatial reasoning — subtracting whatever two numbers are visible without checking directional axis. The directional error check in Early and the Mid workflow (where students discover the NEED through decomposing) are the primary guards.

### **1.1.2 Module Bridges**

**From M12 (Composite Figures Without Grids):**
M12 removed the grid and taught students to trust dimension labels for composite figure area. The decompose-calculate-add workflow is identical to M11; the representation shifted from gridded to dimensions-only. M12 also informally introduced the "related sides add up" principle as a VERIFICATION tool — students checked their work by confirming that segment dimensions sum to the full edge. M12's Synthesis closure explicitly bridges to M13: "What if some sides didn't have measurements? Could you use that same idea to figure out the missing ones?" The "related sides" concept, M12's verification tool, becomes M13's problem-solving tool.

**This Module (M13: Finding Missing Side Lengths):**
M13 adds one new reasoning step to the composite figures workflow: some dimension labels are missing ("?"), and students must use the "related sides add up" principle to find them before decomposing and calculating. The principle is the same one M12 introduced informally; M13 extends it from verification to problem-solving. The decompose-calculate-add workflow is unchanged. The pedagogical progression within M13 is deliberately staged: Early teaches the principle on L-shapes where the missing side isn't needed for area (honest pedagogy), Mid introduces T-shapes where it IS needed (genuine purpose), and Late consolidates the decompose-first workflow independently (decompose → find needed missing sides → calculate → add). New figure types with missing sides (T-shapes, U-shapes) increase the spatial reasoning demand — students must attend to the whole figure's edge structure to identify related sides.

**To M14 (Application — Real-World Room Design):**
M14 applies the complete skill set from M11–M13 (decompose, calculate ungridded areas, find missing sides) to real-world room design problems. M13's bridge previews the application context: "You've learned to find area of all kinds of shapes, with grids, without grids, even with missing measurements. Now let's use all of that to design a room!" M14 may also introduce dependency chains (where one missing side requires another to be found first). M13's independently-solvable missing sides prepare the reasoning foundation.

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

**[REQUIRED]**

### **✅ Must Teach**

* **"Related sides add up" as a problem-solving principle:** The core new skill. Students use the principle that horizontal pieces add up to the total horizontal length (and vertical to total vertical) to find missing dimension labels. This extends M12's informal verification use into active problem-solving. The Guide uses directional language: "Look at the horizontal sides" / "Look at the vertical sides." No formal term is taught for the relationship — students learn the PROCEDURE.
* **Missing side identification and calculation:** Given a composite figure with 1–3 sides marked "?", students identify which known sides relate to each missing side, determine whether to add or subtract, and calculate the missing value. Includes both single subtraction (total − piece = missing) and double subtraction (total − piece₁ − piece₂ = missing).
* **Directional reasoning — horizontal with horizontal, vertical with vertical:** The primary defense against misconception #7. Students learn that horizontal sides constrain each other and vertical sides constrain each other independently. The directional error check makes this explicit.
* **Decompose-first workflow as target state:** Decompose → find needed missing sides → calculate each rectangle's area → add. This is the culminating workflow for the M11–M13 arc, made explicit in Late and consolidated in Synthesis. The decompose-first ordering is deliberate: students discover the NEED for missing-side reasoning through decomposing, which is how the skill stays grounded in geometric purpose rather than becoming a mechanical preliminary step.
* **Figure type progression:** L-shapes (Early), T-shapes (Mid), L/T/U-shapes (Late). Each figure type increases the spatial reasoning demand and introduces new patterns (double subtraction, addition of segments).
* **Decompose-first workflow discovery (Mid):** In Mid, students decompose FIRST, then discover that a sub-rectangle is missing a dimension. This is how the principle's necessity becomes evident — the need emerges from the decomposition, not from teacher instruction.
* **Error cascading awareness (Practice):** When a student gets the missing side wrong but executes the rest of the workflow correctly, the Guide traces the error back to its source and validates the correct steps. This is explicitly specified for Practice.
* **Vocabulary: "missing side"** — The one new term. A side where the measurement isn't shown. Taught as a functional label, not formal definition.
* **Vocabulary reinforcement:** decompose, composite figure, dimensions, area, multiply, add, related sides (from M12 informal preview). All used in the new context of missing-side reasoning.
* **Proactive misconception prevention — directional error check (Early):** After 2nd Early problem, Guide poses an incorrect directional reasoning and asks student to evaluate. Directly addresses misconception #7 before it takes root.

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* **Perimeter** — Per Decision #5. Not mentioned, even for contrast. The "sides add up" principle may superficially resemble perimeter reasoning to an adult, but the SP must NOT draw this connection. Students are reasoning about directional edge constraints, not computing perimeter.
* **Grids** — Per Decision #3. M13 is full abstraction (dimensions only with missing sides). No full grids, no partial grids. Ghost grid is NOT specified in the TVP for M13 — Highlight Related Sides is the scaffold instead.
* **Dependency chains between missing sides** — Where one missing side requires another to be found first. M13 missing sides are independently solvable. Dependency chains are M14 territory. Complexity in M13 comes from double subtraction, add-vs-subtract recognition, and multi-edge identification.
* **Figures with more than 3 components** — M13 uses 2–3 component figures (same as M12). The complexity increase is in missing sides, not figure complexity.
* **Real-world application contexts** — M13 uses generic composite figures (no patio, no room, no real-world wrapping). Application is M14's territory. Per Decision #9: keep application standalone.
* **"Opposite sides" as a general principle term** — Per Module Mapping Teaching Notes: "Use 'opposite sides' ONLY when reviewing single-rectangle properties in warm-up context." The general principle uses "related sides" language (from TVP). "Opposite sides" reserved for warmup single-rectangle context only.
* **Subtraction decomposition** — M12's alternative strategy. M13's cognitive load is on missing sides; adding strategic decomposition choice would overload. All decomposition is additive.
* **New interaction types beyond MC selection for missing sides** — Missing side identification uses MC. Decomposition uses the same student-placed line from M12. No new interaction modalities.
* **Cost calculations or multi-step word problems** — No real-world wrapping at all. Pure geometric reasoning.
* **"Formula" language** — Same constraint as M7–M12. Decomposition is a strategy.
* **Division language** — Not introduced in Unit 2.
* **Order of operations language** — Parentheses remain descriptive.
* **Formal naming of the "sides add up" relationship** — Students learn the procedure. No term like "additive constraint" or "complementary sides" is introduced.

### **Vocabulary Notes**

**"Missing side" (new — functional label):** A side where the measurement isn't shown. Per Module Mapping Teaching Notes: "This is taught as a procedure, not named as vocabulary." The term is functional — it labels a visible feature of the figure ("?" marking). Students don't need to define "missing side"; they need to recognize one and know what to do about it. Introduction path: visual (warmup "?" reveal) → natural language ("One side length is missing") → procedural ("Figure out the missing side using related sides").

**"Related sides" (reinforced — elevated from M12 preview):** In M12, "related sides add up" was introduced informally as a verification concept ("That's how you check your work"). In M13, it becomes the working principle for finding missing dimensions. The term isn't formally taught — it's the Guide's natural language for the constraint relationship between edges. Same preview-then-formalize pattern as "rows of" (M3) → "×" (M4). Students don't need to define "related sides"; they need to recognize which sides relate and apply the constraint.

**Directional language — "horizontal sides" / "vertical sides":** The Guide's primary tool for preventing misconception #7. "Look at the horizontal sides" / "Look at the vertical sides" is the consistent framing. Students must learn that horizontal sides constrain horizontal sides and vertical constrain vertical — this is the directional reasoning that makes the "add up" principle safe. Not vocabulary per se, but essential instructional language.

**"Sides add up":** The student-accessible version of the constraint principle. "The horizontal pieces add up to the total horizontal length." Simpler than any mathematical term. Used consistently in Guide dialogue. NOT "sides must equal" (which could mean all sides are the same length).

**Language patterns for M13:**

* "One side length is missing. Can you figure out what it is?"
* "Look at the horizontal sides. The long bottom is __. This piece is __. What's the other piece?"
* "Horizontal sides add up with horizontal sides. Vertical sides add up with vertical sides."
* "Related sides add up — that's how you find the missing side."
* "Decompose first, then figure out what you need."
* "Decompose. Find any missing side lengths you need. Find each area. Add."

### **Terms to Avoid (Save for Later Modules)**

* **"Perimeter"** — Do not use, even for contrast. Per Decision #5. The "sides add up" principle is about dimensional constraints within a direction, not about the total distance around.
* **"Opposite sides"** (in composite figure context) — Module Mapping lists "opposite sides" in Vocabulary to Teach, but the TVP restricts it to single-rectangle properties in warmup ("opposite sides of a rectangle are equal"). For composite figure reasoning, use "related sides" (TVP's working term throughout). If a warmup interaction reviews single-rectangle properties, "opposite sides" may appear there. It must NOT be used to describe the "sides add up" constraint on composite figures — that's "related sides" language. See Conflict Log entry in Working Notes.
* **"Formula"** (standalone) — Same constraint as M7–M12. Suggests rote memorization.
* **"Divide" / "Division"** — Not introduced in Unit 2.
* **"Order of operations"** — Parentheses are descriptive notation.
* **"Complementary sides" / "additive constraint"** — No formal mathematical term for the relationship. Students learn the procedure without naming it.
* **"Memorize"** — The "sides add up" principle is a reasoning tool, not a fact to memorize.
* **"Subtract"** (as a step name) — The Guide uses "figure out" and "find" rather than naming the arithmetic operation. Students aren't learning subtraction; they're using it as a tool. "What's the other piece?" not "Subtract 5 from 8."
* **"Ruler" / "Measure"** — Per Important Decisions (ruler dropped). Not applicable to M13 but maintaining the unit-wide constraint.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

**[REQUIRED]**

### **1.4.1 #7: Wrong Missing Side Length (PRIMARY)**

**Trigger Behavior:** Given a composite figure with some sides labeled and some marked "?", the student calculates the wrong value for a missing side. Common errors: (1) subtracting a vertical dimension from a horizontal total (or vice versa) — directional confusion, (2) adding when they should subtract (writing 8 + 5 = 13 instead of 8 − 5 = 3), (3) subtracting the wrong pair of values on a multi-edge figure, (4) for double subtraction, subtracting only one segment instead of both (9 − 4 = 5 instead of 9 − 4 − 2 = 3).

**Why It's a Barrier:** The Misconceptions database lists #7 as most likely to surface at M14 (under cognitive load with dependency chains and application contexts). M13 is where the principle is TAUGHT and the misconception is PREVENTED — the heavy investment in directional reasoning here is proactive, ensuring students have robust spatial habits before M14's increased demand. This is the signature error of missing-side problems and M13's PRIMARY instructional target. The "add up" relationship requires spatial reasoning about the WHOLE figure — students must identify which edges are on the same directional axis and which values belong together. Third graders find this genuinely challenging because it requires holding the global shape in mind while reasoning about local dimensions. Unlike M12's misconception #12 (multiplying random numbers), which produces obviously wrong answers, wrong-missing-side errors can produce plausible-looking values that cascade through the rest of the workflow — the student decomposes correctly, multiplies correctly with the wrong dimension, and arrives at a confidently wrong answer.

**Prevention Strategy (M13-specific):**

* **Primary defense — directional reasoning thread:** "Look at the horizontal sides" / "Look at the vertical sides" language in EVERY missing-side interaction. Students learn that horizontal constrains horizontal, vertical constrains vertical. This is taught in Early, reinforced in Mid, and expected in Late.
* **Highlight Related Sides teaching action:** Color-matches the missing side with its related known sides. Constraint equation shows actual values (e.g., 8 − 5 = ?). Makes the relationship visible. Fades: automatic (Early) → guide-controlled (Mid) → remediation-only (Late).
* **Proactive directional error check (Early, after 2nd problem):** Guide poses incorrect directional reasoning: "A student subtracted the vertical side from the horizontal side. Would that work?" Student evaluates and corrects. Costs ~30 seconds, directly prevents the most common error pattern.
* **MC distractor design:** Missing-side MC options always include a wrong-direction distractor (a value derived from subtracting across directions). This makes the directional error visible and diagnosable.
* **For double subtraction:** MC distractors include single-subtraction errors (subtracting only one segment). Diagnoses whether student identified ALL segments on the edge.

### **1.4.2 #12: Multiplying All Given Numbers (SECONDARY)**

**Trigger Behavior:** After finding missing sides, MORE dimension labels are visible on the figure (the found values fill in). The student multiplies some random combination of all visible numbers instead of decomposing first and pairing dimensions to specific rectangles. This is M12's signature error, carried into M13 with increased risk because the figure now has MORE visible numbers.

**Why It's a Barrier:** M13 increases the pool of visible dimension labels — the original labels plus the newly-found missing sides. A figure that had 6 labels in M12 now has 8 after missing sides are resolved. The dimension-pairing discipline from M12 must continue.

**Prevention Strategy (M13-specific):**

* **Maintained M12 workflow:** Decompose → identify dimensions per rectangle → calculate → add. The per-rectangle dimension highlighting from M12 continues in M13.
* **Sequential resolution:** Missing sides are found BEFORE decomposition (in Late) or discovered through decomposition (in Mid). Either way, by the time students multiply, dimensions are already paired to rectangles through the highlighting system.
* **Error cascading guidance (Practice):** When a student gets a wrong answer, the Guide traces back to determine if the error was a missing-side error or a dimension-pairing error, and addresses the ROOT cause.

### **1.4.3 #11: Completing the Rectangle (TERTIARY — awareness only)**

**Trigger Behavior:** Student sees the bounding box dimensions (longest horizontal × longest vertical) and uses those for the whole figure instead of decomposing. May surface when students see the "total" dimensions used in the "sides add up" reasoning and confuse them with the figure's area dimensions.

**Why It's a Barrier:** The total edge lengths that students use to find missing sides (e.g., "the long bottom is 8") are the same values that would produce the bounding-rectangle error. A student who doesn't distinguish "total edge for constraint reasoning" from "dimension for area calculation" could multiply the totals.

**M13 Scope:** Tertiary/awareness — this misconception was actively addressed in M11–M12, where decomposition was the new skill. By M13, students have practiced correct decomposition across two modules. #11 is not an M13 instructional target; it is listed here for completeness because the "total edge" values in missing-side reasoning could re-trigger it. If it surfaces, the existing decomposition + dimension-highlighting workflow from M12 is the intervention. The decompose-first workflow in Mid further reinforces the distinction: students decompose into rectangles with their OWN dimensions, not the overall figure dimensions.

## **1.6 WARMUP (3–4 minutes)**

**[REQUIRED]**

### **Core Purpose**

**One-Line Summary:**
Establish that missing side lengths on composite figures are findable — not blockers — using the "related sides add up" principle from M12.

**Key Function:**
This Warmup reactivates the M12 decomposition workflow (decompose ungridded composite, multiply dimensions, add areas), then introduces the module's threshold moment: one dimension disappears and is replaced with "?". Students see the Highlight Related Sides teaching action for the first time, connecting the "related sides add up" verification concept from M12 to a new purpose: finding a missing dimension. The warmup is a confidence moment, not a calculation moment — students do NOT recalculate area on the reveal figure (they already computed it on the same shape).

**Why This Serves the Concept:**
* Missing sides are findable, not obstacles — lowers anxiety before Lesson
* "Related sides add up" transitions from M12 verification tool to M13 problem-solving tool
* Same figure shown twice (labeled → "?") makes the connection concrete and unambiguous
* No new cognitive demand beyond recognizing the missing side — area is already known
* Warmup includes a brief single-rectangle property review ("opposite sides are equal") before the composite figure, activating the geometric foundation

**Test:**
If we removed this Warmup, would students be ready for the Lesson? **Partially.** Without it, students would encounter "?" labels cold — without the anchor that they already know the answer for this figure. The Warmup makes the "?" feel like a puzzle to solve, not information they're missing.

### Warmup Type Rationale

**Type selected:** Activation + Reveal (same hybrid as M12, adapted for M13's threshold)

**Why this type fits M13:** M13's warmup has two jobs. First, students reactivate the M12 decomposition workflow on a fully-labeled ungridded L-shape — that's pure Activation. Second, the same figure reappears with one dimension replaced by "?" and the Highlight Related Sides teaching action reveals the constraint equation — that's a Reveal moment. The combination ensures students confirm their existing skill AND confront the new demand (missing information) in a low-stakes, already-solved context.

**Why not other types:**
* Mystery Reveal alone — Close, but the mystery here requires the activation step first. Students need to solve the figure fully-labeled before the "?" version means anything.
* Notice & Wonder — Risk: students focus on surface changes (one number is gone) rather than the mathematical relationship (related sides add up to find it). The teacher narration in W.2 is essential to direct attention.
* Number Talk — M13 is about spatial/geometric reasoning, not mental math strategies.

### Interaction W.1: Fully-Labeled L-Shape — Student Solves [ACTIVATION]

* **Purpose:** Activate M12 decomposition workflow quickly and confidently. Student solves a routine task (ungridded L-shape, all dimensions labeled) to establish that the method works and they own it. Brief single-rectangle property recall ("opposite sides are equal") embedded naturally in the guide language during decomposition confirmation. This primes the geometric reasoning that underlies the "sides add up" principle.
* **Visual: Composite Figures (labels only).** L-shaped figure, 2-component. All edges labeled with dimensions. No grid (per Decision #3). No decomposition line pre-placed. Dimensions (clockwise from top-left): top = 7, right-top vertical = 3 (down), notch horizontal = 4 (left), inner vertical = 3 (down), bottom = 3 (left), left = 6 (up). Vertical check: 3 + 3 = 6 ✓. Horizontal check: 3 + 4 = 7 ✓. Component rectangles after decomposition: top rectangle 7 × 3 = 21, bottom-left rectangle 3 × 3 = 9. Total area = 30 square units.

### Interaction W.2: Same L-Shape with Missing Side — Teacher-Led Reveal [REVEAL — OBSERVATION + MC]

* **Purpose:** Introduce the module's threshold moment: one familiar dimension disappears and becomes "?". The Highlight Related Sides teaching action reveals the constraint equation, connecting the "related sides add up" concept from M12's verification use to M13's problem-solving use. Student identifies the missing side value via MC but does NOT recalculate area — the point is the missing-side principle, not the area workflow.
* **Visual: Composite Figures (labels only, Missing Sides mode).** Same L-shape from W.1. One dimension replaced with "?" — the notch horizontal edge (was "4" in W.1, now "?"). All other dimensions remain labeled. System activates Highlight Related Sides: the "?" edge and the two related known horizontal edges (top edge "7" and adjacent horizontal segment "3") color-match in blue. Constraint equation appears: 7 − 3 = ?
* **Guide:** "Same shape. Same area. But now one side length is missing. See the question mark? Here's the thing: you can figure it out. Remember how related sides add up? The long top is 7. This piece is 3. So the other piece must be... let's see. 7 minus 3. Look at the blue sides. They're all horizontal, and the pieces add up to the total. What's 7 minus 3?"
* **Prompt:** "What's the missing side length? Select from: 4, 10, 1, 3."
* **Student Action:** Student selects from MC (4 options: 4 [correct], 10 [addition error], 3 [same as known piece], 7 [total — didn't subtract]).

* **On Correct:** "That's it. The missing piece is 4. You already found the area of this shape. Even with a missing side, you had enough information to figure it out. Related sides add up. That's how you find what's missing. That's what we're going to practice today."

* **Remediation (minimal):** If student selects wrong MC answer, Guide reorients by pointing to the color-matched sides: "Look at the blue sides — they're all horizontal. The long top is 7, this piece is 3. The other piece is 7 minus 3." System re-displays Highlight Related Sides with constraint equation. No heavy remediation — this is a reveal interaction, not an assessment moment.

> **Design Note (No Recalculation):** Per TVP Revision 2 (authoritative). The warmup is a confidence moment, not a calculation moment. Asking students to recalculate would shift focus from the missing-side principle to the area workflow, diluting the threshold moment. The area is already known — the "?" is what's new.

> **Design Note ("Opposite Sides" Scope):** The Guide's language in W.2 uses "related sides add up" (TVP's composite-figure term), not "opposite sides." Per Module Mapping Teaching Notes, "opposite sides" is restricted to single-rectangle property review. The brief property recall in W.1 serves this purpose. For composite figures, "related sides" is the working term throughout.

### Interaction 1.3: Directional Error Check [MISCONCEPTION PREVENTION]

* **Purpose:** Proactively address the directional component of misconception #7. After two successful missing-side problems, the guide poses an incorrect reasoning and asks the student to evaluate. This costs ~30 seconds and directly prevents the most common error pattern before it takes root.
* **Visual: Composite Figures (labels only, Missing Sides mode).** L-shape with 1 missing horizontal side. All dimensions visible including vertical edges. No Highlight Related Sides activated yet — student must reason about direction.
* **Guide:** "Let me ask you something. A student looked at this figure and said: 'The missing horizontal side is the bottom of the shape. I'll subtract the vertical side from the horizontal top to find it.' Would that work?"
* **Prompt:** "Would subtracting a vertical side from a horizontal side give you the right answer? Select: Yes / No."
* **Student Action:** Student selects No.
* **Correct Answer:** No
* **Answer Rationale:** No = Correct (horizontal and vertical sides constrain independently — subtracting across directions produces a meaningless value); Yes = Misconception #7 directional confusion (treating perpendicular sides as related — the most common error pattern for missing-side problems).
* **On Correct:** "Right. Horizontal sides add up with horizontal sides. Vertical sides add up with vertical sides. You always check: are these sides going the same direction?"
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Remediation Note:** If student selects Yes: "Let's check. The missing side is horizontal — it goes left to right. Does a vertical side, one that goes up and down, tell you anything about a horizontal side? No — vertical and horizontal don't constrain each other. You need to look at the OTHER horizontal sides."

Guide dialogue NEVER uses "axis" — students hear "horizontal sides," "vertical sides," and "same direction." ### **Misconception Prevention**

**Misconception #7: Wrong Missing Side Length (PRIMARY)**

*Risk:* Student subtracts a vertical dimension from a horizontal total (directional confusion), or adds instead of subtracting, or subtracts only one segment in a double-subtraction problem.

*Prevention in Early (Interactions 1.1–1.2):*
Highlight Related Sides is automatic — color-matches the missing side with its related known sides on the same directional axis. Constraint equation shows actual values. Guide models directional reasoning: "Look at the horizontal sides."

*Prevention in Early (Interaction 1.3 — Directional Error Check):*
Guide poses incorrect directional reasoning; student evaluates and corrects. Directly addresses the most common error pattern before it takes root. Cost: ~30 seconds.

*Prevention in Mid (Interactions 2.1–2.5):*
Highlight Related Sides is guide-controlled (activated after wrong MC). MC distractors always include a wrong-direction option. Guide reinforces directional language on every problem.

*Prevention in Late (Interactions 3.2–3.5):*
Highlight Related Sides is remediation-only. Students are expected to apply directional reasoning independently. MC distractors continue to include diagnostic wrong-direction options.

**Misconception #12: Multiplying All Given Numbers (SECONDARY)**

*Risk:* After finding missing sides, MORE dimension labels are visible. Student multiplies random combination of all visible numbers.

*Prevention:*
Maintained from M12 — per-rectangle dimension highlighting (on-demand, student taps rectangle) isolates dimensions to their rectangle. The decompose-first workflow ensures students pair dimensions to specific rectangles before multiplying.

**Misconception #11: Completing the Rectangle (TERTIARY)**

*Risk:* Student uses bounding box dimensions (total horizontal × total vertical) instead of decomposing.

*Prevention:*
Less prominent by M13 (practiced correct decomposition across M11–M12). The decompose-first workflow in Mid and Late reinforces decomposition as the first step, making the bounding-box shortcut structurally impossible to apply within the workflow.

### Interaction EC.1: L-Shape, 1 Missing Side — Find and Calculate [APPLY]

* **Purpose:** Assess the basic find-missing-side + decompose-calculate-add workflow. Tests: can student identify the correct missing side value using horizontal "add up," then decompose and calculate total area.
* **Visual: Composite Figures (labels only, Missing Sides mode).** L-shape, 2-component. Dimensions (clockwise from top-left): top = 10, right-top vertical = 5 (down), step horizontal = "?" (left), inner vertical = 4 (down), bottom = 7 (left), left = 9 (up). Vertical check: 5 + 4 = 9 ✓. Horizontal check: 7 + ? = 10, so ? = 3. All six edge values unique (10, 9, 5, 3, 4, 7). No Highlight Related Sides (EC = no scaffolding).

**EC.1a: Find Missing Side (MC)**

* **Guide:** "Find the missing side, then decompose and calculate the total area."
* **Prompt:** "What is the missing side length? Select: 3, 17, 5, 9."
* **Student Action:** MC selection for missing side.
* **Correct Answer:** 3
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 3 = Correct (horizontal: 10 − 7 = 3. The top edge is the total horizontal; the bottom edge is 7; the missing step is the remaining horizontal piece.)
  * 17 = Addition error (10 + 7 = 17 — added the total and the piece instead of subtracting; Misconception #7 variant)
  * 5 = Wrong-direction (grabbed the right-top vertical value; or computed 9 − 4 = 5 using vertical dimensions and applied to a horizontal question; Misconception #7 directional confusion)
  * 9 = Grabbed left edge value (applied the full vertical dimension to the horizontal context)
* **On Correct:** "3. The horizontal pieces add up to 10."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

**EC.1b: Decompose and Calculate Total Area (MC)**

*System fills "?" with 3 on the figure.*

* **Guide:** (no additional guide dialogue; student proceeds to decompose)
* **Prompt:** "Decompose and find the total area."
* **Student Action:** Decomposition line drawing → system color-codes → MC for total area.
  * **Options:** A) 78 square units, B) 50 square units, C) 90 square units, D) 28 square units
* **Correct Answer:** A) 78 square units
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A = Correct (horizontal cut: top strip 10 × 5 = 50, bottom-left 7 × 4 = 28; total = 78. OR vertical cut: left column 7 × 9 = 63, right column 3 × 5 = 15; total = 78.)
  * B = Partial calculation (10 × 5 = 50 — one rectangle only)
  * C = Multiplied wrong dimensions (10 × 9 = 90 — bounding rectangle or grabbed two largest dimensions; Misconception #12 variant)
  * D = Partial calculation (7 × 4 = 28 — other rectangle only)
* **On Correct:** "78 square units."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

### Interaction EC.2: Stepped Figure (T-Like), 2 Missing Sides — Multi-Step [APPLY]

* **Purpose:** Assess multi-step missing-side reasoning + area calculation. Tests: can student find 2 independently solvable missing sides (one horizontal, one vertical) on different axes, then decompose and calculate total area. This is the M13 target competency at full complexity.
* **Visual: Composite Figures (labels only, Missing Sides mode).** Stepped figure (inverted T appearance: wider crossbar on top, narrower base below, base flush with left side). 2-component. Dimensions (clockwise from top-left): top = 8 (right), right-crossbar = 3 (down), step horizontal = "?" (left), stem-right vertical = "?" (down), bottom = 6 (left), left = 10 (up). Vertical check: 3 + ? = 10, stem-right = 7. Horizontal check: 6 + ? = 8, step = 2. All six edge values unique (8, 3, 2, 7, 6, 10). No Highlight Related Sides.

> **Design Note:** Stepped figure (base flush left) provides full-height reference edge for independent solvability while preserving T-shape spatial complexity. See KDD-7.

**EC.2a: Find Missing Horizontal Side (MC)**

* **Guide:** "Two sides are missing this time. Find each one, then decompose and calculate."
* **Prompt:** "What is the missing horizontal side length? Select: 2, 14, 7, 6."
* **Student Action:** MC selection for horizontal missing side (step).
* **Correct Answer:** 2
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 2 = Correct (horizontal: 8 − 6 = 2. The top edge is the total horizontal; the bottom is 6; the missing step is the remaining piece.)
  * 14 = Addition error (8 + 6 = 14)
  * 7 = Wrong-direction (vertical: 10 − 3 = 7, correct vertical computation applied to horizontal question; also equals the other missing side — tests whether student confuses which missing side they're solving)
  * 6 = Grabbed bottom edge value (used the known piece instead of computing the unknown)
* **On Correct:** "2. The horizontal pieces add up to 8."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

**EC.2b: Find Missing Vertical Side (MC)**

*System fills "?" with 2 on the step edge.*

* **Guide:** (no additional guide dialogue; student proceeds to next missing side)
* **Prompt:** "What is the missing vertical side length? Select: 7, 13, 2, 3."
* **Student Action:** MC selection for vertical missing side (stem-right).
* **Correct Answer:** 7
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 7 = Correct (vertical: 10 − 3 = 7. The full left edge is 10; the right-crossbar is 3; the missing stem-right is the remaining vertical piece.)
  * 13 = Addition error (10 + 3 = 13)
  * 2 = Wrong-direction (horizontal: 8 − 6 = 2, correct horizontal computation applied to vertical question; also equals the step just found — tests whether student confuses axes)
  * 3 = Grabbed right-crossbar value (used a known vertical piece instead of computing the unknown)
* **On Correct:** "7. The vertical pieces add up to 10."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

**EC.2c: Decompose and Calculate Total Area (MC)**

*System fills "?" with 7 on the stem-right edge. Both missing sides now resolved.*

* **Guide:** (no additional guide dialogue; student proceeds to decompose)
* **Prompt:** "Decompose and find the total area."
* **Student Action:** Decomposition line drawing → system color-codes → MC for total area.
  * **Options:** A) 66 square units, B) 24 square units, C) 80 square units, D) 42 square units
* **Correct Answer:** A) 66 square units
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A = Correct (horizontal cut: crossbar 8 × 3 = 24, stem 6 × 7 = 42; total = 66. OR vertical cut: left column 6 × 10 = 60, right crossbar piece 2 × 3 = 6; total = 66.)
  * B = Partial calculation (8 × 3 = 24 — crossbar only)
  * C = Bounding rectangle or wrong pairing (8 × 10 = 80 — multiplied top × left, the two largest edges; Misconception #12 variant)
  * D = Partial calculation (6 × 7 = 42 — stem only)
* **On Correct:** "66 square units."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

### Interaction EC.3: Misconception Check — Directional Error [IDENTIFY]

* **Purpose:** Directly target misconception #7 (Wrong Missing Side Length — directional confusion). Tests whether student applies horizontal-with-horizontal reasoning and resists the wrong-direction distractor. No decomposition or area calculation — pure missing-side identification.
* **Visual: Composite Figures (labels only, Missing Sides mode).** L-shape with 1 missing horizontal side. Dimensions (clockwise from top-left): top = 9, right-top vertical = 2 (down), step horizontal = "?" (left), inner vertical = 5 (down), bottom = 6 (left), left = 7 (up). Vertical check: 2 + 5 = 7 ✓. Horizontal check: 6 + ? = 9, so ? = 3. All six edge values unique (9, 7, 2, 3, 5, 6). No Highlight Related Sides.
* **Guide:** "One more. What's the missing side?"
* **Prompt:** "What is the missing side length? Select: 3, 15, 5, 2."
* **Student Action:** MC selection.
* **Correct Answer:** 3
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * 3 = Correct (horizontal: 9 − 6 = 3. The missing step is horizontal; the top edge (9) is the horizontal total; the bottom (6) is the known horizontal piece.)
  * 15 = Addition error (9 + 6 = 15 — added instead of subtracted)
  * 5 = Wrong-direction — THE KEY DISTRACTOR (7 − 2 = 5: subtracted vertical pieces and applied the result to the horizontal missing side. This is Misconception #7's signature pattern: correct arithmetic, wrong axis. The student identified two vertical dimensions and subtracted them, producing a vertical answer for a horizontal question.)
  * 2 = Grabbed right-top value (selected a visible vertical dimension without reasoning about the relationship)
* **On Correct:** "3. Horizontal sides with horizontal sides."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

> **Design Note:** Distractor 5 (7−2=5) is the core #7 diagnostic — correct arithmetic, wrong axis. Signals procedure without directional constraint.

### **[Module-Specific: Practice-Specific Constraints]**

* ⚠️ **CRITICAL — Error cascading guidance (MUST be prominent):** If student correctly decomposes but gets missing side wrong, then correctly multiplies with wrong value, guide acknowledges process: "Your steps were right — decompose, multiply, add. But let's go back and check the missing side. That's where the error started." Address ROOT cause, not downstream effect. This is M13's signature Practice challenge: wrong missing-side values cascade through the entire calculation, producing a plausible-looking but incorrect final answer.
* After every missing-side MC, guide confirms axis: "Horizontal pieces add up" or "Vertical pieces add up" — reinforces directional reasoning (Misconception #7 prevention).
* Mix of horizontal and vertical missing sides across BASELINE tier — at least 2 of each direction to prevent directional association.
* Decompose-first workflow for all BASELINE and STRETCH problems: student decomposes → encounters missing side → finds via MC → calculates. SUPPORT may present missing side before decomposition (scaffolded).
* Error patterns to watch: wrong-direction subtraction (#7 — primary), multiplying all visible numbers (#12 — secondary, risk increases after missing sides add more visible dimensions), single-subtraction error on double-subtraction problems, addition when subtraction needed (or vice versa).
* Transfer gate: 70%+ accuracy across BASELINE + STRETCH, with at least 1 multi-missing-side problem (STRETCH) completed successfully.

### Interaction S.2: Operations Consolidation — Three Ways Sides Add Up [TYPE A: TEACHING ONLY → PATTERN DISCOVERY (consolidation/recognition)]

* **Purpose:** Make explicit that students mastered three arithmetic variations of the same principle. Side-by-side display prevents fragmented understanding and supports recognition that "the principle is the same; the arithmetic changes."
* **Visual: Composite Figures (consolidation display).** Three L/T/U-shaped figures displayed side by side with color-matched related sides highlighted:
  * **Left panel:** L-shape with single subtraction highlighted. Equation: 10 − 7 = 3. Caption: "One piece missing."
  * **Center panel:** T-shape with double subtraction highlighted. Equation: 9 − 4 − 2 = 3. Caption: "Two pieces known, find the third."
  * **Right panel:** L-variant with addition highlighted. Equation: 3 + 5 = 8. Caption: "Two pieces known, find the total."
* **Guide:** "Three different figures, three different problems. But the same idea every time. Related sides add up. Sometimes you subtract one piece. Sometimes you subtract two pieces. Sometimes you add pieces together. The principle doesn't change."
* **No student action.**

> **Design Note:** Display panels reuse Lesson values — recognition, not new computation. Value-difference constraints apply only to assessed interactions.

### Interaction S.3: Generalization — "What's Always True?" [TYPE B: PROGRESSIVE → PATTERN DISCOVERY (generalization)]

* **Purpose:** Students extract the "always true" principle from multiple examples. This is the highest-demand Synthesis task, moving from examples to principle.
* **Connection:** Lesson Early (single subtraction), Mid (double subtraction, addition of segments), Late (consolidation). Students generalize across all three arithmetic variations encountered during the Lesson.
* **Visual: Composite Figures (principle display).** The same three figures from S.2 remain visible. Each has its color-matched related sides and equation displayed.
* **Guide:** "You've seen this work on L-shapes, T-shapes, and U-shapes. What's ALWAYS true about the sides of these figures?"
* **Prompt:** "What's always true about rectilinear figures? Select: A) The horizontal pieces always add up to the total horizontal length, and the vertical pieces always add up to the total vertical length, B) All sides of the figure are equal, C) Opposite sides of the figure are always equal, D) You always subtract to find missing sides."
* **Student Action:** MC selection.
* **Correct Answer:** A
* **Answer Rationale:**
  * A = Correct — the complete directional "add up" constraint: horizontal pieces sum to total horizontal, vertical pieces sum to total vertical. This is the invariant principle across all figure types and all arithmetic variations.
  * B = Incorrect — sides are NOT all equal in composite figures. This distractor tests whether the student confuses the "add up" relationship with equality.
  * C = Incorrect — "opposite sides are equal" applies to single rectangles, not composite figures. This distractor tests whether the student over-generalizes the warmup single-rectangle property to composites. (Per vocabulary restriction: "opposite sides" is warmup-only language.)
  * D = Incorrect — sometimes you ADD to find missing sides (as S.2 showed). This distractor tests whether the student associated the principle only with subtraction, missing the addition-of-segments variation.
* **On Correct:** "Horizontal pieces add up to the total horizontal length. Vertical pieces add up to the total vertical length. That's the principle that makes missing sides findable."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline
  * **Remediation Note:** Light redirect — "Look at the three equations. Do they ALL use subtraction?"

### Interaction S.4: Metacognitive Reflection — Strategy Identification [TYPE 1: STRATEGY IDENTIFICATION]

* **Purpose:** Students identify which strategy helped them most when finding missing sides. Surfaces and validates strategies. Planning-focused (strongest for this age per Synthesis Playbook §3E).
* **Connection:** Lesson Early directional error check (1.3), Mid decompose-first discovery (2.1), Late independent workflow (3.1). Students reflect on which of these strategic moments was most helpful.

## **1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS SUMMARY**

**[REQUIRED]**

Master list of cross-cutting design decisions for this module. References phase-level design notes where relevant. Numbered for easy reference in reviews and feedback.

1. **Early pedagogical asymmetry: the missing side is found but NOT needed for area.** For L-shapes with 1 missing side, there is always a decomposition that avoids the missing dimension. Early teaches the "sides add up" principle as a standalone geometric skill — students learn it honestly, not as a manufactured prerequisite for area calculation. The Guide steers toward the decomposition avoiding the missing side, but if a student CHOOSES the other decomposition (having already found the missing side), that's fine. The constraint is on Guide framing, not student choice. Genuine necessity arrives in Mid with T-shapes. This is the module's most non-obvious design decision and directly shapes the Early section's interaction structure (§1.7.1).

2. **Decompose-first as the target workflow for Mid, Late, EC, and Synthesis.** The four-step workflow is: Decompose into rectangles → Find any missing side lengths you need → Find each rectangle's area → Add. Students discover this ordering in Mid (2.1) when they decompose a T-shape and realize a sub-rectangle is missing a dimension. The ordering is deliberate: it keeps the missing-side skill grounded in geometric purpose (students find sides because their decomposition NEEDS them) rather than becoming a mechanical preliminary step. Early uses find-first because the missing side isn't needed for area — the ordering doesn't matter when the skill is standalone. The progression is: find-first (Early, skill standalone) → decompose-first (Mid, skill necessary) → decompose-first consolidated (Late, full independence).

3. **Highlight Related Sides fading: automatic (Early) → guide-controlled (Mid) → remediation-only (Late).** This matches M12's ghost grid fading pattern and is the primary scaffold for missing-side reasoning. In Early, HRS is automatic — the constraint equation with actual values appears immediately after "?" is displayed. In Mid, HRS is reactive — activated only after wrong MC answer. In Late, HRS is remediation-only (guide-activated after wrong MC). EC and Synthesis have no HRS. This fading mirrors the "learn the tool → need the tool → use the tool independently" progression.

4. **No dependency chains — all missing sides independently solvable in M13.** Complexity comes from: (a) double subtraction (total − piece₁ − piece₂ = missing), (b) recognizing whether to add or subtract, and (c) identifying which sides are "related" on multi-edge figures. True dependency chains (where finding one missing side requires another missing side to be found first) are M14 territory. This was an explicit TVP revision — the earlier draft described dependency chains for Mid, but the authoritative version removed them.

5. **T-shapes introduce genuine necessity for the missing-side skill.** In L-shapes (Early), the missing side can always be avoided via an alternative decomposition. T-shapes are the first figure type where no decomposition avoids the missing dimension — the student NEEDS the principle to calculate area. This is why the figure-type progression (L-shapes → T-shapes → mixed L/T/U) is pedagogically significant: it mirrors the conceptual progression from "learning a skill" to "needing a skill." The Mid section's metacognitive beat ("you couldn't have skipped that") makes this distinction explicit.

6. **EC uses APPLY cognitive type (departure from EC Playbook's CREATE/IDENTIFY/COMPARE restriction).** EC.1 and EC.2 both use APPLY — students execute the complete find-missing-side + decompose-calculate-add workflow on new figures. The Playbook restricts EC to CREATE/IDENTIFY/COMPARE, but for M13: CREATE doesn't fit (students aren't constructing a representation from scratch; they're analyzing a figure with missing information and executing a multi-step procedure). COMPARE doesn't apply. APPLY (transfer of a known procedure to new figures) is the pedagogically appropriate cognitive type. EC.3 uses IDENTIFY (resisting a misconception distractor), which conforms to the Playbook. The Playbook's restriction was designed for fraction modules where CREATE (shading bars) and IDENTIFY (recognizing fractions) are natural assessment types; measurement/area workflow modules require APPLY for authentic assessment.

7. **EC.2 figure is an L-variant (stem flush left) rather than a true symmetric T-shape.** The Backbone specifies "T-shape" for EC.2. For the specific dimension ranges and independent-solvability constraints of M13, a symmetric T-shape with 2 missing sides on different axes requires careful figure construction: when the stem is inset from both crossbar sides, the vertical axis may lack a full-height reference edge needed for independent solvability. The stepped figure (base flush left, creating a natural full-height left edge = 10 for vertical "add up") was selected because it provides this reference edge while preserving the spatial complexity the TVP associates with T-shapes: 2 missing sides, step geometry, reasoning about both horizontal and vertical axes. This maintains the M13 constraint that all missing sides are independently solvable.

8. **Directional error check (1.3) positioned after two successful problems, costing ~30 seconds.** The TVP specifies "after 2nd problem" for the directional check. This is proactive misconception prevention — it costs 30 seconds and directly targets the directional component of Misconception #7 before students encounter more complex figures in Mid. The check asks students to evaluate an incorrect reasoning ("Would subtracting a vertical side from a horizontal side work?") rather than solving a problem — this metacognitive framing builds the directional filter before it's needed for T-shapes.

9. **Synthesis uses decompose-first workflow language (AF-1 resolution).** The TVP's main Synthesis section uses find-first language ("find any missing sides, break into rectangles, find each area, add them up"). The TVP's alternate Synthesis and the authoritative Late workflow, framing, and M14 transition all use decompose-first. Resolution: decompose-first in Synthesis. Rationale: (a) Synthesis consolidates what was learned — it must use the same workflow students practiced in Late, (b) decompose-first is the transferable strategy carried into M14, (c) find-first in Synthesis would introduce a workflow ordering students haven't used since Early (where it had a specific pedagogical purpose — standalone skill — that doesn't apply to Synthesis). The four-step capstone: "Decompose into rectangles. Find any missing side lengths you need. Find each rectangle's area. Add them up."

10. **Error cascading guidance is M13's signature Practice challenge.** When a student gets the missing side wrong but then correctly multiplies and adds with the wrong value, the result is a plausible-looking incorrect answer. Standard Practice remediation would address the final wrong answer — but the root cause is the missing-side error, not the arithmetic. Practice guidance must address the ROOT cause: "Your steps were right — decompose, multiply, add. But let's go back and check the missing side. That's where the error started." This is prominently specified in §1.8.5 Practice Inputs and reflects the TVP's explicit guidance on this pattern.

11. **"Related sides" terminology for composite figures; "opposite sides" restricted to warmup single-rectangle context.** The Module Mapping uses "opposite sides" in its Notes, but the Vocabulary Teaching Notes restrict it: "Use 'opposite sides' ONLY when reviewing single-rectangle properties in warm-up context." The TVP consistently uses "related sides" for composite figure reasoning, matching the Highlight Related Sides tool name. The distinction matters: "opposite sides are equal" is a property of single rectangles; "related sides add up" is a property of rectilinear figure edge structures. Conflating them would create misconceptions when students encounter composite figures where "opposite" edges are NOT equal.

12. **M12→M13 bridge: "related sides" shifts from verification to problem-solving.** M12 introduced the "related sides add up" concept informally as a verification tool (students checked their work by confirming segment dimensions sum to full edges). M12's Synthesis closure explicitly bridges: "What if some sides didn't have measurements? Could you use that same idea to figure out the missing ones?" M13 completes this arc — the verification concept becomes a problem-solving tool. The Warmup (W.2) is designed to make this bridge visible: same figure, now with "?", solved using the same principle. This preview-then-formalize pattern matches the unit's established progression ("rows of" M3 → "×" M4; "related sides" M12 → missing-side reasoning M13).

13. **M13→M14 bridge: "Now let's use all of that to design a room!"** The Synthesis closure previews M14's application context using TVP-exact transition language. The bridge language summarizes the M11–M13 arc in one phrase: "You've learned to find area of all kinds of shapes, with grids, without grids, even with missing measurements." This positions M14 as the payoff for three modules of skill-building, not as new learning. The visual teaser (room floor plan with "?" marks) makes the application preview concrete without introducing M14 content.
