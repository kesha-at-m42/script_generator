# **LESSON PHASE PLAYBOOK**

**Version:** January 2026

---

## **1\. PHASE IDENTITY**

**Core Purpose:**  
Main teaching phase where students learn the module's core concept(s) through explicit instruction with visual grounding. This is where formal vocabulary is introduced (after visual experience) and conceptual understanding is built systematically toward the module's learning goal(s).

**Position in Flow:**

* Follows Warmup (engagement/activation phase)  
* Precedes Exit Check (assessment phase)  
* Transition FROM Warmup: "You’ve been growing your understanding of  \[X\]. Let’s build on that knowledge with \[Y\]."  
* Transition TO Exit Check: "You've got the idea. Let's see what you know."

**Typical Duration:**  
 8-12 minutes (approximate—prioritize interaction quality over time limits)

**Interaction Count:**

* **Minimum:** 6 interactions required  
* **Key principle:** Let good teaching determine length, not arbitrary time limits

**Primary Focus:**

* **Explicit instruction** toward the module's learning goal(s)  
* Building from concrete visual experience to formal terminology  
* Worked examples before student attempts  
* Establishing conceptual foundation for Practice phase  
* Introducing vocabulary ONLY AFTER students have visual/experiential grounding

**Energy & Tone:**

* **Energy Level:** Medium (not as high as Warmup, more instructional)  
* **Voice:** Clear, supportive, explicitly teaching  
* **Example language:** "Let's figure this out together..."  
* **Balance:** Brief observation moments WITH immediate direct instruction

**What Makes Lesson Different from Other Phases:**

| Element | Warmup | Lesson | Practice |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Purpose** | Activate curiosity | Teach core concept explicitly | Apply/consolidate |
| **Interactions** | 2+ minimum | 6+ minimum | 8-12+ problems |
| **Vocabulary** | Forbidden to teach | Introduce AFTER grounding | Use established terms |
| **Tools** | Informal exposure | Formal introduction | Application only |
| **Cognitive Load** | Light (20-30%) | Moderate to substantial | Varies by problem |
| **Remediation** | Light only, minimal | Full support available | Full support available |
| **Energy** | Medium-High (inviting) | Medium (instructional) | Medium (brief, supportive) |
| **Assessment** | None | Includes Exit Check at end | Problem-by-problem |
| **Worked Examples** | None | 1-3 required before student attempts | Modeling in Heavy Remediation |

**Maintaining Conceptual Focus:**  
The Module Starter Pack specifies learning goal(s). The Lesson phase must teach toward those goals with clear conceptual focus—avoid scope creep.

Before writing Lesson, verify: **"What is the learning goal from the Module Starter Pack?"**

Then ask: **"Am I teaching toward this goal, or am I adding concepts not in the goal?"**

### **1A. PURPOSE FRAME** \[RECOMMENDED\]

**What It Is:** A brief, concrete statement at the Lesson opening (10-15 seconds) that orients students to what they will learn and why it matters. The Purpose Frame sits between the Warmup bridge and the first Lesson interaction. It is delivered by the Guide with no student action.

**Why It Matters:** Research on teacher clarity (Hattie, d=0.75) and advance organizers (d=0.41) consistently shows that students learn more when they understand what they are learning and why. For 8-9 year olds, this framing reduces extraneous cognitive load by telling students what to pay attention to — rather than asking them to process new content without a frame for what matters.

However, the developmental research is equally clear: 3rd graders cannot reliably use abstract learning objectives to self-monitor or direct their own learning. Metacognitive self-knowledge remains inaccurate until around age 8 (Clark & Dumas, 2016), and metacognitive skills traditionally emerge at 8-10 years (Flavell, 1979). This means purpose-setting for this age group must be concrete and behavioral — never a formal objective statement.

**The Formula:** \[Connection to what they know\] \+ \[What they'll learn to DO\] \+ \[Why it's useful or interesting\]

**Delivery:** Guide only. No student action. 10-15 seconds maximum.

**Good Purpose Frames:**

- "You've been counting equal groups. Now you're going to learn a way to write that as a math sentence — so you don't have to count every time."  
- "You already know how to read picture graphs. Today you'll learn a faster way to show the same information — with less drawing."  
- "You noticed both expressions gave the same answer. Today we're going to figure out WHY — and whether that's always true."  
- "You know how to read bars on the lines. But what happens when a bar stops BETWEEN the lines? Today you'll learn how to figure that out."

**Bad Purpose Frames (avoid):**

- ❌ "Today's learning objective is to interpret scaled bar graphs with a scale of 5." — Formal, abstract, vocabulary-heavy. Adds cognitive load rather than reducing it.  
- ❌ "Today we will learn about the commutative property of multiplication." — Front-loads vocabulary the student hasn't experienced yet.  
- ❌ "By the end of this lesson you will be able to..." — Assessment framing creates performance pressure. Save evaluation framing for Exit Check.  
- ❌ "Today we're going to do some activities with arrays." — Describes activities, not learning. Students need to know what they'll UNDERSTAND, not what they'll DO procedurally.

**Key Constraints:**

- Must use only vocabulary students already know. No new terms. If the concept requires new vocabulary, describe the behavior without the formal name.  
- Must be concrete and behavioral, not abstract.  
- Must connect backward (what they know) AND forward (what they'll gain).  
- Must NOT duplicate what the Warmup bridge already accomplished. If the bridge created a clear question frame (e.g., M12's "Is it always true?"), an additional Purpose Frame may be redundant.

**When to Omit:** The Purpose Frame is recommended, not required. Omit when:

- The Warmup bridge already establishes a clear question or curiosity frame that functions as purpose-setting.  
- The module is a direct continuation of the prior module's skill (e.g., M2 extending M1's graph reading) and the student's sense of purpose carries over naturally.  
- Including it would delay the Lesson opening past the point where the student is ready to engage with content.

When omitting, the Starter Pack author should note the reason in the KDD section (e.g., "Purpose Frame omitted — bridge already establishes the driving question").

**Division of Labor with Warmup Bridge:** The Warmup bridge creates *anticipation* — it makes students want to know what comes next. The Purpose Frame creates *orientation* — it tells students what they'll learn and why it matters. The bridge is emotional (curiosity, excitement). The Purpose Frame is cognitive (advance organizer, attention-directing).

| Element | Function | Example |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Warmup Bridge** | Create anticipation without teaching | "That arrangement has a name, and there's a lot to discover. Let's find out\!" |
| **Purpose Frame** | Orient to what they'll learn and why | "You're going to learn what this arrangement is called, how to describe it two different ways, and how to write math from it." |

**Connection to Subsequent Interactions:** Once the Purpose Frame establishes what students are learning, subsequent Guide dialogue can reference it to create coherence: "Remember, we said you'd learn to read between the lines? Watch how this works..." This creates a thread the student can follow through the Lesson.

## **2\. THE CRA \+ APPLICATION SEQUENCE**

Pedagogical Foundation:  
 Lesson requires 6+ interactions because conceptual understanding develops through the CRA (Concrete-Relational-Abstract) sequence combined with explicit instruction. Students need mental hooks (concrete experiences) before abstract labels can attach—but for 8-9 year olds, these experiences must be **brief, guided, and immediately followed by explicit instruction**.. This developmental progression cannot be rushed.

Instruction comes immediately after (or alongside) observation, not after extended exploration.

The Structure:

```
CONCRETE → RELATIONAL → ABSTRACT → APPLICATION (with explicit instruction woven throughout)
```

1\. Concrete (1-2 interactions)

Structure for Each Concrete Interaction:

1. **Visual Setup** (Guide narrates): "Here's a rectangle bar divided into parts."  
2. **Attention Direction** (5-10 seconds): "Look at how many parts there are."  
3. **Explicit Naming** (immediate): "There are 4 equal parts. That's important—equal means the same size."  
4. **Student Action** (guided): "Now you shade 3 of those 4 parts."  
5. **Explicit Confirmation**: "You shaded 3 out of 4 equal parts. That shows three-fourths."

**Timing:** Each Concrete interaction should be 60-90 seconds maximum. Observation is SECONDS, not minutes.

**What Students DO:**

* Interact with visual through interface (tapping, shading, selecting)  
* Observe Guide demonstrations  
* Replicate what Guide showed

**What Guide DOES:**

* Directs attention to specific features  
* Names what student is seeing/doing IMMEDIATELY  
* Demonstrates before asking student to try  
* Confirms correct understanding explicitly

2\. Relational (2-3 interactions)

**The "Show-Then-State" Pattern:**

1. **Show:** Display two or more related visuals  
2. **Direct:** "Look at \[specific feature\]"  
3. **State:** "Notice how \[explicit pattern statement\]"  
4. **Verify:** Student confirms/applies the stated pattern

3\. Abstract (1-2 interaction) \- If pedagogically appropriate to the material

**Purpose:** Formal vocabulary introduction ONLY after Concrete and Relational complete

**The Vocabulary Introduction Formula:**

1. **Reference experience:** "You just showed 2 out of 4 parts..."  
2. **Introduce term:** "We call that two-fourths, or 2/4..."  
3. **Connect to visual:** "The bottom number shows total parts, the top shows shaded parts..."  
4. **Student applies:** "Now write the fraction for this one."

**Key Principle:** Students have mental hooks from Phases 1-2. Now the vocabulary ATTACHES to those hooks. This is NOT discovery—it's explicit naming of what they already experienced.

4\. Application (2-3 interactions) \- If pedagogically appropriate to the material

**Purpose:** Students apply formal vocabulary to new examples, building toward Exit Check complexity

**Structure:**

* New visual contexts (not just repeats)  
* Increasing independence (less Guide narration)  
* Explicit success confirmation

**This IS the right place for slightly more independence**—students have received instruction and are now applying it.

Required Bookends:

* Opening: Purpose Frame (see §1A) followed by clear, inviting entry into first interaction. The Purpose Frame orients students to what they'll learn and why; the first Concrete interaction then delivers on that promise.  
* Bridge to Exit Check: "You've practiced this concept. Let's see what you know." (10-15 seconds)

Why This Sequence: Front-loading vocabulary creates cognitive overload because students have no mental hooks yet. The CRA sequence prevents overload by building understanding step-by-step: experience first, then pattern recognition, then formal naming, then application.

## **3\. WORKED EXAMPLE REQUIREMENTS**

### **NEW REQUIREMENT: Example-Problem Pairs**

Research shows **example-problem pairs** (worked example immediately followed by similar problem) produce optimal learning for novices.

**Minimum Worked Examples in Lesson Phase: 2-3**

**Worked Example Structure:**

```
⚫ Guide: "Watch how I solve this. First, I count all the parts: 1, 2, 3, 4. 
          That's my denominator—it goes on the bottom. Then I count the shaded 
          parts: 1, 2, 3. That's my numerator—it goes on the top. So this 
          fraction is 3/4."
[Visual: Guide demonstrates with animation/highlighting]

⚫ Guide: "Now you try one just like it."
[Visual: Similar problem appears]
Prompt: "Write the fraction for this shape."
```

**Worked Example Timing:**

* Full worked example: 45-60 seconds of Guide demonstration  
* Immediately followed by: Student problem (similar difficulty)

**Fading Structure (across Lesson):**

1. **Interaction 1-2:** Full worked example → Student tries similar  
2. **Interaction 3-4:** Partial worked example (Guide starts, student completes)  
3. **Interaction 5-6:** Student attempts with minimal support

### **3A. GUIDE THINK-ALOUD PROTOCOL**

**Research Basis:** For 8-9 year olds, metacognition should be modeled by the Guide, not expected from students. Students at this age are good at planning but ineffective at monitoring. The Guide's think-aloud makes invisible thinking visible.

**The Core Pattern: Guide Models, Student Observes**

During worked examples, the Guide narrates their thinking process aloud. This is NOT asking students "what should you do?"—it's SHOWING them what expert thinking sounds like.

**Required Think-Aloud Elements (1-2 per Lesson):**

| Element | Guide Language | Purpose |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Planning** | "First, I ask myself: what do I need to find?" | Model planning behavior |
| **Attention Direction** | "I'm going to look at \[specific element\] first..." | Model strategic focus |
| **Self-Checking** | "Let me make sure—did I count all the parts?" | Model verification |
| **Connection** | "This reminds me of \[previous concept\]..." | Model transfer thinking |

**Think-Aloud Template for Worked Examples:**

```
⚫ Guide (THINK-ALOUD): "Let me show you how I think about this. 
   [PLANNING]: First, I ask myself: 'What do I need to find?'
   [ATTENTION]: I'm going to look at [specific element] first.
   [ACTION]: I count: 1, 2, 3, 4. Four parts total.
   [SELF-CHECK]: Let me make sure I counted right... yes, four.
   [CONCLUSION]: So my denominator is 4."
```

**Frequency:**

4. Minimum 1 think-aloud per Lesson (during first worked example)  
5. Maximum 2 think-alouds per Lesson (avoid cognitive overload)  
6. Always in Concrete or Relational phase, not Abstract

**Tag:** Use `[Guide_Think_Aloud]` to mark these moments

**What This IS vs. ISN'T:**

| ✅ THIS (Guide Models) | ❌ NOT THIS (Expects Student Metacognition) |
| :---- | :---- |
| "First, I ask myself: how many parts?" | "What should you do first?" |
| "I'm checking if the parts are equal..." | "Are you checking your work?" |
| "This reminds me of what we did with halves..." | "What does this remind you of?" |
| "Let me count again to be sure..." | "Did you check your answer?" |

**Why This Matters:** Research shows 8-year-olds can plan but cannot effectively monitor their own thinking. By having the Guide model metacognition explicitly, students observe and gradually internalize these patterns—without the cognitive overload of generating them independently.

## **4\. PHASE-SPECIFIC REQUIREMENTS**

### **4A. VOCABULARY STAGING PRINCIPLE**

**The Critical Rule:**  
 Vocabulary comes AFTER visual/experiential grounding, never before. The Module Starter Pack specifies WHAT vocabulary to introduce and WHEN. Lesson's role is to execute the introduction following explicit instruction principles.

**Explicit Naming After Experience:**

| Step | What Happens |
| ----- | ----- |
| 1 | Student experiences/succeeds with visual |
| 2 | Guide explicitly names what student did |
| 3 | Student applies term to new example |

**Why this matters:**  
 Students need mental hooks (concrete experiences) before abstract labels can attach. Front-loading vocabulary makes terms feel arbitrary and creates cognitive overload.

---

### **4B. TEACHING MOMENT PRINCIPLES**

**The Core Shift: From "Facilitate Discovery" to "Direct and Name"**

| OLD Framing | NEW Framing |
| :---- | :---- |
| "What do you notice?" | "Notice how..." / "See how..." |
| "Can you find the pattern?" | "Here's the pattern: ..." |
| "What's the same about these?" | "These are the same because..." |
| Wait for student insight | Provide insight after brief observation |

**During Concrete & Relational Phases:**

* **Directs attention:** "Look at these two bars—see how the shading reaches the same point?"  
* **Names what student sees:** "That means they show the same amount."  
* **States key insight explicitly:** "Equal parts are the key—when parts are equal size, we can compare fairly."  
* Uses brief observation windows (15-30 seconds) followed by explicit instruction

**During Abstract & Application Phases:**

* **References specific student actions:** "You split the bar into 4 equal parts—equal parts are the key to creating fractions."  
* **Connects concepts explicitly:** "That bottom number shows how many equal parts."  
* **States key concepts clearly:** "Here's what matters..." / "The key is..."  
* Names patterns explicitly and applies them to new examples

**When to Use Brief Observation vs. Direct Instruction:**

| Use Brief Observation (15-30 sec) | Use Direct Instruction (immediate) |
| :---- | :---- |
| Before naming a visual pattern | For formal vocabulary introduction |
| When comparing two representations | For key conceptual distinctions |
| To build anticipation/engagement | For procedural clarification |
| Before student tries what Guide showed | For misconception correction |

**Critical:** Brief observation is ALWAYS followed immediately by explicit instruction. The purpose of observation is attention-focusing, not independent discovery.

---

### **4C. ACTIVE VS. PASSIVE CHECK**

**The Principle:**  
Default to student ACTION—but action follows demonstration, not precedes it.

**The Pattern for Each Interaction:**

1. Guide demonstrates or directs attention (15-45 seconds)  
2. Guide states key insight explicitly  
3. Student acts (applies what was just shown/stated)  
4. Guide confirms or corrects explicitly

**Audit Pattern:**

| If You See... | Flag It | Consider... |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Student attempts before any demonstration | ⚠️ Discovery-first | Add worked example before attempt |
| "What do you notice?" without follow-up instruction | ⚠️ Open discovery | Change to "Notice how..." \+ explicit statement |
| Multiple observations without explicit naming | ⚠️ Delayed instruction | Add immediate naming after observation |
| "Watch me" without follow-up "now you try" | ⚠️ Incomplete | Add student application immediately after |

### **4D. FIRST EXPOSURE VS. SUBSEQUENT**

*(No changes to this section—the existing guidance is sound)*

**First Time a Concept Appears (20-30 words):**

* Concrete before abstract  
* Experience before vocabulary  
* Include the "why"  
* Complete explanation

**Subsequent Times in Same Module (5-10 words):**

* Compressed reminder  
* Assume familiarity  
* Focus on application

**After Module Break/Next Module (10-15 words):**

* Brief re-anchor to prior learning  
* Key concept reminder  
* No full reteaching

### **4E. REAL-WORLD CONTEXT VARIETY**

**The Principle:**  
When Lesson includes real-world application, use 2+ different contexts rather than repeating one.

**Why It Matters:**

- Single context (only pizza) feels artificial and limits transfer  
- Multiple contexts strengthen generalization  
- Variety maintains engagement across interactions

**Context Categories:**

| Category | Examples |
| :---- | :---- |
| Food/Sharing | Pizza, chocolate bars, sandwiches, oranges |
| Measurement | Ribbon, rope, distance, time |
| Collections | Stickers, marbles, cards, coins |
| Activities | Running laps, reading pages, completing tasks |

**Application Rule:**

If Lesson has 2+ real-world application moments:

- [ ] Use different context categories  
- [ ] Vary the specific items (not just "pizza" twice)

**Example:**

Instead of:

- Application 1: Pizza divided into sixths  
- Application 2: Another pizza divided into eighths

Use:

- Application 1: Pizza divided into sixths  
- Application 2: Ribbon cut into eighths

- [ ] Abstract phase introduces vocabulary AFTER experience

## **7\. COMMON PITFALLS TO AVOID**

| ❌ Pitfall | Why It's a Problem | ✅ Fix |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| "What do you notice about these?" \[wait\] | 8-9 year olds lack schema to notice productively | "Notice how \[explicit observation\]. That's because \[reason\]." |
| Extended exploration before instruction | Research shows this HARMS learning for this age | Brief observation (15-30 sec) → immediate instruction |
| Student attempts before seeing example | Creates unproductive cognitive load | Worked example FIRST, then student tries similar |
| "Can you figure out the pattern?" | Productive failure doesn't work at this age | "Here's the pattern: \[explicit statement\]" |
| Vocabulary before experience | Terms feel arbitrary without mental hooks | Visual experience → then naming |
| Waiting for "Aha\!" moment | The "Aha\!" should be guided, not independent | Guide provides the insight explicitly |
| Lesson starts without framing what students will learn |  Students process content without knowing what matters; increases extraneous cognitive load | Add Purpose Frame (§1A): 10-15 sec concrete preview connecting prior knowledge to today's learning |

## **8\. RECONCILING WARM VOICE WITH EXPLICIT INSTRUCTION**

**The concern:** "Won't explicit instruction sound robotic?"

**The answer:** No—explicit instruction can be warm and natural.

| Explicit \+ Cold ❌ | Explicit \+ Warm ✅ |
| :---- | :---- |
| "The denominator indicates the total number of equal parts." | "See that bottom number? That's how many parts total. We call it the denominator." |
| "Observe the equivalence of these fractions." | "Look—these fractions reach the same spot\! They're equal, even though they look different." |
| "The correct answer is 3/4." | "You got it\! Three out of four parts, so 3/4." |

**The warmth comes from:**

- Conversational phrasing ("See that...?" "Look—...")  
- Genuine enthusiasm about mathematical relationships  
- Specific acknowledgment of student actions  
- Natural contractions and rhythm

**The explicitness comes from:**

- Stating the pattern/concept/insight clearly  
- Not waiting for discovery  
- Demonstrating before asking  
- Naming what students are seeing/doing

**These are not in conflict.** An excellent teacher is both warm AND explicit.
