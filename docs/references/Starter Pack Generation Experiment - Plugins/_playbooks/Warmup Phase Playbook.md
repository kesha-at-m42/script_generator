# WARMUP PHASE PLAYBOOK

**Version:** January 2026  
---

## 1\. PHASE IDENTITY

**Core Purpose:**  
Activate prior knowledge, build curiosity, and create emotional readiness for learning—WITHOUT teaching the lesson content.

**Position in Flow:**  
First instructional phase (after optional Start Recap). Bridges student from "outside the lesson" into "ready to learn" state. Feeds directly into Lesson phase with conceptual priming.

**Typical Duration:**

- 2-5 minutes total  
- 2-3 interactions (minimum 2 required)  
- Keep brisk—this is activation, not instruction

**Pedagogical Rationale (SDT Connection):**  
Warmup builds **autonomy** (low-stakes choices), **competence** (accessible success), and **relatedness** (connection to interests) before cognitive demand increases. This emotional safety net enables risk-taking in the Lesson phase.

---

## 2\. BOUNDARIES & CONSTRAINTS

**What This Phase Is NOT:**

- ❌ NOT a teaching phase (save instruction for Lesson)  
- ❌ NOT vocabulary introduction (formal terms come later)  
- ❌ NOT assessment or evaluation (low-stakes exploration only)  
- ❌ NOT complex problem-solving (keep cognitive load 20-30%)  
- ❌ NOT independent practice (Guide is actively involved)

**Forbidden Elements:**

- **Formal vocabulary introduction:** Save terms like "numerator," "denominator," "equivalent" for Lesson after visual grounding  
- **Complex multi-step instructions:** Keep tasks to single actions per interaction  
- **Pressure language:** Avoid "test," "check if you know," "get it right," or time pressure  
- **More than 2 visual states:** Limit to \[CLEAR\] \+ one \[MODIFY\] or \[NEW\] to avoid overwhelming  
- **Tool introduction tags:** Tools appear informally in Warmup without formal introduction

**Why This Matters:** Cognitive load in Warmup must stay at 20-30%. New vocabulary adds 10-15% cognitive load per term. Save formal naming for Lesson after visual grounding creates mental hooks.  
---

### **Phase Boundary Decision Guide**

When drafting Warmup, if you find yourself writing any of the following, STOP and relocate:

| If Writing... | Wrong Phase | Move To |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| New vocabulary introduction | Warmup | Lesson (after visual grounding) |
| Strategy explanation or teaching | Warmup | Lesson |
| Multi-step demonstration | Warmup | Lesson |
| Tool functionality explanation | Warmup | Lesson (just-in-time before first use) |
| Pattern formalization | Warmup | Lesson or Synthesis |
| "Today we will learn..." | Warmup | Lesson opening |

**Warmup CAN Include:**

✅ Callbacks to prior learning ("Remember when you worked with halves?")  
✅ Informal observation of familiar tools (no formal introduction)  
✅ Curiosity-building questions ("What do you notice?")  
✅ Simple prediction tasks ("Which one do you think shows equal parts?")  
✅ Low-stakes choices between familiar options

**The Test:**  
If removing this interaction would leave students unprepared for Lesson → Keep it  
If removing this interaction would just reduce engagement → Keep it  
If this interaction teaches something new → Move to Lesson

**Common Boundary Violations Found in Review:**

| What Happened | Why It's Wrong | The Fix |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Callback \+ formalization in Warmup | Formalization is teaching | Keep callback, move formalization to Lesson |
| Tool demo in Warmup | Tool demos are instruction | Move to Lesson, just-in-time before first use |
| Guide explains pattern before student notices | Teaching before activation | Flip: student notices → Guide names in Lesson |

## 3\. STRUCTURAL REQUIREMENTS

**Interaction Count:**

- **Minimum:** 2 meaningful visual interactions  
- **Typical:** 2-3 interactions  
- **Maximum:** 5 interactions (if exceeding, split content or move to Lesson)

**Required Elements:**

- **Hook** (15-20 seconds): Curiosity gap, mystery, or surprise that pulls students in immediately  
- **2+ Engagement Anchors**: From approved list (see Section 4A)  
- **1+ Judgment/Noticing Task**: "Which one...?" or "What do you notice...?" that requires thinking, not just clicking  
- **Bridge to Lesson** (10-15 seconds): Clear transition that creates anticipation without teaching

**Sequencing Rules:**

1. MUST start with hook (curiosity/engagement element)  
2. Task 1 typically: Recognition/noticing (30-45 sec)  
3. Task 2 typically: Judgment/comparison (30-45 sec)  
4. MUST end with bridge to Lesson (creates anticipation)  
5. Flexible middle: Can add Task 3 if needed for concept priming

**Temporal Reference Rules:**  
Use session-relative language: "last time/this time/next time" NOT "yesterday/today/tomorrow." Students may complete multiple modules per day or spread across weeks.

## 4\. PHASE-SPECIFIC REQUIREMENTS 

### **4A. ENGAGEMENT ANCHORS** \[REQUIRED: 2+ per Warmup\]

Must include at least 2 of these 4 types:

**1\. Narrative Setup**  
Real-world or metaphorical introduction that contextualizes the math.  
*Example:* "Imagine you're a chef dividing a cake into equal slices..."

**2\. Personalization**  
Reference to student interests, prior work, or choices.  
*Example:* "Remember when you worked with halves? Let's explore thirds now..."

**3\. Choice/Agency**  
Explicit or implicit decision point for the student.  
*Example:* "Which one would you like to explore first—the circle or the rectangle?"

**4\. Strength Prompt**  
Student chooses their approach or focus mode.  
*Example:* "Choose your superpower: Speed Detective or Pattern Finder?"

**Selection Principle:** Choose anchors that **serve the concept**, not just engagement.

**Quality Check \- Does This Anchor Prime Mathematical Thinking?**

✅ **SERVES THE CONCEPT:**

- Choice between two fractions to compare → Student must think about size/comparison  
- "Remember when you worked with halves?" → Activates prior knowledge needed for thirds  
- "Notice what's the same and different" → Focuses attention on mathematical attributes

❌ **ENGAGEMENT THEATER (Doesn't serve concept):**

- "Pick your favorite color\!" → Arbitrary choice, doesn't activate math thinking  
- "Imagine you're a superhero\!" → Fun but disconnected from fraction concepts  
- "Would you like circles or squares?" → Choice for choice's sake if shape doesn't matter

**Test:** If you removed this anchor, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson? If no → reconsider the anchor.

---

### **4B. VOCABULARY STAGING RULES** \[CRITICAL\]

**FORBIDDEN:**

- ❌ Introducing new mathematical terms formally  
- ❌ Using technical language before visual experience  
- ❌ Defining or explaining terminology  
- ❌ "This is called a \[fraction/numerator/denominator\]"

**PERMITTED:**

- ✅ Informal, everyday language ("parts," "pieces," "shaded")  
- ✅ Acknowledging terms if student uses them spontaneously: "Yes, that's the numerator\!"  
- ✅ Using terms students already know from prior modules

**Why:** Vocabulary adds 10-15% cognitive load per term. Warmup must stay at 20-30% total load.

---

### **4C. COGNITIVE LOAD TARGET** \[20-30%\]

**What 20-30% Feels Like:**

- Quick wins (student succeeds easily)  
- Familiar concepts (building on prior knowledge)  
- Simple tasks (one action per interaction)  
- Low stakes (no "right/wrong" emphasis)  
- High interest (connected to student's world)

**Load Reduction Strategies:**

- Use visual support for every interaction  
- Keep instructions to \<15 words  
- Allow errors to pass without heavy remediation  
- Focus on observation over production

**Contrast with Other Phases:**

- Lesson: 60-70% load | Practice: 30-60% load | Exit Check: 40-50% load

### **4D. WARMUP TYPE SELECTOR** \[CORE DECISION POINT\]

Choose the structure that best primes for your Lesson concept. Each type includes everything you need: structure, hooks, contexts, and usage guidance.

**HOOK QUALITY GUIDE**

Every warmup needs a hook in the first 15-20 seconds. But not all hooks are equally effective. Here's how to distinguish weak from strong execution:

**Hook Type: Curiosity Gap**

❌ **Weak:** "Let's explore something interesting about shapes today."  
*Why it fails:* Vague, no actual gap, "interesting" is generic

✅ **Strong:** "These two rectangles look identical, but only ONE has equal parts. Can you spot which?"  
*Why it works:* Specific mystery, creates need to investigate, immediate visual puzzle

**Hook Type: Mystery Setup**

❌ **Weak:** "We have a mystery to solve about fractions."  
*Why it fails:* Announces mystery without creating it

✅ **Strong:** "This fraction says it equals one-half. Do you believe it? Let's investigate."  
*Why it works:* Concrete claim to verify, invites skepticism, frames student as detective

**Hook Type: Real-World Connection**

❌ **Weak:** "Today we'll look at shapes like you see in the real world."  
*Why it fails:* Generic connection, no authentic context

✅ **Strong:** "When you share a pizza with friends, how do you make sure everyone gets the same amount?"  
*Why it works:* Specific scenario students know, poses genuine problem

**Quality Test:** Does your hook create a question in the student's mind within 5 seconds? If not → strengthen it.

---

#### **1\. BINARY CHOICE**

**Best for:** Quick comparison, attribute focus, clear concept activation

**Structure:**

- Show two examples side-by-side  
- "Which one shows \[property\]?"  
- Fast, clear, focused

**Primary Hook:** Direct Surprise or Real-World Connection  
**Also works with:** Curiosity Gap, Challenge/Quest

**Best Contexts:** Fractions (equal vs unequal parts), shapes (attributes), any binary property

**Example:**  
"Which shows equal parts—A or B?" \[Two rectangles: one with equal parts, one without\]

**When to use:**

- ✅ Module 1-3 (concrete, simple activation)  
- ✅ Need fastest warmup structure  
- ✅ Clear binary concept to preview

---

#### **2\. WHICH ONE DOESN'T BELONG (WODB)**

**Best for:** Reducing anxiety, attribute comparison, multiple entry points

**Structure:**

- Show 4 mathematical items  
- All 4 have valid "doesn't belong" reasons  
- Students defend choice with reasoning  
- Low-stakes, no wrong answer

**Primary Hook:** Curiosity Gap  
**Also works with:** Real-World Connection, Mystery Setup

**Best Contexts:** Fractions, shape attributes, arrays, number lines

**Example:**  
Four different ways to show 3/4—"Which one doesn't belong? Why?"

**When to use:**

- ✅ Need low-anxiety entry point  
- ✅ Students have diverse reasoning approaches  
- ✅ Multiple attributes to compare  
- ✅ Module 4-7 (building complexity)

---

#### **3\. NUMBER TALK**

**Best for:** Mental math strategies, number sense, operations

**Structure:**

- Present computation problem  
- Explicit 30-second think time  
- Collect 2-4 solution strategies  
- Emphasize relationships between strategies

**Primary Hook:** Challenge/Quest  
**Also works with:** Direct Surprise, any hook

**Best Contexts:** Works standalone as pure expressions (no heavy context needed)

**Example:**  
"What's 25 × 4? Take 30 seconds to find your way..."

**When to use:**

- ✅ Building mental math fluency  
- ✅ Module 8-12 (abstract thinking ready)  
- ✅ Want strategy diversity focus  
- ✅ No context needed

---

#### **4\. NOTICE & WONDER**

**Best for:** Open exploration, question generation, pattern discovery

**Structure:**

- Present mathematical image/scenario  
- "What do you notice? What do you wonder?"  
- Multiple valid observations  
- Builds curiosity and questioning

**Primary Hook:** Curiosity Gap or Mystery Setup  
**Also works with:** Real-World Connection

**Best Contexts:** Design projects, measurement, real-world photos, patterns

**Example:**  
Image of pattern: "What do you notice?" \[Students observe before analyzing\]

**When to use:**

- ✅ Complex concept needing deep priming  
- ✅ Visual-rich content  
- ✅ Open exploration before structured learning  
- ✅ Module 4-7

---

#### **5\. ESTIMATION 180**

**Best for:** Real-world connection, reasonableness, measurement

**Structure:**

- Show real-world image/scenario  
- Ask for estimation with reasoning  
- Provide think time  
- Reveal answer and discuss

**Primary Hook:** Real-World Connection  
**Also works with:** Challenge/Quest

**Best Contexts:** Measurement, shopping, large quantities, spatial reasoning

**Example:**  
"How many paper clips would fit across this desk?"

**When to use:**

- ✅ Need authentic context  
- ✅ Measurement or quantity focus  
- ✅ Building reasonableness skills

---

#### **6\. VISUAL PATTERNS**

**Best for:** Algebraic thinking, prediction, sequences

**Structure:**

- Show growing/changing pattern  
- Students predict next steps  
- Connect visual to numerical/symbolic

**Primary Hook:** Mystery Setup or Curiosity Gap  
**Also works with:** Challenge/Quest

**Best Contexts:** Geometric patterns, number sequences, tiling, arrays

**Example:**  
"Step 1 has 3 tiles, Step 2 has 5 tiles—what's Step 4?"

**When to use:**

- ✅ Pattern recognition important for Lesson  
- ✅ Module 6-12  
- ✅ Algebraic thinking preview

---

#### **7\. MYSTERY REVEAL**

**Best for:** Building suspense, discovery, counterintuitive concepts

**Structure:**

- Show partial information  
- Students guess/predict  
- Reveal full picture  
- Connect prediction to reality

**Primary Hook:** Mystery Setup or Discrepant Event  
**Also works with:** Direct Surprise

**Best Contexts:** Hidden properties, equivalence, unexpected relationships

**Example:**  
Covered shape: "How many parts?" \[Uncover to reveal\]

**When to use:**

- ✅ Counterintuitive concept coming in Lesson  
- ✅ Need high engagement  
- ✅ Building toward "aha\!" moment

---

#### **OPTIONAL: Narrative Enhancement**

**First, understand: All Warmups use warm, enthusiastic Guide voice.** This is NOT optional. The Guide is always:

- Curious and inviting: "Let's explore..." "Check this out..."  
- Responsive to student: "You noticed..." "Interesting choice..."  
- Enthusiastic about math: "Ready to discover..." "Here's something cool..."

**This baseline warmth is covered in the Guide Design Prompt—it's standard, not "narrative enhancement."**

**"Narrative Enhancement" means adding character roles or storylines BEYOND baseline Guide warmth:**

Most Warmups work fine with standard warm Guide voice. Add narrative enhancement ONLY when it serves the mathematical concept:

**Character-Driven Options:**

- Friendly Guide/Mascot (enthusiastic helper)  
- Kid Detective (co-investigator frame)  
- Embodied Concept (character IS the math idea)

**Scenario-Driven Options:**

- Real-World Investigation (authentic contexts)  
- Game/Competition (challenge frame)  
- Mystery/Puzzle (hidden discovery)  
- Mini-Adventure (brief quest structure)

**Use sparingly:** Only when narrative adds pedagogical value, not just flavor.

---

**BRIDGE TO LESSON QUALITY GUIDE**

The final 10-15 seconds must create genuine anticipation for the Lesson, not just announce what's next.

❌ **Weak Bridges (Announcement Style):**

- "You're ready to learn how we name these equal parts\!" *(just states what's next)*  
- "Now let's move to the lesson." *(transition only, no anticipation)*  
- "Good job\! Time to learn about fractions." *(praise \+ announcement)*

✅ **Strong Bridges (Anticipation Style):**

- "You spotted the equal parts\! But here's the cool thing—mathematicians have a special way to write exactly how many parts there are. Ready to learn the secret code?" *(builds on success \+ creates intrigue)*  
- "You noticed the pattern\! Let's discover why that happens every time." *(acknowledges observation \+ promises explanation)*  
- "Interesting—you found three different ways to show the same amount. Want to learn what we call fractions like these?" *(validates thinking \+ poses question)*

**Quality Test:** Does your bridge make the student *curious* about what comes next? Or does it *tell them* what's next? The bridge should create a question in the student's mind — the Purpose Frame at the Lesson opening answers it.

### **4E. GRADE 3 CONTEXT EXAMPLES** \[REFERENCE\]

Choose contexts appropriate for Grade 3\. Module Starter Pack provides specific constraints.

**Common Grade 3 Contexts:**

- Equal Grouping/Sharing (fruit, school supplies, socks)  
- Shopping/Cost (realistic prices, relatable budgets)  
- Measurement (length, volume, natural items)  
- Design Projects (tiny houses, gardens, playgrounds)  
- Data Collection (surveys, bar graphs, line plots)  
- Games & Recreation (sports teams, board games)  
- Nature & Science (seedlings, specimens, growth)

**Grade 3 Constraints:**

- Operations within 100, extending to 1000 for place value  
- Multiplication/Division: Single digit × single digit  
- Avoid contrived scenarios (47 watermelons) or adult-only contexts

**Conceptual Coherence Check (Keep Short):**

Ask: **"Does this context illuminate WHY the math matters?"**

✅ **Coherent:** Equivalent fractions → Pizza slices (different cuts, same amount eaten)  
*Why:* Context shows WHY equivalence exists in real life

❌ **Decorative:** Equivalent fractions → Shopping for pencils  
*Why:* Context is grade-appropriate but doesn't deepen understanding of equivalence

**Quick test:** If the math concept disappeared, would the context still make sense? If yes → might be decorative only.

### **4F. SUPPORTING ELEMENTS** \[REFERENCE\]

**Transitions (use sparingly in Warmup):**

- Between tasks: Conceptual Bridge, Attention Reset, Confidence Building  
- To Lesson: Always use Confidence Building ("You're ready to learn...")

**Student Agency Moments (0-1 per Warmup):**

- Strategy Sharing: "Did you count or estimate?"  
- Confidence Check: "Ready for a trickier one?"  
- Creates *feeling* of choice without actual branching

**Metacognitive Prompts (0-1 per Warmup, light only):**

- "What did you notice first?" (observation awareness)  
- "Why did you choose that one?" (decision awareness)  
- NOT deep reflection (save for Synthesis)

**Visual Specifications:**

- Maximum 2 visual states: \[CLEAR\] \+ one \[MODIFY\] or \[NEW\]  
- Simple arrangements (avoid busy screens)  
- Exact quantities: "Three bars" not "some bars"  
- Clear reset between tasks

### **4G. WARMUP FLOW TEMPLATE**

```
OPENING (15-20 sec)
├─ Hook: "Let's explore something interesting..." [curiosity]
├─ Engagement Anchor 1: [Narrative OR Choice OR Personalization]
└─ Visual: [CLEAR] Initial display

TASK 1 - Recognition/Noticing (30-45 sec)
├─ Engagement Anchor 2: [Different type from Anchor 1]
├─ Prompt: "Which one shows...?" OR "What do you notice?"
├─ [Student interaction]
└─ Guide: "You noticed [observation]..." [acknowledge without teaching]

TASK 2 - Judgment/Comparison (30-45 sec)
├─ Visual: [MODIFY] OR [NEW] for comparison
├─ Prompt: "Can you find another?" OR "Which is different?"
├─ [Student interaction]
└─ Guide: "Interesting thinking..." [maintain engagement]

BRIDGE TO LESSON (10-15 sec)
├─ Guide: "You're ready to learn how to [preview concept]!"
└─ Creates anticipation without teaching
```

**Bridge vs. Purpose Frame — Division of Labor:** The Warmup bridge creates *anticipation* — it makes students curious about what's coming without specifying the learning content. Purpose-setting (what students will learn and why) is the Lesson's job, handled by the Purpose Frame at the Lesson opening. The bridge makes students *want to know*; the Purpose Frame tells them *what they'll get*. Avoid combining both functions in the bridge, as this risks crossing into teaching territory. See Lesson Phase Playbook §1A.

## 5\. VOICE GUIDELINES

**Voice Adjustments for Warmup:**

- **Energy:** Medium-High (most enthusiastic of all phases)  
- **Tone:** Inviting, curious, playful—"Let's explore\!" not "Today we will learn..."  
- **Language:** Informal—"Check this out\!" not "Observe the following..."  
- **Enthusiasm:** React with delight—"Interesting choice\!" "You noticed that\!"

**Error Handling in Warmup:**

Warmup is low-stakes exploration. The bias should be toward **validating participation and moving forward** rather than correcting errors.

**General approach:**

- **WODB/Notice & Wonder:** ALL answers are valid—these structures have multiple correct responses  
- **Binary Choice/Estimation:** If student makes unusual choice, gentle redirect OR validate reasoning and continue  
- **Number Talk:** All strategies are valid—collect and continue

**Warmup philosophy:** Errors are exploration, not failure. Maintain emotional safety and forward momentum.

*Note: Detailed remediation guidance is in Script Reference Library. In Warmup, use minimally or not at all.*

## 6\. CRITICAL SUCCESS CHECKS

**Must-Haves:**

- [ ] Hook appears in first 15-20 seconds  
- [ ] 2+ engagement anchors from approved list  
- [ ] 2+ meaningful visual interactions (not just clicking "next")  
- [ ] 1+ judgment/noticing task requiring thought  
- [ ] Zero formal vocabulary introduced  
- [ ] Maximum 2 visual states used  
- [ ] Clear bridge to Lesson at end  
- [ ] Total time stays under 5 minutes  
- [ ] Cognitive load feels light (20-30%)

**Red Flags:**

| ⚠️ Warning Sign | Fix |
| :---- | :---- |
| Vocabulary front-loading | Remove all formal terms; use informal language |
| Complex instructions (\>15 words) | Break into simpler single-action tasks |
| Pressure language ("test," "check") | Reframe as exploration: "Let's see...," "Try..." |
| Teaching content | Save explanations for Lesson; just observe in Warmup |
| More than 3 tasks | Cut to 2-3 interactions maximum |

```
   └─ Run through Must-Haves and Red Flags

```

**Quick Decision Support:**

- **Module 1-3?** → Binary Choice or Notice & Wonder  
- **Module 4-7?** → WODB or Estimation  
- **Module 8-12?** → Number Talk or Visual Pattern  
- **Need low anxiety?** → WODB  
- **Need quick activation?** → Binary Choice  
- **Need deep priming?** → Notice & Wonder or Mystery Reveal
