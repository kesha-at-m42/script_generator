## 1.0 THE ONE THING

Students must understand that unit fractions (1/b) represent exactly ONE equal part of a whole, and that the bottom number tells us the SIZE of that part—more parts means smaller pieces. The fundamental insight is that the two numbers in a fraction are deeply connected: the bottom number determines the size of each piece, and the top number (always 1 in unit fractions) counts how many of those pieces.

**CRA Stage:** Representational (Building on concrete partitioning from Module 1, students now use visual models to understand fraction notation and relationships)

**Critical Misconception:** Numerator/Denominator as Independent (#3)—Students may see the two numbers in a fraction as unrelated values rather than understanding that the bottom number equals the size of parts and the top number equals the count of parts

**Success Indicator:** Student can identify unit fractions, explain that "the top number is always 1 because we're showing one part," demonstrate understanding that larger bottom numbers mean smaller pieces (1/8 < 1/4 < 1/2), and recognize that the same fraction looks different on different shapes but represents the same quantity

**Biggest Risk:** Students memorizing fraction labels without understanding the relationship between the numbers, or believing "bigger number = bigger piece" due to whole-number thinking carryover

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

### 1.4.1 Numerator/Denominator as Independent (#3) — PRIMARY

**Trigger Behavior:** Student treats the numbers in a fraction as unrelated. When asked "What does 1/4 mean?", they might say "one and four" rather than "one out of four equal parts" or "one-fourth." They don't connect the bottom number to the SIZE of the piece. May struggle to explain why 1/4 ≠ 4/1 beyond just "it's different."

**Why It Happens:** Students have learned to read multi-digit numbers where each digit has independent place value. A fraction LOOKS like two numbers side by side, so they apply whole-number thinking. Without explicit instruction connecting the numbers to a visual and to each other, the relationship remains invisible.

**Visual Cue:** Grid array or circle showing the whole partitioned into equal parts with one shaded. Guide physically connects: "See these 4 equal parts? That's your bottom number—it tells us each piece is a FOURTH. See ONE part shaded? That's your top number."

**Path C Intervention:**
* Always establish total parts FIRST (bottom number = size of pieces)
* Then count shaded parts (top number = how many of those pieces)
* Use consistent language: "one part out of ___ equal parts"
* Reinforce that bottom number names the piece: "4 on bottom means fourths"
* Addressed in 1.2 and reinforced throughout Section 1

---

### 1.4.2 Reversing Numerator and Denominator (#6) — SECONDARY

**Trigger Behavior:** Student writes or identifies fractions with numbers swapped. When 1 part of 4 is shaded, they might select 4/1 instead of 1/4. May also read "1/4" incorrectly as "four-one."

**Why It Happens:** "Total" feels more important than "part," so they put it first. Confusion about which number "goes where." Reading top-to-bottom doesn't match fraction logic for them.

**Visual Cue:** Grid array with clear visual distinction: shaded part prominent, total grid structure clear. Consistent pattern: "Shaded on top, total on bottom."

**Path C Intervention:**
* Teach "Shaded Over Total" as memorable pattern
* Count total parts first (establish what goes on bottom)
* Then identify shaded (what goes on top)
* In unit fractions, emphasize: "Top is always 1 because we shade just one part"
* Addressed in 1.2 and reinforced in 1.6

---

### 1.4.3 Bigger Number = Bigger Piece (#8 variant) — SECONDARY

**Trigger Behavior:** Student believes 1/8 is bigger than 1/4 because "8 is bigger than 4." When comparing unit fraction sizes, they select the one with the larger bottom number as "bigger."

**Why It Happens:** Whole-number thinking: in whole numbers, 8 > 4 always means "more." Students haven't yet internalized that in fractions, bigger denominators mean MORE pieces, therefore SMALLER pieces.

**Visual Cue:** Side-by-side bars of same size, one cut into 4 parts, one cut into 8 parts. Both with one part shaded. Visual makes size difference obvious. Students need to see the visual BEFORE hearing the rule.

**Path C Intervention:**
* Use same-size wholes for all comparisons (non-negotiable)
* Explicitly teach: "More cuts = smaller pieces"
* Real-world analogy: "Same pizza, cut into 4 slices vs. 8 slices—which slice is bigger?"
* Have students physically or visually compare shaded areas, not just numbers
* Addressed extensively in Section 3 (3.1 through 3.5)

### Warmup Type Rationale

By asking students to identify (rather than generate), the warmup primes their visual memory of what they learned in M1, creating a foundation for the lesson's transition from recognition to understanding *meaning*.

### Interaction W.1: Equal Parts Recognition [ACTIVATION]

* **Purpose:** Activate Module 1 knowledge of equal parts and fraction recognition. Hook with real-world connection that previews today's learning goal.

* **Visual: Grid Arrays.** Four pre-partitioned grids displayed side by side, each with exactly one part shaded:
* Grid A: 2 equal parts (2×1 tall), 1 shaded (shows 1/2)
* Grid B: 3 equal parts (3×1 tall), 1 shaded (shows 1/3)
* Grid C: 4 equal parts (2×2), 1 shaded (shows 1/4)
* Grid D: 4 equal parts (2×2), 2 shaded (shows 2/4) ← single distractor

* **Guide:** "Think about sharing a pizza with friends. Everyone wants the same size slice, right? That's what fractions are all about: equal parts. You learned about this last time. Look at these four rectangles. Three of them show just ONE part shaded. Which one shows MORE than one part shaded?"

* **Prompt:** "Which rectangle shows more than one part shaded?"

* **Student Action:** Click on Grid D (the one with two parts shaded).

* **Correct Answer:** Grid D

* **Answer Rationale:**
- Grid A, B, C: Each shows exactly one part shaded out of different totals (2, 3, 4). These are all unit fractions, though students don't know that term yet.
- Grid D: Two parts shaded out of four. This is the only grid where more than one part is shaded. Student who counts shaded parts correctly will find this immediately.

* **On Correct:** "Grid D has two parts shaded. The other three each have just one part shaded."

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Design Note:** Simplified from original 4-grid design (which showed 2/4 and 3/4 as distractors). Showing multiple non-unit fractions before "unit fraction" is defined creates unnecessary noise. This version keeps one distractor (2/4) while the other three naturally preview the unit fraction pattern (all have 1 part shaded) without explicitly teaching it. The implicit pattern primes students for the Lesson's formal introduction.

---

### Interaction W.2: Fraction Notation Matching [ACTIVATION]

* **Purpose:** Confirm students can connect notation to visuals (M1 skill). Bridge from recognition to the "what do the numbers mean?" question.

* **Visual: Grid Arrays.** Three pre-partitioned and shaded grids without labels, displayed alongside fraction symbols:
* Grid A: 1×2 wide grid, 1 shaded (represents 1/2)
* Grid B: 1×3 wide grid, 1 shaded (represents 1/3)
* Grid C: 1×4 wide grid, 1 shaded (represents 1/4)
* Fraction symbols displayed prominently: "1/2", "1/3", "1/4"

* **Guide:** "You learned to match fractions to pictures last time. Here are three rectangles and three fractions. Can you match the fraction 1/4 to the rectangle that shows it?"

* **Prompt:** "Which rectangle shows 1/4?"

* **Student Action:** Click on Grid C (the one divided into 4 equal parts with 1 shaded).

* **Correct Answer:** Grid C

* **On Correct:** "Correct. The bottom number tells you how many equal parts. Four equal parts, so the bottom number is 4."

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Remediation Note:** If student selects wrong grid, prompt them to count the parts: "How many equal parts do you see in each rectangle?"

* **Design Note:** This interaction primes students for the lesson's focus on "what the numbers mean" by drawing attention to the bottom number's connection to the number of parts.

## 1.7 LESSON (12–16 minutes)

### Core Purpose + Pedagogical Flow

Students develop understanding that unit fractions (1/b) represent exactly ONE equal part of a whole, that the bottom number tells us the size of parts, and that more parts means smaller pieces. The lesson moves through four increasingly abstract sections, each designed to prevent and address specific misconceptions:

1. **Section 1 (Concrete-Representational):** Unit fraction vocabulary grounded in grids. Explicit connection between visual and notation. Establishes the "shaded over total" pattern and introduces the term "unit fraction."
2. **Section 2 (Representational-Bridge):** Extends understanding to hexagons (familiar), then introduces circles (new). Bridge interaction makes explicit that the same fraction looks different on different shapes.
3. **Section 3 (Representational):** Develops the inverse relationship through direct visual comparison. Multiple same-shape comparisons prevent the "bigger number = bigger piece" misconception using real-world analogy.
4. **Section 4 (Abstract-Consolidation):** Transfers learning across all shapes. Gallery view shows pattern recognition. Formal definition of unit fraction emerges from student observation.

All sections use pre-partitioned shapes so students can focus on *meaning* rather than partitioning technique (learned in M1).

---

### Lesson Structure

| Phase | Duration | CRA Stage | Focus |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Section 1: Unit Fraction Vocabulary (Grids)** | 3–4 min | Concrete-Representational | Grids + explicit notation connection; establish "one part out of total" pattern |
| **Section 2: Hexagon Bridge → Circle Introduction** | 3–4 min | Representational-Bridge | Familiar shape bridge; [TOY_INTRO] for circles; cross-shape confirmation |
| **Section 3: Size Patterns (Inverse Relationship)** | 3–4 min | Representational-Abstract | Same-size comparisons; visual evidence before verbal rule; real-world analogy |
| **Section 4: Consolidation** | 2–3 min | Abstract | Gallery view; pattern articulation; consolidation across shapes |

---

### Required Phrases

Students MUST be able to say/understand these phrases by end of lesson. Teachers should use them multiple times, and students should hear them in varied contexts:

* "The BOTTOM number tells us how many equal parts make the whole" — enables students to decode fraction notation
* "The TOP number tells us how many parts are shaded" — enables students to connect visual to symbol
* "Unit means one—so a unit fraction always has 1 on top" — defines the key characteristic of unit fractions
* "More parts means smaller pieces" or "Bigger bottom number means smaller pieces" — articulates the inverse relationship with both linguistic and visual evidence
* All five fraction names used in full context: "one-half," "one-third," "one-fourth," "one-sixth," "one-eighth" — builds automaticity with fraction naming

---

### Forbidden Phrases

Language to AVOID during lesson that would reinforce misconceptions or confuse conceptual development:

❌ **"Today we will learn..."** or **"Today you're going to learn..."** — Non-session-relative language creates performance pressure. Use "Let's discover..." or "Let's practice..."

❌ **"Yesterday"** or **"Tomorrow"** — Non-session-relative. Use "Last time" or "Next time"

❌ **"Perfect!"** or **"Excellent!"** or **"Amazing!"** or **"Great job!"** — Generic overpraise. Use observable acknowledgments: "You matched them all" or "One part shaded—that's what we're looking for"

❌ **"You carefully..."** or **"You understood..."** or **"You're thinking..."** — Assumed internal states. Use observable descriptions: "You shaded one part" or "You clicked on the right grid"

❌ **"You have to..."** or **"You need to..."** — Controlling language. Use invitational: "Try..." or "Can you...?" or "Let's..."

❌ **"Numerator"** or **"Denominator"** — Out of scope until Module 3. Use "top number" and "bottom number"

❌ **"These fractions are equal/equivalent"** — Equivalence is Module 8. Use "same fraction" or "same value"

❌ **"Greater than"** or **"Less than"** with comparison symbols — Save for Module 10. Use "bigger" and "smaller" when comparing fraction sizes

❌ **"Close enough"** for equal parts — Undermines equality requirement. Emphasize exact equality in all partitions

❌ Comparison across different-sized shapes — Keep comparisons within shape type (circles to circles, grids to grids)

---

### Purpose Frame

Teacher opens lesson with this frame. No student action during frame—teacher sets context and relevance.

* **Guide:** "In our last lesson, you learned to partition shapes into equal parts and name them with fractions. Today, we're going to look deeper at what the numbers in a fraction actually mean. You're going to discover why the top number and bottom number are connected to each other, and why some fractions are bigger than others. By the end of today, you'll be able to look at any unit fraction and explain exactly what it means."

---

### 1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Unit Fraction Vocabulary (Grids)

In this section, students explicitly connect visual models to fraction notation for the first time. The key moves are: (1) establish total parts first (bottom number), (2) count shaded parts (top number), (3) introduce the term "unit fraction" when top number is always 1. All interactions use grids, a familiar toy from M1.

---

### Interaction 1.1: Establishing the Whole + First Shade (Halves) [WORKED EXAMPLE]

* **Purpose:** Model the full process of shading one part and connecting it to notation. Establish the "count total first, then shaded" routine.

* **Visual: Grid Arrays.** Pre-partitioned grid array showing 2 equal parts (2×1 tall rectangle with one horizontal cut), unshaded, with clear part boundaries. Notation "1/2" displayed to the right.

* **Guide:** "Here's a rectangle divided into 2 equal parts. I'm going to shade just ONE of the two parts. Watch me." [Teacher shades one part.]

* **Guide:** "Now let me connect what we see to the fraction: 1/2. The bottom number is 2, because the whole rectangle is cut into 2 equal parts. The top number is 1, because we shaded 1 part."

* **Student Action:** No student action (worked example, teacher demonstrates).

* **Design Note:** This is a pure teaching moment. No clicking or interaction from student. Teacher models the exact mental sequence students will repeat in subsequent interactions.

* **Voice Note:** Contractions and clear chunking ("Here's a rectangle..." then "I'm going to..." then connection phrase) keep language natural and paced.

---

### Interaction 1.2: Introduce "Unit Fraction" + Notation [INSTRUCTION]

* **Purpose:** Formally introduce the term "UNIT FRACTION" and make explicit the connection between visual and notation. Includes a quick confirmation click to break consecutive passive moments.

* **Visual: Grid Arrays.** Same grid from 1.1 with one part shaded; fraction "1/2" displayed prominently beside the grid with top and bottom numbers clearly labeled.

* **Guide:** "Look at what we just shaded. We shaded exactly ONE part out of two equal parts. We call this a UNIT FRACTION. 'Unit' means one, so a unit fraction always has 1 on top because we're showing just one part shaded."

* **Guide:** "The BOTTOM number tells us how many equal parts make the whole. You see 2 equal parts, so the bottom number is 2. The TOP number tells us how many parts are shaded. We shaded 1 part, so the top number is 1."

* **Prompt:** "Click on the number that tells us how many equal parts make the whole."

* **Student Action:** Click on the bottom number (2) in the fraction display.

* **Correct Answer:** The bottom number (2)

* **On Correct:** "The bottom number, 2. That tells us the whole is cut into 2 equal parts."

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Remediation Note:** If student clicks the top number, redirect: "That's the top number. It tells us how many parts are shaded. The BOTTOM number tells us how many equal parts make the whole."

**Vocabulary:** Introduce "unit fraction," "top number," "bottom number" formally with visual reference. Use ALL CAPS on first mention.

* **Design Note:** Originally a pure teaching moment; the confirmation click was added to break the 1.1→1.2 consecutive passive sequence. The click is low-stakes (single identification) but ensures students are actively processing the top/bottom distinction rather than passively hearing it.

---

### Interaction 1.3: Thirds on Grid [GUIDED PRACTICE]

* **Purpose:** Students apply the "count total first, then shaded" routine introduced in 1.2. Reinforce unit fraction pattern with a new denominator.

* **Visual: Grid Arrays.** New pre-partitioned grid array showing 3 equal parts (3×1 tall), unshaded, with clear part boundaries.

* **Guide:** "Here's a rectangle divided into 3 equal parts. Can you shade just ONE part to show a unit fraction?"

* **Prompt:** "Shade just ONE part to show a unit fraction."

* **Student Action:** Click on one cell/section to shade it.

* **Correct Answer:** Any single section is shaded (1 of 3 parts).

* **On Correct:** "Yes. One part out of three equal parts. That's one-third. The top number is 1, and the bottom number is 3."

* **Answer Rationale:** Shading exactly one section demonstrates understanding of "unit" = one.

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Remediation Note:** If student shades more than one part, say "Try again. A unit fraction has just 1 on top—shade just ONE part."

* **Connection:** "The pattern is the same: count the total parts first (that's your bottom number), then count the shaded parts (that's your top number)."

* **Design Note:** This is guided practice on familiar grid toy. Students should complete this easily; if they don't, review 1.1 and 1.2 before proceeding.

---

### Interaction 1.4: Fourths on Grid [GUIDED PRACTICE]

* **Purpose:** Extend the pattern to a new denominator (4). Prepare for size comparisons later in lesson.

* **Visual: Grid Arrays.** New pre-partitioned grid array showing 4 equal parts (2×2 configuration), unshaded.

* **Guide:** "Now we have 4 equal parts. Let's shade one part to make a unit fraction."

* **Prompt:** "Shade ONE part to show one-fourth."

* **Student Action:** Click on one cell to shade it.

* **Correct Answer:** Any single cell is shaded (1 of 4 parts).

* **On Correct:** "One out of four equal parts: 1/4. The top number is 1, the bottom number is 4."

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Design Note:** Seamless transition to a new grid configuration. Students should see that the routine doesn't change even though the shape of the grid does.

---

### Interaction 1.5: Pattern Recognition [IDENTIFY]

* **Purpose:** Students recognize the defining feature of unit fractions: "top number is always 1." Consolidate pattern across multiple denominators at once.

* **Visual: Grid Arrays.** Three grids side by side, each with one part shaded and labeled:
* 2×1 tall grid showing 1/2 (with notation label "1/2" visible)
* 3×1 tall grid showing 1/3 (with notation label "1/3" visible)
* 2×2 tall grid showing 1/4 (with notation label "1/4" visible)

* **Guide:** "Look at all three unit fractions side by side. What's the SAME in all three fractions? Is it the top number, the bottom number, or something else?"

* **Prompt:** "What's the same in all three unit fractions?"

* **Options:**
* "Top number"
* "Bottom number"
* "The shape they're drawn on"

* **Student Action:** Click on the correct option.

* **Correct Answer:** "Top number"

* **On Correct:** "The top number is always 1 in unit fractions. That's what makes them unit fractions: we always shade exactly ONE part."

* **Answer Rationale:**
- "Top number": Correct. The invariant feature across all unit fractions. Recognizing this builds deep understanding.
- "Bottom number": Distractor. Bottom numbers vary (2, 3, 4). Student who selects this is attending to the denominators rather than the defining feature.
- "The shape they're drawn on": Distractor. All three happen to be on grids, but this is incidental, not the mathematical pattern. Student who selects this is attending to surface features rather than the fraction structure.

* **Connection:** "The bottom number is different each time. It tells us the SIZE of each part: halves, thirds, or fourths. More parts means smaller pieces."

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Remediation Note:** If student selects "bottom number," say "Look again. Which numbers are the same? The 1s on top or the numbers on the bottom (2, 3, 4)?"

* **Design Note:** This is a critical conceptual question. It crystallizes the pattern students have been working with. The answer should feel obvious to them by this point.

---

### Interaction 1.6: Notation Matching [INDEPENDENT PRACTICE]

* **Purpose:** Students match fraction symbols to visual models with no guidance. Test their ability to read the fraction and find the correct representation.

* **Visual: Grid Arrays.** Three pre-partitioned and shaded grids WITHOUT labels, displayed alongside three fraction symbols to match:
* 1×2 wide grid showing 1/2
* 1×3 wide grid showing 1/3
* 2×2 tall grid showing 1/4

Fraction symbols displayed prominently: "1/2", "1/3", "1/4"

* **Guide:** "Now let's practice reading these fractions. Match each fraction symbol to its visual model."

* **Prompt:** "Match each fraction to its grid. You can drag or click to connect them."

* **Student Action:** Drag-to-match or click-to-connect each fraction to its corresponding grid.

* **Correct Answer:** 1/2 → 1×2 grid; 1/3 → 1×3 grid; 1/4 → 2×2 grid

* **On Correct:** "You matched them all correctly. When you see 1/4, look for a grid divided into 4 equal parts with one shaded."

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Remediation Note:** If student struggles, prompt: "Look at the bottom number first. How many parts should the grid have?"

* **Design Note:** This is a fluency builder on Section 1 concepts. Students should feel confident and ready to move to Section 2.

---

→ **SECTION 1 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 2.**

### 1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Hexagon Bridge → Circle Introduction

In this section, students see the same unit fractions on different shapes. The key pedagogical move is: (1) activate hexagon knowledge from M1 (bridge), (2) introduce circles formally with [TOY_INTRO], (3) show that the same fraction notation represents the same quantity regardless of shape. This combats the misconception that fractions are shape-specific.

---

### Interaction 2.1: Hexagon Recognition (Bridge) [GUIDED PRACTICE]

* **Purpose:** Activate hexagon knowledge from Module 1. Reinforce unit fraction pattern on a shape students already know.

* **Visual: Hexagons.** Pre-partitioned hexagon showing 3 equal parts (thirds), unshaded.

* **Guide:** "We practiced with hexagons in our last lesson. This hexagon is divided into 3 equal parts. Can you shade one part to show a unit fraction?"

* **Prompt:** "Shade ONE part to show one-third on this hexagon."

* **Student Action:** Click on one section of the hexagon to shade it.

* **Correct Answer:** Any single section shaded (1 of 3 parts).

* **On Correct:** "Yes. One out of three equal parts: 1/3, just like on the rectangles."

* **Remediation:** Pipeline (light)

* **Remediation Note:** Not needed; this is familiar toy from M1

* **Design Note:** This is a bridge interaction. It activates prior knowledge with minimal cognitive load, preparing students for the new toy introduction that follows.

---

### Interaction 2.2: Bridge Insight [INSTRUCTION]

* **Purpose:** Make explicit the connection: same fraction, different shapes.

* **Visual: Hexagons and Grid Arrays.** Hexagon from 2.1 (shaded 1/3) displayed alongside the thirds grid from 1.3. Both clearly labeled "1/3" or shown side by side to emphasize similarity.

* **Guide:** "Look at the hexagon and the rectangle side by side. This is 1/3 of the hexagon and this is 1/3 of the rectangle. Both show 1/3: one part out of three equal parts. Same fraction, different shapes."

* **Student Action:** No student action (observation moment).

* **Design Note:** Teacher draws the connection explicitly so students don't have to infer it. This prepares them for the cognitive jump to circles.

---

### Interaction 2.3: Circle Introduction [TOY_INTRO]

* **Purpose:** Formally introduce circles as a new shape for partitioning and shading. Explicitly connect to hexagon radial cutting experience from M1.

* **Visual: Circles.** An unpartitioned circle with center point visible and radial guide lines faintly shown (suggesting where cuts can be made).

* **Guide:** "Here's a new shape: a circle. See the center point? Just like with hexagons, we can make cuts from the center to create equal parts. We will use cuts from the center to make equal parts on circles too."

* **Prompt:** "Let's partition this circle into 4 equal parts. Make cuts from the center to create equal sections."

* **Student Action:** Click or drag to make radial cuts creating 4 equal sections. (Or system auto-partitions on student's instruction.)

* **Correct Answer:** 4 equal sections created via radial cuts from center.

* **On Correct:** "You made 4 equal parts on the circle, just like you did with hexagons. Circles work the same way."

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Remediation Note:** If student creates unequal parts, say "Let's try again. Use the center point and make equal sections."

* **Design Note:** This is the formal [TOY_INTRO] for circles. It explicitly connects to M1 hexagon learning, reducing cognitive load of learning a new shape. Once circles are introduced here, all subsequent circle interactions can assume students know how to interact with them.

* **Voice Note:** "Just like with hexagons" is key language that bridges prior knowledge.

---

### Interaction 2.4: First Circle Shade [GUIDED PRACTICE]

* **Purpose:** Apply unit fraction shading to circles. Extend the "count total, then shade one" pattern to the new shape.

* **Visual: Circles.** Pre-partitioned circle showing 3 equal parts (thirds, created in previous interaction or displayed fresh), unshaded.

* **Guide:** "Now, can you shade one part to show 1/3 on this circle?"

* **Prompt:** "Shade ONE part to show one-third."

* **Student Action:** Click on one section of the circle to shade it.

* **Correct Answer:** Any single section shaded (1 of 3 parts).

* **On Correct:** "Yes. One out of three equal parts: 1/3. Same fraction, different shape!"

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Design Note:** This is straightforward application of the established pattern. If students struggle, the pattern itself needs review; they may not be ready to move to different shapes yet.

---

### Interaction 2.5: Circle with Fourths [GUIDED PRACTICE]

* **Purpose:** Show that circles support more denominators than hexagons (specifically 1/4, which hexagons cannot partition into due to geometry).

* **Visual: Circles.** Pre-partitioned circle showing 4 equal parts, unshaded.

* **Guide:** "Here's a circle with 4 equal parts. Please shade one part to show one-fourth."

* **Prompt:** "Shade ONE part to show 1/4."

* **Student Action:** Click on one section to shade it.

* **Correct Answer:** Any single section shaded (1 of 4 parts).

* **On Correct:** "Yes. 1/4 of a circle. We can make fourths on a circle, but remember, we can't make fourths on a hexagon because of the hexagon's shape. Circles are more flexible."

* **Design Note:** This highlights a constraint of different shapes while reinforcing the concept that unit fractions work across shapes.

---

### Interaction 2.6: Cross-Shape Confirmation [INSTRUCTION]

* **Purpose:** Consolidate understanding that the same unit fraction looks different on different shapes but represents the same quantity. Prepare for size comparisons in Section 3.

* **Visual: Grid Arrays and Circles.** Two shapes side by side, each showing 1/4 with one part shaded:
* 2×2 tall grid (4 parts, 1 shaded) labeled "1/4"
* Circle with 4 equal sections (1 shaded) labeled "1/4"

Both shapes are the same overall size/area to support valid visual comparison.

* **Guide:** "Here is a rectangle and a circle. They're different shapes, but they both show 1/4. One part out of four equal parts. This is one-fourth of the rectangle and this is one-fourth of the circle."

* **Guide:** "The bottom number tells us the size of the parts. The top number tells us how many are shaded. That pattern works on ANY shape: grids, circles, hexagons, any shape at all."

* **Student Action:** No student action (consolidation moment).

* **Design Note:** This is a key generalization point. Students should leave this interaction understanding that the fraction notation is shape-independent.

---

→ **SECTION 2 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 3.**

### 1.7.3 LESSON SECTION 3: Size Patterns (Inverse Relationship)

In this section, students discover and articulate the inverse relationship: bigger bottom number = smaller pieces. The pedagogy is: (1) show visual evidence first with same-sized shapes, (2) ask students to compare before stating the rule, (3) use real-world analogy to make sense of the pattern, (4) have students articulate the pattern themselves. This section is critical for preventing Misconception #8 (bigger number = bigger piece).

---

### Interaction 3.1: Compare Halves and Fourths (Circles) [IDENTIFY]

* **Purpose:** First explicit size comparison using same-sized circles. Visual evidence precedes verbal rule.

* **Visual: Circles.** Two identical-sized circles side by side:
* Circle A: 2 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/2)
* Circle B: 4 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/4)

Both circles have the same diameter. Shaded parts are visually prominent. Notation labels visible.

* **Guide:** "Let's look at these two circles. They're the same size, but divided differently. Which fraction model shows a bigger shaded part?"

* **Prompt:** "Click on the model with the BIGGER shaded part."

* **Student Action:** Click on Circle A (the half).

* **Correct Answer:** Circle A (the half)

* **On Correct:** "Right. The half is bigger than the fourth. When you cut the whole into MORE pieces, each piece gets SMALLER. Two pieces means bigger parts. Four pieces means smaller parts."

* **Answer Rationale:** Visual comparison makes this concrete. Students can see the shaded area and measure with their eyes.

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Remediation Note:** If student selects Circle B, say "Look at the shaded parts side by side. Which one looks bigger?" Then pause and let them observe.

* **Design Note:** This is the first comparison question. It should feel visually obvious. If it doesn't, students may not be ready for the pattern articulation in later interactions.

---

### Interaction 3.2: Compare Thirds and Sixths (Circles) [IDENTIFY]

* **Purpose:** Extend the pattern to a different pair of unit fractions. Reinforce that more cuts = smaller pieces with a new example.

* **Visual: Circles.** Two identical-sized circles side by side:
* Circle A: 3 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/3)
* Circle B: 6 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/6)

Both circles same diameter. Shaded parts clearly visible. Notation labels visible.

* **Guide:** "These circles also show unit fractions. Both circles are the same size. Which one has the BIGGER shaded part?"

* **Prompt:** "Click on the model that shows the BIGGER shaded part."

* **Student Action:** Click on Circle A (the third).

* **Correct Answer:** Circle A (the third)

* **On Correct:** "Correct. The third is bigger than the sixth. 1/3 is bigger than 1/6. More cuts means smaller pieces."

* **Answer Rationale:** The visual size relationship should be clear. The sixth is half the size of the third.

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Design Note:** This second comparison should reinforce the pattern without requiring new explanation. Students should start internalizing the rule.

---

### Interaction 3.3: Compare Thirds and Fourths (Circles) [IDENTIFY]

* **Purpose:** Test student understanding with a less obvious pair. Thirds and fourths are harder to distinguish visually than previous pairs, requiring students to rely on understanding (not just visual estimation).

* **Visual: Circles.** Two identical-sized circles side by side:
* Circle A: 3 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/3)
* Circle B: 4 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/4)

Both circles same diameter. Shaded parts clearly visible. Notation labels visible.

* **Guide:** "Let's try another comparison. Which is bigger: one-third or one-fourth?"

* **Prompt:** "Which shaded part is bigger?"

* **Student Action:** Click on Circle A (the third).

* **Correct Answer:** Circle A (the third)

* **On Correct:** "Right. 1/3 is bigger than 1/4. Three parts means bigger pieces than four parts. More parts = smaller pieces."

* **Answer Rationale:** Students must now use their developing understanding, not just gross visual size differences.

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Design Note:** This is a transition question—not obvious visually, but understandable through the pattern they've been building. If students struggle, return to 3.1 or 3.2.

---

### Interaction 3.4: Real-World Connection [GUIDED PRACTICE]

* **Purpose:** Connect the abstract inverse relationship to a concrete, familiar real-world scenario. Make the pattern tangible and meaningful.

* **Visual: Circles.** Two identical circles styled as cakes:
* Cake A: Cut into 4 slices, 1 slice highlighted or separated
* Cake B: Cut into 8 slices, 1 slice highlighted or separated

Both cakes same size. Real-world visual styling (frosting, texture, etc.) increases engagement.

* **Guide:** "Think about this like cake at a birthday party. Two cakes exactly the same size, but one is cut into 4 slices and one is cut into 8 slices."

* **Guide:** "If you get one slice from each cake, which slice is bigger?"

* **Prompt:** "Click on the bigger slice."

* **Student Action:** Click on the slice from Cake A (the one cut into 4 slices).

* **Correct Answer:** Cake A (the slice from the 4-slice cake)

* **On Correct:** "The slice from the cake cut into 4 is bigger than the slice from the cake cut into 8. 1/4 is bigger than 1/8. Fewer parts means bigger pieces."

* **Answer Rationale:** Real-world context makes the abstract relationship concrete and memorable.

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Connection:** "This is the same pattern we've been seeing: more parts in the whole, smaller each part is."

* **Design Note:** The real-world cake scenario is powerful for cementing understanding. Students can relate to it experientially. Use it liberally in discussion.

---

### Interaction 3.5: Pattern Articulation [IDENTIFY]

* **Purpose:** Students articulate the pattern themselves. Test their ability to state the inverse relationship verbally or select it from options.

* **Visual: Circles.** All five unit fractions on same-sized circles, arranged left to right from largest piece to smallest piece:
* Circle showing 1/2 (largest shaded part)
* Circle showing 1/3
* Circle showing 1/4
* Circle showing 1/6
* Circle showing 1/8 (smallest shaded part)

All circles same diameter. Shaded sections clearly visible. Arranged in visual order showing size progression.

* **Guide:** "Let's look at all five unit fractions in order: 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8. Look at the shaded parts as you go from left to right. As the bottom number gets bigger, what happens to the shaded pieces?"

* **Prompt:** "As the bottom number gets bigger, what happens to the shaded pieces?"

* **Options:**
* "They get bigger"
* "They get smaller"
* "They stay the same size"

* **Student Action:** Click on the correct option.

* **Correct Answer:** "They get smaller"

* **On Correct:** "Smaller. That's one of the most important patterns in fractions: a BIGGER BOTTOM NUMBER means SMALLER pieces. 1/2 > 1/3 > 1/4 > 1/6 > 1/8. More parts, smaller each piece."

* **Answer Rationale:** This is the core insight of the inverse relationship. The visual gallery makes it undeniable.

* **Remediation:** Pipeline

* **Remediation Note:** If student selects "bigger," say "Look at the circles. As the bottom number goes up (2, 3, 4, 6, 8), look at how big the pink parts are. Are they getting bigger or smaller?"

* **Design Note:** This question crystallizes the entire Section 3 learning. By this point, students should know the answer. If they don't, the section needs to be revisited before proceeding.

---

→ **SECTION 3 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 4.**

### 1.7.4 LESSON SECTION 4: Consolidation

In this section, students consolidate learning across all shapes and all unit fractions. The key moves are: (1) show all shapes together in gallery view, (2) remind students of key patterns (unit fractions have 1 on top, bottom number tells size), (3) exit the lesson ready for independent application in the exit check.

---

### Interaction 4.1: Transfer to Grids (Gallery Transition) [INSTRUCTION]

* **Purpose:** Show that the size pattern works on grids just as it does on circles. Consolidate across shapes.

* **Visual: Circles and Grid Arrays.** Display same five circles from 3.5 (1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8) showing size progression. Then transition to display five grids showing the same fractions:
* 1×2 wide grid showing 1/2
* 1×3 wide grid showing 1/3
* 2×2 tall grid showing 1/4
* 2×3 tall grid showing 1/6
* 4×2 tall grid showing 1/8

All grids same overall size. Shaded sections clearly visible. Arranged to parallel the circle gallery.

* **Guide:** "We've practiced this pattern with circles, and it works exactly the same with rectangles."

* **Guide:** [After visual transition] "The same five fractions follow the same pattern on grids. The bigger the bottom number, the smaller the pieces. It works on ANY shape."

* **Student Action:** No student action (observation/consolidation moment).

* **Design Note:** This is a powerful visual consolidation. Seeing the same pattern across two different shape types cements the understanding.

---

### Interaction 4.2: Final Check [IDENTIFY]

* **Purpose:** Test students' ability to identify what unit fractions have in common. Synthesize the module's core vocabulary and concept.

* **Visual: Grid Arrays, Circles, and Hexagons.** Three unit fractions displayed, one of each shape:
* Circle showing 1/4 (4 equal parts, 1 shaded)
* Hexagon showing 1/3 (3 equal parts, 1 shaded)
* 2×3 tall grid showing 1/6 (6 equal parts, 1 shaded)

All shapes clearly labeled with fraction notation (1/4, 1/3, 1/6).

* **Guide:** "Now let's look at these three UNIT FRACTIONS on different shapes. What do they ALL have in common?"

* **Prompt:** "Why are all three of these fractions called UNIT FRACTIONS?"

* **Options:**
* "They all have 1 on top"
* "They all have the same bottom number"
* "They're all the same size"

* **Student Action:** Click on the correct option.

* **Correct Answer:** "They all have 1 on top"

* **On Correct:** "Right. They all have 1 on top. That's what makes them unit fractions. One part shaded, no matter the shape."

* **Answer Rationale:** The defining characteristic of unit fractions is that the numerator (top) is always 1.

* **Remediation:** Pipeline (light)

* **Design Note:** This is the final conceptual anchor of the lesson. Students should leave this interaction able to define unit fractions by their most important feature.

---

### Transition to Exit Check

* **Guide:** "We've practiced identifying what unit fractions are and why bigger bottom numbers mean smaller pieces. You're ready to show what you know. Let's move to the next part."

### Misconception Prevention

This section maps each primary and secondary misconception from §1.4 to specific lesson interactions where it is addressed:

**#3 (Numerator/Denominator as Independent):**
* Interaction 1.2 explicitly connects both numbers to visual elements
* Consistent "count total first (bottom), then shaded (top)" routine throughout Section 1 (1.3, 1.4)
* Pattern recognition in 1.5 reinforces the connection between the numbers and the visual
* 4.2 returns to this definition at module's end

**#6 (Reversing Numerator and Denominator):**
* "Shaded Over Total" pattern established in 1.2 and reinforced in 1.6
* Repeated emphasis in Section 1: "Top is always 1 in unit fractions"
* Section 4 gallery view (4.1) visually reinforces pattern across multiple examples
* 4.2 final check anchors the invariant feature (1 on top)

**#8 variant (Bigger Number = Bigger Piece):**
* Section 3 devoted entirely to inverse relationship (3.1 through 3.5)
* Multiple comparisons with same-sized wholes to ensure visual evidence
* Real-world cake analogy in 3.4 makes the pattern tangible and memorable
* Explicit pattern articulation in 3.5 with both verbal and visual evidence
* 4.1 reinforces pattern across grids

### Success Criteria

**The One Thing:** Students understand that unit fractions (1/b) represent exactly ONE equal part, and that the bottom number tells us the SIZE of that part—more parts means smaller pieces.

**Observable Behaviors by End of Lesson:**
* ✅ Student can identify a unit fraction and explain that the top is always 1
* ✅ Student can match fraction notation to visual models with accuracy
* ✅ Student can compare two unit fractions on same-sized shapes and state which is bigger
* ✅ Student can articulate the pattern: "More parts means smaller pieces" or equivalent
* ✅ Student can recognize the same fraction on different shapes and explain they represent the same quantity
* ✅ Student uses all five fraction names with accuracy (one-half, one-third, one-fourth, one-sixth, one-eighth)

**Ready for Module 3:** Student can identify and explain unit fractions, is prepared for composing non-unit fractions (e.g., 3/4 = three copies of 1/4), and can transition to formal numerator/denominator terminology.

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS (KDD)

### Purpose

This section documents the non-obvious choices that hold Module 2 together. These are design trade-offs, pedagogical decisions, or constraint choices that deserve explanation so readers unfamiliar with the module can understand the "why" behind structural choices. Each KDD identifies an alternative that was considered and rejected, and explains how the chosen path supports the learning goal and misconception prevention.

---

### KDD-1: Why Circles Are Introduced in Module 2 (Not Deferred to Module 3)

**Decision:** Introduce circles as a new shape in Module 2's Section 2, immediately after establishing unit fraction vocabulary on grids, rather than waiting until Module 3.

**Alternative Considered:** Delay circles to Module 3, spending all of Modules 1-2 on grids and hexagons only, then add circles as new representational complexity in M3.

**Why This Path Wins:**
Introducing circles in M2 serves two critical pedagogical functions: (1) It combats the misconception that fractions are shape-specific. If students learn unit fractions only on grids in M1 and hexagons in M2, they may over-generalize that "1/3 is the hexagon picture" rather than understanding that 1/3 is an abstract quantity. By showing the same unit fractions on circles, we force students to separate the notation from any single visual. (2) Circles are essential for later modules (M4-M5 with number lines, M6 with operations). Building comfort with circles now prevents a shape-change shock later. The hexagon bridge in 2.1 makes the circle transition smooth—students activate familiar radial-cutting knowledge before encountering a new shape.

**How This Supports Learning Goals and Misconception Prevention:**
* Supports L1 ("understand a fraction as a quantity") by showing multiple representations of the same quantity
* Prevents Misconception #8 (bigger number = bigger piece) by widening the representation set—students see the inverse relationship holds across shapes
* Builds foundational spatial flexibility that supports later modules

---

### KDD-2: Why "Unit Fraction" and "Top/Bottom Number" Are Introduced Before "Numerator/Denominator"

**Decision:** In Module 2, formalize vocabulary as "unit fraction," "top number," and "bottom number." Defer formal "numerator" and "denominator" terminology to Module 3.

**Alternative Considered:** Introduce all terminology (unit fraction, numerator, denominator) in Module 2 to maximize vocabulary coverage.

**Why This Path Wins:**
Students need to understand what the numbers DO before learning what they're CALLED. Module 2's focus is on meaning: "The bottom number tells us the size of the parts" and "The top number tells us how many parts." These conceptual anchors must come before adding the formal names. "Numerator" and "denominator" are abstract Latin-rooted terms that add cognitive load without adding conceptual depth in M2. In Module 3, when students compose non-unit fractions (building 3/4 from three 1/4s), they'll be ready to learn formal terminology because they'll have rich visual and concrete understanding of what's being named.

**How This Supports Learning Goals and Misconception Prevention:**
* Supports L1 by grounding understanding in observable visual connections before introducing abstract terminology
* Prevents Misconception #3 (numerator/denominator as independent) by establishing deep, observable relationships first, then naming them
* Follows the Informal-Before-Formal Principle embedded in the Module 2 design

---

### KDD-3: Why the Hexagon Bridge (Section 2, 2.1) Precedes Circle Introduction

**Decision:** In Section 2, have students shade one part of a hexagon BEFORE introducing circles, using hexagon shading as a bridge to the circle introduction that immediately follows.

**Alternative Considered:** Skip the hexagon bridge entirely, moving directly from grids to circles in Section 2, saving hexagons for practice only.

**Why This Path Wins:**
The hexagon bridge leverages the power of familiar-to-new scaffolding. Students completed Module 1 with confident hexagon-partitioning skills. By asking them to shade a unit fraction on a hexagon (2.1), we activate that confidence and create a smooth cognitive pathway: "You know how to work with hexagons. Circles work the same way—radial cuts from the center." Without this bridge, jumping straight from grids to circles feels like a bigger conceptual leap. The familiarity of hexagons buys cognitive space for learning about circles. Additionally, the bridge moment (2.2) explicitly connects the two shapes, cementing the insight that different shapes = same fraction.

**How This Supports Learning Goals and Misconception Prevention:**
* Reduces cognitive load during the new-shape introduction, allowing students to focus on unit fraction meaning rather than shape mechanics
* Supports L1 by showing unit fractions on three different shapes, building abstract understanding
* Prevents distraction from the core learning goal (fraction meaning) by using familiar tools

---

### KDD-4: Why All Size Comparisons in Section 3 Use Identically-Sized Wholes

**Decision:** In Section 3 (Size Patterns), every size-comparison interaction uses shapes that are identical in overall size and area. For example, when comparing 1/2 and 1/4, both circles have the same diameter.

**Alternative Considered:** Vary the whole sizes to show that comparison is possible at different scales (e.g., compare a small circle showing 1/2 to a large circle showing 1/4).

**Why This Path Wins:**
The Same Whole Principle is non-negotiable for preventing a dangerous misconception: if you compare 1/4 of a small shape to 1/2 of a large shape, you might conclude that 1/4 > 1/2 in absolute terms. This fractures understanding. By fixing the whole size, we isolate the variable: ONLY the number of parts changes. The visual difference in piece size becomes unambiguous. Students can trust their eyes: "More parts = smaller pieces" is not a rule to memorize but an observation they can verify visually. In later modules (M8 onwards, when students study equivalent fractions and comparing fractions), they'll revisit different-sized wholes deliberately. For now, keeping wholes identical is pedagogically necessary.

**How This Supports Learning Goals and Misconception Prevention:**
* Supports L2 by making the inverse relationship visually obvious, requiring no inferencing
* Directly prevents Misconception #8 (bigger number = bigger piece) by removing the confounding variable of whole size
* Builds robust understanding that generalizes later

### KDD-5: Why the Inverse Relationship (Section 3) Gets Its Own Dedicated Section

**Decision:** Devote an entire lesson section (Section 3, ~4 minutes and 5 interactions) exclusively to the inverse relationship. Don't assume students will pick up the pattern as an afterthought.

**Alternative Considered:** Mention the pattern once or twice during Section 1 (grids) and move on to Section 4 (consolidation), trusting students to generalize from limited exposure.

**Why This Path Wins:**
The inverse relationship—that bigger bottom numbers mean smaller pieces—contradicts whole-number intuition ("8 > 4 always"). This is a counterintuitive concept that deserves dedicated instructional time and multiple examples. One mention is insufficient. By devoting Section 3 to ONLY this pattern (five interactions, four different comparisons, one real-world analogy, one explicit pattern articulation), we give students multiple opportunities to (1) observe the pattern visually, (2) apply the pattern in guided practice, (3) articulate the pattern themselves, (4) relate it to real-world experience. This high-engagement, focused approach is what prevents the misconception from taking root. Additionally, Section 3 serves as a conceptual anchor for Module 3's work (comparing non-unit fractions) and all future comparison work.

**How This Supports Learning Goals and Misconception Prevention:**
* Supports L2 ("understand the denominator represents the number of equal parts") by unpacking the logical consequence: more parts = smaller each part
* Directly targets Misconception #8 with repeated visual evidence, real-world grounding, and student articulation
* Builds confidence for later comparison tasks
