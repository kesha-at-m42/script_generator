## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**When fractions have the SAME denominator, you're comparing same-sized pieces—so just compare how many pieces. More pieces \= greater fraction. This works ONLY when denominators match.**

**Critical Misconception:** \#10 (Overgeneralization)—Students discover that 3/4 \> 2/4 because "3 \> 2" and incorrectly conclude that comparing numerators always works. They may later claim 3/8 \> 2/4 because "3 \> 2." The "same denominator" condition must be explicit and repeated.

**Success Indicator:** Student compares 3/6 and 5/6 by stating "both are sixths, so I'm comparing same-sized pieces—5 pieces is more than 3 pieces, so 5/6 \> 3/6." When asked "Could you compare 3/6 and 3/8 this way?" student says "No—different denominators means different-sized pieces."

**Biggest Risk:** Rushing to symbolic comparison (3 \> 2, therefore 3/4 \> 2/4) without grounding in the "same-sized pieces" reasoning. If students learn the shortcut without the conceptual foundation, they will overgeneralize and fail in M11 when denominators differ.

## **1.1 LEARNING GOALS**

*Verbatim — Script Must Achieve These*

**L1:** "Compare fractions with the same denominator using \>, \<, \= and justify with visual models."

**Module Goal (Student-Facing):**  
"You discovered that fractions at the same position are equal. Now you'll learn to compare fractions at DIFFERENT positions—and you'll discover a powerful shortcut that works when denominators match."

**Exit Check Tests:**

* Can compare two fractions with same denominator and write correct symbol (\>, \<, \=)  
* Can justify comparison with "same-sized pieces" reasoning  
* Can order 3-4 fractions with same denominator from least to greatest  
* Can identify which fractions CAN be compared using this method (same denominator) vs. which cannot

### **1.1.2 Module Bridges**

**From Module 9 (Equivalent Fractions Abstract):**  
Students understand that fractions at the SAME position on a number line are equivalent. They can identify equivalent fractions abstractly (without visuals) and recognize fractions equal to whole numbers. The key M9 insight—"same position \= same value"—becomes the foundation for M10's extension: "DIFFERENT position \= different value \= one is greater."

**This Module:**  
Students discover that when denominators match, comparison is simple: same-sized pieces means just count which has more. They learn the symbols \>, \<, \= as notation for relationships they can SEE and REASON about. Critically, students learn the CONDITION for this shortcut—it only works when denominators are the same. By end of lesson, students can compare abstractly (without visuals) BECAUSE they understand WHY the shortcut works.

**To Module 11 (Compare Same Numerators):**  
Students will encounter the opposite case: fractions with the SAME numerator but DIFFERENT denominators. The M10 shortcut ("just compare numerators") will FAIL—forcing students to reason about piece SIZE. M11 teaches the counterintuitive insight that larger denominators mean smaller pieces, so 3/4 \> 3/8. M10's explicit "same denominator" constraint prepares students to recognize WHEN they need different reasoning.

**Conceptual Progression (3.NF.A.3.d Arc):**

* **Module 10 (This Module):** Same DENOMINATOR → same-sized pieces → compare numerators (more pieces \= greater)  
* **Module 11:** Same NUMERATOR → same number of pieces → compare piece size (bigger pieces \= greater; larger denominator \= smaller pieces)  
* **Module 12:** Unlike both → benchmark strategies (compare each to 1/2, etc.)

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

* Same denominator \= same-sized pieces → compare by counting pieces  
* More pieces of same size \= greater fraction; fewer \= lesser; same \= equal  
* Comparison symbols: \>, \<, \= with visual justification  
* Ordering 3-4 fractions with same denominator  
* Recognizing WHEN this strategy applies (same denominator only)  
* Vocabulary: compare, greater than, less than, equal to, same denominator

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* Comparing fractions with DIFFERENT denominators (Module 11-12)  
* Same-numerator reasoning ("3/4 vs 3/8") — Module 11  
* Benchmark fractions (comparing to 1/2) — Module 12  
* Cross-multiplication or common denominators — Grade 4  
* Fractions greater than 1 in comparisons  
* Adding or subtracting fractions — Unit 6

⚠️ **OVERGENERALIZATION TRAP:** When students see 3/4 \> 2/4 because "3 \> 2," they may conclude this works universally. ALWAYS pair the shortcut with its condition: "When denominators are the same..." If a student claims 5/8 \> 3/4 because "5 \> 3," redirect: "Are these the same denominator?"

⚠️ **SAME WHOLE PRINCIPLE (per 3.NF.A.3.d):** Comparisons are valid only when fractions refer to the same whole. If comparing visually, shapes must be identical.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Overgeneralizing Rules (\#10) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student compares 3/8 and 2/4 by saying "3 \> 2, so 3/8 \> 2/4." Or student states the rule as "bigger top number \= bigger fraction" without mentioning denominators.

**Why It Happens:**  
The same-denominator shortcut is powerful and feels like a universal rule. Students who learn "compare numerators" without understanding the CONDITION will apply it everywhere. This is especially likely if we rush past the "same-sized pieces" reasoning.

#### **Path C Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Show two fractions with DIFFERENT denominators (e.g., 3/8 and 2/4) on a number line or grid. Ask: "Can we compare these the same way?" Make visible that 2/4 is actually GREATER than 3/8 despite 3 \> 2\.

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. Repeat the condition constantly: "When denominators are the same..."  
2. Include "trap" problems in Practice: "Can you use today's strategy to compare 3/8 and 2/4?" (Answer: No—different denominators)  
3. Synthesis explicitly asks: "When does this shortcut work? When does it NOT work?"

---

### **1.4.2 Comparison Errors (\#8) — SECONDARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student selects wrong symbol (e.g., 2/6 \> 5/6) or confuses symbol orientation. May say "2/6 is greater because 2 comes first."

**Why It Happens:**  
Symbol orientation is arbitrary and requires memorization. Students may also confuse "greater" with position (left vs. right) or order of presentation.

#### **Path C Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Use the "hungry alligator" or "open mouth faces the bigger one" mnemonic sparingly. Better: always show the comparison visually first, then add the symbol. "The open side of the symbol points to the greater fraction."

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. Introduce one symbol at a time (\> first, then \<, then \=)  
2. Always ground symbol in visual: "We can SEE that 5/6 is more. We write 5/6 \> 2/6."  
3. Practice reading both directions: "5/6 \> 2/6" AND "2/6 \< 5/6"

---

### **1.4.3 Losing the Reasoning (Symbol Without Understanding)**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student can produce correct symbols but cannot explain WHY. When asked "How do you know 5/8 \> 3/8?" student says "Because 5 is bigger" without mentioning same-sized pieces.

**Why It Happens:**  
Students learned the shortcut without the conceptual foundation. This is a failure of instruction (rushed to abstract) rather than student misconception.

#### **Path C Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Return to grid or number line: "Show me why 5/8 is greater." Student must demonstrate with visual model.

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. Require justification language: "5 eighths is greater because..."  
2. Don't remove visual scaffold until reasoning is solid  
3. Synthesis includes "Explain to a friend" task

## **1.7 LESSON (8-10 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

### **Core Purpose**

Teach students to compare fractions with the same denominator using visual reasoning, then introduce comparison symbols (\>, \<, \=) as notation for relationships they can see. By end of lesson, students understand WHY same-denominator comparison works (same-sized pieces) and can compare abstractly.

### **Pedagogical Flow**

This lesson follows a modified CRA sequence where the "concrete" grounding comes from M8-M9's visual work with fractions:

- **Section 1 (Relational):** Students SEE that same-denominator fractions can be compared by counting pieces. Connects to prior position reasoning from M9.  
- **Section 2 (Abstract):** Symbols introduced as names for visual relationships. Extended practice with all three symbols across multiple representations.  
- **Section 3 (Application):** Abstract comparison without visuals, culminating in ordering multiple fractions.

Scaffold removal occurs within the lesson: full visual support in Sections 1-2, abstract reasoning in Section 3\.

### **Lesson Structure**

| Section | Focus | Interactions | Time |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | Visual Comparison Discovery \+ Method | 1.1–1.4 | \~3 min |
| Section 2 | Symbol Introduction \+ Practice | 2.1–2.8 | \~5 min |
| Section 3 | Abstract Reasoning \+ Ordering Capstone | 3.1–3.3 | \~3 min |

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Visual Comparison Discovery**

**Interaction 1.1: Modeling the Comparison Method (Sixths)**

* **Visual:** Two identical grids side by side, both partitioned into 6 equal parts:  
  * Left grid: 2 parts shaded (2/6)  
  * Right grid: 5 parts shaded (5/6)  
  * Labels beneath each: "2/6" and "5/6"  
* **Guide:** "Watch how I compare these two fractions."  
* **Guide:** "First, I check: Do both fractions have the same denominator? Yes—both have sixths. That means same-sized pieces."  
* **Guide:** "Next, I count the shaded parts. This one has two sixths. This one has five sixths."  
* **Guide:** "Since the pieces are the same size, I just compare: five pieces is more than two pieces. So 5/6 is greater."  
* **Guide:** "Now you try. Which fraction shows more?"  
* **Prompt:** "Click on the fraction that has more shaded."  
* **Remediation:** Light — "Count the shaded parts in each grid."  
* **On Correct:** "Right. 5/6 has more—five pieces is more than two pieces."

**Interaction 1.2: Circle Comparison (Fourths)**

* **Visual:** Two identical circles side by side, both partitioned into 4 equal slices:  
  * Left circle: 1 slice shaded (1/4)  
  * Right circle: 3 slices shaded (3/4)  
  * Labels beneath each: "1/4" and "3/4"  
* **Guide:** "Let's use the same method. First—do both have the same denominator?"  
* **Guide:** "Yes, both are fourths. Same-sized pieces. Now count and compare."  
* **Prompt:** "Click on the greater fraction."  
* **Remediation:** Light — "Count the shaded slices in each circle."  
* **On Correct:** "3/4 is greater. Three pieces is more than one piece."

**Interaction 1.3: Number Line Comparison (Eighths)**

* **Visual:** Number line from 0 to 1, partitioned into eighths. Two points marked:  
  * Point at 2/8 (labeled)  
  * Point at 5/8 (labeled)  
* **Guide:** "You learned that position on a number line shows value. The same method works here."  
* **Guide:** "Both fractions are eighths—same denominator. Which one is further from zero?"  
* **Prompt:** "Click on the greater fraction."  
* **Remediation:** Light — "Further from zero means greater."  
* **On Correct:** "5/8 is further from zero, so 5/8 is greater. More eighths means further right."

**Interaction 1.4: Metacognitive Check (Method)**

* **Visual:** Text display with the question. Small thumbnails of previous comparisons visible.  
* **Guide:** "You've compared three pairs of fractions. What's the FIRST thing to check when comparing?"  
* **Prompt:** "Select the first step."  
* **Options:**  
  * A) Check if denominators are the same  
  * B) Count the shaded parts  
  * C) Look at the numerators  
* **Correct Answer:** A  
* **Remediation:** Light — "What did we check before counting?"  
* **On Correct:** "Right. Always check denominators first. If they're the same, the pieces are the same size—then you can compare by counting."

**\[SECTION 1 TEACHING MOMENT\]**

* **Visual:** Summary display showing the method:  
  * Step 1: Check denominators (same \= same-sized pieces)  
  * Step 2: Count and compare numerators  
* **Guide:** "Here's the method: First, check that denominators are the same. That tells you the pieces are the same size. Then just count—more pieces means a greater fraction."  
* **No prompt—direct instruction consolidation.**

### **Required Phrases (Must Appear in Script)**

* "same denominator" (condition for this strategy)  
* "same-sized pieces" (conceptual anchor)  
* "GREATER THAN" with \> symbol  
* "LESS THAN" with \< symbol  
* "EQUAL" or "EQUAL TO" with \= symbol  
* "More pieces of the same size means a greater fraction" (or equivalent)  
* "The open side faces the greater fraction" (or "points to the bigger fraction")

### **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**

* ❌ "Bigger number means bigger fraction" (without specifying same denominator)  
* ❌ "Just compare the top numbers" (loses the reasoning)  
* ❌ "This always works" (overgeneralization—only works for same denominator)  
* ❌ "3 is bigger than 2" without unit (must say "3 fourths is bigger than 2 fourths")  
* ❌ "Different denominators" in any comparison (out of scope)  
* ❌ "Benchmark" or "compare to one-half" (Module 12 vocabulary)

---

### **Misconception Prevention**

**\#10 (Overgeneralizing Rules):**

* Every comparison explicitly states "same denominator" or "both are \[eighths/sixths/etc.\]"  
* Language always includes the unit: "5 sixths" not "5"  
* Section 1 teaching moment emphasizes the CONDITION: "When fractions have the SAME denominator..."  
* Synthesis (1.9) will ask "When does this strategy work?"

**\#8 (Comparison Errors / Symbol Confusion):**

* Symbols introduced one at a time (\> first, then \<, then \=)  
* Mnemonic reinforced: "open side faces the greater fraction"  
* Equality case (2.3, 2.6) ensures students don't assume all comparisons are inequalities  
* Visual models present during symbol introduction to ground meaning

## **1.9 SYNTHESIS (6-8 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

### **Purpose**

Connect comparison strategy to the "same-sized pieces" reasoning. Recognize that the method works ONLY when denominators match. Transfer understanding across multiple visual representations. Surface the limitation explicitly with a same-numerator contrast to preview M11. This is NOT more practice—it's connection-making and consolidation.

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 6 (Opening \+ 5 connection tasks \+ closure) |
| Task Types | Contrast Discovery (Type A variant), Representation Transfer (Type D), Real-World Bridge (Type C), Limitation Challenge (Generalization), Metacognitive Reflection |
| Remediation | Light only—mastery assumed |
| Tone | Reflective, warm, discovering together |
| Energy | Medium (not exhausted, not high-energy) |

### **Constraints**

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Show working AND non-working cases together | Only show cases where strategy works |
| Include real-world context (reading, sharing) | Skip real-world bridge |
| Use 2/3 vs 2/5 for M11 preview (same numerator) | Use different-numerator examples for M11 preview |
| Multi-select for connected reasoning | Binary choice for complex connections |
| Transfer across representations (grid, circle, number line) | Stick to single representation |
| Light remediation if needed | Extended teaching or Heavy remediation |
| Specific identity closure with M11 preview | Generic praise ("Great job\!") |

### **Opening Frame (30-45 seconds)**

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from Exit Check to reflection/connection.  
* **Visual:** Three comparison examples from lesson: 5/6 \> 2/6, 1/4 \< 3/4, 3/8 \= 3/8  
* **Guide:** "You've learned to compare fractions with the same denominator using \>, \<, and \=. Now let's figure out WHY this works and see how it connects to bigger ideas."

---

**Task S.1: Contrast Discovery — Why Does This One NOT Work?**

* **Purpose:** Immediately surface the limitation by contrasting working and non-working cases.  
* **Visual:** Three comparison pairs displayed side by side:  
  * Pair 1: 3/8 vs 5/8 (stacked grids, same denominator) — labeled "Pair A"  
  * Pair 2: 2/6 vs 4/6 (stacked grids, same denominator) — labeled "Pair B"  
  * Pair 3: 3/4 vs 3/6 (stacked grids, DIFFERENT denominators) — labeled "Pair C"  
* **Guide:** "Look at these three comparisons. Pairs A and B work with our method—just compare numerators. But Pair C is different."  
* **Guide:** "What makes Pair C impossible to compare with our 'count and compare' method?"  
* **Prompt:** \[Multiple choice\]  
  * A) Different denominators  
  * B) Different-sized pieces  
  * C) Can't count the same way  
* **On any selection:** "Nice thinking\! All of these ideas connect. It starts with the denominator—that tells us how many pieces the whole is split into. Different denominators mean different-sized pieces. That's why we can't just count and compare. In fractions, we always check the denominator first."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look at the bottom numbers in Pair C. Are they the same or different?"

---

**Task S.2: Representation Transfer — Same Comparison, Different Tools**

* **Purpose:** Recognize that the comparison method works across ANY visual representation.  
* **Visual:** Three representations showing the SAME comparison (2/4 \< 3/4):  
  * Grid: Two grids, 2/4 and 3/4 shaded  
  * Circle: Two circles, 2/4 and 3/4 shaded  
  * Number line: Points at 2/4 and 3/4  
* **Guide:** "Here's 2/4 compared to 3/4 shown three different ways. The tool changed—grid, circle, number line."  
* **Guide:** "What stayed the SAME across all three?"  
* **Prompt:** \[Multiple choice\]  
  * A) The shapes look identical  
  * B) 3/4 is always greater than 2/4  
  * C) They all use fourths  
* **Correct Answer:** B (also accept C as valid observation)  
* **On Correct:** "The tool changed, but the mathematical truth stayed the same: 3/4 \> 2/4, no matter how you show it. That's the power of understanding WHY the method works."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "In each picture, which fraction shows more? Is it always the same one?"

---

**Task S.3: Real-World Bridge — Reading Progress**

* **Purpose:** Connect comparison method to authentic real-world context.  
* **Visual:** Two progress bars labeled "Reading Progress":  
  * Top bar: "Alex — 5/12 of the book" (5 of 12 sections shaded)  
  * Bottom bar: "Sam — 8/12 of the book" (8 of 12 sections shaded)  
* **Guide:** "Our comparison method works in real life too. Alex and Sam are reading the same book—it has 12 chapters. These bars show how much each person has read."  
* **Prompt:** "Who has read more chapters?"  
  * A) Alex read more because 5 \> 8  
  * B) Sam read more because 8 \> 5  
  * C) They read the same amount  
* **Correct Answer:** B  
* **On Correct:** "Right. Sam read 8/12, more than Alex's 5/12. Whether you're counting chapters in a book or pieces of a shape, when the TOTAL is the same—same denominator—you can compare by counting what's been done."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Compare 5/12 to 8/12. Which number of chapters is more—5 or 8?"

---

**Task S.4: Limitation Challenge — M11 Preview**

* **Purpose:** Explicitly surface the limitation with same-numerator contrast to preview M11.  
* **Visual:** Two bars with SAME numerator but different denominators:  
  * Top bar: 2/3 (2 of 3 parts shaded)  
  * Bottom bar: 2/5 (2 of 5 parts shaded)  
  * Question mark between them  
* **Guide:** "Here's a challenge: Which is bigger—2/3 or 2/5? Both have 2 pieces shaded. Can we just compare numerators?"  
* **Guide:** "What makes this problem different from what we've been doing?"  
* **Prompt:** \[Multi-select\] "Select ALL reasons why our method doesn't work here:"  
  * A) Different denominators ✓  
  * B) Different-sized pieces ✓  
  * C) Need a different method ✓  
* **Correct Answer:** All three selected  
* **On Correct:** "Exactly right\! All three ideas connect. Different denominators mean different-sized pieces—a third is BIGGER than a fifth. And when pieces are different sizes, we can't just count and compare. We need a different method for this kind of problem."  
* **If incomplete selection:** "You're on the right track. These ideas are all connected—can you select all of them?"  
* **Guide:** "There ARE ways to compare these fractions. You'll learn that method next time\!"

---

**Task S.5: Metacognitive Reflection**

* **Purpose:** Help students identify what made the strategy work for them.  
* **Visual:** Summary showing the method: "Same denominator → Same-sized pieces → Compare by counting"  
* **Guide:** "Think about how you compared fractions today. What helped you most?"  
* **Prompt:** \[Selection\]  
  * "Checking that denominators match first"  
  * "Counting the shaded pieces"  
  * "Using the symbol mnemonics (open side faces bigger)"  
  * "Looking at position on the number line"  
* **On any selection:** Guide validates: "That's a powerful strategy. Noticing what helps YOU is how you become a stronger mathematician."

### **Identity-Building Closure (30-45 seconds)**
