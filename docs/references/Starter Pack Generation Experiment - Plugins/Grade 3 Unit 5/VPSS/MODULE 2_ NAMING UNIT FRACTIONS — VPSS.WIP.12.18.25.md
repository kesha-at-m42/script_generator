## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**Students must understand that unit fractions (1/b) represent exactly ONE equal part of a whole, and that the bottom number tells us the SIZE of that part—more parts means smaller pieces.**

**Critical Misconception:** \#3 (Numerator/Denominator as Independent)—Students may see the two numbers in a fraction as unrelated values rather than understanding: bottom number \= size of parts, top number \= count of parts.

**Success Indicator:** Student can identify unit fractions, explain that "the top number is always 1 because we're showing one part," and demonstrate understanding that larger bottom numbers mean smaller pieces (1/8 \< 1/4 \< 1/2).

**Biggest Risk:** Students memorizing fraction labels without understanding the relationship between the numbers, or believing "bigger number \= bigger piece."

### **✅ Must Teach**

- Unit fraction concept: 1/*b* means "one of *b* equal parts"  
- What the bottom number represents: how many equal parts make the whole  
- What the top number represents in unit fractions: always 1 (one part shaded)  
- Inverse relationship: bigger bottom number \= smaller pieces  
- Informal vocabulary: "top number," "bottom number"  
- Unit fractions: 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8  
- **Circles** as new shape (requires \[Toy\_Intro\])  
- **Grids and hexagons** (familiar from M1) for reinforcement  
- Size comparison within SAME shape type (grids to grids, circles to circles)

### **❌ Must Not Include**

- Non-unit fractions like 2/3, 3/4 (Module 3\)  
- Formal "numerator" terminology (Module 3\)  
- Formal "denominator" terminology (Module 4\)  
- Improper fractions (fractions \> 1\) (Module 6\)  
- Number line representations (Module 4-5)  
- Equivalent fractions (Module 8-9)  
- Formal comparison using \> \< symbols (Module 10-12)  
- Size comparison between different-sized shapes (Module 8\)

⚠️ **"Informal Before Formal" Principle:** Students need to understand what the numbers DO before learning what they're CALLED. Use "top number" and "bottom number" consistently—formal terms (numerator, denominator) come after conceptual grounding in later modules.

⚠️ **Unit Fractions Only:** This module focuses exclusively on using unit fractions with 1 on top. Students compare unit fraction SIZES but do not work with non-unit fractions yet.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Numerator/Denominator as Independent (\#3) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student treats the numbers in a fraction as unrelated. When asked "What does 1/4 mean?", they might say "one and four" rather than "one out of four equal parts" or "one-fourth." They don't connect the bottom number to the SIZE of the piece.

**Why It Happens:**  
Students have learned to read multi-digit numbers where each digit has independent place value. A fraction LOOKS like two numbers, so they apply whole-number thinking. Without explicit instruction connecting the numbers, the relationship remains invisible.

#### **Path C Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Grid array or circle showing the whole partitioned into equal parts with one shaded. Guide physically connects: "See these 4 equal parts? That's your bottom number—it tells us each piece is a FOURTH. See ONE part shaded? That's your top number."

**Prevention Strategy:**

- Always establish total parts FIRST (bottom number \= size of pieces)  
- Then count shaded parts (top number \= how many of those pieces)  
- Use consistent language: "one part out of \_\_\_ equal parts"  
- Reinforce that bottom number names the piece: "4 on bottom means fourths"

---

### **1.4.2 Reversing Numerator and Denominator (\#6) — SECONDARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student writes or identifies fractions with numbers swapped. When 1 part of 4 is shaded, they might select 4/1 instead of 1/4. May also read "1/4" incorrectly.

**Why It Happens:**

- "Total" feels more important than "part," so they put it first  
- Confusion about which number "goes where"  
- Reading top-to-bottom doesn't match fraction logic for them

#### **Path C Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Grid array with clear visual distinction: shaded part prominent, total grid structure clear. Consistent pattern: "Shaded on top, total on bottom."

**Prevention Strategy:**

- Teach "Shaded Over Total" as memorable pattern  
- Count total parts first (establish what goes on bottom)  
- Then identify shaded (what goes on top)  
- In unit fractions, emphasize: "Top is always 1 because we shade just one part"

---

### **1.4.3 Bigger Number \= Bigger Piece (\#8 variant) — SECONDARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student believes 1/8 is bigger than 1/4 because "8 is bigger than 4." When comparing unit fraction sizes, they select the one with the larger bottom number as "bigger."

**Why It Happens:**  
Whole-number thinking: in whole numbers, 8 \> 4 always means "more." Students haven't yet internalized that in fractions, bigger denominators mean MORE pieces, therefore SMALLER pieces.

#### **Path C Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Side-by-side bars of same size, one cut into 4 parts, one cut into 8 parts. Both with one part shaded. Visual makes size difference obvious.

**Prevention Strategy:**

- Use same-size wholes for all comparisons  
- Explicitly teach: "More cuts \= smaller pieces"  
- Real-world analogy: "Same pizza, cut into 4 slices vs 8 slices—which slice is bigger?"  
- Have students physically compare shaded areas, not just numbers

## **1.7 LESSON (10-14 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

### **Core Purpose**

Develop understanding that unit fractions (1/b) represent exactly ONE equal part, that the bottom number tells us the size of parts, and that more parts means smaller pieces. Transfer this understanding across multiple shapes.

### **Pedagogical Flow**

Module 2 builds on Module 1's partitioning foundation. All shapes are pre-partitioned—students focus on UNDERSTANDING what the numbers mean, not creating partitions. The lesson moves through four sections:

1. Grid mastery with unit fraction vocabulary  
2. Hexagon bridge → Circle introduction  
3. Size patterns (inverse relationship)  
4. Consolidation across shapes

| Section 4 | Consolidation | All shapes |

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Unit Fraction Vocabulary (Grids)**

**Interaction 1.1: Establishing the Whole \+ First Shade (Halves)**

* **Visual:** Pre-partitioned grid array showing 2 equal parts (2 x 1 tall rectangle with one horizontal cut), unshaded  
* **Guide:** "Here's a rectangle divided into 2 equal parts. Please shade one of the two parts."  
* **Prompt:** "Shade just ONE of the two parts."  
* **Remediation:** Light only (familiar action from M1)  
* **On Correct:** "Right. You shaded one part out of two equal parts."

**Interaction 1.2: Introduce "Unit Fraction" \+ Notation**

* **Visual:** Same grid from 1.1 with one part shaded; fraction 1/2 appears nearby  
* **Guide:** "Let me show you what the numbers mean when a fraction is written like this: ½. "

* **Guide:** "The TOP number tells us how many parts are shaded. You shaded 1 part, so the top number is 1."  
* **Guide:** "When we shade exactly ONE part, we call it a **UNIT fraction**. 'Unit' means one—so a unit fraction always has 1 on top."  
* **Vocabulary:** Introduce "unit fraction," "top number," "bottom number"  
* **No prompt—teaching moment.**

## **1.9 SYNTHESIS (6-8 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

### **Purpose**

Connect unit fraction concepts across representations. Recognize the "1 on top" pattern. Discover real-world applications of unit fractions. Consolidate the inverse size relationship. This is NOT more practice—it's connection-making and consolidation.

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 4 (3 connection tasks \+ metacognitive reflection) |
| Task Types | Pattern Discovery (Type A), Representation Transfer (Type D), Real-World Bridge (Type C) |
| Remediation | Light only—mastery assumed |
| Tone | Reflective, warm, discovering together |
| Energy | Medium (not exhausted, not high-energy) |

### **Constraints**

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Connection tasks (pattern, transfer, real-world) | More practice problems |
| Name principles students discovered | Introduce new procedures |
| Light remediation if needed | Extended teaching or Heavy remediation |
| Specific identity closure | Generic praise ("Great job\!") |
| Visual support for every task | Abstract discussion without visuals |
| Use grids, circles, hexagons (M2 Toys) | Introduce new visual models |

---

### **Opening Frame**

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from practice to reflection/connection.  
* **Guide:** "We've been working with unit fractions:1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8. Let's discover how these ideas connect to bigger patterns."

---

**Task S.1: Pattern Discovery (Type A)**

* **Purpose:** Recognize the defining feature of unit fractions (1 on top) by identifying what doesn't belong.  
* **Visual:** Four fractions displayed with visual representations:  
  * Grid showing 1/2 (2 parts, 1 shaded)  
  * Circle showing 1/4 (4 parts, 1 shaded)  
  * Grid showing 2/3 (3 parts, 2 shaded) ← BREAKS THE PATTERN  
  * Hexagon showing 1/3 (3 parts, 1 shaded)  
* **Guide:** "Take a look at all four fractions. Three of them follow a pattern. One breaks the pattern. Click on the fraction that breaks the pattern."  
* **Prompt:** "Which fraction breaks the pattern?"  
* **Correct Answer:** 2/3 (the non-unit fraction)  
* **On Correct:** "You got it\!"  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "The other three all have 1 on top, so they're unit fractions. 2/3 has 2 on top, so it's not a unit fraction. Unit fractions always have 1 on top because they show just one part."

---

**Task S.2: Representation Transfer (Type D)**

* **Purpose:** Recognize that the same unit fraction looks different on different shapes but represents the same number.  
* **Visual:** Three shapes side by side, all showing 1/3:  
  * Grid: 3 equal parts (1×3), 1 shaded  
  * Circle: 3 equal parts, 1 shaded  
  * Hexagon: 3 equal parts, 1 shaded  
* **Guide:** "Now, let’s look at these three shapes. They look different, but what's the SAME about all of them?"  
* **Prompt:** "What do all three shapes have in common?" \[Options: Same shape, Same fraction (1/3), Same color\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Same fraction (1/3)  
* **On Correct:** "They all show 1/3—one part out of three equal parts."  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "The shape can change, but the fraction stays the same. 1/3 means one out of three equal parts whether it's a rectangle, a circle, or a hexagon. Same fraction, different shapes.."

---

**Task S.3: Real-World Bridge (Type C)**

* **Purpose:** Connect unit fractions to familiar real-world contexts.  
* **Visual:** Three real-world scenarios displayed:  
  * Scenario A: Pizza cut into 8 slices, 1 slice on a plate (1/8)  
  * Scenario B: Chocolate bar with 4 sections, 1 section broken off (1/4)  
  * Scenario C: Glass of water filled halfway (1/2)  
* **Guide:** "Unit fractions aren't just useful in math class. You see them all the time."  
* **Guide:** "All three of these show unit fractions. Click on the picture that shows 1/4?"  
* **Prompt:** "Click on the picture that shows 1/4."  
* **Correct Answer:** Scenario B (chocolate bar)  
* **On Correct:** "Right\! One section out of four—that's 1/4."  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "Every time you share something equally and take one piece, that's a unit fraction. Fractions are everywhere once you know how to see them, like pizza slices, candy bars and measuring cups."

---

**Metacognitive Reflection**

* **Purpose:** Build awareness of learning growth in this module.  
* **Visual:** Gallery showing all five unit fractions (1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8) on various shapes  
* **Guide:** "Think back to the beginning of our lesson. You matched fractions to fraction models."  
* **Guide:** "What's the most important thing you learned about unit fractions today?"  
* **Prompt:** \[Options: The top number is always 1, Bigger bottom number means smaller pieces, Fractions work on any shape\]  
* **On Selection (any choice valid):** "That's a key idea. You'll use that understanding as we keep learning about fractions."

---

**Identity-Building Closure**

* **Purpose:** Specific, growth-oriented affirmation connecting to future learning.  
* **Guide:** "You practiced working with unit fractions that have 1 on top, which means exactly one part. And you figured out the pattern: bigger bottom number, smaller pieces."  
* **Guide:** “Next time, you'll use unit fractions to build fractions like 2/3 and 3/4.”
