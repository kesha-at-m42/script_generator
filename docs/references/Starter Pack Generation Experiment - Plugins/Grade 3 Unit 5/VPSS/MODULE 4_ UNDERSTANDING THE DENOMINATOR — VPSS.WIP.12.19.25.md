## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**Students must understand that the DENOMINATOR defines how many EQUAL INTERVALS (spaces, not tick marks) fit between 0 and 1 on a number line—and that these intervals are the same equal parts they've been working with in shapes, just arranged on a line. When we keep counting these intervals past 1, fractions continue in the same pattern.**

**Critical Misconception Cluster:** \#4 (Improper Spacing) \+ \#5 (Counting Tick Marks Instead of Spaces)—These are two manifestations of the same root misunderstanding: students don't recognize that **intervals (the spaces between marks) define fractions**, not the marks themselves. In VPSS, the gradient scaffold makes intervals visible and countable without requiring visual overlay of grids.

**Success Indicator:** Student can explain that the denominator tells us "how many equal spaces from 0 to 1," can count intervals (not ticks) to locate fractions, and can extend the pattern past 1 by continuing to count intervals.

**Biggest Risk:** Treating number line work as procedural placement ("put 3/4 here") rather than understanding WHY denominator creates the interval structure; rushing the conceptual bridge before students see that counting parts in shapes \= counting spaces on lines; students counting tick marks (there are 4 marks\!) instead of spaces (there are 3 intervals\!); confusing AREA (shapes) with DISTANCE (number lines).

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

- The denominator defines the number of equal intervals between 0 and 1  
- Intervals are the SPACES between tick marks (not the marks themselves)  
- Counting parts in shapes \= counting spaces on lines (conceptual transfer)  
- **Key distinction:** In shapes, fractions show AREA (shaded parts); on number lines, fractions show DISTANCE from 0 (position)  
- Vocabulary: "interval" as the math word for these equal spaces  
- Counting intervals from 0 to locate fractions (fractions as POSITIONS/distances)  
- The pattern continues past 1 (fractions \> 1 follow the same interval logic)  
- Unit fractions: 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/5, 1/6, 1/8  
- Non-unit fractions: 2/3, 3/4, 2/4, 3/5, 3/6, 5/6, 3/8  
- Beyond 1: 5/4, 6/4, 7/4

### **❌ Must Not Include**

- Procedural precision in placement (Module 5's focus)  
- Student-created partitioning of blank number lines (Module 5\)  
- Comparing fractions using number lines (Module 10+)  
- Equivalent fractions as same point (Module 8-9)  
- Mixed number notation (Module 7\)  
- Fractions in measurement contexts (later modules)  
- Operations with fractions (Unit 6\)

⚠️ **"Conceptual Before Procedural" Principle:** This module prioritizes UNDERSTANDING over ACCURACY. Students should grasp WHY denominator creates intervals before being held accountable for precise placement. Accept approximate placement in M4; require precision in M5.

⚠️ **Intervals, Not Ticks:** The critical teaching point is that we count SPACES (intervals), not marks (ticks). Every interaction should reinforce this distinction. The gradient scaffold makes intervals countable without requiring grid overlay.

⚠️ **Separate Representations:** Grids and number lines are SEPARATE Toys in VPSS. Students transfer meaning conceptually ("same counting, different Toy"), not through visual overlay. This preserves VPSS shape diversity.

⚠️ **Area vs. Distance:** In shapes, fractions represent AREA (how much is shaded). On number lines, fractions represent DISTANCE from 0 (how far from the start). Same denominator, same counting, different meaning. This distinction must be made explicit.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Counting Tick Marks Instead of Spaces (\#5) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student counts tick marks instead of intervals. When asked "How many equal parts between 0 and 1?" on a line showing thirds, they answer "4" (counting 0, 1/3, 2/3, 1\) instead of "3" (counting the three spaces). When placing 3/4, they may go to the 3rd tick mark rather than after the 3rd interval.

**Why It Happens:**  
Tick marks are visually prominent; spaces are "empty." Students have experience counting objects (marks are objects), not spaces. The shape equivalent would be counting the dividing lines instead of the sections.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
The gradient bands make intervals visible as countable regions. Each gradient band IS one interval. Students can see "three bands \= three spaces \= thirds."

**Prevention Strategy:**

- Gradient scaffold makes intervals visually prominent (not just "empty space")  
- Explicit instruction: "Count the spaces, not the marks. See how each colored band is one space?"  
- Connect to shapes: "Remember counting parts in the grid? Same thing—count the sections, not the lines between them."  
- Heavy remediation includes interval-by-interval counting with gradient highlighting

**Remediation Language:**

- Light: "Count the spaces between marks, not the marks."  
- Medium: "Look at the colored bands—each one is a space. Count the intervals."  
- Heavy: "Let me count with you: interval 1 \[highlight band\], interval 2 \[highlight band\], interval 3 \[highlight band\]. Three spaces for thirds."

---

### **1.4.2 Improper Spacing on Number Line (\#4) — PRIMARY (LINKED TO \#5)**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student creates or interprets unequal intervals. May cluster tick marks near certain numbers or accept visually unequal spacing as valid for fractions.

**Why It Happens:**  
Without the "equal parts" constraint being visually reinforced, students may not transfer the equality requirement from shapes to lines. The number line looks different enough that prior learning about "equal parts" doesn't automatically apply.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Gradient bands are uniform width—students see that each interval/band must be the same size. Unequal spacing would mean unequal bands, which is visually obvious.

**Prevention Strategy:**

- Gradient scaffold enforces visual equality of intervals  
- Explicit connection: "Just like the grid needed equal parts, the line needs equal spaces"  
- All M4 number lines come pre-partitioned with equal intervals (students don't create unequal spacing)  
- If student suggests unequal spacing: "Look at the bands—are they the same size? For fractions, they must be equal."

---

### **1.4.3 Misidentifying the Whole (\#2) — SECONDARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student loses track of what constitutes "one whole" on the number line. May think the whole line (0 to 2\) is the whole, or may not recognize that 1 marks the end of the first whole.

**Why It Happens:**  
In shapes, the whole is bounded and visible. On a number line, the line extends in both directions, and "1" is just another tick mark. The unit segment (0 to 1\) must be explicitly established.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Emphasize the 0-to-1 segment explicitly. When extending past 1, show clear visual break: "One whole complete, now we're in the second whole."

**Prevention Strategy:**

- Always establish "0 to 1 is our first whole" before discussing fractions  
- When extending past 1: "Now we have a SECOND whole from 1 to 2"  
- Connect to shapes: "Remember our grid was one whole? This segment from 0 to 1 is also one whole."  
- Label 1 prominently as the "one whole" mark

---

### **1.4.4 Fractions Only Exist in Shapes (\#9) — TERTIARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student doesn't recognize that the same fraction (e.g., 1/3) exists on a number line. May treat number line fractions as "different" from shape fractions.

**Why It Happens:**  
First three modules used area models exclusively. Without explicit bridging, students may compartmentalize "shape fractions" and "line fractions" as separate concepts.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Show familiar grid BESIDE (not overlaid on) number line. Same denominator, same counting, different representation.

**Prevention Strategy:**

- Lesson opens with familiar shape, then introduces number line as "another way to show the same fraction"  
- Explicit language: "Same counting—3 parts here, 3 spaces there"  
- Show both representations side by side: "1/3 in the shape, 1/3 on the line—same fraction, same distance from zero"  
- Synthesis returns to this connection

---

### **1.4.5 Confusing Area with Distance — TERTIARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student attempts to "shade" intervals on the number line, or describes number line fractions using area language ("3/4 of the line is shaded").

**Why It Happens:**  
M1-M3 established fractions as shaded AREA. Students need explicit instruction that number lines show DISTANCE/POSITION, not area.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Contrast shape (shaded area) with line (point at position). "In the shape, we shade. On the line, we place."

**Prevention Strategy:**

- Explicit teaching: "On shapes, fractions show how much is shaded—that's area. On number lines, fractions show how far from zero—that's distance."  
- Consistent interaction: shapes \= shade cells; lines \= place points  
- Language shift: "3/4 shaded" (shapes) vs. "3/4 from zero" (lines)

### **1.5.1 Gradient Number Line (Primary Toy — NEW)**

#### **Design Rationale**

VPSS grids maintain consistent 2D configurations (2×3, 3×4, etc.) that don't visually align with number line intervals. Rather than forcing single-row grids (which would sacrifice VPSS visual identity), Module 4 introduces a **Gradient Scaffold Number Line**:

- Grids and number lines remain SEPARATE representations  
- Students transfer meaning CONCEPTUALLY ("same counting, different Toy")  
- Gradient bands make intervals visible and countable WITHOUT grid overlay  
- This preserves VPSS shape diversity while enabling number line work

#### **Core Specifications**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Structure** | Standard number line with gradient bands making intervals visible |
| **Gradient Bands** | Single color, subtle; fade treatment (strong middle → fade at edges) |
| **Layer Order** | Back to front: Gradient bands → Number line → Tick marks → Labels → Points |
| **Tick Marks** | At 0, each fraction position, and whole numbers (1, 2\) |
| **Labels** | Tick marks labeled with fraction values; emphasize DISTANCE from 0 |
| **Student Interaction** | PLACE points/markers at tick positions (NO shading on number lines) |
| **Gradient Opacity** | 100% throughout all M4 phases |

#### **Visual Architecture**

```
     [gradient band 1] [gradient band 2] [gradient band 3]   ← Gradient scaffold (behind)
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────    ← Number line
    |         |         |         |                          ← Tick marks
    0        1/3       2/3        1                          ← Labels
                  •                                          ← Student-placed point
```

**Key Visual Elements:**

- Gradient bands live ABOVE the line (or above and below), always BEHIND line and tick marks  
- Each band spans one interval (tick to tick)  
- Bands make "spaces" into visible, countable regions  
- Student places POINT at tick mark position—no shading of intervals

#### **Interaction Model: Critical Distinction**

| Representation | What Shows Fraction | Student Action | Fraction Meaning |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Grid/Shape | Shaded cells/parts | SHADE | AREA (how much) |
| Number Line | Tick mark position | PLACE point | DISTANCE from 0 (how far) |

**⚠️ CRITICAL:** There is NO shading on number lines. Students PLACE markers at tick positions. The gradient is scaffold for counting—it is NOT the representation of the fraction. The tick mark position IS the fraction.

#### **Supported Configurations**

| Denominator | Intervals (0 to 1\) | Labels |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Halves** | 2 gradient bands | 0, 1/2, 1 |
| **Thirds** | 3 gradient bands | 0, 1/3, 2/3, 1 |
| **Fourths** | 4 gradient bands | 0, 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 1 |
| **Fifths** | 5 gradient bands | 0, 1/5, 2/5, 3/5, 4/5, 1 |
| **Sixths** | 6 gradient bands | 0, 1/6, 2/6... 1 |
| **Eighths** | 8 gradient bands | 0, 1/8, 2/8... 1 |

#### **Beyond-1 Configuration**

For fractions greater than 1 (5/4, 6/4, 7/4):

- Number line extends from 0 to 2  
- Gradient bands continue past 1 with same interval size  
- Visual distinction at 1: "One whole complete" (subtle break or emphasis)

```
[band][band][band][band] | [band][band][band][band]
   |     |     |     |   |   |     |     |     |
   0    1/4   2/4   3/4  1  5/4   6/4   7/4    2
                         ↑
                  One whole complete
```

| **Count Intervals** | None (observation) | Guide counts bands, highlighting each |
| **Identify Position** | Click tick mark | System validates; feedback on correct/incorrect |
| **Place Fraction** | Click to place point at tick | Point appears at tick; label shown |

**Interaction W.1: Hook \+ Fraction Recognition**

* **Purpose:** Activate M3 knowledge—students can read fractions by counting shaded parts (numerator) and total parts (denominator).  
* **Hook:** Callback to M3 learning—"You've been building fractions by counting parts. Let's see if you remember how that works."  
* **Visual:** Pre-partitioned grid array showing 4 equal parts, 3 shaded (3/4). Fraction notation area blank (student will identify).  
* **Task:** "Look at this fraction model. How many parts are shaded? How many total parts? What fraction does this show?"  
* **Prompt:** "What fraction is modeled?" \[Options: 3/4, 4/3, 1/4, 1/3\]  
* **Correct Answer:** 3/4  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Count the pink parts—that's your numerator. Count all the parts—that's your denominator."

**Interaction W.2: Counting Emphasis**

* **Purpose:** Reinforce that COUNTING parts is the key action. This directly sets up counting intervals on the number line.  
* **Visual:** Two pre-partitioned grids side by side:  
  * Grid A: 6 equal parts, 5 shaded (5/6)  
  * Grid B: 8 equal parts, 5 shaded (5/8)  
  * Both show "5 shaded parts" but different denominators  
* **Task:** "Both fraction models have 5 parts shaded. But look at the TOTAL parts. Which model  shows 5/6?"  
* **Prompt:** "Which fraction model shows five-sixths?" \[Click to select Grid A or Grid B\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Grid A  
* **Remediation (Light):** "The denominator 6 tells us: look for 6 total parts."  
* **On Correct:** "You counted 6 total parts in that fraction model. The denominator tells us how many equal parts to look for."

**Interaction W.3: Bridge to Lesson**

* **Purpose:** Create anticipation for Lesson without teaching. Tease that counting parts works "on something new."  
* **Visual:** Keep Grid A (5/6) visible. Add visual hint of curiosity (subtle "?" or "coming next" indicator, but NO number line shown).  
* **Content:**  
  * Acknowledge what student just did: "You counted parts to find fractions. Counting is the key."  
  * Create curiosity gap: "But what if I told you there's ANOTHER way to show fractions—where you count the exact same way, but it doesn't look like a grid at all?"  
  * Tease Lesson: "Let's find out what that looks like."  
* **No student action.**

## **1.7 LESSON (10-14 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

### **Core Purpose**

Develop understanding that the denominator defines the number of equal INTERVALS between 0 and 1 on a number line. Students discover that counting intervals on a line is the same as counting parts in a shape (conceptual transfer), learn that fractions on number lines show DISTANCE from 0 (not area), and extend the interval pattern past 1 to locate fractions like 5/4.

### **Pedagogical Flow**

Module 4 bridges from shape-based fraction understanding (M1-M3) to number line representation. The lesson emphasizes conceptual transfer rather than procedural skills:

1. **Conceptual Bridge** — Connect familiar shapes to new number line representation  
2. **Interval Discovery** — Denominator defines equal spaces; establish counting pattern  
3. **Placing Fractions** — Count intervals from 0 to place points showing fraction locations  
4. **Beyond 1** — Extend the pattern past one whole

### **Lesson Structure**

| Section | Focus | Interactions |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | Conceptual Bridge (Shape → Line) | 1.1 – 1.4 |
| Section 2 | Denominator as Interval Definer | 2.1 – 2.5 |
| Section 3 | Placing Fractions (Point Placement) | 3.1 – 3.6 |
| Section 4 | Beyond One Whole | 4.1 – 4.4 |

### **Number Line Interaction Types (M4)**

| Element | Description | Student Action in M4 |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Tick Marks** | Define interval boundaries (like partition lines in shapes) | Pre-placed; students do NOT place tick marks in M4 |
| **Points** | Dots showing fraction locations | Students PLACE points to show where fractions are |
| **Labels** | Fraction names at tick positions | May be pre-shown, hidden, or appear after correct placement |

* ❌ "Count the tick marks" (count intervals/spaces, not marks)  
* ❌ "The bottom number" without also using "denominator" (use formal vocab from M3)  
* ❌ "This is harder" or "This is different" (emphasize continuity, not difficulty)  
* ❌ "Improper fraction" or "mixed number" (Module 6-7 vocabulary)

## **1.9 SYNTHESIS (6-8 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

### **Purpose**

Connect number line concepts to grid representations (same counting, different Toy). Recognize that denominator-defines-intervals works across ALL denominators. Apply fraction-on-a-line understanding to real-world measurement contexts. This is NOT more practice—it's connection-making and consolidation.

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 6 (5 connection tasks \+ metacognitive reflection) |
| Task Types | Representation Transfer (Type D), Pattern Discovery (Type A), Real-World Bridge (Type C), Generalization |
| Shapes/Toys | Grid arrays AND number lines (transfer focus) |
| Remediation | Light only—mastery assumed |
| Tone | Reflective, warm, discovering together |
| Energy | Medium (not exhausted, not high-energy) |

### **Constraints**

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Connection tasks showing grid↔line transfer | More point placement drills |
| Emphasize "same counting" across representations | Introduce new denominators not in Lesson |
| Real-world measurement connection | Abstract discussion without visuals |
| Light remediation if needed | Extended teaching or Heavy remediation |
| Specific identity closure | Generic praise ("Great job\!") |
| Visual support for every task | New procedures or Toys |

### **Opening Frame**

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from practice to reflection/connection.  
* **Guide:** "You've learned to find fractions on number lines by counting intervals. Now let's discover how this connects to everything you already knew about fractions."

---

**Task S.1: Representation Transfer (Type D)**

* **Purpose:** Recognize that the same fraction can be shown across multiple representations—shapes AND number lines share the same underlying structure.  
* **Visual:** Four representations all showing 1/3:  
  * Grid array (1×3), one part shaded  
  * Circle partitioned into thirds, one part shaded  
  * Hexagon partitioned into thirds, one part shaded  
  * Number line 0-1 with thirds marked, point at 1/3  
* **Guide setup:** "Look at differentall four ways to show a fractionone-third."  
* **Prompt:** "What is the SAME about all of these?" \[Multiselect \- select all that apply\]  
  * All divided into three equal parts ✓  
  * All show one-third ✓  
  * All show one-fifth  
  * All are circles  
* **Correct Answers:** "All divided into three equal parts" AND "All show one-third"  
* **On Correct:** "You found it\!"  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "Whether it's a rectanglegrid, circle, hexagon, or number line—they all show thirds because they all have three equal parts. Same fraction, different ways to show it."

---

**Task S.3: Pattern Discovery (Type A)**

* **Purpose:** Recognize that denominator-defines-intervals is a consistent pattern across different denominators.  
* **Visual:** Three number lines stacked vertically, all 0-1:  
  * Line A: Thirds (3 intervals), point at 2/3  
  * Line B: Fourths (4 intervals), point at 3/4  
  * Line C: Sixths (6 intervals), point at 5/6  
  * Each labeled with its fraction  
* **Guide setup:** "Look at these three number lines. Each shows a different fraction."  
* **Prompt:** "What pattern do you notice about the denominator and the intervals?" \[Options: The denominator matches the number of intervals / The denominator matches the point location / The denominators are all even numbers\]  
* **Correct Answer:** The denominator matches the number of spaces  
* **On Correct:** "You spotted it\!"  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "Thirds have 3 intervals. Fourths have 4 intervals. Sixths have 6 intervals. The denominator ALWAYS tells you how many equal intervals fit between 0 and 1\. That pattern never changes."

---

**Task S.4: Real-World Bridge (Type C)**

* **Purpose:** Connect number line fractions to familiar measurement contexts.  
* **Visual:** Image of a ruler showing 0 to 1 inch, with eighth-inch marks visible. A point/arrow indicates the 3/8 position.  
* **Guide setup:** "You see number lines like this every day—on rulers\! This ruler shows one inch divided into eighths."  
* **Prompt:** "What fraction of an inch does the arrow show?" \[Options: 3/8, 8/3, 1/3, 3/7\]  
* **Correct Answer:** 3/8  
* **On Correct:** "Three spaces from zero—3/8 of an inch."  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "Rulers, measuring tapes, even the marks on a thermometer—they all use the same idea. Count the spaces from zero to find the fraction. You already know how to read them\!"

---

**Task S.5: Generalization (Beyond 1\)**

* **Purpose:** Consolidate understanding that fractions continue past 1, connecting to Section 4 learning.  
* **Visual:** Number line from 0 to 2 with fourths marked. Points placed at 3/4 AND 7/4. Both labeled.  
* **Guide setup:** "Look at where 3/4 and 7/4 are on this line."  
* **Prompt:** "What's the same about finding both fractions?" \[Options: Both use counting intervals from zero / Both are less than 1 / Both have 4 in them\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Both use counting intervals from zero  
* **On Correct:** "Right\!"  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "Whether the fraction is less than 1 or greater than 1, you always count intervals from zero. 3/4 is three intervals from zero. 7/4 is seven intervals from zero. The same strategy works everywhere on the line."
