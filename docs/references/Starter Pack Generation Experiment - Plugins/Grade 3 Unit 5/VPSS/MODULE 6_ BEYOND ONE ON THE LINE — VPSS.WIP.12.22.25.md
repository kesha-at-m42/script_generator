## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

Fractions don't stop at 1—the same-sized jumps continue, and the numerator tells how far from zero regardless of whether the fraction is less than, equal to, or greater than one whole.

**Critical Misconception:** Spacing errors (\#4) become more pronounced without background color support, and numerator/denominator confusion (\#3) resurfaces when fractions exceed 1\.

**Success Indicator:** Student places 7/4 accurately by counting seven fourth-sized jumps from 0, maintaining consistent spacing through and past 1—WITHOUT background color support.

**Biggest Risk:** Students who relied on colored background bands to verify spacing may struggle to maintain equal intervals independently. The visual scaffold removal must be positioned as earned independence, not loss of support.

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

* Locating fractions \> 1 on number lines with fluency and independence  
* Iteration concept: counting unit fraction jumps from 0 continues unchanged past 1  
* Distance concept: fraction tells how far from 0 (5/4 \= "five fourths distance from zero")  
* Consistent spacing without background color support  
* Working independently on plain number lines (background colors removed)  
* Celebrate when fractions equal whole numbers (4/4 \= 1, 8/4 \= 2, 6/3 \= 2)—callback to M3's n/n \= 1  
* Celebrate pattern: "Every four fourths makes another whole"  
* Vocabulary: fractions greater than 1, beyond one whole

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* Mixed number notation (1 1/4, 2 3/4) — Module 7+  
* Comparing fractions \> 1 — Module 10+  
* Equivalent fractions — Module 8-9  
* New partitioning procedures — M5 covered this; M6 applies existing skill  
* Division interpretation of fractions (8 ÷ 4 \= 2\) — even when 8/4 \= 2 is observed  
* Formal "improper fraction" terminology — use descriptive "fractions greater than 1"

⚠️ **THE TRAP TO AVOID:** When students see 8/4 \= 2, celebrate the pattern ("every four fourths makes a whole") but do NOT pursue division interpretation ("8 ÷ 4 \= 2"). Keep the focus on counting jumps landing on whole numbers, not on division relationships.

⚠️ **PEDAGOGICAL COMMITMENT:** This is THE SCAFFOLD REMOVAL MODULE. Students transition from number lines with background colors (100% opacity in M4-M5) to plain number lines (0% opacity). This scaffold removal is the major pedagogical event of M6.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Spacing Errors (\#4) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student places tick marks or fractions with unequal spacing, particularly after passing 1\. May compress intervals as they approach 2 or stretch them inconsistently. Without colored background bands to guide them, spacing errors become more frequent.

**Why It Happens:**  
M4-M5 used colored background bands to make intervals visible and countable. Without this scaffold, students must internalize equal spacing. Cognitive load increases when working past 1\. Students may rush or not attend to consistent spacing without visual verification.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
During remediation, tick marks can briefly highlight to show spacing comparison. For Heavy remediation only, background colors return temporarily at 100%.

**Prevention Strategy:**

* Explicit teaching: "The jumps don't change size when we pass 1"  
* Lesson begins WITH background colors (familiar), then removes them mid-lesson  
* Removal framed as earned independence: "I think you're ready to try without them"  
* Guide language: "Same size jumps whether before or after one whole"

**Remediation Progression:**

* **Light (10-20 words):** "Check your spacing—are all the jumps the same size?"  
* **Medium (25-35 words):** "Think about fourths. Each fourth takes the same space whether it's before 1 or after 1\. Try placing those intervals with equal spacing."  
* **Heavy (40-60 words \+ background colors return):** \[Modeling\] "Let me bring back the colored backgrounds for a moment. See how each band is the same width? 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4, 5/4—same size. Now I'll remove them again. You try." \[Background colors at 100% temporarily, then removed\]

---

### **1.4.2 Numerator/Denominator Confusion (\#3) — SECONDARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student confuses the roles when working with fractions \> 1\. May think 5/4 means "four and five" or "five divided by four" instead of "five fourths." May change the denominator when going past 1 (treating fourths as fifths, for example).

**Why It Happens:**  
Fractions \> 1 feel unfamiliar—the numerator being larger than denominator contradicts early mental model that "top number is smaller." May not have consolidated that denominator defines interval SIZE while numerator counts JUMPS regardless of how many.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Iterate explicitly on screen: highlight each jump as it's counted. Show that interval SIZE stays constant while COUNT increases.

**Prevention Strategy:**

* Emphasize consistent language: "In 5/4, the 4 tells us fourths—the size of each jump. The 5 tells us five jumps."  
* Count aloud on screen: "1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4... now 5/4"  
* Never change denominator when crossing 1: "Still counting by fourths"

**Remediation Progression:**

* **Light:** "Remember, 7/4 means seven jumps of size 1/4. The denominator doesn't change."  
* **Medium:** "Let's count together: 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4 lands on 1\. Keep going—5/4, 6/4, 7/4. Still fourths\!"  
* **Heavy:** \[Modeling with background colors\] "Let me show you with the colored backgrounds. Each band is one fourth. Count with me: one, two, three, four, five, six, seven. Seven fourths. The 7 tells us how many, the 4 tells us the size."

---

### **1.4.3 \[Module-Specific\] Fractions Can't Exceed 1 (\#11) — SECONDARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student hesitates or expresses confusion when asked to place fractions like 5/4. May say "that's not possible" or try to represent it as 4/5 (flipping to keep numerator smaller). May believe the number line "ends" at 1 for fractions.

**Why It Happens:**  
Early fraction experiences focused on "parts of one whole"—shading 3 of 4 parts, etc. Although M5 exposed students to fractions \> 1, some may not have consolidated the concept. The idea of having "more than one whole" as a single fraction remains counterintuitive.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Extended number line visually continues past 1 to 2 (or beyond). Whole number positions (1, 2\) clearly marked to show continuity.

**Prevention Strategy:**

* Build from M5's exposure (this isn't first encounter, but some students need consolidation)  
* Frame positively: "Fractions can go anywhere on the number line"  
* Demonstrate continuity: "We just keep counting—no stopping at 1"  
* Celebrate boundary crossing: "5/4—you went past one whole\!"

**Remediation Progression:**

* **Light:** "Fractions can go past 1\! Just keep counting your fourths."  
* **Medium:** "You're at 4/4 which equals 1\. But we can keep going—5/4 is just one more fourth."  
* **Heavy:** \[Modeling\] "Watch me count: 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4—there's 1\. But the number line doesn't stop\! 5/4, 6/4, 7/4, 8/4—there's 2\! Fractions can live anywhere on the line."

**Interaction W.1: Hook \+ Line Extension**

* **Purpose:** Create curiosity about what happens past 1\. Show the number line extending.  
* **Hook:** "You've been placing fractions between 0 and 1\. But what happens when the number line keeps going?"  
* **Visual:** Number line from 0 to 1 with colored background bands showing halves. Tick marks at 0, 1/2, and 1\. Labels at 0, 1/2, and 1\.  
* **Visual Action:** Number line smoothly extends to show 0 to 2\. The space from 1 to 2 glows softly, empty and waiting.  
* **Guide:** "Look at that\! The line goes past 1\. I wonder what fractions go there?"  
* **No student action—observation moment.**

**Interaction W.2: Partition the New Space (Halves)**

* **Purpose:** Activate M5 partitioning skill—students place tick mark to divide 1-2 into halves.  
* **Visual:** Extended number line 0 to 2 with colored background bands. The 0-1 section shows halves marked (0, 1/2, 1). The 1-2 section is empty.  
* **Guide:** "You know how to partition. The space from 0 to 1 is divided into halves. Can you divide the space from 1 to 2 the same way? Place a tick mark right in the middle."  
* **Prompt:** "Click to place a tick mark for halves."  
* **Correct Answer:** Tick mark placed at midpoint between 1 and 2  
* **On Correct:** "You divided 1 to 2 into halves—same spacing as before."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look at where 1/2 is between 0 and 1\. Place your tick mark at the same spot between 1 and 2."

**Interaction W.3: Place 3/2**

* **Purpose:** Students actively place a fraction past 1, activating the counting pattern.  
* **Visual:** Same number line with tick mark now visible at 1.5 position (not yet labeled).  
* **Guide:** "Now let's count the halves: one-half, two-halves—that's 1\. What comes next? Place three-halves on the line."  
* **Prompt:** "Click to place a point at 3/2."  
* **Correct Answer:** Point placed at the 1.5 position (the tick mark they just placed)  
* **On Correct:** "Three-halves. You placed a fraction past 1\. The counting just keeps going."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Count from zero: 1/2, 2/2, 3/2. Where does the third half land?"

**Interaction W.4: Fourths Observation \+ Bridge**

* **Purpose:** Show the pattern works with other denominators; bridge to Lesson.  
* **Visual:** New number line from 0 to 2 with colored background bands showing fourths. Tick marks at all positions. Labels at 0, 1, and 2 only.  
* **Guide Demo:** "The pattern works with fourths too. Watch: 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4—that's 1\. But it keeps going\! 5/4, 6/4, 7/4, 8/4—that's 2."  
* **Visual Action:** Each position highlights as Guide counts. Points appear sequentially.  
* **Guide:** "Same spacing before 1 and after 1\. Now it's your turn to try this with different fractions."  
* **No student action—transition to Lesson.**

## **1.7 LESSON (10-14 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

### **Core Purpose**

Build fluency with fractions greater than 1 on number lines, explicitly connect counting jumps to distance (position from 0), and transition students from number lines with background colors to independent work on plain number lines.

### **Pedagogical Flow**

Module 6 consolidates M4-M5 understanding and builds independence. Students have seen fractions \> 1 in M5 but haven't built fluency. The lesson moves through four sections:

1. **Counting Past 1** — Place fractions \> 1 with familiar background colors  
2. **Jumps \= Distance \+ Scaffold Removal** — The key conceptual link and background color removal  
3. **Fluency Without Background Colors** — Apply understanding independently  
4. **Whole Number Discovery** — Celebrate that some fractions equal whole numbers (8/4 \= 2, 6/3 \= 2\)

| Section | Focus | Interactions |

| Section 4 | Whole Number Discovery | 4.1 – 4.4 |

## **1.9 SYNTHESIS (6-8 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

### **Purpose**

Connect the "fractions greater than 1" concept to the broader pattern of counting and distance. Recognize when fractions equal whole numbers (the n/n pattern extended). Discover that the same counting principle works regardless of denominator. Apply understanding to multiple real-world contexts. This is NOT more practice—it's connection-making and consolidation.

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 6 (5 connection tasks \+ metacognitive reflection) |
| Task Types | Pattern Discovery (Type A), Transfer with Placement (Type D), Real-World Matching (Type C), Generalization |
| Remediation | Light only—mastery assumed |
| Tone | Reflective, warm, discovering together |
| Energy | Medium (not exhausted, not high-energy) |

### **Constraints**

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Active placement on number lines | Multiple choice only |
| Stacked number lines for transfer scaffolding | Single isolated visuals |
| Multiple real-world scenarios with matching | Single scenario with selection |
| Explicit "what's the same" consolidation | Skip articulation of principle |
| Light remediation if needed | Extended teaching or Heavy remediation |
| Specific identity closure | Generic praise ("Great job\!") |

---

### **Opening Frame**

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from practice to reflection/connection.  
* **Guide:** "You've worked hard placing fractions past 1\! Now let's connect what you discovered."

---

**Task S.1: Pattern Discovery — What Makes a Fraction Greater Than 1? (Type A)**

* **Purpose:** Recognize the structural pattern of fractions \> 1 through visual comparison.  
* **Visual:** Three number lines (0 to 2), each with one fraction placed:  
  * Line A: Point at 3/4 (before 1\)  
  * Line B: Point at 5/4 (past 1\)  
  * Line C: Point at 7/4 (past 1\)  
* **Guide:** "Look at these three fractions on number lines. Two of them are past 1, and one is before 1\. What do the fractions past 1 have in common?"  
* **Prompt:** "What's true about fractions greater than 1?" \[Selection: The numerator  is bigger than the denominator  / the denominator  is bigger than the numerator  / They all have 4 on the bottom\]  
* **Correct Answer:** The numerator r is bigger than the denominator.  
* **On Correct:** "You spotted it. When the numerator is bigger than the denominator, the fraction goes past 1\. 5/4 and 7/4 both have top numbers bigger than 4."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look at 5/4 and 7/4. Compare the top and bottom numbers in each."

---

**Task S.2: Transfer — Extending the Pattern to Thirds (Type D with Placement)**

* **Purpose:** Transfer the whole number pattern from fourths to thirds using stacked number lines.  
* **Visual:** Two number lines stacked vertically:  
  * Top line (fourths): 0 to 2 with 4/4 marked at 1 and 8/4 marked at 2, labeled "4/4 \= 1" and "8/4 \= 2"  
  * Bottom line (thirds): 0 to 2 with tick marks at thirds, but no fractions placed yet  
* **Guide:** "Here's a number line with fourths. See how 4/4 equals 1 and 8/4 equals 2? The pattern continues with equal jumps."  
* **Guide:** "Now look at the line for thirds below. If we follow the same pattern, where would 6/3 go? Use the pattern you see above to help you place it."  
* **Prompt:** "Click to place 6/3."  
* **Correct Answer:** Point placed at 2  
* **On Correct:** "Right. Just like 8/4 equals 2, so does 6/3. Different denominators, same pattern—equal jumps all the way."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look at the fourths above. How many fourths make 2? Same idea with thirds—how many thirds make 2?"

---

**Task S.3: Dual Placement — Two Denominators (Type D)**

* **Purpose:** Consolidate transfer by placing fractions with different denominators, then articulating what's the same.  
* **Visual:** Two number lines stacked vertically:  
  * Top line: 0 to 2 with tick marks for fourths (no fractions placed)  
  * Bottom line: 0 to 2 with tick marks for thirds (no fractions placed)  
* **Guide:** "Now you try both. On the top line, place 6/4. On the bottom line, place 4/3."  
* **Prompt:** "Place 6/4 and 4/3."  
* **Correct Answer:** 6/4 placed at 1.5 position (between 1 and 2); 4/3 placed at 1.33 position (between 1 and 2\)  
* **On Correct:** "Nice work. Both 6/4 and 4/3 are between 1 and 2."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "For 6/4, count six equal fourths from 0\. For 4/3, count four equal thirds from 0."

**Consolidation Question:**

* **Guide:** "Even though they use different denominators, what was the SAME about how you placed them?"  
* **Prompt:** \[Selection: I counted equal jumps from 0 / I looked at where 1 was / I guessed\]  
* **Correct Answer:** I counted equal jumps from 0  
* **On Correct:** "That's the key. The denominator tells you the jump size, the numerator tells you how many jumps. That pattern works for ANY fraction."

---

**Task S.4: Real-World Matching — Fractions Past 1 in Life (Type C)**

* **Purpose:** Connect fractions \> 1 to multiple familiar real-world contexts through active matching.  
* **Visual:** Three number lines (0 to 2), each with one fraction marked:  
  * Line A: 5/4 marked (between 1 and 2, closer to 1\)  
  * Line B: 7/4 marked (between 1 and 2, closer to 2\)  
  * Line C: 4/3 marked (between 1 and 2\)  
* **Guide:** "These fractions show up in everyday life. I'll describe three situations. Match each one to the correct fraction."

**Scenario 1:**

* **Guide:** "Running laps: You complete one full lap plus another quarter lap."  
* **Prompt:** "Which fraction shows this?" \[Student clicks on a line\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Line A (5/4)  
* **On Correct:** "Right. One lap plus a quarter is 5/4 laps."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "One whole lap is 4/4. Add one more quarter—that's one more fourth."

**Scenario 2:**

* **Guide:** "Pizza: Your family ate seven-fourths of a pizza."  
* **Prompt:** "Which fraction shows this?" \[Student clicks on a line\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Line B (7/4)  
* **On Correct:** "Exactly. Seven-fourths means more than one whole pizza, but not quite two."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Seven-fourths means seven jumps of one-fourth each. Find it on the fourths line."

**Scenario 3:**

* **Guide:** "Time: A project takes 1 hour and 20 more minutes. Since 20 minutes is one-third of an hour, what fraction shows the total time?"  
* **Prompt:** "Which fraction shows this?" \[Student clicks on remaining line\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Line C (4/3)  
* **On Correct:** "Yes. Four-thirds of an hour—one hour plus one-third more."  
* **On Correct (after all 3):** "See? Anytime something goes beyond one whole, you can describe it with a fraction greater than 1\. They're everywhere\!"

---

**Task S.5: Metacognitive Reflection**

* **Purpose:** Help students identify where their learning edge was—genuine reflection.  
* **Visual:** Simple reflection prompt on screen.  
* **Guide:** "Think about today's work. Which part took more thinking?"  
* **Prompt:** \[Selection: Placing fractions past 1 / Figuring out when fractions equal whole numbers\]  
* **On "Placing fractions past 1":** "That makes sense—keeping track of the count past 1 takes focus. The good news? It's the same pattern every time. More practice and it'll feel automatic."  
* **On "Figuring out when fractions equal whole numbers":** "That's a big idea—seeing that 8/4 and 2 are the same point. You're building a number sense that connects fractions and whole numbers."

---

### **Identity-Building Closure**

* **Purpose:** Specific, growth-oriented affirmation connected to future learning.  
* **Visual:** Split screen showing progression:  
  * Top: Number line 0 to 1 with 3/4 marked  
  * Bottom: Number line 0 to 2 with 5/4, 6/4, 7/4, 8/4 marked  
* **Guide:** "You started placing fractions between 0 and 1\. Now you can place them anywhere on the number line—even past 1, all the way to 2 and beyond."  
* **Guide:** "You learned that 8/4 equals 2 by keeping equal jumps going. Seeing patterns that continue no matter where you are—that's mathematical thinking\!"  
* **Guide:** "Next time, we'll explore more about how these fractions connect to whole numbers. You're ready\!"
