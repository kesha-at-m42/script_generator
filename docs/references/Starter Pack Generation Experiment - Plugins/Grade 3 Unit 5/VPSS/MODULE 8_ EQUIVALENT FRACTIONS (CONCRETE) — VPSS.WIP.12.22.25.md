## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**Equivalent fractions show the SAME AMOUNT—they look different but cover the same size. Visual proof (same area in shapes, same point on number lines) establishes equivalence, not symbolic rules.**

**Critical Misconception:** \#7 (Difficulty Recognizing Equivalence)—Students may not see that 2/4 \= 1/2 even when visually aligned, because the numbers look different.

**Success Indicator:** Student can identify that 1/2 \= 2/4 \= 3/6 by comparing visual representations, explain that "they cover the same amount," and verify equivalence using both shapes AND number lines.

**Biggest Risk:** Students discovering the "doubling pattern" and wanting to pursue it algorithmically. When a student says "I doubled both numbers\!" redirect to "You found one that's the same size\!" The symbolic pattern comes in M9—this module is about VISUAL PROOF.

## **1.1 LEARNING GOALS**

*Verbatim — Script Must Achieve These*

**L1:** "Identify equivalent fractions; two fractions are equivalent if they are the same size and refer to the same whole."

**L2:** "Generate and justify simple equivalent fractions using visual models."

**Module Goal (Student-Facing):**  
"You learned that fractions like 4/4 equal 1\. Now you'll discover something surprising—different fractions can equal EACH OTHER\! 1/2 and 2/4 look different, but they're the same amount. Let's prove it."

**Exit Check Tests:**

* Can identify which fractions from a set are equivalent to a given fraction (e.g., "Which equals 1/2?")  
* Can justify equivalence using visual proof ("They cover the same amount" / "They're at the same point")  
* Understands that equivalence requires the SAME WHOLE

### **1.1.1 Standards Cascade**

| Building On | 3.NF.A.1 (Understand fractions as parts of a whole) |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Addressing** | 3.NF.A.3a (Understand two fractions as equivalent if same size, same point); 3.NF.A.3b (Recognize and generate simple equivalent fractions) |
| **Building Toward** | 3.NF.A.3d (Compare fractions with same numerator or denominator) |

### **1.1.2 Module Bridges**

**From Module 7 (Fractions Equal to One):**  
Students understand that certain fractions equal whole numbers (4/4 \= 1, 8/4 \= 2); they can write whole numbers as fractions (1 \= 4/4 \= 6/6 \= 8/8); they recognize that b/b \= 1 and 2b/b \= 2; they work confidently on number lines without gradient scaffolds.

**This Module:**  
Students discover that fractions can equal OTHER fractions, not just whole numbers; they learn that "equivalent" means "same size" or "same amount"; they prove equivalence visually using both shapes (same area) AND number lines (same point); they work with the 1/2 family (1/2 \= 2/4 \= 3/6 \= 4/8) and introduce 2/3 \= 4/6; they understand the SAME WHOLE requirement for valid comparison.

**To Module 9 (Equivalent Fractions Abstract):**  
Students will confirm equivalence abstractly using number lines as the PRIMARY tool ("same point" reasoning); they will express whole numbers as fractions and recognize fractions equivalent to whole numbers; they will begin to notice patterns in how equivalent fractions are related (preparing for Grade 4's multiplication rule).

**Conceptual Progression:**

* **Module 7:** Fractions can equal WHOLE NUMBERS (4/4 \= 1\)  
* **Module 8 (This Module):** Fractions can equal OTHER FRACTIONS (1/2 \= 2/4) — VISUAL PROOF  
* **Module 9:** Same equivalences confirmed ABSTRACTLY (same point on number line)

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

* Recognizing equivalent fractions through visual matching (same size, same position)  
* Core equivalences: 1/2 \= 2/4 \= 3/6 \= 4/8 (primary chain)  
* Secondary equivalence: 2/3 \= 4/6 (introduced in Practice)  
* Understanding "same whole" requirement for equivalence  
* Justifying equivalence with visual models (shapes AND number lines)  
* Vocabulary: "equivalent," "same size," "same amount," "same whole"  
* Multiple visual representations: grid arrays, circles, hexagons, number lines  
* Recognition in Lesson; generation begins in Lesson Section 6 and continues in Practice

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* Simplifying fractions algorithmically (Grade 4\)  
* Cross-multiplication or symbolic rules (Grade 4\)  
* Equivalent fractions with denominators beyond 8 (later modules)  
* Comparing non-equivalent fractions using \< or \> (Module 10+)  
* Mixed numbers or improper fractions in equivalence context (Module 9\)  
* Explicit teaching of "multiply/divide top and bottom by same number"  
* Any pursuit of the "doubling pattern" as a procedure

⚠️ **THE TRAP TO AVOID:** When students see 2/4 \= 1/2, they may want to explore the "dividing by 2" pattern or discover simplification rules. DON'T GO THERE. We're establishing VISUAL equivalence only—the symbolic patterns come in M9 and Grade 4\.

⚠️ **SAME WHOLE PRINCIPLE (CRITICAL):** Equivalence comparisons are ONLY valid when comparing fractions on identical-sized wholes. Before ANY comparison, explicitly check: "Are these the same size whole?" Show counterexample: 1/2 of a small circle ≠ 1/2 of a large circle—different amounts of stuff\!

⚠️ **PEDAGOGICAL COMMITMENT:** Visual proof before symbolic patterns. Students must SEE why fractions are equivalent, not memorize rules. Every equivalence must be grounded in "same size" or "same position" reasoning.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Difficulty Recognizing Equivalence (\#7) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student says "2/4 is not the same as 1/2 because 2 is not 1 and 4 is not 2"; focuses on numbers rather than visual size; rejects equivalence even when shapes show same area.

**Why It Happens:**  
Students have learned that different numbers mean different things; fraction notation looks different so fractions must be different; prior experience comparing numbers (2 ≠ 1\) overrides visual evidence.

#### Path C (VPSS) Intervention

**Visual Cue:**  
Use stacked comparison with alignment line to show exact match; show same point on number line; use grid arrays where squares can be counted.

**Prevention Strategy:**

* Always show visual BEFORE introducing notation  
* Use alignment verification: "Let's check—do they cover the same space?"  
* Multiple representations: "Grids show same area, number line shows same point—both prove they're equal"  
* Count grid squares: "This one covers 4 out of 8 squares. This one covers 2 out of 4\. Same total squares shaded\!"

**Remediation Language:**

* **Light:** "Check if these fractions cover the same amount of space."  
* **Medium:** "You're working on it\! Compare the shaded areas. Even though 1/2 has fewer pieces than 2/4, they fill the same space."  
* **Heavy:** "Let me show you with grids: Here's 1/2—half shaded. Now here's 2/4—two out of four parts shaded. Look\! They cover exactly the same amount. Different fractions, same value. That's equivalence."

---

### **1.4.2 Reversing Numerator and Denominator (\#6)**

**Trigger Behavior:**   
Student writes 4/2 when meaning 2/4; says "two-fourths" but writes 4/2; confuses which number goes on top vs bottom when working with equivalent pairs.

**Why It Happens:**  
Equivalence work involves seeing relationships between numbers, which can trigger confusion about position; "2 and 4" might be remembered without position.

#### Path C (VPSS) Intervention

**Visual Cue:**  
Always show fraction notation alongside visual; use consistent "shaded over total" language.

**Prevention Strategy:**

* Reference grid: "How many parts shaded? (2) How many total? (4) So we write 2/4—shaded over total."  
* Pattern check: "The top number should be smaller than or equal to the bottom when we're working with fractions less than 1."

**Remediation Language:**

* **Light:** "Check your fraction—which number shows shaded parts?"  
* **Medium:** "Careful with your numbers\! Even with equivalent fractions, the pattern stays consistent—numerator on top, denominator on bottom."  
* **Heavy:** "Let's look at the grid together. How many parts are shaded? (2) That's your numerator—it goes on top. How many parts total? (4) That's your denominator—it goes on the bottom. So we write 2/4."

---

### **1.4.3 Overgeneralizing Rules (\#10)**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student assumes "bigger numbers \= bigger fraction" and therefore 2/4 must be bigger than 1/2; applies comparison rules inappropriately to equivalence.

**Why It Happens:**  
Prior learning about comparing numbers creates expectation that "more is bigger"; the insight that 2/4 \= 1/2 (despite 2 \> 1 and 4 \> 2\) seems counterintuitive.

#### Path C (VPSS) Intervention

**Visual Cue:**  
Side-by-side comparison with identical wholes; alignment line to show exact match despite different numbers.

**Prevention Strategy:**

* Emphasize visual over numbers: "Forget the numbers for a moment—look at the shaded amount."  
* Counterexample: "More pieces doesn't mean more pizza if the pieces are smaller\!"  
* Same Whole check: "When we use the same size whole, we can trust what our eyes see."

**Remediation Language:**

* **Light:** "Bigger numbers don't always mean bigger fractions. Look at the actual size."  
* **Medium:** "I see why that's confusing\! But look—2/4 has more pieces, but they're smaller pieces. 1/2 has fewer pieces, but they're bigger. Same total amount\!"  
* **Heavy:** "This is tricky\! Let's compare with pizza. If I cut my half into two pieces, I now have 2 out of 4 slices. But I didn't get more pizza—I just cut it differently\! Same amount, different numbers."

## **1.5 TOOL SPECIFICATIONS**

### **1.5.1 Grid Arrays (Primary Shape Tool)**

#### Core Specifications

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Purpose** | Show equivalence through area matching; count squares for verification |
| **Fixed Whole** | All grid arrays use SAME rectangle dimensions for valid comparison |
| **Partitioning Options** | Pre-partitioned for comparison (student doesn't create partitions in M8) |
| **Shading** | One array pre-shaded (e.g., 1/2), student identifies matching equivalent |
| **Comparison Method** | Stacked vertically with alignment line showing where shading ends |
| **Validation** | Count squares: 4/8 shaded \= 2/4 shaded \= 1/2 shaded (same count) |

#### Specific Grid Configurations for Equivalence

| Fraction | Grid Configuration | Squares Shaded |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1/2 | 2×1 (2 parts) | 1 of 2 |
| 2/4 | 2×2 (4 parts) | 2 of 4 |
| 3/6 | 3×2 (6 parts) | 3 of 6 |
| 4/8 | 4×2 (8 parts) | 4 of 8 |
| 2/3 | 3×1 (3 parts) | 2 of 3 |
| 4/6 | 3×2 (6 parts) | 4 of 6 |

**Critical Constraint:** All grids in a comparison MUST be the same overall size (Same Whole Principle).

|  | 2/3 hexagon \= 4/6 hexagon |
| **Visual Proof** | Stacked comparison showing same amount shaded |

---

### **1.5.4 Number Lines (Position Tool)**

#### Core Specifications

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Purpose** | Verify equivalence through position—equivalent fractions are at the SAME POINT |
| **Range** | 0 to 1 (no fractions \> 1 in M8 equivalence work) |
| **Scaffold Status** | No background colors (removed in M6) |
| **Pre-Partitioning** | Lines pre-partitioned at different denominators for comparison |
| **Key Insight** | 1/2, 2/4, 3/6, 4/8 all land at the SAME POINT |

#### Number Line Configurations for Equivalence

| Line | Partitions | Key Points |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Line A | Halves (2 intervals) | 1/2 at midpoint |
| Line B | Fourths (4 intervals) | 2/4 at midpoint |
| Line C | Sixths (6 intervals) | 3/6 at midpoint |
| Line D | Eighths (8 intervals) | 4/8 at midpoint |

**Display Option:** Stacked lines showing same point across different partitions, with vertical alignment line.

---

### **1.5.5 Dual Representation (VPSS Signature)**

#### Purpose

VPSS learners benefit from seeing equivalence proven in MULTIPLE ways simultaneously. Synchronized displays show that grids (area) and number lines (position) both confirm equivalence.

#### Implementation

* Split view: Grid on left, number line on right  
* Both showing same equivalence (e.g., 1/2 \= 2/4)  
* "Different tools, same mathematical truth"

#### Pedagogical Sequence in Lesson

1. **Warmup:** Number line bridge from M7 (activates prior knowledge)  
2. **Lesson Sections 1-4:** Shapes for discovery (grids → circles → hexagons)  
3. **Lesson Section 5:** Number line verification (confirms "same point")  
4. **Lesson Section 6:** Generation (create equivalent fractions)

---

### **1.5.6 Same Whole Verification**

#### Purpose

Before any equivalence comparison, verify that wholes are identical.

#### Implementation

- Visual display: All shapes in a comparison are same size  
- Counterexample in Lesson Section 2: "Different size wholes—can't compare\!"  
- Language reinforcement: "These rectangles are the same size—same whole."

## **1.6 WARMUP (2-3 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

### **Purpose**

Activate Module 7 knowledge (fractions equal to whole numbers on number lines) and create curiosity about whether different fractions can land at the same position. Bridge from M7's "4/4 \= 1" to M8's equivalence discovery—WITHOUT teaching equivalence or using the term "equivalent."

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 3 (2 tasks \+ bridge) |
| Warmup Type | Binary Choice (W.1) \+ Notice & Wonder (W.2) |
| Engagement Anchors | Personalization (M7 callback) \+ Strength Prompt ("detective") |
| Cognitive Load | 20-30% (low stakes) |
| Remediation | Light only |
| Tone | Curious, inviting, investigative |

### **Constraints**

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Use number line callback from M7 | Teach equivalence |
| Create curiosity about "same position" | Use the word "equivalent" |
| Pose questions, don't announce answers | Explain WHY fractions are at same position |
| Keep visual focus | Require complex reasoning |
| Use "investigate" / "detective" framing | Use pressure language |

---

### **Interaction W.1: Opening Hook**

* **Visual:** Number line from 0 to 1, with 4/4 marked at position 1\. The point pulses gently.  
* **Guide:** "Remember this? 4/4 lands right at 1\. You proved that fractions can equal whole numbers."  
* **Guide:** "But here's a puzzle: Could TWO DIFFERENT fractions land at the exact same spot on a number line? Let's investigate."  
* **No prompt—hook moment.**

**Interaction W.2: M7 Callback (Recognition)**

* **Purpose:** Activate M7 knowledge that fractions can equal whole numbers. Quick win to build confidence.  
* **Visual:** Number line from 0 to 1, partitioned into sixths. Point marked at position 1 (at 6/6).  
* **Guide:** "First, a quick check. Here's a number line showing sixths. A fraction is marked at position 1."  
* **Prompt:** "What fraction is at this position?" \[Options: 5/6, 6/6, 1/6\]  
* **Correct Answer:** 6/6  
* **On Correct:** "Yes\! 6/6 equals 1—just like 4/4 equals 1\. When the top number equals the bottom number, the fraction equals one whole. You remember this well."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Count the spaces from 0 to the point. How many sixths does it take to reach 1?"

**Interaction W.3: Curiosity Primer (Noticing)**

* **Purpose:** Create curiosity about fractions at the SAME position without teaching equivalence. Student plays "detective" role.  
* **Visual:** Two number lines stacked vertically:  
  * Top line: 0 to 1, partitioned into halves, with 1/2 marked  
  * Bottom line: 0 to 1, partitioned into fourths, with 2/4 marked  
  * Both points are at the SAME horizontal position (midpoint)  
  * Vertical dashed alignment line connecting the two points  
* **Guide:** "Now for the puzzle. I'm going to show you two number lines with different fractions marked."  
* **Guide:** "Your job: play detective and look for something interesting about their positions."  
* **Prompt:** "What do you NOTICE about where 1/2 and 2/4 are?" \[Same position / Different positions\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Same position  
* **On Correct:** "You spotted it\! 1/2 and 2/4 are at the exact same spot on their number lines. Different fractions, same position. Interesting..."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look at the dashed line connecting the two points. Are they lined up or not?"

**Interaction W.4: Bridge to Lesson (10-15 seconds)**

* **Purpose:** Create anticipation for Lesson without teaching equivalence. Transition from number lines to shapes.  
* **Visual:** Keep both number lines visible with alignment line. Add a subtle "?" icon between the fraction labels.  
* **Guide:** "Two different fractions—1/2 and 2/4—land at the same position. That's not a coincidence\!"  
* **Guide:** "Let's discover what's really going on. We'll use shapes to prove something surprising about these fractions."  
* **No prompt—bridge moment.**

## **1.7 LESSON (12-13 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

### **Core Purpose**

Develop understanding that equivalent fractions show the SAME AMOUNT through visual proof. Students discover equivalence using shapes (grids, circles, hexagons), verify with number lines, and create simple equivalent fractions. Vocabulary ("equivalent fractions") is introduced AFTER visual grounding.

### **Pedagogical Flow**

Module 8 follows the CRA sequence applied to equivalence:

1. **Concrete:** See and compare pre-shaded shapes (is this the same amount?)  
2. **Relational:** Notice the pattern across multiple pairs and shapes  
3. **Abstract:** Name the pattern with "equivalent fractions" vocabulary  
4. **Application:** Verify equivalence using number lines; generate equivalent fractions

### **Lesson Structure**

| Section | Focus | Interactions | CRA Phase |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | Grid Array Discovery (1/2 \= 2/4) | 1.1-1.3 | Concrete |
| Section 2 | Same Whole Requirement | 2.1-2.2 | Concrete |
| Section 3 | Transfer to Other Shapes | 3.1-3.3 | Relational |
| Section 4 | Vocabulary Introduction | 4.1-4.2 | Abstract |
| Section 5 | Number Line Verification | 5.1-5.3 | Application |
| Section 6 | Beginning Generation | 6.1-6.3 | Application (L2) |

---

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Grid Array Discovery**

**(Concrete Phase — 3 interactions, \~2.5 minutes)**

**Interaction 1.1: Same Amount Discovery**

* **Visual:** Two grid arrays displayed stacked vertically, SAME SIZE:  
  * Grid A (top): 2×1 array (2 parts), 1 part shaded pink, labeled "1/2"  
  * Grid B (bottom): 2×2 array (4 parts), 2 parts shaded pink, labeled "2/4"  
  * Both grids are identical in overall dimensions (same width)  
* **Guide:** "Look at these two rectangles. They're the same size—the same whole. One shows 1/2, the other shows 2/4."  
* **Guide:** "Here's the question: Do they show the SAME AMOUNT of pink?"  
* **Prompt:** "Do these show the same amount?" \[Yes / No\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Yes  
* **On Correct:** "You can see it. Different fractions, but the same amount of shading."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look at how much pink there is in each rectangle. Compare the shaded areas."

**Interaction 1.2: Visual Verification**

* **Visual:** Both grids remain stacked vertically. A vertical dashed alignment line appears at the midpoint, showing where the shading ends on both grids.  
* **Guide:** "Look at where the shading ends on each bar. I've added a line to help you see."  
* **Guide:** "The shaded parts reach the exact same point. Same amount of pink—just cut into different numbers of pieces."  
* **No prompt—verification moment.**

**Interaction 1.3: Pattern Articulation**

* **Visual:** Both grids remain stacked. Notation visible: "1/2" and "2/4"  
* **Guide:** "1/2 and 2/4 look different—different numbers, different number of pieces. But something important stayed the same."  
* **Prompt:** \[Multi-select\] "What stayed the SAME? Select all that are true."  
  * "The amount of pink shading" ✓  
  * "The number of pieces"  
  * "The size of the whole rectangle" ✓  
  * "The fraction names"  
* **Correct Answer:** First and third options  
* **On Correct:** "Yes. The shaded AMOUNT stayed the same, and we used the same size whole. That's why we can trust the comparison—different pieces, same total area."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look at the pink area in each. Look at the rectangle size. What didn't change?"

**→ SECTION 1 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 2\.**

---

### **1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Same Whole Requirement**

**(Critical Constraint — 2 interactions, \~2 minutes)**

**Interaction 2.1: Same Whole Check**

* **Visual:** Two grid arrays of DIFFERENT sizes displayed side by side:  
  * Small grid (left): 2×1 (2 parts), 1 shaded, labeled "1/2"  
  * Large grid (right): 2×2 (4 parts), 2 shaded, labeled "2/4"  
  * The large grid is visibly bigger than the small grid (different overall dimensions)  
* **Guide:** "Wait—can we always compare fractions this way? Look at these two shapes."  
* **Guide:** "Are these the same size whole?"  
* **Prompt:** "Can we compare these fairly?" \[Yes, they match / No, different sizes\]  
* **Correct Answer:** No, different sizes  
* **On Correct:** "Good. These rectangles are different sizes. We can't compare fairly."  
* **Remediation (Medium):** "Look at the overall size of each rectangle, not just the shading. Are the rectangles themselves the same size?"

**Interaction 2.2: Same Whole Rule**

* **Visual:** Return to two SAME-SIZE grids (1/2 and 2/4) stacked vertically with a checkmark badge: "Same Size Whole ✓"  
* **Guide:** "Here's the rule: To compare fractions fairly, we need the SAME WHOLE—shapes that are the same size."  
* **Guide:** "1/2 of a small rectangle is NOT the same as 1/2 of a big rectangle—they're different amounts of stuff\!"  
* **Guide:** "But when the wholes are the same size, we can trust what our eyes see."  
* **No prompt—rule establishment.** 

**→ SECTION 2 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 3\.**

### **1.7.3 LESSON SECTION 3: Transfer to Other Shapes**

**(Relational Phase — 3 interactions, \~2.5 minutes)**

**Interaction 3.1: Circle Equivalence**

* **Visual:** Two circles stacked vertically, SAME SIZE:  
  * Circle A (top): Divided into halves (1 radial cut), 1 half shaded, labeled "1/2"  
  * Circle B (bottom): Divided into fourths (2 radial cuts), 2 quarters shaded, labeled "2/4"  
  * Dashed alignment line at midpoint showing shaded areas match  
* **Guide:** "Let's try circles. These are the same size—same whole. One shows 1/2, one shows 2/4."  
* **Prompt:** "Do these circles show the same amount of shading?" \[Yes / No\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Yes  
* **On Correct:** "Same pattern. Different cuts, but the same amount of pizza. 1/2 equals 2/4 on circles too."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look at how much of each circle is shaded. Same amount?"

**Interaction 3.2: Hexagon Equivalence (1/2 \= 3/6)**

* **Visual:** Two hexagons stacked vertically, SAME SIZE:  
  * Hexagon A (top): Divided into halves, 1 half shaded, labeled "1/2"  
  * Hexagon B (bottom): Divided into sixths (6 triangular pieces), 3 triangles shaded, labeled "3/6"  
* **Guide:** "Now hexagons. Same size whole. One shows 1/2, one shows 3/6."  
* **Prompt:** "Do these show the same amount?" \[Yes / No\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Yes  
* **On Correct:** "Yes. 3 out of 6 pieces equals half the hexagon. 1/2 \= 3/6."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Count how many pieces are shaded in each. Compare the total shaded area."

**Interaction 3.3: Pattern Consolidation**

* **Visual:** Gallery view showing all three pairs stacked:  
  * Grids: 1/2 \= 2/4 ✓  
  * Circles: 1/2 \= 2/4 ✓  
  * Hexagons: 1/2 \= 3/6 ✓  
* **Guide:** "Look at what you discovered across three different shapes."  
* **Prompt:** \[Multi-select\] "What's TRUE about ALL three pairs? Select all that apply."  
  * "They all show the same amount shaded" ✓  
  * "They all use the same shape"  
  * "They all use the same size whole" ✓  
  * "They all have the same numbers"  
* **Correct Answer:** First and third options  
* **On Correct:** "That's the pattern\! Different shapes, different numbers of pieces—but the SAME AMOUNT shaded each time, on the SAME SIZE whole. That's what makes them equal."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Compare the shaded areas. Compare the whole sizes. What's true for all three?"

**→ SECTION 3 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 4\.**

### **1.7.4 LESSON SECTION 4: Vocabulary Introduction**

**(Abstract Phase — 2 interactions, \~1.5 minutes)**

**Interaction 4.1: Naming the Pattern \[Vocabulary\_Staging\]**

### **Required Phrases (Must Appear in Script)**

* "Same whole" (equivalence requires identical-sized shapes)  
* "Same amount" or "same size" (before "equivalent")  
* "EQUIVALENT FRACTIONS" (formal vocabulary, introduced after visual grounding)  
* "Different fractions, same amount" or equivalent phrasing  
* "Same point on the number line" (verification language)  
* "To compare fractions fairly, we need the SAME WHOLE"  
* "1/2 equals 2/4" or "1/2 \= 2/4" (explicit equivalence statement)  
* "Let's prove it" or "Let's check" (verification language)  
* "You just created an equivalent fraction" (generation celebration)

### **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**

* ❌ "Double both numbers" or "multiply by 2" (procedural pattern—save for later)  
* ❌ "Simplify" or "reduce" (Grade 4 vocabulary)  
* ❌ "The rule is..." followed by multiplication/division pattern  
* ❌ "Improper fraction" (not in scope for M8)  
* ❌ "Greater than" or "less than" with symbols (comparison is M10+)  
* ❌ "Close enough" (undermines exact equality)  
* ❌ Any comparison across different-sized wholes without flagging it

### **Misconception Prevention**

**\#7 (Difficulty Recognizing Equivalence):**

* Section 1 uses stacked comparison with alignment line to PROVE visual match  
* Multiple representations (grids, circles, hexagons) reinforce same concept  
* Section 5 adds number line verification as third proof method  
* Language emphasizes "same amount" before "equivalent"

**\#6 (Reversing Numerator/Denominator):**

* All fractions shown with notation alongside visuals  
* "Shaded over total" pattern maintained from prior modules  
* If error occurs, reference grid: "How many shaded? That's the top number."

**\#10 (Overgeneralizing Rules):**

* Section 2 explicitly teaches Same Whole Requirement  
* Counterexample shows WHY different-sized wholes don't work  
* Visual proof always precedes any verbal claim

**Same Whole Confusion:**

* Section 2 devoted entirely to this concept  
* Every comparison includes same-size verification  
* Counterexample (Interaction 2.1) makes the rule concrete

## **1.9 SYNTHESIS (6-8 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

### **Purpose**

Connect equivalent fraction concepts through pattern discovery across multiple visual representations. Recognize that equivalent fractions ALWAYS show the same amount regardless of tool or context. Transfer understanding to real-world fair sharing contexts. Consolidate the "Same Whole" requirement and visual proof strategies. This is NOT more practice—it's connection-making and generalization.

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 6 (Opening \+ 4 connection tasks \+ metacognitive reflection \+ closure) |
| Task Types | Pattern Discovery (Type A, two-step), Representation Transfer (Type D, active), Real-World Bridge (Type C, progressive \+ multi-select), Generalization |
| Remediation | Light only—mastery assumed |
| Tone | Reflective, warm, discovering together |
| Energy | Medium (not exhausted, not high-energy) |
| Interaction Style | Active (placement, multi-select) over passive selection |

### **Constraints**

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Active interactions (place, multi-select) | Passive selection-only tasks |
| Pattern observation (numbers getting larger) | Teach doubling as a PROCEDURE |
| Progressive reveal for real-world | Static displays |
| Use multiple representations to show transfer | Stick to single representation |
| Light remediation if needed | Extended teaching or Heavy remediation |
| Specific identity closure with M9 preview | Generic praise ("Great job\!") |

---

### **Opening Frame (30-45 seconds)**

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from Exit Check to reflection/connection.  
* **Visual:** Gallery showing the four equivalent fractions: 1/2 \= 2/4 \= 3/6 \= 4/8, each with a small visual (grid, circle, hexagon, number line).  
* **Guide:** "You've been identifying and creating equivalent fractions. Now let's discover what makes them ALL work the same way."

**Task S.1: Pattern Discovery — Two Steps (Type A)**

**Step 1: Visual Pattern**

* **Purpose:** Recognize the underlying visual pattern (same amount shaded).  
* **Visual:** Three bars stacked vertically, SAME SIZE:  
  * Bar 1: 1/2 (1 of 2 parts shaded)  
  * Bar 2: 2/4 (2 of 4 parts shaded)  
  * Bar 3: 4/8 (4 of 8 parts shaded)

* **Guide:** "Look at these three equivalent fractions. They all equal 1/2. What's the SAME about all of them?"  
* **Prompt:** \[Multi-select\] "Select ALL that are true:"  
  * "Half of each bar is shaded" ✓  
  * "They use the same number of pieces"  
  * "The shaded amount looks equal" ✓  
* **Correct Answer:** First and third options  
* **On Correct:** "You spotted it\! No matter how many pieces—2, 4, or 8—exactly HALF is always shaded. The amount stays the same."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look at how much of each bar is pink. Is it the same amount?"

**Step 2: Numerical Pattern**

* **Purpose:** Observe what happens to the numbers (consolidation, not procedure).  
* **Visual:** Same three bars remain. Numbers are highlighted: 1/2, 2/4, 4/8.  
* **Guide:** "Now look at the NUMBERS. What happens to the top and bottom numbers as we go down?"  
* **Prompt:** \[Selection\]

  * "Both numbers get smaller"  
  * "Only the top number changes"  
* **Correct Answer:** "Both numbers get larger"  
* **On Correct:** "Right\! Both numbers get larger—but the AMOUNT stays the same. That's how equivalent fractions work: different numbers, same value."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Compare 1/2 to 2/4. Did the numbers get larger or smaller?"
