## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**You now have TWO comparison strategies: When denominators match, compare how many pieces. When NUMERATORS match, compare piece SIZE—larger denominator means smaller pieces, so the fraction with the smaller denominator is greater.**

**Critical Misconception:** \#8 (Comparison Errors) \+ \#10 (Overgeneralization)—Students discover that 3/4 \> 2/4 because "3 \> 2" (M10) and incorrectly apply this to 3/8 vs 3/4, claiming "8 \> 4 so 3/8 is greater." The counterintuitive insight that larger denominators mean SMALLER pieces must be visually grounded before any shortcut.

**Success Indicator:** Student compares 2/3 and 2/6 by stating "Both have 2 pieces, so I compare piece size. Thirds are bigger than sixths because 3 parts means each is larger than when you have 6 parts. So 2/3 \> 2/6." When asked "Could you compare 2/3 and 5/6 this way?" student says "No—different numerators AND different denominators. I'd need a different method."

**Biggest Risk:** Rushing to the "smaller denominator wins" shortcut without grounding in piece-size reasoning. If students memorize "smaller denominator \= bigger fraction" without understanding WHY (fewer cuts \= bigger pieces), they will confuse it with the M10 rule and apply rules inconsistently.

### **1.1.2 Module Bridges**

**From Module 10 (Compare Same Denominators):**  
Students can compare fractions with the same denominator using \>, \<, \= symbols with visual justification. They understand that same denominator means same-sized pieces, so comparison is simply counting pieces. Critically, they learned the CONDITION for this shortcut—it only works when denominators match. M10 Synthesis (Task S.4) explicitly previewed M11 by showing 2/3 vs 2/5 and noting "different denominators means we need a different method."

**This Module:**  
Students reinforce same-denominator comparison with extended visual practice (L1), then discover the complementary strategy for same-numerator comparison (L2). The key insight is counterintuitive: when you have the SAME NUMBER of pieces, the fraction with BIGGER pieces is greater—and bigger pieces come from SMALLER denominators (fewer cuts \= larger pieces). Students learn to identify WHICH strategy applies by checking what matches first. By end of lesson, students have TWO comparison Toys and can apply them flexibly.

**To Module 12 (Compare Unlike Fractions — Benchmarks):**  
Students will encounter fractions where NEITHER numerator NOR denominator matches (e.g., 3/4 vs 5/8). The M10-M11 strategies won't directly apply—students need benchmark reasoning (comparing each to 1/2, comparing to 1, etc.). M11's explicit "check what matches" decision process prepares students to recognize WHEN they need yet another strategy.

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

**Same Denominator Reinforcement (L1):**

* Visual comparison with extended denominators (up to 12ths)  
* Ordering 3-4 same-denominator fractions  
* Explicit restatement of M10 condition: "This works because denominators match"

**Same Numerator Comparison (L2):**

* Same numerator \= same count of pieces → compare piece SIZE  
* Larger denominator \= more cuts \= smaller pieces  
* Smaller denominator \= fewer cuts \= bigger pieces \= greater fraction  
* Visual proof using identical wholes with different partitioning  
* Unit fraction comparison as special case (numerator \= 1\)

**Strategy Integration:**

* Decision process: "First check—what's the same?"  
* Distinguishing when each strategy applies  
* Vocabulary reinforcement: compare, greater than, less than, equal to, same denominator, same numerator

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* Comparing fractions with DIFFERENT numerators AND different denominators (Module 12\)  
* Benchmark reasoning ("closer to 1/2") — Module 12  
* Finding common denominators algorithmically — Grade 4  
* Cross-multiplication — Grade 4+  
* Fractions greater than 1 in comparisons  
* Symbolic comparison without visual grounding (visuals required throughout M11)

⚠️ **SAME WHOLE PRINCIPLE (per 3.NF.A.3.d):** Same-numerator comparisons are ONLY valid when fractions refer to the same whole. If comparing 2/3 vs 2/6 visually, both shapes must be identical in size. This is non-negotiable—comparing pieces from different-sized wholes produces invalid results.

⚠️ **OVERGENERALIZATION TRAP:** Students may try to apply the new "smaller denominator \= greater" rule universally. ALWAYS pair the shortcut with its condition: "When numerators are the same..." If a student claims 3/8 \> 5/6 because "8 \< 6 so eighths are bigger," redirect: "Wait—do these have the same numerator? 3 and 5 are different, so we can't use that strategy."

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Comparison Strategy Errors (\#8) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student sees 3/4 vs 3/8 and claims "3/8 is greater because 8 is bigger than 4."

**Why It Happens:**  
Students learned in M10 that "bigger number \= greater fraction" when comparing numerators (with same denominators). They overapply this pattern to denominators, not recognizing that denominators work inversely—larger denominator means MORE cuts, which means SMALLER pieces.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Display two identical shapes side by side: one cut into 4 equal parts, one cut into 8 equal parts. Highlight ONE piece from each. The visual makes obvious that fourths are larger than eighths.

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. Ground EVERY same-numerator comparison in visual FIRST  
2. Ask "Which pieces are BIGGER?" before asking "Which fraction is greater?"  
3. Use the pizza/cake analogy: "If you cut a pizza into 4 slices vs 8 slices, which slices are bigger?"  
4. State the relationship explicitly: "Fewer cuts \= bigger pieces"

**Remediation Progression:**

- **Light:** "Look at the shaded pieces. Which ones are bigger?"  
- **Medium:** "Both have \[n\] pieces shaded. But are the pieces the same size? Which pieces are bigger—the fourths or the eighths?"  
- **Heavy:** \[Visual highlight\] "See how fourths are bigger pieces than eighths? When pieces are bigger, the fraction is greater. 3/4 \> 3/8."

---

### **1.4.2 Overgeneralization (\#10) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
After learning that "smaller denominator \= greater fraction" for same-numerator comparisons, student applies this rule to ALL comparisons, claiming 3/8 \> 5/6 because "8 \< 6."

**Why It Happens:**  
Students seek efficient rules and may latch onto "smaller denominator wins" without recognizing the CONDITION (same numerator required). This mirrors the M10 pattern where students overgeneralized "larger numerator wins."

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Show a comparison where the rule doesn't apply (different numerators AND denominators). Ask student to identify what matches—when neither matches, surface the limitation.

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. ALWAYS pair the shortcut with its condition: "When NUMERATORS are the same..."  
2. Include "identify which strategy" tasks in Section 3 and Exit Check  
3. End lesson with a "neither matches" example (3.6) to show limitation  
4. Frame as decision process: "First check—what's the same?"

**Remediation Progression:**

- **Light:** "Wait—check the numerators. Are they the same?"  
- **Medium:** "For 'smaller denominator \= greater' to work, the numerators must match. Do 3 and 5 match? No—so we can't use that strategy here."  
- **Heavy:** "Let me show you. \[Visual\] This strategy only works when you have the SAME NUMBER of pieces. Here you have 3 pieces vs 5 pieces—different amounts. We need a different approach."

---

### **1.4.3 Whole Confusion — SECONDARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student compares 2/3 of a small circle to 2/6 of a large rectangle and makes invalid conclusions about piece size.

**Why It Happens:**  
Students may not recognize that comparing piece SIZE requires identical wholes. If the wholes are different sizes, the "pieces" aren't comparable.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
All same-numerator comparisons use TWO IDENTICAL shapes side by side. Never mix shape types or sizes for this comparison type.

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. Always use identical shapes for same-numerator comparison  
2. Explicitly state: "To compare pieces fairly, we need the SAME WHOLE"  
3. If different shapes must appear, use only for same-denominator comparison (where piece size doesn't matter—only count)

**Remediation Progression:**

- **Light:** "Are both shapes the same size? We need identical wholes to compare pieces fairly."  
- **Medium:** "To compare piece sizes, we need shapes that start the same. Look—these are both the same hexagon."  
- **Heavy:** \[Show identical shapes side by side\] "See how both wholes are exactly the same size? Now when we compare the pieces, it's a fair comparison."

## **1.5 Toy SPECIFICATIONS**

### **1.5.1 Grid Arrays (Primary for Both Strategies)**

#### **Core Specifications**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Purpose** | Visual comparison showing both piece count AND piece size |
| **Configuration** | Two identical grids displayed side by side (horizontal arrangement) |
| **Grid Sizing** | Rows × columns match denominator (e.g., 1×8 for eighths, 2×6 for twelfths, 2×4 for eighths) |
| **Shading** | Shaded parts in pink; unshaded in blue (consistent with prior modules) |
| **Same Whole Requirement** | For same-numerator comparison, both grids MUST be identical dimensions |
| **Interaction** | Student may click to count parts; comparison symbol selector appears between grids |
| **Feedback** | Correct symbol highlights; incorrect triggers visual emphasis on relevant feature (count or size) |

#### **Same-Denominator Display (L1)**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Grid Partitioning** | Both grids partitioned identically |
| **Visual Emphasis** | Shaded squares easily countable |
| **Comparison Cue** | "Count the shaded parts" |

#### **Same-Numerator Display (L2)**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Grid Partitioning** | Grids partitioned differently (same dimensions, different internal divisions) |
| **Piece Size Visibility** | Individual piece size visually obvious (e.g., 2/3 shows 2 large parts, 2/6 shows 2 small parts) |

### **1.5.2 Circles (Pizza/Pie Context)**

#### **Core Specifications**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Purpose** | Real-world context for piece-size reasoning (pizza slices) |
| **Configuration** | Two identical circles with radial partitions, side by side |
| **Partitioning Options** | Halves (2), thirds (3), fourths (4), sixths (6), eighths (8) |
| **Shading** | Shaded slices represent numerator |
| **Same Whole Requirement** | Both circles identical size for same-numerator comparison |
| **Interaction** | Count slices; select comparison symbol |

#### **Piece Size Emphasis**

For same-numerator comparisons, circles make the "fewer cuts \= bigger pieces" principle viscerally clear:

- "If you cut a pizza into 4 slices, each slice is big."  
- "If you cut the SAME pizza into 8 slices, each slice is smaller."  
- "3 big slices (3/4) vs 3 small slices (3/8)—which gives you more pizza?"

### **1.5.3 Hexagons (Pattern Block Context)**

#### **Core Specifications**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Purpose** | VPSS-specific Toy for thirds and sixths comparison |
| **Configuration** | Two identical hexagons side by side |
| **Partitioning Options** | Halves (2 trapezoids), thirds (3 rhombuses), sixths (6 triangles) |
| **Natural Affordance** | 2/3 vs 2/6 comparison: 2 rhombuses vs 2 triangles — size difference is dramatic |
| **Same Whole Requirement** | Both hexagons identical size |

#### **Recommended Use**

Hexagons are ideal for introducing same-numerator comparison because the piece-size difference is visually striking:

- 1/3 (one rhombus) vs 1/6 (one triangle) — obvious size difference  
- 2/3 (two rhombuses) vs 2/6 (two triangles) — same count, very different coverage

### **1.5.4 Number Lines (Verification/Transfer Toy)**

#### **Core Specifications**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Purpose** | Position-based verification of comparison (connects to M9-M10 reasoning) |
| **Configuration** | Single number line (0 to 1\) with two points marked |
| **Partitioning** | Tick marks match one or both denominators as appropriate |
| **Comparison Visual** | "Further from zero \= greater" |
| **Interaction** | Points pre-placed; student selects comparison symbol |

#### **Position Reasoning**

Number line reinforces that comparison results are consistent across representations:

- 3/4 lands further from zero than 3/8  
- This confirms the shape-based reasoning: bigger pieces \= greater fraction \= further right

#### **Use Cases**

- **Section 1 (Same Denominator):** Quick verification that position matches count reasoning  
- **Section 2 (Same Numerator):** Verification that bigger pieces \= further from zero  
- **Synthesis:** Transfer task showing both strategies produce correct position results

- Symbol selection via \[\<\] \[=\] \[\>\] button interface  
- Questions in Guide speech must be either rhetorical (Guide answers) or answered through on-screen action  
- For same-numerator comparison, both shapes must ALWAYS be identical wholes

## **1.7 LESSON (10-12 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

### **Core Purpose**

Reinforce same-denominator comparison with extended visual practice (L1), then teach same-numerator comparison through piece-size reasoning (L2). By end of lesson, students have two comparison strategies and can identify which applies by asking "What's the same?"

### **Pedagogical Flow**

This lesson has two distinct learning goals requiring a two-part structure:

- **Section 1 (Reinforcement):** Brief M10 callback \+ extension to larger denominators. Explicitly names the "same denominator" condition before introducing the contrasting case.  
- **Section 2 (CRA for New Concept):** Students discover same-numerator comparison through visual grounding. The counterintuitive insight (larger denominator \= smaller pieces) requires concrete experience before the shortcut.  
- **Section 3 (Integration):** Extended practice identifying which strategy applies and executing it. Multiple strategy identification tasks build the decision process.

| Section | Focus | Interactions | Time |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | Same Denominator Reinforcement (L1) | 1.1–1.3 | \~2.5 min |
| Section 2 | Same Numerator Discovery (L2) | 2.1–2.6 | \~4.5 min |
| Section 3 | Strategy Integration (Extended) | 3.1–3.6 (with sub-interactions) | \~5 min |

### **Required Phrases (Must Appear in Script)**

* "Same denominator" (condition for counting strategy)  
* "Same-sized pieces" or "same size pieces" (why counting works)  
* "Same numerator" (condition for size strategy)  
* "Piece size" or "size of the pieces" (size strategy reasoning)  
* "Smaller denominator means bigger pieces" or "fewer cuts means bigger pieces"  
* "What's the same?" or "What matches?" (decision process)  
* "comparison strategy" or "comparison strategies" (naming the Toys without numbering)

### **Forbidden Phrases (Create Misconceptions)**

* ❌ "Just compare the numbers" (promotes overgeneralization)  
* ❌ "Bigger is always better" (ambiguous—bigger what?)  
* ❌ "The rule is..." without the condition (invites misapplication)  
* ❌ "Common denominator" (Grade 4 vocabulary)  
* ❌ "Benchmark" or "compare to 1/2" (Module 12\)  
* ❌ "Cross multiply" (Grade 4+)

---

### **Misconception Prevention**

**\#8 (Comparison Errors — "larger denominator \= larger fraction"):**

* Interactions 2.1-2.4 ground piece-size reasoning in visual before any shortcut  
* Pizza/slice analogy makes "more cuts \= smaller pieces" intuitive  
* Number line verification (2.6) confirms position matches piece-size reasoning  
* Explicit verbalization: "Smaller denominator means fewer cuts, bigger pieces"

**\#10 (Overgeneralization):**

* Interaction 1.3 explicitly names the condition for counting strategy  
* Section 3 has 5 strategy identification tasks requiring "what's the same?" thinking  
* Interaction 3.6 surfaces the limitation—neither strategy works for unlike both  
* Summary consolidation frames both strategies with their conditions

- [ ] Missing any required phrase  
- [ ] Contains any forbidden phrase

**Ready for Module 12:** Students recognize that when NEITHER numerator NOR denominator matches, they need a different approach (benchmark strategies).
