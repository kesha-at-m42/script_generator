## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**Students must understand that non-unit fractions are BUILT by composing multiple copies of the same unit fraction—3/4 means "1/4 \+ 1/4 \+ 1/4"—and that the NUMERATOR counts how many unit fraction pieces are shaded while the DENOMINATOR names the type of parts. When we compose enough unit fractions to fill the whole, n/n \= 1\.**

**Critical Misconception:** \#3 (Numerator/Denominator as Independent)—Students may see the two numbers as unrelated labels rather than understanding: the numerator COUNTS how many unit-fraction pieces, and the denominator tells the SIZE/TYPE of each piece.

**Success Indicator:** Student can build any non-unit fraction by shading the correct number of parts, explain that the numerator counts shaded parts, AND recognize that when numerator equals denominator (4/4, 6/6), the fraction equals one whole.

**Biggest Risk:** Students memorizing "count shaded, count total" as a disconnected procedure without understanding that non-unit fractions are composed FROM unit fractions (3/4 \= 1/4 \+ 1/4 \+ 1/4). Also: rushing past the n/n \= 1 discovery without celebrating it.

* Recognizes that n/n \= 1 (when numerator equals denominator, we have one whole)

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

* Non-unit fractions as composed from unit fractions (3/4 \= 1/4 \+ 1/4 \+ 1/4)  
* The numerator COUNTS how many unit-fraction parts are shaded  
* Formal term "numerator" for the top number (PRIMARY FOCUS)  
* Formal term "denominator" for the bottom number (INTRODUCED, not focus)  
* The denominator tells the type/size of parts; stays the same as we compose  
* Building fractions by shading multiple parts progressively  
* **Key discovery:** When numerator equals denominator (n/n), the fraction equals 1 whole  
* Compose to non-unit: 2/3, 3/4, 2/4, 3/6, 5/6  
* Compose to wholes: 3/3 \= 1, 4/4 \= 1, 5/5 \= 1, 6/6 \= 1, 8/8 \= 1  
* Extension: 3/8, 5/8, 7/8  
* All shapes: grids, circles, hexagons, pentagons (grids primary; others for variety)

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* Deep exploration of denominator meaning (Module 4's focus)  
* Number line representations (Module 4-5)  
* Fractions greater than 1 / improper fractions (Module 6\)  
* Equivalent fractions (Module 8-9)  
* Comparing fractions with symbols (Module 10-12)  
* Fractions as division concepts (later unit)  
* Mixed numbers (Module 7\)

⚠️ **"Composition Before Naming" Principle:** Students should BUILD the fraction by shading unit fraction pieces BEFORE seeing or writing the notation. The action of composing (shading one part, then another, then another) must precede the abstract symbol.

⚠️ **Numerator FOCUS / Denominator INTRODUCED:** Both terms are taught in this module. Numerator gets the discovery treatment—it's the star. Denominator is named and explained ("tells us how many equal parts make up the whole" / "tells us the type of parts" / "stays the same as we add parts") but Module 4 will be the denominator-focused module.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Numerator/Denominator as Independent (\#3) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student treats the numerator and denominator as unrelated numbers. When asked "What does 3/4 mean?", they say "three and four" rather than "three parts out of four equal parts" or "three one-fourths." They don't connect the numerator to COUNTING shaded parts or the denominator to the SIZE of parts.

**Why It Happens:**  
Students have learned to read multi-digit numbers where digits have independent place value. A fraction LOOKS like two numbers, so they apply whole-number thinking. Without explicit instruction connecting each number to a visual action, the relationship remains invisible.

#### **Path C Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Grid array with explicit counting: "Watch the numerator grow as we shade: 1... 2... 3\. Three parts shaded. The denominator stays at 4 because we're still working with fourths."

**Prevention Strategy:**

* Always BUILD fractions through progressive shading (1/4 → 2/4 → 3/4)  
* Explicitly connect numerator to the COUNT of shaded parts  
* Show that denominator names the TYPE of parts and doesn't change as we compose  
* Use production language: "The numerator says 'I have three parts' and the denominator says 'out of four equal parts total'"

---

### **1.4.2 Reversing Numerator and Denominator (\#6) — SECONDARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student writes or identifies fractions with numbers swapped. When 3 parts of 4 are shaded, they might write 4/3 instead of 3/4.

**Why It Happens:**

* "Total" feels more important, so they put it first  
* Confusion about which number "goes where"  
* Reading top-to-bottom doesn't match their intuition

#### **Path C Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Progressive building with running count: "Shaded parts on top, total parts on bottom. We've shaded 1, 2, 3 parts—that's our numerator. The bar has 4 total parts—that's our denominator. 3/4."

**Prevention Strategy:**

* Always count shaded FIRST (what goes on top)  
* Building pattern reinforces: what we ADD goes on top (numerator grows)  
* "Shaded Over Total" pattern from M2 reinforced  
* Denominator established first (the bar already has 4 parts), then numerator grows

---

### **1.4.3 Procedural Counting Without Understanding — TERTIARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student can correctly identify numerator and denominator by rote ("count shaded, count total") but cannot explain that 3/4 means "three one-fourths" or build 3/4 from unit fractions.

**Why It Happens:**  
Instruction focused on labeling rather than composition. Student never experienced building a fraction piece by piece.

#### **Path C Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Explicit addition notation alongside visual: "1/4 \+ 1/4 \+ 1/4 \= 3/4"

**Prevention Strategy:**

* NEVER skip progressive building (1/4 → 2/4 → 3/4 → 4/4)  
* Show explicit addition to connect composition to notation  
* Celebrate n/n \= 1 discovery to cement "filling the whole" concept  
* Ask "How many one-fourths did we use?" not just "What's the numerator?"

**Interaction W.1: Unit Fraction Recognition**

* **Purpose:** Activate M2 knowledge—students can identify unit fractions by counting total parts.  
* **Hook:** Callback to M2 learning—"You learned about unit fractions in the last lesson. Let's review some of the key concepts."  
* **Guide:** “To start, please select the bar with one out of three equal parts shaded.”  
* **Visual:** Three pre-partitioned and shaded tall rectangles  3 x 1, 2 x 2, and 3 x 2:  
  * Bar A: 3 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/3)  
  * Bar B: 4 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/4)  
  * Bar C: 6 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/6)  
* **Task:** Student selects the bar showing 1/3.  
* **Remediation (Light):** Direct attention to counting total parts.

**Interaction W.2: Size Comparison**

* **Purpose:** Activate M2 inverse relationship understanding (bigger bottom number \= smaller piece).  
* **Visual:** Two pre-partitioned and shaded wide rectangles side by side:  
  * Bar A: 2 x 2, 4 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/4) — labeled  
  * Bar B: 3 x 2, 6 equal parts, 1 shaded (1/6) — labeled  
* **Guide:** “Now, which of these two bars has bigger shaded pieces?”  
* **Task:** Student selects the bar with the BIGGER shaded piece.  
* **Remediation (Light):** Direct attention to visual size of shaded pieces; connect to "fewer parts \= bigger pieces."

**Interaction W.3: Bridge to Lesson**

* **Purpose:** Create curiosity about non-unit fractions without teaching.  
* **Visual:** Keep one unit fraction bar visible; tease additional shading.  
* **Content:**  
  * Acknowledge what student knows: "Unit fractions always show just ONE part."  
  * Create curiosity gap: "But what if you want to show TWO parts? Or THREE parts?"  
  * Tease Lesson: "Let's find out how to build bigger fractions."  
* **No student action.**

## **1.7 LESSON (10-14 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

### **Core Purpose**

Develop understanding that non-unit fractions are BUILT by composing multiple unit fractions (3/4 \= 1/4 \+ 1/4 \+ 1/4). Students discover that the numerator COUNTS shaded parts, learn formal vocabulary (numerator as FOCUS, denominator as introduced), and celebrate the discovery that n/n \= 1\.

### **Pedagogical Flow**

Module 3 builds on Module 2's unit fraction foundation. All shapes are pre-partitioned—students focus on COMPOSING (shading multiple parts) and UNDERSTANDING what the numbers mean. The lesson follows the production lesson pattern:

1. **Composition Foundation** — Build non-unit fractions through progressive shading  
2. **Vocabulary Introduction** — Numerator (FOCUS) and Denominator (introduced)  
3. **Building to One Whole** — Discover n/n \= 1  
4. **Transfer & Consolidation** — Apply to different denominators

### **Lesson Structure**

| Section | Focus | Interactions |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | Composition Foundation (Fourths) | 1.1 – 1.5 |
| Section 2 | Vocabulary Introduction | 2.1 – 2.4 |
| Section 3 | Building to One Whole (n/n \= 1\) | 3.1 – 3.3 |
| Section 4 | Transfer to Sixths & Consolidation | 4.1 – 4.4 |

---

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Composition Foundation**

**Interaction 1.1: Unit Fraction Review \+ Setup**

* **Visual:** Pre-partitioned grid array showing 4 equal parts (tall  2×2), with ONE part shaded (pink).   
* **Guide:** "Look at this **fraction model**. See how only one piece is shaded? That's one-fourth, —a unit fraction. Remember, a unit fraction always has just one part shaded out of the whole."  
* **No prompt—observation moment.**

**Interaction 1.2: First Composition (1/4 → 2/4)**

* **Visual:** Same grid from 1.1 with one part shaded; three unshaded parts available.  
* **Guide:** "But sometimes we want to show MORE than one part. Watch what happens when we add another fourth."  
* **Prompt:** "Click one unshaded part to add another fourth."  
* **Remediation:** Full L-M-H (composition focus)  
* **On Correct:** "You just shaded another fourth."

**Interaction 1.3: Counting Connection**

* **Visual:** Grid now showing 2/4 (two parts shaded).  
* **Guide:** "Now we have one-fourth PLUS another one-fourth. Count the shaded parts with me: one part shaded, two parts shaded. Look at that—we just made two-fourths\! When we combine unit fractions, we're making a bigger fraction."  
* **No prompt—counting and naming moment.**

**Interaction 1.4: Continue Building (2/4 → 3/4)**

* **Visual:** Grid from 1.3 showing 2/4. Two unshaded parts remain.  
* **Guide:** "Now we have 2/4. Time to create an even bigger fraction."  
* **Prompt:** "Click an unshaded part to create 3/4."  
* **Remediation:** Full L-M-H (composition focus)  
* **On Correct:** "Right. You put together three parts. That's 3/4."

**Interaction 1.5: Explicit Addition**

* **Visual:** Grid showing 3/4. Display explicit addition: "1/4 \+ 1/4 \+ 1/4 \= 3/4" appears nearby.  
* **Guide:** "We built 3/4 by adding 1/4 \+ 1/4 \+ 1/4. Three parts, each the size of one-fourth. That's what 3/4 means—three of these fourth-sized pieces."  
* **No prompt—composition insight moment.**

**→ SECTION 1 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 2\.**

---

### **1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Vocabulary Introduction**

**Interaction 2.1: Counting Shaded Parts**

* **Visual:** Grid showing 3/4 with fraction notation prominent. The "3" and "4" are visually distinct.  
* **Guide:** "Now look at this fraction: 3/4. To figure out what these numbers mean, let's start by counting. How many parts are shaded?"  
* **Prompt:** "How many shaded parts?" \[Options: 1, 2, 3\]  
* **Remediation:** Light only  
* **On Correct:** "Right. Three shaded parts. The top number counts how many of the parts are shaded."

**Interaction 2.2: Introduce NUMERATOR**

* **Visual:** Grid showing 3/4. The "3" in the fraction notation is highlighted/emphasized.  
* **Guide:** "WeMath experts call this top number the **NUMERATOR**. Here, we can count 3 shaded parts. So 3 is the numerator."  
* **Vocabulary:** Introduce "numerator" — PRIMARY FOCUS term  
* **No prompt—vocabulary introduction moment.**

**Interaction 2.3: Count Total Parts \+ Introduce DENOMINATOR**

* **Visual:** Grid showing 3/4 with fraction notation prominent. Visual animation/highlight counts ALL four parts (shaded and unshaded).  
* **Guide:** "Now look at the 4 on the bottom. Let's count ALL the parts in the whole bar: one, two, three, four. Four parts total. The bottom number tells us how many equal parts make up the WHOLE thing."  
* **Guide:** "This bottom number is called the DENOMINATOR. It tells us how to name the type of parts we're working with. When the denominator is 4, we're working with fourths."  
* **Vocabulary:** Introduce "denominator" — named and explained, but not the focus  
* **No prompt—vocabulary introduction moment.**

**Interaction 2.4: Vocabulary Connection**

* **Visual:** Grid showing 3/4 with both "3" (numerator) and "4" (denominator) labeled.  
* **Guide:** "So in 3/4: the numerator 3 says 'I have three parts' and the denominator 4 says 'out of four equal parts total.' Watch how the numerator changes as we add parts, but the denominator stays the same."  
* **No prompt—key insight moment.**

**→ SECTION 2 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 3\.**

---

### **1.7.3 LESSON SECTION 3: Building to One Whole (n/n \= 1\)**

**Interaction 3.1: Build to 4/4**

* **Visual:** Grid showing 3/4 (three shaded, one unshaded). Fraction notation visible.  
* **Guide:** "We have 3/4. Let’s shade that last unshaded part to make one whole."  
* **Prompt:** "Can you shade a part to complete the whole?"  
* **Remediation:** Full L-M-H (completing whole focus)  
* **On Correct:** "Nice. All four parts are shaded now."

**Interaction 3.2: CELEBRATE n/n \= 1 Discovery**

* **Visual:** Grid showing 4/4 (all four parts shaded). Notation shows "4/4 \= 1" with celebration visual.  
* **Guide:** "When we put together all 4 pieces to make 4/4, we get ONE WHOLE. The parts together make exactly 1\. When we add up enough unit fractions to fill everything, we get 1."  
* **No prompt—celebration and discovery moment.**  
* **⚠️ CRITICAL:** This is a key "aha moment"—do not rush past it.

**Interaction 3.3: Pattern Confirmation**

* **Visual:** Show the progression: 1/4 → 2/4 → 3/4 → 4/4 \= 1 as a sequence.  
* **Guide:** "Look at the pattern: 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4. Each time we added another fourth, the numerator went up by 1\. And when the numerator reached 4—the same as the denominator—we had one whole."  
* **No prompt—pattern recognition moment.**

**→ SECTION 3 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 4\.**

---

### **1.7.4 LESSON SECTION 4: Transfer to Sixths & Consolidation**

**Interaction 4.1: Apply to Sixths (Build to 5/6)**

* **Visual:** NEW pre-partitioned grid array showing 6 equal parts (wide 2×3), with ONE part pre-shaded. Notation shows "1/6".  
* **Guide:** "Let's try sixths. Start with 1/6 and build up to 5/6."  
* **Prompt:** "Click parts to make 5/6."  
* **Remediation:** Full L-M-H (multi-shade composition focus)  
* **On Correct:** "Right. You built 5/6 step by step."

**Interaction 4.2: Explicit Addition for Sixths**

* **Visual:** Grid showing 5/6. Display explicit addition: "1/6 \+ 1/6 \+ 1/6 \+ 1/6 \+ 1/6 \= 5/6" appears.  
* **Guide:** "Adding 1/6 \+ 1/6 \+ 1/6 \+ 1/6 \+ 1/6 \= 5/6. Five parts shaded out of six. The numerator is 5, it counts the shaded parts. The denominator is 6, so it tells us we're working with sixths."  
* **No prompt—vocabulary reinforcement.**

**Interaction 4.3: Complete the Whole (6/6 \= 1\)**

* **Visual:** Grid showing 5/6 (five shaded, one unshaded).  
* **Guide:** "Please shade the last part to complete the whole."  
* **Prompt:** "Can you shade a part to complete the whole?"  
* **Remediation:** Full L-M-H (completing whole focus)  
* **On Correct:** "Right. 6/6 equals 1, or one whole\! It’s the same pattern as 4/4."

**Interaction 4.4: Consolidation**

* **Visual:** Side-by-side display: Grid showing 4/4 \= 1 and Grid showing 6/6 \= 1\.  
* **Guide:** "You've learned to build fractions by combining unit fractions. The numerator counts how many parts you've composed. When the numerator equals the denominator—like 4/4 or 6/6—you have one whole."  
* **No prompt—consolidation moment.**

**LESSON TRANSITION TO EXIT CHECK**

* **Guide:** "You've got the idea. Let's see what you know."

**→ LESSON COMPLETE.**

##    **1.9 SYNTHESIS (6-8 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

### **Purpose**

Connect non-unit fraction concepts through pattern discovery across multiple visual representations. Recognize that position/arrangement doesn't matter—only the count. Consolidate the n/n \= 1 discovery with varied denominators AND varied shapes. Apply understanding to real-world context. This is NOT more practice—it's connection-making and consolidation.

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 6 (4 connection tasks \+ metacognitive reflection \+ identity closure) |
| Task Types | Pattern Discovery (Type A), Generalization, Real-World Bridge (Type C), Pattern Confirmation |
| Shapes | Grid arrays, circles, hexagons (VPSS variety) |
| Remediation | Light only—mastery assumed |
| Tone | Reflective, warm, discovering together |
| Energy | Medium (not exhausted, not high-energy) |

### **Constraints**

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Connection tasks (pattern, real-world, generalization) | Repetitive practice drills |
| Use shape variety to show transfer | Stick to single shape type |
| Name principles students discovered | Introduce new procedures |
| Light remediation if needed | Extended teaching or Heavy remediation |
| Specific identity closure | Generic praise ("Great job\!") |
| Visual support for every task | Abstract discussion without visuals |

---

### **Opening Frame**

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from practice to reflection/connection.  
* **Content:** "You've been making fractions by adding unit parts. Now let's explore how these fractions connect to bigger mathematical ideas."

---

**Task S.1: Pattern Discovery — Position Doesn't Matter (Type A)**

* **Purpose:** Discover that the COUNT of shaded parts matters, not their position/arrangement.  
* **Shape:** Grid array  
* **Visual:** Three grid arrays showing 2/3, but arranged differently:  
  * Grid A: 3 equal parts, parts 1-2 shaded (contiguous)  
  * Grid B: 3 equal parts, parts 1 and 3 shaded (separated)  
  * Grid C: 3 equal parts, parts 2-3 shaded (contiguous)  
* **Task:** "Look at these shapes. Two have the shaded parts together, one has them separated. Do these shapes model the same fraction or different fractions?"  
* **Prompt:** Select all the shapes that model 2/3.  
* **Correct Answer:** All three selected.  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "All three show two-thirds. Two parts are shaded in each, even though they're arranged differently. The position doesn't matter—only the count."

---

**Task S.2: Generalization — n/n \= 1 Across Shapes**

* **Purpose:** Recognize that when numerator equals denominator, we have one whole—regardless of shape.  
* **Shapes:** Mixed — grid array, circle, hexagon  
* **Visual:** Three fully-shaded shapes displayed with labels:  
  * Grid array: 4 parts, all shaded → 4/4 \= 1  
  * Circle: 3 parts, all shaded → 3/3 \= 1  
  * Hexagon: 6 parts, all shaded → 6/6 \= 1  
* **Task:** "Look at these three shapes. They all equal 1\. What do you notice about the top and bottom numbers?"  
* **Prompt:** Student selects: \[The numerator is always bigger / The numerator and the denominator are equal / The denominator is always bigger\]  
* **Correct Answer:** The numerator and the denominator are equal  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "When the numerator \- top number equals the denominator \- bottom number, we always get 1\. That's because we've used ALL the parts. The shape doesn't matter—grid, circle, hexagon—the pattern works everywhere."  
* **Extension (Guide states):** "10/10 \= 1, 100/100 \= 1—any fraction with the same numerator and denominator equals one whole."

---

**Task S.3: Real-World Bridge — Pizza (Type C)**

* **Purpose:** Apply composition understanding to familiar real-world context through action.  
* **Shape:** Circle (pizza context)  
* **Visual:** Circle partitioned into 6 equal slices, unshaded. Styled as pizza.  
* **Task:** "Imagine this is a pizza with 6 slices. Your friend asks for four-sixths of it. Show me which slices you'd give them."  
* **Prompt:** Student shades 4 of the 6 slices.  
* **Correct Answer:** Any 4 slices shaded (position doesn't matter—connects to S.1)  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "That's right—4 slices out of 6\. Fractions help us talk about parts of things we share."

---

**Metacognitive Reflection**

* **Purpose:** Build awareness of composition strategy.  
* **Visual:** Keep S.3 pizza visible (4/6 shaded)  
* **Guide:** "Think about how you built that fraction. Did you count as you shaded, or did you picture the whole amount first?"  
* **No prompt—reflective pause only.**

---

**Task S.4: Pattern Confirmation — Pentagon**

* **Purpose:** Confirm n/n \= 1 pattern works on different shape through action.  
* **Shape:** Pentagon  
* **Visual:** Pentagon partitioned into 5 equal parts (radial cuts), unshaded. Fraction notation building as student shades.  
* **Task:** "You've discovered that 4/4 \= 1 and 3/3 \= 1\. Let's confirm that pattern on a hexagon. Show me one whole."  
* **Prompt:** Student shades all 5 parts.  
* **Correct Answer:** All 5 parts shaded (5/5 \= 1\)  
* **Connection (Guide states after task):** "5/5 \= 1\. When we use ALL the parts—no matter the shape or how many parts—we get one complete whole. That pattern always works."

---

### **Identity-Building Closure**

* **Purpose:** Specific, growth-oriented affirmation connecting to future learning.  
* **Visual:** Grid array showing 1/3 \+ 1/3 \+ 1/3 \= 3/3 \= 1 with animation/building.  
* **Content:**  
  * Key insight: "You learned an important way to think about fractions—they're all made up of unit fractions. When you see 2/3, you can think: 'That's just two one-third pieces put together.' And 6/6? That's six one-sixth pieces, which makes one whole."  
  * Value statement: "This way of thinking will make it easier when you start comparing fractions or adding them up later."  
  * Preview Module 4: "Next time, we'll explore more about how the denominator helps us compare different fractions and understand their sizes."
