## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**When neither numerator nor denominator matches, use BENCHMARK reasoning: compare each fraction's shaded area to a 1/2 reference shape (or to 1\) to determine which is greater.**

**Critical Misconception:** \#8 (Comparison Errors) \+ \#10 (Overgeneralization)—Students attempt to apply M10-M11 strategies when they don't apply. They may claim "3/4 \> 2/3 because 4 \> 3" (misapplying denominator comparison) or "3/4 \> 2/3 because 3 \> 2" (misapplying numerator comparison). The explicit "check what matches FIRST" decision step must precede any comparison attempt.

**Success Indicator:** Student sees 3/4 vs 2/5 and says: "Different denominators—can't count pieces. Different numerators—can't compare piece sizes. I need benchmarks. Looking at the 1/2 reference shape: 3/4 covers MORE area than half. 2/5 covers LESS area than half. So 3/4 \> 2/5."

**Biggest Risk:** Students skip the "check what matches" step and jump to an inappropriate strategy. If they don't explicitly verify that neither M10 nor M11 strategies apply, they will misapply rules and get incorrect answers. The decision process must be automatic before benchmark reasoning can succeed.

## **1.1 LEARNING GOALS**

*Verbatim — Script Must Achieve These*

**L1:** "Compare fractions with different denominators using benchmarks (e.g., 1/2) and justify symbolically."

**Module Goal (Student-Facing):**  
"You have two comparison strategies—but what happens when NEITHER numerator NOR denominator matches? Today you'll discover a third strategy using benchmarks. By the end, you'll be able to compare ANY two fractions."

**Exit Check Tests:**

* Can compare two fractions using 1/2 as benchmark (fractions on opposite sides)

**Note:** Strategy identification (knowing WHEN to use benchmarks vs. M10/M11 strategies) is tested in Synthesis, not Exit Check. Exit Check verifies students can APPLY benchmark reasoning; Synthesis verifies they can SELECT the appropriate strategy.

### **1.1.2 Module Bridges**

**From Module 11 (Compare Same Numerators):**  
Students have TWO comparison strategies: (1) same denominator → count pieces, (2) same numerator → compare piece sizes. They learned the decision process: "First check—what's the same?" M11 Synthesis (Task S.4) explicitly previewed M12 by showing that when NEITHER matches (3/8 vs 2/4), both strategies fail. Students left M11 with the puzzle: "How do we compare when nothing matches?"

**This Module:**  
Students discover that benchmark reasoning solves the "neither matches" problem. The key insight: even when fractions can't be directly compared, they can BOTH be compared to a reference point (1/2 or 1). Using a 1/2 reference shape, students compare shaded areas: if one fraction covers more area than half and another covers less, the comparison is clear. If both are on the same side of 1/2, compare which covers more (or less) area relative to the reference. For fractions close to 1, compare how much area is MISSING from each (the "gap" reasoning). Students complete the module with THREE comparison strategies and the decision process to select among them.

**To Grade 4 (4.NF.A.1, 4.NF.A.2):**  
Students will learn to compare fractions by finding common denominators algorithmically (4.NF.A.2) and to generate equivalent fractions using multiplication (4.NF.A.1). M12's benchmark reasoning provides conceptual foundation—students understand that comparison requires a common reference point. The visual benchmark strategy (comparing to 1/2) is explicitly named in 4.NF.A.2 as a valid comparison method, so M12 gives students a head start on Grade 4 content.

**Conceptual Progression (3.NF.A.3.d Arc — COMPLETE):**

* **Module 10:** Same DENOMINATOR → same-sized pieces → compare numerators (more pieces \= greater)  
* **Module 11:** Same NUMERATOR → same count of pieces → compare piece size (bigger pieces \= greater)  
* **Module 12 (This Module):** Unlike BOTH → benchmark strategies (compare each to 1/2 or 1 reference)

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

**Strategy Selection (Decision Process):**

- Explicit "check what matches" step before ANY comparison  
- When denominators match → use M10 strategy (count pieces)  
- When numerators match → use M11 strategy (compare piece sizes)  
- When NEITHER matches → use benchmark strategy

**Benchmark Strategy with 1/2:**

- Using a 1/2 reference shape to compare shaded areas  
- Determining if a fraction covers MORE, LESS, or EQUAL area compared to the 1/2 reference  
- Mental check: "Is the numerator more than half the denominator?"  
- Comparing fractions on OPPOSITE sides of 1/2 (clear winner)  
- Comparing fractions on SAME side of 1/2 (compare relative distances from 1/2)

**Benchmark Strategy with 1:**

- For fractions close to 1 (like 7/8 vs 5/6), compare "gaps" from 1  
- Visual reasoning: "How much area is MISSING from each shape?"  
- Smaller gap \= closer to 1 \= greater fraction

**Capstone Application:**

- Applying benchmark reasoning to novel fraction pairs  
- Shading to demonstrate understanding  
- Selecting correct comparison without visual scaffold

**Vocabulary:** benchmark, compare (any fractions), justify, reference point

### **❌ Must Not Include**

- Finding common denominators algorithmically (Grade 4\)  
- Cross-multiplication (Grade 4+)  
- Converting to decimals for comparison (Grade 4\)  
- Fraction addition or subtraction (Unit 6\)  
- Fractions greater than 1 in comparisons  
- New fraction concepts beyond comparison  
- Requiring verbal/typed explanation (use action-based demonstration instead)

⚠️ **STRATEGY SELECTION TRAP:** Students may try to force M10 or M11 strategies when they don't apply. ALWAYS require explicit verification: "Do the denominators match? Do the numerators match?" before allowing comparison to proceed. If a student attempts to compare 3/4 vs 2/3 by saying "4 \> 3 so 3/4 is smaller," redirect: "Wait—are the denominators the same? Are the numerators the same? What strategy do we need?"

⚠️ **SAME WHOLE PRINCIPLE (per 3.NF.A.3.d):** Benchmark comparisons are valid only when fractions refer to the same whole. All shapes in a comparison (including the benchmark reference shape) must be identical size.

⚠️ **BENCHMARK SELECTION:** Use 1/2 as primary benchmark for most comparisons. Use 1 as benchmark ONLY for fractions that are both close to 1 (like 7/8 vs 5/6). Don't overcomplicate—1/2 handles most cases.

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Comparison Strategy Errors (\#8) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student sees 3/4 vs 2/3 and claims "3/4 \> 2/3 because 4 \> 3" (comparing denominators as if they were numerators) or "2/3 \> 3/4 because 3 \> 2" (misapplying numerator comparison to different-denominator fractions).

**Why It Happens:**  
Students learned shortcuts in M10-M11 ("compare the numbers") without fully internalizing the CONDITIONS for each shortcut. When faced with unlike fractions, they grab whichever rule seems applicable without checking prerequisites.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Display both fractions on grids alongside a 1/2 reference shape. Students compare shaded AREAS to the reference—not positions relative to a line.

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. ALWAYS require explicit strategy selection: "First—do denominators match? Do numerators match?"  
2. Make the "neither matches" case visually obvious with different-sized grid partitions  
3. Introduce 1/2 reference shape BEFORE asking for comparison  
4. Model the full decision process every time in early examples

**Remediation Progression:**

- **Light:** "Check first—do these have the same denominator? Same numerator? What strategy do we need?"  
- **Medium:** "Neither matches, so we use our benchmark. Compare each to the 1/2 reference—which covers more area than half?"  
- **Heavy:** \[Visual demonstration\] "Watch: 3/4 covers MORE area than the 1/2 shape. 2/3 also covers more, but not as much as 3/4. So 3/4 is greater."

---

### **1.4.2 Overgeneralization (\#10) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student applies a memorized rule without checking conditions. For example: "Bigger denominator means smaller pieces, so 3/8 \< 2/5 because 8 \> 5." This incorrectly applies M11 reasoning (which requires same numerators) to unlike fractions.

**Why It Happens:**  
Students remember the M11 insight ("bigger denominator \= smaller pieces") but forget it only applies when numerators match. They overgeneralize rules that worked in constrained situations.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Show both fractions on same-sized grids alongside 1/2 reference. Highlight: "3/8 has 3 pieces. 2/5 has 2 pieces. Different counts AND different sizes—our old strategies don't work here. Let's use the reference shape."

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. Always verbalize the CHECK before the strategy: "Do numerators match? No—3 and 2 are different."  
2. Show the visual proof that piece-count comparison fails when pieces are different sizes  
3. Establish benchmark as the "universal" strategy when others fail

**Remediation Progression:**

- **Light:** "Wait—do these have the same numerator? Can we use piece-size comparison?"  
- **Medium:** "3 eighths and 2 fifths have different counts AND different sizes. We need benchmarks instead."  
- **Heavy:** \[Visual with 1/2 reference shape\] "Compare each to the 1/2 shape. 3/8 covers LESS area than half. 2/5 also covers less than half. Now we need to see which is closer to half..."

---

### **1.4.3 Fractions Only Exist in Shapes (\#9) — SECONDARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student can compare fractions when visual shapes are present but struggles when only symbolic fractions are shown. May say "I need to see the pictures" or make errors when visuals are removed.

**Why It Happens:**  
Student has relied on visual counting/comparing without developing internalized number sense for fractions. The benchmark strategy helps because it provides a mental reference point that works even without visuals.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Number line with benchmark at 1/2 provides a mental model that transfers more easily to abstract reasoning than area models alone.

**Prevention Strategy:**

1. Include number line representations alongside grid arrays for transfer  
2. Explicitly teach the mental check: "Is the numerator more than half the denominator?"  
3. Fade visual scaffolds gradually within the lesson  
4. Capstone includes comparison with reduced visual support

**Remediation Progression:**

- **Light:** "Picture the 1/2 reference shape in your mind. Would 3/4 cover more or less area?"  
- **Medium:** "To check if 3/4 is more than half, ask: Is 3 more than half of 4? Yes—so 3/4 covers more area than 1/2."  
- **Heavy:** \[Number line visual\] "Let's check on a number line. Here's 0, here's 1, here's 1/2 in the middle. Where does 3/4 land?"

## **1.5 TOOL SPECIFICATIONS**

### **1.5.1 Grid Arrays with Benchmark Reference Shape (Primary Tool)**

**\[PATH-SPECIFIC: VPSS\]**

#### **Core Specifications**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Purpose** | Visual area-based benchmark comparison of unlike fractions |
| **Configuration** | THREE same-sized grids displayed horizontally: Fraction A, 1/2 Reference, Fraction B |
| **Grid Sizing** | 2×3, 2×4, or 2×6 grids (multi-row required; NOT 1-row bars) |
| **Shading** | Shaded parts in pink; unshaded in blue |
| **Benchmark Shape** | Middle grid shows exactly 1/2 shaded — serves as visual area reference |
| **Comparison Method** | Student compares SHADED AREA of each fraction grid to the 1/2 reference shape |

#### **Why Reference Shape (Not Line)**

| Approach | Reasoning Type | Path Alignment |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| ❌ Horizontal line at 1/2 | Position/distance reasoning | Singapore path (linear) |
| ✅ Reference shape with 1/2 shaded | Area comparison reasoning | **VPSS path (spatial)** |

**Rationale:** VPSS emphasizes spatial/area reasoning. A benchmark LINE converts the task to position reasoning (Singapore approach). A benchmark SHAPE keeps students comparing shaded regions—true area reasoning.

#### **Interaction Flow**

1. Student sees three grids: Fraction A, 1/2 Reference, Fraction B  
2. Scaffold prompt: "Compare to the benchmark. Does 3/4 cover MORE or LESS area than 1/2?" \[More / Less / Same\]  
3. Scaffold prompt: "Does 2/5 cover MORE or LESS area than 1/2?" \[More / Less / Same\]  
4. Student concludes comparison using \[\<\] \[=\] \[\>\] selector  
5. (Later interactions may reduce scaffolding—direct to symbol selection)

#### **Grid Dimension Constraints**

| Constraint | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Minimum Rows** | 2 rows minimum (NOT 1×n bars) |
| **Rationale** | 1-row grids resemble Singapore rectangle bars; multi-row grids emphasize area |
| **Recommended Configs** | 2×3 (sixths), 2×4 (eighths), 2×5 (tenths), 3×4 (twelfths) |
| **Same Whole Requirement** | All three grids MUST be identical dimensions |

#### **Benchmark Reference Shape Options**

| Benchmark | Reference Shape | Use Case |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **1/2** | Grid with exactly half shaded | Primary benchmark for most comparisons |
| **1** | Fully shaded grid (or unshaded "gap" comparison) | For fractions close to 1 (7/8 vs 5/6) |

### **1.5.2 Number Lines with Benchmark Mark (Transfer/Verification Tool)**

#### **Core Specifications**

| Aspect | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Purpose** | Position-based benchmark comparison; transfers area reasoning to linear model |
| **Configuration** | Single number line (0 to 1\) with prominent benchmark at 1/2 |
| **Partitioning** | Tick marks can show relevant denominators |
| **Benchmark Mark** | Prominent 1/2 mark—larger tick, bold label, distinct color |
| **Points** | Both fractions marked as points on the line |
| **Comparison Reasoning** | Position relative to 1/2 mark; "Which side of 1/2?" then "Which is farther?" |

#### **Why Lines Work Here**

Number lines ARE about position/distance—so a benchmark MARK (not shape) is appropriate. This provides:

- Transfer opportunity: "Area reasoning on grids matches position reasoning on number lines"  
- Abstract bridge: Number lines support mental visualization when shapes are removed  
- Verification: Students can check grid-based conclusions on number line

#### **Number Line Display**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Endpoints** | 0 and 1 clearly labeled |
| **1/2 Mark** | Prominent—larger tick, bold label, possibly highlighted color |
| **Fraction Points** | Two distinct colors for fractions being compared |
| **Labels** | Fractions labeled at their points |

#### **Use Cases in M12**

- **Section 2:** Verification that grid-based area reasoning matches position reasoning  
- **Section 3:** "Close to 1" comparisons (gap from 1 visible as distance)  
- **Exit Check:** Transfer task—can student apply benchmark on number line?  
- **Synthesis:** Demonstrate that benchmark strategy works across representations

**Interaction W.1: Strategy Check**

* **Purpose:** Activate M10-M11 decision process and establish that neither strategy applies. Quick check, not dwelling on failure.  
* **Hook:** "Here's a comparison challenge. Let's see which strategy works."  
* **Visual:** Two 2×5 grids side by side (same total size):  
  * Left grid: Partitioned into fourths, 3/4 shaded, labeled "3/4"  
  * Right grid: Partitioned into fifths, 2/5 shaded, labeled "2/5"  
* **Guide:** "Three-fourths and two-fifths. Let's check our strategies."  
* **Guide:** "Same denominator?"  
* **Task:** Student selects \[YES / NO\]  
* **On Correct (NO):** "No—fourths and fifths are different."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Check the bottom numbers. 4 and 5—same or different?"  
* **Guide:** "Same numerator?"  
* **Task:** Student selects \[YES / NO\]  
* **On Correct (NO):** "No—3 and 2 are different. Neither strategy applies here."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Check the top numbers. 3 and 2—same or different?"  
* **Guide:** "But we can still figure this out. Let's try something."

**Interaction W.2: Intuitive Benchmark Discovery**

* **Purpose:** Guide students to reason about each fraction's relationship to one-half WITHOUT showing a reference shape. Students discover benchmark reasoning intuitively.  
* **Visual:** Same two grids remain. No reference shape added.  
* **Guide:** "Think about where each fraction sits compared to one half."  
* **Guide:** "Three-fourths... is that more than half or less than half?"  
* **Task:** Student selects \[MORE than half / LESS than half\]  
* **On Correct (MORE):** "Yes—three out of four is more than half."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Half of 4 would be 2\. You have 3\. Is 3 more or less than 2?"  
* **Guide:** "Now two-fifths. More than half or less than half?"  
* **Task:** Student selects \[MORE than half / LESS than half\]  
* **On Correct (LESS):** "Right—two out of five is less than half."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Half of 5 would be 2 and a half. You have 2\. Is that more or less than half?"

**Interaction W.3: Positive Conclusion \+ Bridge**

* **Purpose:** Students successfully complete the comparison. Celebrate the discovery, then bridge to Lesson where this reasoning will be formalized.  
* **Visual:** Same two grids remain.  
* **Guide:** "So three-fourths is MORE than half, and two-fifths is LESS than half. Which fraction is greater?"  
* **Task:** Student selects \[3/4 / 2/5\]  
* **On Correct (3/4):** "You got it\! Three-fourths is greater."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "One fraction is above half, one is below. Which one is above?"  
* **Guide:** "You just compared fractions when nothing matched—by thinking about where each one sits compared to half."  
* **Guide:** "There's a name for this strategy, and a tool that makes it even easier. Let's learn more."  
* **No further student action.**

## **1.7 LESSON (11-13 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

### **Core Purpose**

Formalize and extend students' intuitive benchmark reasoning from Warmup. Introduce the 1/2 reference shape as a visual tool, name "benchmark" vocabulary, then apply to increasingly complex cases (same side, close to 1). Students learn symbolic justification and verify reasoning transfers to number lines. By end of lesson, students have THREE comparison strategies and can apply them flexibly.

### **Pedagogical Flow**

This lesson builds on Warmup's intuitive discovery:

- **Section 1 (Formalization):** Callback to Warmup success. Introduce 1/2 reference SHAPE as visual tool. Name "benchmark" vocabulary. Students see how the tool makes their reasoning visible.  
- **Section 2 (Application — Opposite Sides):** Practice benchmark strategy with reference shape on NEW opposite-sides pairs. Build fluency with the visual tool.  
- **Section 3 (Extension — Same Side):** Apply benchmark strategy when both fractions are on the SAME side of 1/2. Requires comparing distances from benchmark—more complex reasoning.  
- **Section 4 (Extension — Close to 1 \+ Number Line):** Introduce "1" as secondary benchmark for fractions close to 1\. Number line verification shows benchmark strategy works across representations.  
- **Section 5 (Abstract — Symbolic):** Students learn to justify comparisons using symbolic notation. Connects visual reasoning to mathematical writing.  
- **Section 6 (Capstone):** Apply complete benchmark reasoning independently with reduced scaffolding.

### **1.7.1 LESSON SECTION 1: Formalization — Reference Shape \+ Vocabulary**

**Interaction 1.1: Introducing the Reference Shape**

* **Visual:** THREE 2×5 grids appear horizontally:  
  * Left grid: 3/4 shaded, labeled "3/4" (from Warmup)  
  * **Center grid: Exactly HALF shaded, labeled "1/2" with highlight border (NEW)**  
  * Right grid: 2/5 shaded, labeled "2/5" (from Warmup)  
* **Guide:** "You just compared 3/4 and 2/5 by thinking about half. Here's a tool that makes your reasoning visible."  
* **Guide:** "See the shape in the middle? That's exactly half shaded. Compare the shaded areas."  
* **Guide:** "Three-fourths covers MORE area than the half reference. Two-fifths covers LESS. That's why you knew 3/4 \> 2/5."
