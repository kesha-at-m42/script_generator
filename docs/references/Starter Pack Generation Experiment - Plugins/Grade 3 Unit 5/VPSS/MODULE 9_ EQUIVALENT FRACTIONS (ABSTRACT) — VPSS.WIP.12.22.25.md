## **1.0 THE ONE THING**

**Equivalent fractions occupy the same POSITION on a number line—and whole numbers can be written as fractions in multiple ways (4/4 \= 1, 8/4 \= 2, AND 4/1 \= 4).**

**Critical Misconception:** \#7 (Difficulty Recognizing Equivalence)—Without the visual area support from M8, students may struggle to see that 1/2 and 2/4 land at the exact same point. Position equivalence requires trusting the number line, not the "look" of the fraction.

**Success Indicator:** Student places 1/2 and 2/4 at the same point on a number line and explains "same point means equivalent." Student also recognizes that 3/1 \= 3 and 6/3 \= 2—whole numbers and fractions share the same positions.

**Biggest Risk:** Students may still rely on "same area" thinking from M8 and fail to transfer to pure position reasoning. The b/1 concept (3/1 \= 3\) may confuse students who think "you can't have 1 as a denominator" or who expect fractions to always look like "parts of a whole."

## **1.1 LEARNING GOALS**

*Verbatim — Script Must Achieve These*

**L1:** "Identify and generate equivalent fractions as the same point on a number line."

**L2:** "Express whole numbers as fractions and recognize fractions equivalent to whole numbers."

**Module Goal (Student-Facing):**  
"You proved fractions are equivalent by matching their size. Now you'll prove it a different way—by matching their POSITION on a number line. You'll also discover that whole numbers can be written as fractions\!"

**Exit Check Tests:**

* Can identify that equivalent fractions occupy the same point on a number line  
* Can verify equivalence using position (not just visual area)  
* Can recognize fractions equal to whole numbers (4/4 \= 1, 8/4 \= 2\)  
* Can express whole numbers as fractions (3 \= 3/1, 4 \= 4/1)

### **1.1.1 Standards Cascade**

| Building On | 3.NF.A.2 (Represent fractions on number line); 3.NF.A.3a (Equivalent fractions same size/same point—M8 established "same size") |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Addressing** | 3.NF.A.3a (Same point on number line); 3.NF.A.3b (Generate simple equivalent fractions); 3.NF.A.3c (Express whole numbers as fractions: 3 \= 3/1; recognize 6/1 \= 6\) |
| **Building Toward** | 3.NF.A.3d (Compare fractions by reasoning about size—M10 will use "different points" for comparison) |

### **1.1.2 Module Bridges**

**From Module 8 (Equivalent Fractions Concrete):**  
Students can identify equivalent fractions through visual matching (same size/area) with concrete models. They proved 1/2 \= 2/4 \= 3/6 \= 4/8 using grids, circles, hexagons, AND number lines—but shapes were primary discovery tools while number lines provided verification. They understand "same whole" requirement and can explain equivalence using "same amount" language.

**This Module:**  
Students discover that equivalent fractions can be proven through POSITION alone—same point on a number line means same value. This is the **abstract confirmation** of what M8 showed concretely. Number lines become the PRIMARY tool; shapes appear only as callbacks or remediation support. Students extend whole-number equivalence in two directions: fractions that equal whole numbers (8/4 \= 2, 9/3 \= 3\) AND whole numbers written as fractions (3 \= 3/1 per 3.NF.A.3.c). Position reasoning extends to fractions \> 1 (3/2 \= 6/4).

**To Module 10 (Compare Like Denominators):**  
Students will use position reasoning to compare fractions—if fractions are at DIFFERENT points, they have different values. The "same point \= equivalent" understanding becomes the foundation for "different point \= not equivalent → can compare." Students will use \>, \<, \= symbols based on position.

**Conceptual Progression:**

* **Module 7:** Fractions can equal WHOLE NUMBERS (4/4 \= 1, 8/8 \= 1\)  
* **Module 8:** Fractions can equal OTHER FRACTIONS (1/2 \= 2/4) — CONCRETE/VISUAL PROOF  
* **Module 9 (This Module):** Same equivalences confirmed ABSTRACTLY (same position) \+ whole numbers AS fractions (3 \= 3/1)

## **1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES**

### **✅ Must Teach**

**Position Equivalence:**

* Equivalent fractions occupy the same point on a number line  
* "Same point" as the abstract proof of equivalence (confirms M8's "same size")  
* Multiple number line instances with comparison line tool for seeing equivalent positions  
* Core equivalences ≤ 1: 1/2 \= 2/4 \= 3/6 \= 4/8 (primary); 1/3 \= 2/6, 2/3 \= 4/6 (secondary)  
* At least ONE equivalence \> 1: 3/2 \= 6/4 (same position principle extends beyond 1\)

**Whole Numbers as Fractions (Two Directions):**

* FROM fractions: b/b \= 1 (review), 2b/b \= 2, 3b/b \= 3 (e.g., 4/4 \= 1, 8/4 \= 2, 9/3 \= 3\)  
* FROM whole numbers: b/1 \= b (e.g., 3/1 \= 3, 4/1 \= 4\) — **per 3.NF.A.3.c**  
* Recognition that whole numbers and fractions share the same number line

**Vocabulary:**

* "same point" (formalized from M8's informal use)  
* "position" (as proof of equivalence)  
* "equivalent to a whole number" (new phrase)

### **❌ Must Not Include**

* Mixed number notation (3/2 as 1½) — Module 10+  
* Division interpretation of fractions (8/4 \= 8 ÷ 4\) — Grade 4  
* Systematic simplification algorithms — Grade 4  
* Cross-multiplication or common denominators — Grade 4  
* Comparing non-equivalent fractions with \< or \> — Module 10  
* Teaching "multiply both by same number" as a RULE — Grade 4  
* Denominators \> 8 in Lesson (Practice may extend to 10\)

⚠️ **THE TRAP TO AVOID (Position Only):** When students see 8/4 \= 2, celebrate the position match but do NOT pursue "how many 4s fit in 8?" or any division reasoning. When students see 3/2 and 6/4 at the same point, do NOT convert to 1½. Stay focused on POSITION, not operations.

⚠️ **THE TRAP TO AVOID (Patterns):** When students notice "the numbers doubled" in 1/2 → 2/4, accept the observation ("Interesting\!") but do NOT teach it as a procedure ("To find equivalents, multiply both by the same number"). Pattern NOTICING is encouraged; pattern TEACHING is Grade 4\.

⚠️ **PEDAGOGICAL COMMITMENT (Abstract Through Position):** This module transitions from concrete (same area in shapes) to abstract (same position on line). However, "abstract" doesn't mean "no visual"—position IS visual, just a different kind. The number line is the primary proof tool. Shapes appear only for:

1. Brief Warmup callback to activate M8 knowledge  
2. Heavy remediation when position alone isn't clicking  
3. Synthesis reflection comparing methods

⚠️ **SCAFFOLDING SEQUENCE:** Single line before stacked lines. Fractions ≤ 1 before fractions \> 1\. Recognition before generation. b/b \= n before b/1 \= n (more familiar structure first).

## **1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS**

### **1.4.1 Difficulty Recognizing Equivalence (\#7) — PRIMARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student says 1/2 and 2/4 are "different fractions" even when shown at the same point on a number line. May focus on the numerals (2 ≠ 1, 4 ≠ 2\) rather than the position. Without area support, the equivalence feels less "visible."

**Why It Happens:**  
In M8, equivalence was proven by matching shaded areas—a direct visual comparison. Position equivalence is more abstract; the "sameness" is in location, not appearance. Students who relied heavily on area reasoning may struggle to trust position alone.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Visual Cue:**  
Multiple number line instances with comparison line tool showing exact position match. Comparison line used PROACTIVELY once in Lesson Section 1 to establish the proof method, then becomes casual confirmation animation thereafter.

**Prevention Strategy (Built into Instruction):**

- Start with KNOWN equivalence from M8 (1/2 \= 2/4) — students already trust this is true  
- Seeing same position builds confidence in the new proof method  
- Comparison line shows alignment proactively BEFORE students work independently  
- Guide doesn't keep mentioning the comparison line; it becomes background confirmation

**Remediation Progression:**

- **Light:** "Check the positions. Do these fractions land at the same point?"  
- **Medium:** "Even though the numbers look different, 1/2 and 2/4 land at exactly the same position—the same distance from zero. Same point means equivalent."  
- **Heavy:** \[Show grid callback\] "Remember your grids from last time? Same area meant equivalent. On the number line, same POINT means equivalent. Both are true\!"

---

### **1.4.2 Improper Spacing on Number Line (\#4) — STRUCTURAL PREVENTION**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student spaces intervals unevenly, making it impossible to see that 1/2 and 2/4 land at the same point.

**Why It Happens:**  
Without gradient scaffolds (removed in M6), students must maintain equal spacing mentally.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Prevention Strategy (Structural):**

- **All number lines are PRE-PARTITIONED in M9** — students focus on position, not partitioning  
- Partitioning was the focus of M5; M9 applies that skill without requiring it  
- Spacing errors are essentially eliminated by design

**Remediation (Unlikely to be needed):**

- **Light:** "Check the spacing—are the intervals equal?"  
- **Medium:** "Remember, fourths means 4 equal spaces from 0 to 1\. Count the spaces to find 2/4."  
- **Heavy:** \[Modeling\] "Watch me count: one-fourth, two-fourths. That's the same distance from zero as one-half."

---

### **1.4.3 Overgeneralizing Rules (\#10) — TERTIARY**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student thinks "bigger denominator \= smaller value" and concludes that 2/4 \< 1/2 because "fourths are smaller than halves."

**Why It Happens:**  
Students correctly learned that larger denominators create smaller pieces (1/8 \< 1/4). They may overgeneralize this to all fractions without considering the numerator's role.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Prevention Strategy (Built into Instruction):**

- Use **counting language** when showing equivalence: "One half-sized jump reaches here. One-fourth, two-fourths — same position\!"  
- Emphasizes "smaller pieces but more of them" rather than piece size alone  
- Position proof bypasses size confusion: "Don't think about piece sizes—just check where they land"

**Remediation Progression:**

- **Light:** "Check the position, not just the denominator."  
- **Medium:** "Yes, fourths are smaller pieces than halves. But 2/4 means TWO fourths—and two fourths reaches the same position as one half."  
- **Heavy:** \[Visual\] "Watch: 1/2 reaches here \[mark\]. Now 1/4, 2/4—reaches the same point\! Smaller pieces, but enough of them to get to the same place."

---

### **1.4.4 Confusion About b/1 Fractions — EMERGING (SEQUENCING PREVENTION)**

**Trigger Behavior:**  
Student is confused by 3/1 or 4/1. May say "you can't have 1 as a denominator" or struggle to visualize what 3/1 means on a number line.

**Why It Happens:**  
All prior work has positioned denominators as "how many equal parts in the whole" (between 0 and 1). A denominator of 1 means "1 part from 0 to 1"—which spans the entire unit.

#### **Path C (VPSS) Intervention**

**Prevention Strategy (Sequencing):**

- **Build from familiar first:** Section 2 shows 9/3 \= 3 (fractions reaching whole numbers)  
- **Then introduce new notation:** Section 3 shows 3/1 \= 3 as "another way to write 3 as a fraction"  
- Pattern (fractions reaching wholes) is established before "strange" b/1 notation appears  
- Frame as: "You just saw 9/3 reaches 3\. Here's another fraction that reaches 3..."

**Remediation Progression:**

- **Light:** "If each piece is one whole, how many wholes is 3/1?"  
- **Medium:** "The denominator 1 means each piece is the size of one whole. So 3/1 means three whole-sized pieces. That reaches... 3\!"  
- **Heavy:** \[Visual\] "Watch: 1/1 is one whole—lands at 1\. 2/1 is two wholes—lands at 2\. 3/1 is three wholes—lands at 3\. The fraction equals the top number\!"

## **1.6 WARMUP (2-3 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

### **Purpose**

Activate M8 equivalence knowledge, then bridge to position-based proof. Create curiosity about WHY equivalent fractions land at the same position. Students should feel: "I already know these fractions are equal—but there's something interesting happening on the number line."

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 3 (M8 callback \+ position observation \+ bridge) |
| Format | 3-option selection (W.1), Yes/No prediction (W.2), observation |
| Student action | Click to select; observe position |
| Remediation | Light only |
| Tone | Curious, puzzle-like |
| Warmup Type | Binary Choice \+ Notice & Wonder |
| Engagement Anchors | Curiosity Gap (hook) \+ Strength Prompt (detective framing) |
| Fractions | 1/3 \= 2/6 (different from Lesson's 1/2 \= 2/4) |

### **Constraints**

| USE | AVOID |
| :---- | :---- |
| "equivalent fractions," "same amount," "same size" (M8 vocabulary) | Teaching position equivalence (save for Lesson) |
| Pre-shaded grids for callback | Extended shape work |
| Pre-partitioned number lines for preview | Student partitioning or extended placement |
| "same position," "same point" as OBSERVATION only | Formal position vocabulary instruction |
| Light remediation only | Medium/Heavy remediation |

---

**Interaction W.1: Hook \+ M8 Equivalence Callback**

* **Purpose:** Activate M8 knowledge with a curiosity-creating hook.  
* **Hook (Curiosity Gap):** "Here's a puzzle: These two fractions have DIFFERENT numbers—1/3 and 2/6. But look at the shading..."  
* **Visual:** Two same-sized grids stacked vertically:  
  * Grid A: 1×3 (3 parts), 1 shaded, labeled "1/3"  
  * Grid B: 2×3 (6 parts), 2 shaded, labeled "2/6"  
* **Guide:** "Does one show more, or are they equal?"  
* **Prompt:** \[Top has more / Bottom has more / They're equal\]  
* **Correct Answer:** They're equal  
* **On Correct:** "Different numbers, same amount. That's what makes them equivalent fractions."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Compare the pink areas. Does one have more shading, or is it the same?"

**Interaction W.2: Position Observation (Strength Prompt)**

* **Purpose:** Preview that equivalent fractions share the same position—observation only, not instruction.  
* **Visual State 1:** Two number lines (0 to 1\) stacked vertically:  
  * Top line: partitioned into thirds, point marked at 1/3  
  * Bottom line: partitioned into sixths, point marked at 2/6  
  * NO comparison line yet  
* **Guide:** "Time to be a position detective. I've marked 1/3 on the top line and 2/6 on the bottom."  
* **Guide:** "Look carefully at where each point sits. Do they line up... or not?"  
* **Prompt:** "Do 1/3 and 2/6 land at the same position?" \[Yes / No\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Yes  
* **On Correct:**  
  * **Visual State 2:** Comparison line animates in, confirming alignment  
  * **Guide:** "You spotted it. Same position on the number line. Interesting..."  
* **Remediation (Light):** "Look straight down from the 1/3 point. Where does 2/6 sit?"

**Interaction W.3: Bridge to Lesson**

* **Purpose:** Create anticipation for learning position-based proof.  
* **Visual:** Keep both number lines visible with comparison line.  
* **Guide:** "Different numbers. Same amount shaded. AND same position on the number line."  
* **Guide:** "That's not a coincidence. Let's discover why equivalent fractions ALWAYS land at the same point."  
* **No prompt—bridge moment.**

---

### **Verification Checklist**

- [ ] Hook in first 15-20 seconds (curiosity gap: "different numbers, same shading")

## **1.7 LESSON (11-13 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Full interaction specification (AI formats and tags)

### **Core Purpose**

Establish that equivalent fractions occupy the same POSITION on a number line—this is an abstract proof that confirms M8's concrete visual proof. Extend to whole numbers as fractions (both directions: 8/4 \= 2 AND 3/1 \= 3). Introduce one equivalence beyond 1 (3/2 \= 6/4) to show the principle works everywhere.

### **Pedagogical Flow**

M9 builds on M8's concrete foundation. Students already KNOW 1/2 \= 2/4 from visual proof. M9's job is to confirm this abstractly through position and extend to whole numbers.

**CRA Application for M9:**

- **Concrete:** M8 provided this (shapes). M9 Warmup briefly activates it.  
- **Relational:** Section 1 — Students see that KNOWN equivalences also share position (connecting two proof methods)  
- **Abstract:** Sections 2-3 — Position reasoning extended to whole numbers and b/1 fractions  
- **Application:** Section 4 — Position reasoning for fractions \> 1

| Section | Focus | Interactions | Time |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Section 1 | Position Equivalence (≤ 1\) | 1.1-1.4 | \~3 min |
| Section 2 | Fractions Equal to Whole Numbers | 2.1-2.3 | \~2.5 min |
| Section 3 | Whole Numbers AS Fractions (b/1) | 3.1-3.3 | \~2.5 min |
| Section 4 | Equivalence Beyond 1 \+ Pattern \+ Abstract | 4.1-4.5 | \~4 min |

* **On Correct:**  
  * Point appears at 2/4  
  * **Comparison line animates** showing vertical alignment between both points

* **Remediation:** Full L-M-H  
* **On Correct:**

### **1.7.2 LESSON SECTION 2: Fractions Equal to Whole Numbers**

**Interaction 2.1: 4/4 \= 1 Position Proof**

* **Visual:** Single number line from 0 to 2, partitioned into fourths. Labels at 0, 1, 2\.  
* **Guide:** "Now let's look at fractions that equal whole numbers. Where does 4/4 land?"  
* **Prompt:** "Place a point at 4/4."  
* **Correct Answer:** Point placed at 1  
* **Remediation:** Full L-M-H — "Count four fourths from zero: 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4..."  
* **On Correct:**  
  * Point appears at 1  
  * **Guide:** "4/4 lands at 1\. Same position—so 4/4 equals 1\. The fraction and the whole number share the same point."

**Interaction 2.2: 8/4 \= 2 Extension**

* **Visual:** Same number line (0 to 2, fourths). Point at 1 remains (labeled "4/4 \= 1").  
* **Guide:** "What about 8/4? If 4/4 reaches 1, where does 8/4 reach?"  
* **Prompt:** "Place a point at 8/4."  
* **Correct Answer:** Point placed at 2  
* **Remediation:** Full L-M-H — "Keep counting fourths past 1: 5/4, 6/4, 7/4, 8/4..."  
* **On Correct:**  
  * Point appears at 2  
  * **Guide:** "8/4 reaches 2\. Same position as the whole number 2—so 8/4 \= 2."

**Interaction 2.3: Transfer to Thirds — 9/3 \= 3**

* **Visual:** NEW number line from 0 to 3, partitioned into thirds. Labels at 0, 1, 2, 3\.  
* **Guide:** "Let's try thirds. 3/3 equals 1, 6/3 equals 2\. Where does 9/3 land?"  
* **Prompt:** "Place a point at 9/3."  
* **Correct Answer:** Point placed at 3  
* **Remediation:** Full L-M-H — "Count: 3/3 is 1, 6/3 is 2, 9/3 is..."  
* **On Correct:**  
  * Point appears at 3  
  * **Guide:** "9/3 reaches 3\. When you count enough of any unit fraction, you land on whole numbers. 9/3 and 3 share the same position—they're equivalent."

**\[SECTION TRANSITION\]**

* **Guide:** "You've seen fractions that equal whole numbers. Now here's a question: Can you write a whole number AS a fraction?"

**→ SECTION 2 COMPLETE. PROCEED TO SECTION 3\.**

### **1.7.3 LESSON SECTION 3: Whole Numbers AS Fractions (b/1)**

**Interaction 3.1: Introducing Denominator 1**

* **Visual:** Number line from 0 to 4, with tick marks at whole numbers only (0, 1, 2, 3, 4). No fractional partitions visible.  
* **Guide:** "You just saw 9/3 reaches 3\. Here's another way to write 3 as a fraction."  
* **Guide:** "What if each piece is the size of ONE WHOLE? Then the denominator is 1."  
* **Guide:** "3/1 means 3 pieces, and each piece is one whole. Where does 3/1 land?"

## **1.9 SYNTHESIS (6-8 minutes)**

**Detail Level:** Structure \+ parameters (AI writes dialogue)

### **Purpose**

Connect position equivalence concepts through pattern discovery. Recognize that position proves equivalence abstractly—the key insight of this lesson. Consolidate the multiple ways to name the same position (fractions, whole numbers, b/1 fractions). Transfer understanding to predict equivalence without visual tools. This is NOT more practice—it's connection-making and generalization.

### **Parameters**

| Element | Specification |
| :---- | :---- |
| Interactions | 5 (4 connection tasks \+ metacognitive reflection) \+ identity closure |
| Task Types | Pattern Discovery (Type A), Representation Transfer (Type D), Generalization (Type B), Real-World Bridge (Type C) |
| Visual Tools | Number lines, comparison displays |
| Remediation | Light only—mastery assumed |
| Tone | Reflective, warm, discovering together |
| Energy | Medium (not exhausted, not high-energy) |

### **Constraints**

| DO | DO NOT |
| :---- | :---- |
| Connection tasks (pattern, transfer, generalization) | Repetitive practice problems |
| Reference position reasoning across examples | Return to shape-based proofs |
| Name principles students discovered | Introduce new procedures |
| Light remediation if needed | Extended teaching or Heavy remediation |
| Specific identity closure | Generic praise ("Great job\!") |
| Visual support for every task | Overcrowded screens |
| Use student-facing language | Reference "modules" or internal terms |

### **Opening Frame**

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from practice to reflection/connection.  
* **Guide:** "You've been proving equivalence using position on number lines. Now let's step back and see the bigger patterns you've discovered."

---

**Task S.1: Pattern Discovery — Multiple Names, Same Position (Type A)**

* **Purpose:** Surface the pattern that one position can have many fraction names.  
* **Visual:** Single number line from 0 to 3 with ONE point marked at position 2\. Below the point, four labels stacked:  
  * 2  
  * 8/4  
  * 6/3  
  * 2/1  
* **Guide setup:** "Look at this point. It sits at position 2\. But it has four different names."  
* **Prompt:** "What do all these names have in common?" \[Select one\]  
  * They all have the same denominator  
  * They all land at the same point  
  * They all have the same numerator  
* **Correct Answer:** They all land at the same point  
* **On Correct:** "Different numbers, same position, same value."  
* **Connection (Guide states):** "Any fraction that lands at position 2 equals 2—no matter how the numbers look."

---

**Task S.2: Representation Transfer — From Shapes to Position (Type D)**

* **Purpose:** Connect earlier concrete proof (shapes) to today's abstract proof (position).  
* **Visual:** Two-panel display (side by side, not stacked):  
  * Left panel: Two small grids showing 2/4 and 3/6 with same shading, labeled "Same amount shaded"  
  * Right panel: Two short number lines showing 2/4 and 3/6 at same position, labeled "Same position"  
* **Guide setup:** "Earlier you proved 2/4 and 3/6 are equivalent because they show the same amount shaded. Today you proved it a different way."  
* **Prompt:** "What's the new way to prove equivalence?" \[Select one\]  
  * Same color  
  * Same position on the number line  
  * Same denominator  
* **Correct Answer:** Same position on the number line  
* **On Correct:** "Two different proofs, same truth."  
* **Connection (Guide states):** "Shapes show amount. Number lines show position. Both prove equivalence."

---

**Task S.3: Generalization — What's Always True? (Type B)**

* **Purpose:** Extract the universal principle from today's examples.  
* **Visual:** Simple text display (no number lines needed):  
  * "Today you proved:"  
  * 1/2 \= 2/4 ✓  
  * 3/2 \= 6/4 ✓  
  * 3 \= 3/1 ✓  
* **Guide setup:** "Some of these are less than 1, some are greater than 1, some equal whole numbers. But you used the same test for all of them."  
* **Prompt:** "What's always true about equivalent fractions?" \[Select one\]  
  * They have the same denominator  
  * They land at the same position  
  * They have the same numerator  
* **Correct Answer:** They land at the same position  
* **On Correct:** "That's the principle. Same position means same value—always."  
* **Connection (Guide states):** "This works for any fractions: halves, thirds, fourths, less than 1, greater than 1\. Position reveals equivalence."

---

**Task S.4: Real-World Bridge — Distance and Position (Type C)**

* **Purpose:** Connect position equivalence to real-world measurement context.  
* **Visual:** Simple track diagram with two markers at the same position:  
  * Marker A: "1/2 of the track"  
  * Marker B: "4/8 of the track"  
* **Guide setup:** "Two runners describe how far they've run using different fractions. But look at where they're standing."  
* **Prompt:** "Did they run the same distance?" \[Yes / No\]  
* **Correct Answer:** Yes  
* **On Correct:** "Same position on the track—same distance run."  
* **Connection (Guide states):** "Position works the same way everywhere. Half the track and four-eighths of the track are the same distance."
