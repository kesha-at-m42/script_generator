## §1.0 THE ONE THING

**[REQUIRED]**

### The Core Idea
**Same shape + different unit = different number. And that's okay.**

Students will understand that a square inch and a square centimeter are both valid measures of area, that the same rectangle will produce different area numbers depending on which unit is used, and that this difference is caused by the difference in unit SIZE — not by error or confusion.

### CRA Stage
**Representational:** Full grids are visible throughout because the GRID SIZE (unit size) is the core concept being learned. Concrete models (physical unit squares) may be used to establish what 1 inch and 1 centimeter are, but the main learning happens with visual grids showing unit tiles side-by-side.

### Critical Misconception
**M8: Square Unit Confusion**
* Students believe all square units are equivalent (e.g., 12 sq cm = 12 sq in)
* Students confuse the linear unit (inch, centimeter) with the square unit (square inch, square centimeter)
* Surfaces as: "If the rectangle is the same, the area should be the same number" or "80 is bigger than 12, so 80 sq cm is a bigger area"

### Secondary Misconception
**Bigger Number = Bigger Shape (from TVP)**
* When shown 12 sq in and 80 sq cm for the same rectangle, students assume 80 sq cm is a larger area (not understanding that the unit size determines the count)

### Success Indicator (Exit Criteria)
Students exit M5 when they can:
1. Identify a square inch and a square centimeter visually (recognize the 1×1 grid)
2. Measure the area of the same rectangle using both units and write the answer with the correct unit label
3. Explain (or demonstrate through comparison) why the same rectangle gives different numbers in different units: "It takes more of a smaller unit to cover the same area"
4. Consistently include unit labels (square inches, square centimeters) in all area answers

### Biggest Risk
**Unit label omission or inconsistency.** If students routinely drop the unit label or mix them ("12 inches" instead of "12 square inches"), the misconception that units don't matter will persist into M6. Prevention: Unit labels are REQUIRED in every problem; reference displays must be visible; synthesis explicitly consolidates this.

## §1.1 LEARNING GOALS

**[REQUIRED]**

### Primary Learning Goals (Verbatim from Module Mapping)
1. **Describe square units based on different linear units of measurement.**
   * Students recognize that a square unit is defined by a linear unit (square inch from 1-inch-per-side; square centimeter from 1-cm-per-side)
   * Students understand that square units are STANDARD (everyone agrees on the size of an inch and a centimeter)

2. **Measure the area of the same figure in square inches and square centimeters.**
   * Students apply the area formula (length × width) using two different unit types
   * Students see concretely that the same figure produces different area numbers when measured in different units
   * Students articulate why: unit size determines how many tiles fit

* **3.MD.C.6 (M5):** Measure areas by counting unit squares of a given unit (square centimeters, square inches, and improvised units). [M5 introduces NAMED standard square units.]

## §1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES

**[REQUIRED]**

### Must Teach (Explicit Content)

#### Required Concepts
1. **What a square inch is:** A square with side length of 1 inch (visual + definition)
2. **What a square centimeter is:** A square with side length of 1 centimeter (visual + definition)
3. **Standard units:** Inches and centimeters are agreed-upon by everyone; everyone uses the same-sized square inch or square centimeter
4. **The size relationship:** 1 inch > 1 centimeter (from Grade 2 linear knowledge); therefore 1 square inch > 1 square centimeter
5. **The inverse counting relationship:** Smaller units require MORE tiles to cover the same area
6. **Area formula with units:** Area = length × width; unit label is part of the answer (e.g., "12 square inches," not just "12")
7. **Distinguishing linear units from square units:** 1 inch (length) vs. 1 square inch (area); 1 centimeter (length) vs. 1 square centimeter (area)

#### Required Vocabulary (from Table A, complete list)
* **New terms:** square inch, square centimeter, standard unit
* **Related (review from M4):** area, rectangle, grid, rows, columns
* **Related (from Grade 2):** inch, centimeter, unit (as linear measure)

#### Required Multiplication/Calculation
* Fluent multiplication of the dimensions (length × width) using facts within the stated ranges
* Recording the result with the unit label

#### Required Practice Types
1. Measuring area with square inches (factors 2–10, products ≤100)
2. Measuring area with square centimeters (factors 3–10, products ≤100)
3. Linear vs. square unit distinction in Early Lesson (3-4 MC problems; SME requirement from TVP)
4. Linear vs. square unit reinforcement in Practice (2-3 MC problems interspersed with area calculations, not grouped separately; per TVP requirement — maintains the Early Lesson learning)
5. Comparison pairs: same rectangle in two units; selecting which measurement corresponds to which unit

---

### Must NOT Include (Explicit Out-of-Scope)

#### Concepts/Vocabulary NOT in M5
* **Square feet, square meters, square miles, cubic units, or any 3D volume concepts** (deferred to M6 and beyond)
* **Abbreviations** (sq in, sq cm, in², cm²) — full names only in M5 (TVP SME Decision overrides Module Mapping note)
* **Perimeter, perimeter formulas, or mixed area-perimeter problems**
* **Irregular shapes or non-rectangular figures** (M5 focuses on rectangles)
* **Conversion between units** (e.g., "How many square centimeters equal 1 square inch?") — deferred to M6+
* **Real-world applications or context problems** (e.g., "Design a room"; deferred to application module M14+)
* **Multiplication facts drills or times table mastery** — multiplication is a TOOL in M5, not a content focus

#### Terms NOT to Teach
* "Two-dimensional" / "2D" (jargon; use "area" or "rectangle")
* "Linear inch" vs. "square inch" (confusing; use "inch" for length, "square inch" for area)
* "Metric" vs. "standard" (imprecise; use "centimeter" and "inch" directly; both are standard units)

### Vocabulary Notes

#### Square Inch vs. Inch
**Frequent Misconception:** Students conflate "inch" (a linear length) with "square inch" (an area).
**Prevention Strategy:**
* ALWAYS say "square inch" when measuring area; never abbreviate to "inch" in an area context
* When discussing dimensions, say "1 inch" (for the side length); when discussing unit square, say "1 square inch"
* Use visual labels on grids: dimension side is labeled "1 in"; the grid square is labeled or framed as "1 square inch"

#### Centimeter vs. Square Centimeter
**Same prevention:** "centimeter" is a LINEAR unit; "square centimeter" is an AREA unit. Not interchangeable.

#### Standard Unit
**Definition in child-friendly language:** "A standard unit is a size that everyone in the world agrees on. An inch is the same size no matter who measures it. A square inch is the same size for everyone. That's why we call them standard units."

**Why it matters:** Prepares students for later (M6): "You could use any size unit, but standard units let us communicate about measurements."

### Terms to Avoid

| Term | Why Avoid | Better Language |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Two-dimensional / 2D** | Jargon; confuses students in Grade 3 | Use "area" or "flat shape" |
| **Linear inch** | Awkward phrasing | Use "inch" for length; "square inch" for area; context is clear |
| **Abbreviations (sq in, sq cm, in², cm²)** | Not introduced in M5 per TVP SME Decision; deferred to M6+ | Always use "square inches," "square centimeters" (full names) |
| **Metric vs. standard** | Imprecise and creates false dichotomy | Use "centimeters" and "inches" directly; both are standard units |
| **Improvised unit** | Jargon from 3.MD.C.6 standard; not needed in M5 | Use "non-standard unit" or "made-up unit" if needed (rare in M5) |
| **Two-unit comparison** (e.g., "inches and centimeters") | Ambiguous: refers to linear, not area | Use "square inches and square centimeters" or just one unit type per problem in M5 |

### Vocabulary Teaching Strategy (Phase-by-Phase)

**Early Lesson — Naming the Units:**
1. Display a 1×1 inch grid. Dimension labels show "1 in" on each side.
2. Say: "You know what an INCH is — you've measured with inches. A SQUARE INCH is a square shape with 1-inch sides." Point to the grid square.
3. Repeat with centimeter.
4. Have students SAY the terms aloud; write on a reference card.

**Mid Lesson — Comparing:**
1. Show the same rectangle with both grids side-by-side.
2. Point to a unit square in the inch grid: "This is one square inch."
3. Point to a unit square in the centimeter grid: "This is one square centimeter. Which is bigger?" (Expect: "This one" — pointing to square inch.)
4. "That's right. So the centimeter unit is smaller. Smaller units fit more times. That's why we get different numbers."

**Late Lesson & Practice:**
1. Require students to write or say the full unit name in every answer.
2. Use reference displays (see §1.5 tools) so students can always check what a square inch or square centimeter looks like.
3. Repeat the mantra: "Always say SQUARE inches or SQUARE centimeters — it tells us we're measuring area, not length."

## §1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS

**[REQUIRED]**

### Primary Misconception: M8 — Square Unit Confusion

#### Definition
Students confuse different-sized square units or believe that all square units are equivalent in value. They may think that a measurement of 12 square centimeters is the same as 12 square inches, or that "12" is "12" regardless of unit.

#### Observable Behaviors
1. Student says: "12 is 12. The numbers are the same, so the areas are the same."
2. Student drops the unit label: "The area is 12" (without specifying inches or centimeters).
3. Student conflates linear and square units: "The area is 5 inches" (using linear unit for area measurement).
4. Student sees 12 sq in and 80 sq cm for the same rectangle and assumes one must be wrong, rather than understanding both are correct for different units.

#### Why It Surfaces
* **Concept Confusion:** Students may not yet have internalized that the UNIT determines the NUMBER. In M4, students worked with unnamed "square units," so the number was tied only to the rectangle dimensions, not to unit choice.
* **Notation Gap:** Without explicit abbreviations (sq in vs. sq cm), students may not read or parse the unit label carefully.
* **Magnitude Misunderstanding:** Students may assume a "bigger number" (80) indicates a "bigger area" without considering that a smaller unit (cm) produces a larger count.

#### Assessment Frequency
* **Surfaces first:** Middle of M5 lesson (when comparing same rectangle in two units)
* **Checked at Exit:** Exit Check Problem 3 (MC: "Which measurement is square inches? Which is square centimeters?" for the same rectangle)
* **Monitored in Practice:** Type B MC problems include misconception traps (see §1.5, Practice Specifications)
* **Revisited in M6:** Misconception deepens if left unchecked; M6 ("Square Feet and Square Meters") will see students applying M8 to larger units

#### Mastery Tracking Note (from TVP)
* **Unit-label errors** (correct calculation, wrong unit) are **CONCEPTUAL** errors, not PROCEDURAL. These loop to M5 Early activities (vocabulary reinforcement).
* **Calculation errors** (wrong area, correct unit) are **PROCEDURAL** errors. These address multiplication fluency.
* This distinction drives intervention routing and must be preserved in Practice/EC problem tagging.

#### Intervention Strategy (Core Design)
1. **Prevent** via side-by-side visual comparison (same rectangle, visibly different grids)
2. **Confront** via counting or calculating area both ways in real-time ("Count these tiles. [pause]. Now count these. SAME rectangle, DIFFERENT numbers. Why?")
3. **Consolidate** via explicit mantra: "It takes MORE of a smaller unit to cover the SAME area."
4. **Practice** via Type B MC traps (see §1.5)

---

### Secondary Misconception: Bigger Number = Bigger Shape

#### Definition
When shown 12 square inches and 80 square centimeters for the same rectangle, students assume the shape measured as 80 square centimeters is actually BIGGER because "80 is a bigger number than 12."

#### Observable Behaviors
1. Student says: "This rectangle is bigger because 80 is more than 12."
2. Student chooses the larger number when asked "Which is the bigger area?" without understanding the unit difference.
3. Student assumes: "If the number is bigger, the shape is bigger."

#### Why It Surfaces
* **Perceptual Bias:** Magnitude comparison (80 > 12) is perceptually salient; unit labels are abstract.
* **Lack of Unit Integration:** Students haven't yet learned to treat the unit as PART of the measurement, so they compare only the numbers.

#### Assessment Frequency
* **Surfaces at:** Mid-Lesson Comparison activity (first time 12 sq in and 80 sq cm are shown together)
* **Checked at Exit:** Exit Check Problem 3 (if designed as MC with distractors including "80 is bigger")

#### Intervention Strategy (Core Design)
1. **Prevent** via side-by-side visual (same rectangle remains same size even though number changes)
2. **Confront** via overlay or flip: "Look. [Show inch grid.] This rectangle is covered by 12 tiles. [Flip to cm grid.] This same rectangle is now covered by 80 tiles. Did the rectangle grow? No. Did the tiles get smaller? Yes. Smaller tiles fit more times."
3. **Consolidate** via mantra: "The UNIT is part of the answer. 12 square inches and 80 square centimeters measure the SAME rectangle in DIFFERENT units."
4. **Practice** via comparison Type B traps (see §1.5)

### Interaction Tools Required

#### Multiple Choice (MC) Formats

**Type A: Direction Prediction (Assessment of Unit Recognition)**
* Student is shown a rectangle with a grid (inch or cm)
* Question: "What is the area?" or "Which unit is being used?"
* Distractors: numbers from the wrong unit, unit-less numbers, misconceptions (e.g., choosing "bigger number" for a smaller unit)

**Type B: Misconception Traps (Assessment & Remediation)**
* Student is shown a rectangle measured in BOTH units (side-by-side or sequential)
* Question: "Which measurement is in square inches? Which is in square centimeters?"
* Trap options:
  * "Both are the same; the numbers don't matter"
  * "The bigger number (80) is the bigger area"
  * "12 is correct; 80 is wrong"
  * (Correct:) "12 square inches and 80 square centimeters measure the same rectangle"
* **Diagnostic distractor requirement (per TVP):** At least 2 Practice problems must include "correct number, wrong unit" as an MC option (e.g., for a 4×3 inch-grid problem: options include "12 square centimeters"). **Linear vs. Square Unit Distinction (3-4 Problems, SME Requirement)**
* Student is shown a dimension label and asked: "Is this measuring length or area?"
* Options: "1 inch" (length), "1 square inch" (area), "1 centimeter" (length), "1 square centimeter" (area)
* Or: "Which unit is used for measuring area?" Options: "inch," "centimeter," "square inch," "square centimeter"

# §1.6 WARMUP (2-4 minutes)

## Core Purpose

Activate students' area-finding confidence from M4, create a visual mystery (same rectangle, different tile counts), and build curiosity about WHY the numbers differ — positioning the Lesson to name and explain standard square units.

**Key Function:** The Warmup creates the conceptual NEED for standard units. By showing the same rectangle tiled two ways (with visibly different-sized squares) and withholding unit labels, it generates a genuine question: "Why does the same rectangle have different numbers of tiles?" This question becomes the Lesson's driving force. Secondarily, the Warmup validates M4 transfer — students still see rectangles as "rows × columns = area."

**Why this serves the concept:** M5's core risk is students treating square inches and square centimeters as arbitrary labels with no conceptual weight. If students encounter "square inch" and "square centimeter" as vocabulary-first, the terms feel like trivia. By first experiencing the PUZZLE (same shape, different counts), students have a felt need for the vocabulary — the unit names EXPLAIN what they're seeing. The Notice & Wonder structure creates student-owned questions that the Lesson answers. Additionally, this structure directly prevents M8 (square unit confusion) by making the visual contrast salient BEFORE names are attached.

**Test:** "If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson?" **YES.** Without the side-by-side unlabeled comparison, students enter the Lesson without having noticed the core phenomenon (same shape, different counts). The Lesson's unit naming would arrive without a context to anchor it. The "Why are the numbers different?" question wouldn't be the student's question — it would be the Guide's question imposed on them. The Warmup makes the mystery the student's own.

**Conscious Tradeoff — Multiplication Activation:** This Warmup activates area VOCABULARY and VISUAL REASONING (rectangles, tiles, same-shape observation) but does NOT include a multiplication calculation check. The rationale: (1) adding a calculation step pushes the Warmup toward assessment, violating WR-12 (no assessment framing); (2) multiplication is an M4 prerequisite skill that students are expected to have — it is activated in the Lesson at 1.1b (first student area calculation) before any interaction depends on it; (3) the Warmup's purpose is conceptual priming (the mystery), not skill verification. If playtesting reveals that students struggle with multiplication in Section 1, consider inserting a brief "How many tiles on the left?" count-and-multiply check into W.1 as a future revision.

* Applying prior knowledge of unit sizes

EC.3 implements this for the first time. Practice extends it to single-unit problems (not just comparison).

# §1.9 SYNTHESIS (6-8 minutes)

### Opening Frame [Type A]

* **Purpose:** Signal shift from Practice to reflection. Callback to the Warmup's mystery — the same rectangle that started the lesson now returns with its unit names revealed. This completes the narrative arc: the Warmup asked "Why are the numbers different?" and now students can name the answer.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display, side-by-side mode).** The Warmup's 4×3 inch / 10×8 cm rectangle pair returns. Left panel: 4×3 inch grid, dimension labels "4 in" and "3 in," unit label "1 square inch" on one cell, text overlay "Area = 12 square inches." Right panel: Same rectangle, 10×8 cm grid, dimension labels "10 cm" and "8 cm," unit label "1 square centimeter" on one cell, text overlay "Area = 80 square centimeters."
* **Guide:** "Remember this rectangle from the start? Back then, all you knew was: 12 tiles on one side, 80 on the other. Now you can name them — 12 square inches and 80 square centimeters. Same rectangle. Different units. Different numbers."
* **No student action.**

### Connection Task S.1: What Makes a Unit "Standard"? [Type A — Pattern Discovery]

* **Purpose:** Assess understanding of "standard unit" (§7D carry-forward). The term was introduced in Lesson 1.3 (Type A — teaching only) but never assessed. This is the first time students must identify WHAT makes a unit "standard" — it's not about size, it's about agreement.
* **Visual:** Four squares displayed in a 2×2 grid:

| Square | Description |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Top-left** | A 1-inch square, labeled "1 square inch." Visibly large. |
| **Top-right** | A 1-centimeter square, labeled "1 square centimeter." Visibly small. |
| **Bottom-left** | A medium square with no label, labeled "My square — I drew it." |
| **Bottom-right** | A square of intermediate size, labeled "1 square unit." No specific unit name. |

* **Guide:** "Here are four different shapes people could use to measure area. Which ones are STANDARD units?"
* **Prompt:** "Select the standard units."
* **Student Action:** Multi-select (select 2 of 4)
  * **Expected:** Top-left (1 square inch) AND top-right (1 square centimeter)
* **Correct Answer:** 1 square inch and 1 square centimeter
* **On Correct:** "Those two — the square inch and the square centimeter. They're standard because everyone agrees on exactly how big they are. A 'square unit' could be any size — but a square inch is always the same, and a square centimeter is always the same. That's what makes them standard."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

### Connection Task S.2: What If the Tiles Were Even Smaller? [Type A — Generalization]

* **Purpose:** Prediction question deferred from Lesson 2.4 (§7G carry-forward). Students extend the inverse relationship to a hypothetical case: tiles even smaller than square centimeters. This works in Synthesis's reflective context because students have now demonstrated mastery of the pattern through Lesson, EC, and Practice — they're ready to GENERALIZE rather than just APPLY.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display, side-by-side mode).** The Opening Frame's 4×3 pair remains visible. Text overlay: "12 square inches" (left) and "80 square centimeters" (right). Below both panels, a dotted outline of the same rectangle with a "?" inside, labeled "Even smaller tiles."
* **Guide:** "You know the pattern: smaller tiles, bigger number. The square centimeter is smaller than the square inch — so 80 is more than 12. Now imagine tiles even SMALLER than square centimeters. What would happen to the number?"
* **Prompt:** "If you measured this rectangle with even smaller tiles, the number would be ___."
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:** "More than 80" / "Less than 80" / "Exactly 80" / "It depends on the rectangle"
* **Correct Answer:** "More than 80"
* **On Correct (per selection):**
  * "More than 80" → "More than 80 — even MORE tiles to cover the same rectangle. The smaller the tiles, the more you need. That pattern works every time."
  * "Less than 80" → "Think about what happened with centimeters — the tiles got smaller, and the number went from 12 to 80. Smaller tiles, MORE of them. So even smaller tiles would mean even more — more than 80."
  * "Exactly 80" → "80 was the number for square centimeters. Even smaller tiles would be different from square centimeters — and smaller tiles always need more to cover the same space. The number would go up past 80."
  * "It depends on the rectangle" → "Good thinking about variables! But for THIS rectangle, the pattern always works — smaller tiles, bigger number. It would be more than 80."
* **Remediation:** Pipeline

### Metacognitive Reflection S.3: What Helped You Know Which Unit? [Type 1 — Strategy Identification]

* **Purpose:** Students reflect on what strategy helped them determine the correct unit label — the key M5 skill. Planning-focused reflection (strongest for age 8-9). M5's core cognitive demand is unit IDENTIFICATION (reading the grid or dimension labels to determine the unit type), so Type 1 (Strategy Identification) is the natural fit.
* **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Display).** A single rectangle with a cm grid, dimension labels "7 cm" and "5 cm" visible. Area text overlay: "35 square centimeters." This is the Lesson 3.2 rectangle, reused to anchor reflection in a familiar example.
* **Guide:** "You measured lots of rectangles today with square inches and square centimeters. What helped you most when figuring out which unit to use?"
* **Prompt:** "What helped you most?"
* **Student Action:** MC selection
  * **Options:**
    * "Reading the labels on the sides of the rectangle"
    * "Looking at how big the grid squares are"
    * "Remembering which unit name goes with which grid"
    * "Something else"
* **Correct Answer:** Any selection (no wrong answer — reflection has no incorrect response)
* **On Correct (per selection):**
  * "Reading the labels" → "The dimension labels tell you everything — 'cm' means square centimeters, 'in' means square inches. Reading carefully is a powerful strategy."
  * "Looking at how big the grid squares are" → "You used the SIZE of the tiles to figure out the unit. Big tiles, square inches — small tiles, square centimeters. That's visual reasoning."
  * "Remembering which unit name goes with which grid" → "You connected the name to the picture. That's how vocabulary sticks — by linking it to something you can see."
  * "Something else" → "Everyone finds their own way in. What matters is you found a way that works for you."
* **Remediation:** N/A (no wrong answer — reflection)

# §1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS (KDD)

### KDD-1: Full Grids Throughout — Grid Size IS the Concept

Full grids are visible in every interaction from Warmup through Synthesis (Decision #3: M1-M6). In M5, the grid SIZE is the core concept — students must see the unit squares to understand why different units produce different numbers. Removing grids would remove the very thing being taught; fading begins at M7.

---

### KDD-2: Multiplication Is Tool, Not Content

Per Decision #8, M5 uses multiplication as a TOOL for area calculation, not as content to teach — students arrive fluent from M4. Multiplication difficulty is kept within standard facts (factors 2-10, products ≤100) so calculation never competes with unit-concept work. Multiplication errors are classified as PROCEDURAL (M4 review), not CONCEPTUAL (M5 remediation).

---

### KDD-3: Unit Labels Required in Every Answer

Unit-label omission is M5's "biggest risk" — if students drop labels, the misconception that units don't matter persists into M6. The EB includes a unit-label slot from Lesson Section 3 through EC, MC options always include "correct number, wrong unit" distractors, and Practice classifies missing/wrong units as CONCEPTUAL errors. No interaction accepts a bare number as correct.

---

### KDD-4: Inverse Relationship Emerges from Comparison, Not Direct Instruction

M5 never states "smaller units give bigger numbers" before students have seen it — Lesson Section 2 presents two concrete comparison pairs and lets students observe the pattern themselves. The explicit statement comes only AFTER the student confirms the pattern in interaction 2.3. The prediction question was deferred to Synthesis (§7G) because hypothetical extension works better in a reflective context after demonstrated mastery.

---

### KDD-5: EC Scaffolding Fade — Cell Labels Removed Progressively

Cell labels fade across EC: EC.1 retains "1 square inch" on a grid cell (scaffolded entry), EC.2 removes it (student reads dimension labels to identify the cm grid), and EC.3 has no cell labels on either grid. This progression — Lesson (labels present) → EC.1 (present) → EC.2-EC.3 (removed) — tests whether students have internalized unit identification or were merely copying visible labels.

---

### KDD-6: Same Warmup Rectangle Reused Across Session

The 4×3 inch / 10×8 cm pair appears in Warmup (unlabeled mystery), Lesson 2.1-2.3 (progressively labeled), and Synthesis Opening (fully labeled resolution) — the only intentional dimension reuse in the module. This serves a narrative function: the same rectangle transforms from "12 tiles and 80 tiles — why different?" to "12 square inches and 80 square centimeters — because the units are different sizes."

---

### KDD-7: Linear vs. Square Distinction Split Across Lesson and Practice

The TVP's 3-4 lin/sq MC problems are distributed: 2 in Lesson (1.4 inch, 1.5 cm) + 1-2 in Practice (§7E carry-forward, interspersed with area calculations). Distributing them across phases — rather than grouping consecutively in the Lesson — ensures students encounter the distinction interspersed with area work, better testing whether the concept transfers to applied contexts.

---

### KDD-8: No Equation Builder in Synthesis

Synthesis uses Grid Rectangles (Display) only — no Equation Builder, following M4's precedent (M4 KDD-9). The EB is a learning scaffold; using it in Synthesis would make the phase feel like more Practice rather than post-mastery reflection. All Synthesis interactions use MC or multi-select with visual support from Grid Rectangles.

---

### KDD-9: Type D (Representation Transfer) Omitted by Design

M5 uses one primary visual representation (Grid Rectangles) throughout — the "representation transfer" IS the unit switch (same rectangle, different grid), which is the Lesson's core cognitive work. A dedicated Type D task in Synthesis would repackage the Lesson's comparison activity as a reflection task, making Synthesis feel like more Practice rather than consolidation.

---

### KDD-10: "Standard Unit" Assessed in Synthesis, Not Lesson

"Standard unit" is introduced in Lesson 1.3 (Type A, no student action) but assessed in Synthesis S.1 because the Lesson's assessment bandwidth is allocated to higher-priority operational skills (lin/sq distinction, unit-label production, inverse relationship). Synthesis is the appropriate place to verify understanding of WHAT makes a unit "standard" (agreement on size) versus just knowing the term.

---

### KDD-11: Prediction Question Deferred from Lesson to Synthesis

Lesson 2.4 uses a second concrete comparison pair (4×4 in / 10×10 cm) rather than the originally planned prediction question ("even smaller tiles?") because prediction requires extending the inverse relationship to a hypothetical case — developmentally ambitious for Grade 3 in a discovery context. The prediction question appears instead in Synthesis S.2, where students have demonstrated mastery through Lesson, EC, and Practice and are ready to generalize.

---

### KDD-12: Type B Misconception Trap Has Hard Floor of 2 Problems in Practice

Practice allocates 15% to "bigger number = bigger area" misconception traps with a hard floor of 2 problems, exceeding the TVP's minimum of 1. The Backbone identifies this secondary misconception as M5's "biggest risk" — if students leave M5 confusing larger area numbers with larger physical areas, the confusion compounds in M6 with larger units.

---

### KDD-13: Full Unit Names Only — No Abbreviations

The TVP SME Decision specifies "full unit names required" in M5 — no "sq in" or "sq cm." Students need to hear "square inches" and "square centimeters" enough times that the SQUARE prefix becomes automatic; abbreviations compress the term and may let students skip connecting "square" to the 2D unit. Abbreviations may be introduced in M6+ once the full terms are internalized.

---

### KDD-14: EC Uses 3×3/8×8 Square Pair — Fresh Geometry for Assessment

The EC comparison pair (3×3 in = 9 sq in / 8×8 cm = 64 sq cm) uses a SQUARE shape distinct from Lesson's rectangles and the Lesson's 4×4 square, testing whether students apply the inverse relationship to a shape type they've seen but with fresh dimensions. All factors are ≤ 10 with no overlap with any Lesson rectangle or rotation, and the 9 vs. 64 contrast is dramatic enough to make the comparison unambiguous.

---

### KDD-15: M6 Bridge Mentions "Bigger Standard Units" Without Naming Them

The Synthesis closure mentions "even bigger standard units" as a PREVIEW of M6 without naming square feet or square meters — those terms are M6's responsibility. The bridge implies the inverse (bigger units → smaller numbers) and leaves students with curiosity, following M4's precedent of mentioning next-module terms as preview without teaching.

---

### KDD-16: Warmup Prioritizes Conceptual Mystery Over Prerequisite Verification

The Warmup activates M4 vocabulary ("area," "square units," "rows × columns") but does not include a multiplication calculation — the Notice & Wonder structure requires withholding answers, and inserting a calculation would shift the phase from observation to performance. Multiplication fluency is first verified at Lesson 1.1b; if prerequisite gaps surface there, Pipeline remediation handles them. The risk (a student with M4 regression encounters new content before the gap is caught) is accepted because the Warmup's curiosity function is load-bearing for M5's vocabulary-anchoring strategy.
