## 1.0 THE ONE THING
**Testable Core:**<br>By the end of M7, students can **determine the area of a rectangle from its dimensions alone** (no grid, no tick marks) **by multiplying side lengths**, and can **state the area with the correct square unit**.
**CRA Stage: Abstract.** M7 is the first module in the Grade 3 progression WITHOUT full visual grid support. Students transition from "counting visible squares" to "inferring the complete rectangular array from side length dimensions." This is the critical abstraction step in the CRA spiral.
**Critical Misconception:#5: Not Multiplying Side Lengths** (PRIMARY, HIGH PRIORITY)
- **Observable Behavior:** Student reads dimensions correctly (e.g., "5 and 4") but does not multiply them. May instead:
	- Add the dimensions (5 + 4 = 9, stated as area)
	- Leave the answer as two separate numbers ("5 and 4")
	- Count squares if any visual support is present, ignoring dimension labels
	- Fail to state the answer with a square unit
- **Why It Emerges in M7:** Without a full grid to count, students must infer the array. If they don't connect dimensions to multiplication, they lose the tool entirely.
- **Surfaces First:** M7 (Early, Mid phases); may persist into M8-M9 if not addressed
**Success Indicator:** Given a rectangle with dimensions labeled but no visible grid, the student independently multiplies the side lengths, states the area with the correct square unit (square feet, square meters, square inches, or square centimeters), and can do this whether the rectangle is shown with tick marks, dimension labels only, or described in text with no visual at all.
**Biggest Risk:Grid fading pace.** M7 must complete all three transitions (partial → tick marks → dimensions only) within a single module. If partial grid is extended too long, students may not reach dimensions-only reasoning. If fading is too fast, students may add or guess without understanding. The pacing MUST support the abstraction without scaffolding collapse.

## 1.2 SCOPE BOUNDARIES
### ✅ Must Teach
- Grid Fading Progression (complete in M7): Warm-up uses full grid as reference for familiar rectangle (e.g., 4×6). Early Phase uses partial grid (first row and first column filled; interior empty). Mid Phase uses tick marks only (evenly spaced marks on top and left edges; no grid interior). Late Phase uses dimensions only (rectangle outline with dimension labels; no grid, no tick marks). **Critical:** All three transitions must complete within M7. No backsliding to M8.
- All four unit types across phases: Square feet (sq ft), square meters (sq m), square inches (sq in), square centimeters (sq cm). All four units must appear in Mid (tick marks) and Late (dimensions only) phases. Early (partial grid) uses square units generically (e.g., "square tiles").
- Text-only problems (Late Phase & Exit Check): Rigid sentence template: "A \[object\] is \[number\] \[unit\] long and \[number\] \[unit\] wide. What is its area?" No visual support; student must extract dimensions from text and multiply. Introduction: Late Phase; extensive practice: EC (Exit Check).
- Multiply without visual grid: Students can multiply side length dimensions to find area without a full grid. This is the primary abstraction of M7.
- Side length and dimension vocabulary (introduced after concrete experience): Side length = how long one side of a rectangle is (not introduced before students have manipulated partial grids). Dimension = a measurement; one of the factors in the area multiplication. Introduce after Early phase, not before. Connect explicitly: "Dimensions ARE the factors in multiplication."
### ❌ Must Not Include
- Perimeter concept: Even as a differentiation tool for the "add side lengths" misconception, do not introduce perimeter language, formula, or concept. Misconception #5 is addressed through multiplication reasoning alone. M7 is area-only.
- Composite figures: No L-shaped, T-shaped, or irregular figures. M7 is limited to simple rectangles in all four grid-fading states.
- New units: All four unit types are from M5/M6. Do not introduce square kilometers, acres, or any new units in M7.
- Ruler measurement: No student measurement activities with physical rulers. All dimensions are given or read from grid/tick marks.
### Scope Confirmation Checklist
- [ ] Grid fading progresses: partial (Early) → tick marks (Mid) → dimensions only (Late)
- [ ] All transitions complete within M7
- [ ] All four unit types present in Mid and Late phases
- [ ] Text-only problems introduced in Late phase and expanded in EC
- [ ] Side length and dimension vocabulary taught AFTER concrete experience (Early phase complete)
- [ ] No perimeter language or concept introduced
- [ ] No composite figures
- [ ] No new units
- [ ] No ruler measurement activities
- [ ] Equation Builder supports multiplication with unit labels (Mid/Late)
- [ ] Guide reveal available for remediation (not visible to student; 2+ reveals flag M3 reinforcement need)

### Vocabulary Notes
**Side Length:** How long one side of a rectangle is. Instructional sequence: (1) Partial grid: "How many tiles are in one row?" Count concretely. (2) Mid phase: "We call the number of tiles in one row the side length across. We have 7 across and 4 down." (3) Dimensions-only: "The side lengths are 7 and 4. We multiply them to find area."<br>**Dimension:** A measurement; one of the factors in multiplication. Critical connection: "Dimensions ARE the factors in multiplication." Introduce after Early phase concrete experience, not before.<br>**Square Units (all four types):** Students must read and apply square feet, square meters, square inches, and square centimeters correctly in Mid and Late phases. Emphasis: "Area is always measured in square units, not plain units."<br>**Factor & Product:** Factor = one of the numbers in a multiplication. Product = the result of multiplication. M7 connection: "The dimensions are the factors. The area is the product." This explicit connection supports conceptual understanding of why multiplication gives area.
### Terms to Avoid (Save for Later Modules)
- "Formula" (standalone) — suggests rote memorization; M7 is about reasoning why multiplication works from dimensions, not applying a formula. Use: "Multiply the dimensions to find area."
- "Perimeter" — do not use, even for contrast or clarification. If student adds side lengths, address via: "We multiply to find area, not add."
- "Decompose" — M7 does not decompose rectangles. Composite figures and decomposition appear in later units.
- "Feet squared," "Meters squared" — ambiguous; students confuse with exponent notation. Use: "Square feet," "Square meters."

## 1.4 MISCONCEPTIONS
### **1.4.1 #5: Not Multiplying Side Lengths (PRIMARY)**
**Trigger Behavior:** Students read side length dimensions correctly but fail to connect them to multiplication. Instead, they add the dimensions ("5 + 4 = 9"), state them as two separate numbers ("The area is 5 and 4"), count squares when any partial grid is visible (ignoring dimension labels), or leave answers without units ("9" instead of "9 square feet"). Observable triggers: reading tick marks or dimension labels but responding with addition, repetition of separate numbers, or omitted units.
**Why It Happens:** In M6, students had full grids to count. Dimensions were labels on the grid, not the primary tool. In M7, grid support disappears progressively. Dimensions become the ONLY information available in Late phase. If students don't construct the connection, "I see the dimensions 4 and 7. That means 4 rows of 7 squares. So I multiply: 4 × 7 = 28," they have no fallback tool. The abstraction from "visible array" to "inferred array" depends entirely on this conceptual link.
**Prevention Strategy:** (1) Concrete Row/Column Counting (Early Phase): Return to partial grid. "How many are in one row?" Count concretely. "How many rows?" Guide to 4 × 7, not 4 + 7. Concrete experience before terminology. (2) Grid Persistence with Fading (Mid Phase): Partial grid to tick marks on same rectangle. "We don't see all the squares now, but we still have 7 across and 4 down. The area is still the same. 4 × 7 = 28." Equation Builder requires student to build the multiplication expression explicitly. (3) Equation Builder with Units (Mid & Late Phases): Student must construct multiplication expression with dimension labels. Forces the multiplication action even without visual grid. All solutions include unit labels (e.g., "4 × 7 = 28 square feet"). (4) Guide Reveal (Late Phase, Temporary): If student cannot solve dimensions-only rectangle, guide can activate grid overlay temporarily. 2+ reveals flag M3 reinforcement need. (5) Vocabulary Anchor (Throughout): "The dimensions are your factors. Multiply them to find the product, which is the area." Repeated in EXIT CHECK and Synthesis.
**Anti-Pattern Detection:** Student's calculation includes addition of dimensions (e.g., "5 + 4 = 9" for a 5×4 rectangle), repetition of separate numbers without multiplication, or correct multiplication but omitted unit. These indicate the dimension-multiplication link has not been constructed. Loop to Early Phase concrete counting.
---
### **1.4.2 #8: Square Unit Confusion (SECONDARY)**
**Trigger Behavior:** Without grids, students lose visual anchor for "square" units. They may state area without a unit ("28"), use a linear unit instead ("28 feet" instead of "28 square feet"), use an incorrect unit ("28 centimeters" when the problem is in square centimeters), or state two separate units for two dimensions ("5 feet and 4 feet" instead of "20 square feet"). Observable triggers: calculating correctly ("5 × 4 = 20") but omitting the unit, using a linear unit, or repeating input units separately.
**Why It Happens:** Full grids in M6 showed squares visually. Each cell was an obvious square unit. In M7 Mid and Late phases, there is no visual grid. The word "square" becomes abstract. Students may remember "we measure area" but forget that area is always in *square* units, derived from multiplying two linear measurements. Without explicit unit labels throughout the module, this misconception persists into M8.
**Prevention Strategy:** (1) Explicit Unit Labels in Equation Builder (Mid & Late Phases): All solutions include unit labels. Students must write or select full unit: "6 × 4 = 24 square feet," not just "24." Visible unit labeling reinforces that area is always square units. (2) Vocabulary Anchor (Throughout): "Area is always in SQUARE units. When we multiply two measurements, we get a square unit." Connection: "Dimensions ARE linear. Their product IS square." (3) Text-Only Problem Structure (Late Phase & EXIT CHECK): Student must respond with full unit: "35 square feet," not "35 feet" or "7 and 5 feet." Full unit required in writing. (4) Guide Reminder (Any phase, if unit omitted): "What are we measuring? Area. Area is always in what kind of unit? Square units. So the answer is __ square __." (5) Synthesis: "Area is a multiplication of two dimensions. That's why it's always SQUARE units." Bridge to M8.
**Anti-Pattern Detection:** Student's calculation is correct but the unit is missing, wrong, or swapped (e.g., answering "24" instead of "24 square feet," or "24 feet" instead of "24 square feet"). These are CONCEPTUAL errors indicating the linear-to-square unit connection was not made. Loop to vocabulary anchor activities.

## 1.6 WARMUP (2-4 minutes)
### Core Purpose
Activate students' M6 grid-counting fluency with a familiar rectangle, then create a dramatic curiosity moment by removing the grid entirely — showing that the rectangle and its area haven't changed even though the squares are gone. This primes the central question of M7: how do you find area when you can't see the grid?
**Key Function:** The Warmup does two things: (1) confirms M6 prerequisite transfer (students can calculate area of a full-grid rectangle by multiplying), and (2) creates the M7 driving question through a Mystery Reveal — students watch a familiar grid disappear and confront the fact that the area is still determinable from the dimension labels alone. This positions the Lesson to teach the systematic progression from partial grids to dimensions-only reasoning.
**Why this serves the concept:** M7's core risk is that students will treat grid removal as "losing information" rather than recognizing that dimensions encode the same information the grid provided. By showing the grid disappear on a rectangle students already know (4×6 = 24), the Warmup makes two things simultaneously true: (a) the area hasn't changed, and (b) the only remaining information is the two dimension numbers. This creates the productive tension that drives the entire Lesson — "If I can't see the squares, but the numbers are still there, maybe the numbers are enough." Without this moment, the Lesson's partial grids would feel like degraded tools rather than scaffolded steps toward independence.
**Test:** "If we removed this Warmup, would students lose mathematical preparation for the Lesson?" **YES.** Without the full-grid → dimensions-only reveal, students enter the partial grid phase without experiencing the core insight: that dimensions alone carry the area information. They would encounter partial grids as a novel tool rather than as a step in a progression they've already previewed. The Mystery Reveal makes grid fading feel purposeful rather than arbitrary.

### Warmup Type Rationale
TVP specifies: "Full grid, familiar rectangle (e.g., 4×6), quick area calculation, then SAME rectangle with grid removed — only outline + dimension labels." This maps directly to **Mystery Reveal** (Playbook §4D): show partial information → students predict/observe → reveal full picture → connect prediction to reality. The "mystery" is the grid disappearing; the "reveal" is that the numbers (dimensions) remain and the area is unchanged. The dramatic visual transition (grid fading to nothing while dimension labels persist) is the hook. Personalization comes from the M6 callback ("You've been finding area with grids"), and the Narrative anchor is the transformation itself. This Warmup type also appears in the Playbook's Module 4-7 recommended range and the "counterintuitive concept" selection criteria — the counterintuitive element is that removing visual information doesn't remove mathematical information.

### Interaction W.1: The Familiar Grid \[ACTIVATION\]
- **Purpose:** Quick callback to M6 grid-counting. Student sees a familiar full-grid rectangle and calculates area by multiplying — confirming prerequisite fluency before the grid disappears.
- **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Full Grid).** 4×6 rectangle (4 rows, 6 columns). All 24 squares visible. Grid lines clear. Labeled "square tiles."
- **Guide:** "Here's a rectangle you've seen before. 4 rows, 6 in each row. What's the area?"
- **Prompt:** "What is the area of this rectangle in square tiles?"
- **Student Action:** MC selection
	- **Options:** A) 10 square tiles, B) 20 square tiles, C) 24 square tiles, D) 46 square tiles
- **Correct Answer:** C) 24 square tiles
- **Answer Rationale:** A) 10 — adds 4+6 (misconception #5 preview: adding dimensions instead of multiplying); B) 20 — common multiplication error or rounding; C) 24 — correct (4 × 6 = 24); D) 46 — concatenates digits "4" and "6"
- **On Correct:** "24 square tiles. 4 rows of 6, you multiplied to find the area."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Design Note:** The 4×6 rectangle is the TVP-specified Warmup rectangle. MC distractor A (10) tests whether the addition misconception (#5) is active before the Lesson addresses it. This serves as a lightweight diagnostic: if a student selects A, the Pipeline can flag this for the Lesson's targeted remediation in Early Phase. The On Correct uses "multiplied" — vocabulary from M4, not new for M7.

### Interaction W.2: The Grid Disappears \[MYSTERY REVEAL — JUDGMENT\]
- **Purpose:** The core Mystery Reveal moment. The SAME 4×6 rectangle from W.1 transitions via animation: grid squares fade, grid lines disappear, until only the rectangle outline and dimension labels ("4" on the left side, "6" on the top) remain. Student makes a judgment about whether the area changed — the key insight is that it did NOT.
- **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Full Grid → Dimensions Only, animated transition).** Same 4×6 rectangle. Animation: grid interior fades over 3-4 seconds. Grid lines dissolve. Outline remains. Dimension labels "4" and "6" appear (or persist) on the left and top edges. Final state: bare outline with two numbers.
- **Guide:** "Watch what happens. \[ANIMATED GRID FADE.\] The squares are gone. But look, those two numbers are still there: 4 and 6! Here's my question: did the area change?"
- **Prompt:** "Did the area of the rectangle change? Tap Yes or No."
- **Student Action:** Binary tap (Yes / No)
- **Correct Answer:** No
- **On Correct:** "The area didn't change. It's still 24 square tiles. The rectangle is the same rectangle. We just can't see all the squares anymore."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Design Note:** Type B — binary judgment with student tap. This is the hook AND the judgment task (satisfying WR-3 and WR-5 simultaneously). The animated transition must feel like the SAME rectangle losing its grid, not a new rectangle appearing. The dimension labels (4 and 6) are the only visual residue — this plants the seed that dimensions carry the area information. The binary Yes/No format is low-stakes (WR-12) but requires genuine mathematical judgment. The On Correct deliberately echoes the TVP's key message verbatim: "The rectangle didn't change. The area didn't change. We just can't see all the squares anymore."
> **Voice Note:** "We just can't see all the squares anymore" — the word "anymore" implies loss, creating productive tension. The Guide names what happened (grid disappeared) and what DIDN'T happen (area didn't change) without explaining HOW to solve it without the grid. That's the Lesson's job. The numbers 4 and 6 are referred to informally as "those two numbers," NOT "dimensions" — that vocabulary is reserved for Section 2, interaction 2.0. Script writers must not introduce "dimension" or "side length" language in the Warmup.

### Interaction W.3: Bridge to Lesson
- **Purpose:** Create anticipation for the Lesson without teaching. The question "How do you find the area when you can't see the grid?" is left deliberately unanswered.
- **Visual:** Remains on the dimensions-only 4×6 rectangle from W.2.
- **Guide:** "You've always had a grid to count. Every time you found area, you could see the squares. But what if there's no grid: just those two numbers? Let's figure out how to find area when the squares aren't there."
- **No student action.**
> **Design Note:** Bridge per Warmup Playbook §4G. Creates anticipation ("Let's figure out how...") without explaining the method. The phrase "just those two numbers" points to dimensions as the key information source — this is the thread the Purpose Frame will pick up. "Let's figure out" is invitational per Voice Script Prompt §3.

## 1.7 LESSON (8-12 minutes)
### Core Purpose + Pedagogical Flow
Students learn to determine the area of a rectangle by multiplying its dimensions — without counting grid squares. The grid fading progression (partial grid → tick marks → dimensions only) systematically removes visual scaffolding while proving at each step that the mathematical relationship (dimensions × dimensions = area) holds regardless of what's visible. By the end, students solve text-only area problems using multiplication of side lengths alone.

### Tile Palette Design
**Palette source:** Number tiles 2-10 per Backbone §1.5.2.
**Progressive expansion:** 6-7 tiles in S1, 5-6 tiles in S2, 8 number tiles + \{×, =\} in S3.
**Distractor types for M7:**
- **Off-by-one:** Factor ±1 (e.g., for a 4-row rectangle, include 3 and 5). Catches row/column miscounting.
- **Adjacent:** Numbers near correct factors that test imprecise reading of partial grid, tick marks, or dimension labels.
- **Sum of factors:** When sum ≤ 10 (e.g., 3+8=11 — not applicable, but 4+5=9 is). Catches addition-instead-of-multiplication (misconception #5).
- **Carryover factor:** A factor from the previous interaction. Catches recall-instead-of-reading.

### Forbidden Phrases
- ❌ **"formula"** (standalone) — Avoid: "Use the formula length × width." Instead: "Multiply the dimensions to find the area." Reasoning: "Formula" suggests rote memorization. M7 is about reasoning why multiplication works from dimensions. Per §1.3 Terms to Avoid.
- ❌ **"perimeter"** — Do not use, even for contrast or clarification. If student adds dimensions, address via multiplication reasoning only. Per Important Decision #5.
- ❌ **"decompose"** — Not in M7 scope. Composite figures appear in later units. Per §1.3 Terms to Avoid.
- ❌ **"You have to..." / "You need to..."** — Controlling language. Use "Try..." or "You can..." Per Voice Script Prompt anti-patterns.
- ❌ **"carefully" / "thoroughly" / "systematically"** — Red flag words that assume internal states. Per Voice Script Prompt §1.
- ❌ **"You understood that" / "You're thinking about..."** — Assumed internal states. Reference observable behavior only.
- ❌ **"Perfect!" / "Excellent!" / "Amazing!"** — Generic overpraise. Use specific observable acknowledgments.
- ❌ **"Yesterday..." / "Today we will learn..."** — Calendar language. Use session-relative: "last time," "this time."
- ❌ **"feet squared" / "meters squared"** — Ambiguous; students confuse with exponent notation. Use "square feet," "square meters." Per §1.3 Terms to Avoid.
- ❌ **"Count the squares"** (in Mid/Late) — Students cannot count squares from tick marks or dimensions only. Use "Multiply the dimensions."
- ❌ **"Bigger number means bigger area"** — False without qualifying unit. Per §1.3 (from M6).

### Misconception Prevention
**#5: Not Multiplying Side Lengths (PRIMARY, HIGH)**
- **Where targeted:** Throughout all three Lesson sections.
- **Section 1 (Early — Partial Grid):** Interactions 1.1-1.4. The partial grid makes the row/column structure visible. Student sees "I count 7 in the row, 4 rows down. 4 groups of 7. Multiply: 4 × 7." The Equation Builder FORCES the multiplication action — student cannot submit without building a multiplication expression. If student adds dimensions instead (e.g., enters 11 for a 4×7), Pipeline redirects to row-counting from the partial grid.
- **Section 2 (Mid — Tick Marks):** Interactions 2.1-2.4. Tick marks remove interior squares but preserve dimensional information. Equation Builder with unit labels makes the multiplication explicit: "6 × 3 = 18 square feet." The unit label reinforces that the product IS the area.
- **Section 3 (Late — Dimensions Only):** Interactions 3.1-3.5. No visual scaffolding. If student adds dimensions instead of multiplying, guide reveal (§1.5) can temporarily show the grid. 2+ reveals flag M3 reinforcement.
- **Warmup diagnostic:** W.1 distractor A (10 = 4+6) pre-screens for this misconception.
**#8: Square Unit Confusion (SECONDARY, MEDIUM)**
- **Where targeted:** Sections 2-3 (Mid and Late phases).
- **Section 2 (Mid):** Equation Builder requires unit labels in all answers. Student must produce "18 square feet," not "18" or "18 feet." All four unit types appear across 2.1-2.4, requiring correct unit selection.
- **Section 3 (Late):** Text-only problems (3.4-3.5) require the student to state the answer with the full square unit. On Correct always includes the complete unit: "27 square inches," not "27."
- **Vocabulary anchor from 2.0:** "Dimensions are the factors in multiplication" — connecting dimensions to area language helps students remember that multiplying two lengths produces a SQUARE unit.
### Success Criteria (§1.7.5)
- Student reads partial grid and builds correct multiplication (Interactions 1.2, 1.4)
- Student reads tick marks and builds correct multiplication with unit labels (Interactions 2.1-2.4)
- Student reads dimensions-only and builds correct equation independently (Interactions 3.1-3.3)
- Student extracts dimensions from text and calculates area with correct unit (Interactions 3.4-3.5)
- All four unit types used correctly in Mid and Late phases
- Both factor orders accepted as correct (dual-orientation validation)
- "Side length" and "dimension" used correctly in context (Guide models; assessed in EC)

### EC.1: Partial Grid — Area in Square Feet \[CREATE\]
- **Purpose:** Can the student read a partial grid and multiply to find area? Tests S1 skill (partial-grid reading + Equation Builder construction) — the first level of M7's grid-fading progression. Uses the same Equation Builder unlabeled-slot mode as S1, with square feet as the unit.
- **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Partial Grid).** 3×7 rectangle. First row: 7 squares across. First column: 3 squares down. Interior empty. Unit label: "square feet." **Equation Builder (unlabeled slots):** `[___] × [___] = [___] square feet`. **Tile palette: \{2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8\}.**
- **Guide:** "Here's a partial grid. Find the area in square feet."
- **Prompt:** "Count the row and column. Build the multiplication to find the area."
- **Student Action:** Drag-to-place (Equation Builder) — select 2 tiles from palette
	- **Expected:** 3 into first slot, 7 into second (or 7, 3). Product 21 auto-computes.
- **Correct Answer:** 3 × 7 = 21 square feet (or 7 × 3 = 21 square feet)
- **Answer Rationale:**
	- 21 = Correct (3 rows × 7 columns = 21 square feet)
	- If student places 4, 7 → 28 (off-by-one on rows, miscounting column)
	- If student places 3, 8 → 24 (off-by-one on columns)
	- If student places 3, 7 but reads product as 10 → added instead of multiplied (misconception #5 — EB auto-computes, so this only triggers if using MC fallback)
- **On Correct:** "3 × 7 = 21 square feet."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Design Note:** Type B — brief instruction in Guide + complete instruction in Prompt. EC is assessment, not teaching. EC.1 integrates S1 skill (partial-grid reading) with S2 vocabulary (unit naming) as a cumulative M7 assessment. Although the grid mode matches S1, the unit-labeled Equation Builder and named-unit answer format draw on vocabulary introduced in S2. This is by design: EC tests the student's ability to combine skills across the Lesson progression, not just replicate a single section's task. The partial grid rectangle uses factors within Early data constraints (one factor ≤5: 3 rows; other 3-9: 7 across; product 21 ≤ 45). EB unlabeled-slot mode matches S1's Equation Builder configuration exactly. Tile palette includes off-by-one distractors (2, 4 near 3; 6, 8 near 7) and the sum (10 = 3+7, within palette as implicit distractor pair). Feedback is minimal per Playbook §3E. EC.1 intentionally combines partial-grid reading (S1) with named-unit production (S2+) to test the integrated M7 skill — by EC time, students have used all four unit names across S2 and S3. This is a cumulative assessment of the M7 learning goal, not a section-specific retest of S1 in isolation. "Square feet" is more representative of what students will encounter in M8+ than the generic "square tiles" used during Early teaching.

### EC.2: Dimensions Only — Area in Square Meters \[CREATE\]
- **Purpose:** Can the student read dimension labels and multiply to find area without any grid or tick marks? Tests S3 skill (dimensions-only reading + unstructured Equation Builder) — the second level of M7's grid-fading progression. Square meters as the unit.
- **Visual: Grid Rectangles (Dimensions Only).** 6×8 rectangle. Outline only. Labels: "6 m" on top, "8 m" on left. No grid, no tick marks. **Equation Builder (no template, no scaffolding)** — number tiles \{3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9\} + \{×, =\} available. Student constructs expression independently.
- **Guide:** "Now just the dimensions, no grid at all. Find the area."
- **Prompt:** "Use the dimensions to find the area of this rectangle in square meters."
- **Student Action:** Drag-to-place (Equation Builder — unstructured) — arrange number tiles + operators
	- **Expected:** 6 × 8 = 48 square meters (or 8 × 6 = 48 square meters). Product auto-computes after valid expression formed.
- **Correct Answer:** 48 square meters
- **Answer Rationale:**
	- 48 = Correct (6 × 8 = 48 square meters)
	- If student builds 6 + 8 = 14 → misconception #5 (adding instead of multiplying)
	- If student places 6, 9 → 54 (off-by-one on second dimension)
	- If student places 5, 8 → 40 (off-by-one on first dimension)
- **On Correct:** "6 × 8 = 48 square meters."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Design Note:** Type B — assessment, minimal teaching. Dimensions-only mode with unstructured EB matches S3 Lesson configuration (3.1-3.3). 6×8 = 48 is within Late constraints (factors 2-10, product ≤ 100) and is mid-range — harder than S3's smallest (2×7=14) but easier than S3's largest (6×9=54). The unstructured EB means the student must construct "6 × 8 = " from available tiles and operators — same cognitive demand as Lesson S3 interactions.

### EC.3: Text Only — Area in Square Feet \[IDENTIFY + CREATE\]
- **Purpose:** Can the student extract dimensions from text and calculate area with no visual support at all? Tests S3 Late skill (3.4-3.5) — the highest abstraction level in M7 and the ultimate demonstration of the learning goal: "Determine the area of rectangles not displayed on a grid." IDENTIFY component: locating dimensions in text. CREATE component: building the multiplication.
- **Visual:** Text only. No Grid Rectangles displayed. The problem appears as text on screen: **"A rectangular rug is 4 feet long and 8 feet wide. What is its area?"** **Equation Builder (no template, no scaffolding)** — number tiles + operators available alongside text. MC fallback per ISF-4: 32 sq ft, 12 sq ft, 32 feet, 48 sq ft.
- **Guide:** "Last one. Read the problem and find the area."
- **Prompt:** "A rectangular rug is 4 feet long and 8 feet wide. What is its area?"
- **Student Action:** Drag-to-place (Equation Builder — unstructured). Fallback: MC with options 32 sq ft (correct), 12 sq ft (addition: 4+8), 32 feet (wrong unit — linear instead of square), 48 sq ft (common multiplication error 4×12 or misread as 6×8).
	- **Expected:** 4 × 8 = 32 square feet
- **Correct Answer:** 32 square feet
- **Answer Rationale:**
	- 32 = Correct (4 × 8 = 32 square feet)
	- 12 = Added dimensions (4 + 8) — misconception #5
	- 32 feet = Correct number but wrong unit (linear instead of square) — misconception #8
	- 48 = Common multiplication error or dimension misread
- **On Correct:** "4 × 8 = 32 square feet."
- **Remediation:** Pipeline
> **Design Note:** Type B — assessment. Text uses the TVP rigid template: "A \[object\] is \[number\] \[unit\] long and \[number\] \[unit\] wide. What is its area?" The object "rug" is fresh (Lesson used "carpet" in 3.4 and "garden" in 3.5). MC fallback includes both misconception #5 (addition: 12) and misconception #8 (wrong unit: 32 feet) distractors — the two primary M7 misconceptions. Product 32 is mid-range. EC.3 uses square feet to bookend with EC.1 (also sq ft) — the student demonstrates the same unit across the full abstraction range (partial grid → text-only), isolating the effect of grid removal on performance.

### Connection Task S.2: Where Did You Start? \[M3-M4 Callback\]
- **Purpose:** Students connect M7's grid-fading progression back to their M3-M4 learning trajectory. M3 taught rows and columns as the structure of area. M4 taught that multiplying rows × columns gives the area. M7 proved that you don't need to SEE the rows and columns — the dimensions encode them. This task makes that connection explicit by asking students to identify WHICH prior knowledge made the grid unnecessary.
- **Task Type:** Type C (Real-World Bridge) — connecting today's learning to the broader learning trajectory.
- **Visual:** Two side-by-side images:
	- Left: A full-grid rectangle (Grid Rectangles, Full Grid mode) with rows and columns clearly marked, labeled "What you used to see"
	- Right: A dimensions-only rectangle with just the outline and labels, labeled "What you see now"
	- Below both: the equation `6 × 7 = 42 square feet` (connecting both images)
- **Guide:** "Look at both rectangles. On the left is how you started: a full grid where you could count every square. On the right is where you are now: just the dimensions. The equation is the same: 6 × 7 = 42. What made it possible to stop counting squares?"
- **Prompt:** "What let you find the area without counting every square?"
- **Student Action:** MC selection
	- **Options:**
		- "Knowing that the dimensions tell you how many rows and columns"
		- "Counting the squares along the edges instead of all of them"
		- "Remembering that bigger rectangles have bigger areas"
- **Correct Answer:** "Knowing that the dimensions tell you how many rows and columns"
- **Answer Rationale:**
	- Option 1 = Correct — the dimensions encode the row/column structure (M3-M4 → M7 connection)
	- Option 2 = Incorrect — describes the partial-grid counting strategy, not the conceptual insight. A student selecting this recognizes that edge-counting helped but hasn't generalized to the dimension-multiplication principle
	- Option 3 = Incorrect — correlational reasoning ("bigger = more area") is true but non-computational. A student selecting this may have a size intuition but can't produce exact area from dimensions
- **On Correct:** "The dimensions tell you the rows and columns. Once you know that, you don’t need to see every square. 6 rows of 7 is 6 × 7 is 42, whether you can see the squares or not."
- **Connection:** This explicitly names the M3-M4 → M7 connection per TVP: "Students have been building toward this since M3 (rows/columns) and M4 (multiplication)." The key insight is that dimensions ARE rows and columns — "side length" and "dimension" are formalized names for the same row/column structure M3 introduced with physical arrays. The equation appearing below BOTH images makes the mathematical invariance visible.
- **Remediation:** Pipeline (light — "Think about it: the dimensions say 6 and 7. That means 6 rows of 7. Does that sound familiar?")
> **Design Note:** This task fulfills the TVP's "callback to M3-M4 progression" beat. The two-image comparison (full grid vs. dims-only with same equation) is a visual argument for why dimensions make the grid unnecessary. The three MC options target different levels of understanding: conceptual understanding of dimension-multiplication (correct), partial-grid edge-counting strategy (incorrect — recognizes a procedural step but not the generalizable principle), and correlational size reasoning (incorrect — true but non-computational). Option 2 specifically targets students who grasped the partial-grid counting shortcut but haven't generalized to the dimension-multiplication principle — they're stuck one level short of the full insight. Option 3 targets students who have an intuitive size-area correlation but can't produce exact answers from dimensions. The feedback reinforces "6 rows of 7" language from M3 to show the vocabulary continuity.

### Metacognitive Reflection S.3: What Stayed the Same? \[Type 2 — Connection Recognition\]
- **Purpose:** Students reflect on what was CONSTANT across the entire grid-fading progression — a Type 2 (Connection Recognition) reflection that helps them identify the invariant principle. This is the metacognitive complement to S.1's visual demonstration: S.1 showed them that the area is the same; S.3 asks them to name WHY.
- **Reflection Type:** Type 2 (Connection Recognition) — recognizing connections between different representations and identifying the invariant. Appropriate for M7 per Playbook §3E (Types 2 and 4 introduced for M7-12).
- **Visual:** The four-mode display from S.1 remains visible (or a simplified version showing just the four mode labels: "Full grid → Partial grid → Tick marks → Dimensions only" with "= 42 square feet" at each step).
- **Guide:** "You found the same area (42 square feet) four different ways. The pictures looked different every time. What stayed the same?"
- **Prompt:** "What stayed the same across all four?"
- **Student Action:** MC selection
	- **Options:**
		- "The dimensions — 6 and 7"
		- "The number of squares I could see"
		- "The multiplication — 6 × 7 = 42"
		- "Something else"
- **Correct Answer:** Any selection (no wrong answer — reflection has no incorrect response). Options 1 and 3 are both conceptually strong.
- **On Correct (per selection):**
	- "The dimensions — 6 and 7" → "The dimensions never changed. 6 and 7 were there in every version — they're the information that matters."
	- "The number of squares I could see" → "The number of squares you could SEE changed — but the total number of squares was always 42. What carried that information? The dimensions."
	- "The multiplication — 6 × 7 = 42" → "The multiplication was always the same. Different pictures, same math. That's why dimensions are so powerful — they give you the multiplication every time."
	- "Something else" → "You noticed something. The big takeaway: the dimensions carry all the information you need. The rest is just how it looks."
- **Connection:** This reflection targets the conceptual invariant of M7: dimensions encode the mathematical relationship regardless of visual representation. Option 1 (dimensions) and Option 3 (multiplication) are both valid — they represent two ways of naming the same invariant (the structural information vs. the operation). Option 2 is a common but incomplete answer — the number of VISIBLE squares changes, but the total area doesn't. The feedback for Option 2 redirects gently to dimensions without marking the response as wrong. Type 2 (Connection Recognition) is the right fit because M7's core learning IS about recognizing connections between representations.
- **Remediation:** N/A (no wrong answer — reflection)
> **Design Note:** Type 2 (Connection Recognition) is new for M7 per Playbook §3E (Types 2 and 4 introduced for M7-12). This is appropriate because M7's entire pedagogy is about recognizing that different representations encode the same mathematical content — the very definition of connection recognition. The four options are designed to surface different levels of understanding: Option 1 (dimensions) is the structural insight, Option 3 (multiplication) is the procedural insight, Option 2 (visible squares) is a common misconception that the feedback redirects, and Option 4 allows for unexpected thinking. All responses are validated — this is metacognitive, not assessed. The S.1 visual remains visible to ground the reflection in a concrete, just-completed experience.

### Identity-Building Closure S.4 + M8 Bridge \[No Student Action\]
- **Purpose:** Specific, growth-oriented affirmation referencing what the student actually did across M7. Bridge to M8 per TVP: "You found area when dimensions were GIVEN. What if you know the AREA and need to figure out what the dimensions could be?"
- **Visual:** The dimensions-only rectangle from S.1 (6×7, outline + labels only) with "42 square feet" displayed below it. Clean, uncluttered — the final image of M7 is a rectangle that needs no grid.
- **Guide (Closure):** "You started with a full grid and a simple question: did the area change when the grid disappeared? Now you can look at any rectangle, even just words describing one, read the dimensions, and find the area. No grid needed. You learned that the dimensions carry all the information."
- **Guide (M8 Bridge):** "But here's something to wonder about. You found area when the dimensions were given. What if you know the area... and need to figure out what the dimensions could be? That's next."
- **No student action.**
> **Design Note:** The closure references the specific behavioral arc: started with "did the area change?" (W.2) → now can find area from dimensions or text alone. "No grid needed" is M7's behavioral punchline — it frames the grid-fading progression as a completed journey. "You learned that the dimensions carry all the information" echoes S.3's reflection. The M8 bridge uses the TVP verbatim: "You found area when dimensions were GIVEN. What if you know the AREA and need to figure out what the dimensions could be?" This is deliberately phrased as a "wonder" — curious, not anxious. The bridge doesn't answer the question or preview M8's method; it just poses the reverse-reasoning challenge that M8 will address. The final visual (dimensions-only rectangle) is M7's thesis image — a rectangle that communicates everything through labels alone.
> **Voice Note:** "No grid needed" parallels M6's closure line "That's the full measurement toolkit." Both are module-specific behavioral punchlines. The M8 bridge uses "wonder about" rather than "learn" — keeping M8 as a curiosity rather than a task. "What if you know the area... and need to figure out what the dimensions could be?" should sound intriguing, like a puzzle worth solving — not like a harder problem to dread.

## 1.10 KEY DESIGN DECISIONS (KDD)
### Purpose
KDD documents the pedagogical design choices that make this module work. Each entry explains WHAT was decided and WHY it matters for student learning. A writer seeing this module for the first time should understand why it works this way from the KDDs alone.
---
### KDD-1: Grid Fading Is the Instructional Strategy — Not a Scaffold That Happens to Fade
M7's grid-fading progression (full grid → partial → tick marks → dimensions only → text) is not just a support structure that gradually reduces — it IS the teaching. Each transition proves to the student that removing visual information doesn't change the mathematical relationship. The sequence of binary judgments (W.2: "Did the area change?", 1.5: "Is the area still 30?") is the mechanism by which students build conviction that dimensions alone encode area. Altering the progression order or skipping levels breaks the proof.
---
### KDD-2: Three Animated Transitions Carry the Pedagogical Weight
W.2 (full → dims-only), 1.5 (partial → ticks), and 3.0 (ticks → dims-only) are the three scaffold-removal moments. Each shows the SAME rectangle losing visual information while the Guide narrates what didn't change. These transitions must feel like the same object transforming, not like new objects replacing old ones — the identity persistence IS the insight. If animations are unavailable, the fallback (Guide narration + new visual) degrades the experience significantly because students lose the continuity.
---
### KDD-3: Partial Grid Reveals Row/Column Structure Without Showing Every Square
The partial grid mode (first row + first column visible, interior empty) is the critical bridge between full grids (M6) and tick marks. It shows enough structure for students to apply M3-M4 language ("I see 7 in each row and 4 rows") while requiring them to infer the interior. This is why the Early data constraint limits one factor to ≤5 — the visible column must be short enough for reliable counting. If both factors were large, row/column counting from the edges would become error-prone and shift cognitive load from the conceptual insight to the counting procedure.
---
### KDD-4: Tick Marks Are a Teaching Scaffold, Not an Assessed Representation
Tick marks appear in the Lesson (Section 2) but NOT in the EC. They exist to bridge partial grid to dimensions-only by showing where grid lines "used to be" — they're a transitional representation that makes the connection between countable structure and numerical labels. The EC tests the terminal representations (partial grid, dims-only, text-only) because those are the skill endpoints. Testing tick marks separately would over-assess the scaffold rather than the target skill.
---
### KDD-5: Vocabulary Introduction After Concrete Experience — Not Before
"Side length" and "dimension" are introduced at interaction 2.0, after the student has completed four partial-grid problems (1.1-1.4) and the Relational transition (1.5). This follows the vocabulary-after-experience principle: students first SEE and USE the row/column structure, then HEAR the formalized names. The key connection — "Dimensions are the factors in your multiplication" — lands because the student has already done the multiplication four times. Introducing vocabulary before concrete experience would make it an arbitrary label; introducing it after makes it a name for something the student already understands.
---
### KDD-6: Intentional Rectangle Reuse Across Phases Proves Representation Equivalence
Three rectangles are deliberately reused: 4×6 (Warmup → Warmup faded), 5×6 (S1 → Relational transition → vocabulary bridge), and 7×4 (S2 → S2→S3 transition). Each reuse serves the same pedagogical purpose — the student sees that the SAME rectangle yields the SAME area across different representations. This is M7's core proof strategy: identity persistence across visual changes. Without reuse, students might attribute area differences to the rectangles being different rather than the representations being different.
---
### KDD-7: Equation Builder Scaffolding Fades in Parallel with Grid Scaffolding
The Equation Builder progresses from unlabeled slots (S1: template with blanks) to labeled slots with unit labels (S2: template with unit) to unstructured mode (S3: no template, no slots — student builds from tiles). This parallel fading ensures the student never has MORE tool support than visual support — the EB scaffold removal tracks the grid scaffold removal. If EB stayed fully scaffolded while grids disappeared, students would rely on the EB template to structure their thinking rather than the dimensions.
---
### KDD-8: Text-Only Problems Use a Rigid Sentence Template
All text-only problems follow the TVP template: "A \[object\] is \[number\] \[unit\] long and \[number\] \[unit\] wide. What is its area?" This rigid structure is deliberate — M7 is the first module where students encounter text-only area problems, and the consistent sentence structure reduces reading load so students can focus on the mathematical task (extracting and multiplying dimensions). Varied sentence structures are appropriate for M8+ after students have practiced the basic text-to-dimensions extraction.
---
### KDD-9: No Full-Grid Problems After the Warmup
M7's Warmup (W.1) is the LAST full-grid interaction. From Section 1 onward, every rectangle has reduced visual information. This boundary applies to M7-tagged problems. Spiral review problems (M5-M6 tagged, 15% of Practice) may use full grids because they're reinforcing prior-module skills, not M7 content. The distinction is load-bearing: if full-grid problems appeared under the M7 tag, students would receive mixed signals about whether counting squares is still a valid M7 strategy. The tag boundary lets M7 enforce "no grid needed" within its own content while letting the spiral maintain prior skills without contradiction.
---
### KDD-10: Forward and Reverse Practice Ratio — Reverse Tagged as EXPOSURE
> **Author Flag A9** (see §1.8.5 Practice Distribution): Reverse problem ratio (70/15/15 forward/reverse-EXPOSURE/spiral) pending SME resolution. Andrea must confirm before publication. See §1.8.5 for full breakdown and rationale.
Per TVP, Practice includes both forward problems (given dimensions, find area) and reverse problems (given area and one dimension, find the other). Reverse problems are tagged EXPOSURE because they preview M8's reverse reasoning without requiring mastery — M8 introduces reverse as new content, and M7 should not pre-assess it. The reduced ratio (15% reverse, down from TVP's 50%) ensures students encounter reverse reasoning as a low-stakes preview rather than a mastery expectation. Reverse problems use the same grid modes as forward problems — the only change is which value is unknown.
---
### KDD-11: EC Tests Three Grid-Fading Levels, Not Four
The EC includes partial grid, dimensions-only, and text-only — but not tick marks. The three tested levels represent the skill endpoints: partial grid (some visual structure), dimensions-only (labels only), and text (no visual at all). Tick marks are a teaching scaffold that bridges partial grid to dimensions-only; they're not a terminal representation. Testing all four would extend the EC beyond 3-5 minutes and would over-assess the scaffold.
---
### KDD-12: Misconception #5 Prevention Through Equation Builder Forcing
The Equation Builder forces students to build a multiplication expression — they cannot submit an addition or a single number. This structural constraint makes misconception #5 (adding dimensions instead of multiplying) mechanically difficult to execute in the EB interaction. In the MC fallback (text-only), the addition distractor (e.g., 12 = 4+8) is always present for diagnostic purposes. If a student consistently selects addition distractors in MC, the Pipeline flags for misconception #5 remediation. The EB is not just a calculation tool — it's a misconception-prevention mechanism.
---
### KDD-13: The Synthesis Progression Display Is M7's Thesis Image — Built Through Card Grouping
S.1 uses Card Grouping to have students sort 8 cards (two rectangles × four grid modes) into two groups. When each group is formed, the resulting fan IS the progression display: four representations of the same rectangle, visible simultaneously. This is the visual thesis of M7. Students have experienced each mode sequentially across the Lesson; the Card Grouping task makes them BUILD the simultaneous comparison rather than passively view it. The classification requires reading dimensions across ALL four modes — a student can't sort correctly by looking at only full grids. The two-rectangle design (6×7 sq ft and 5×4 sq cm) is pedagogically essential: if the student can group correctly for TWO different rectangles with different units, they've demonstrated that representation equivalence is a GENERALIZABLE PRINCIPLE — not a one-off observation about a single rectangle they just practiced. A single-rectangle design would leave open the possibility that the student recognizes "this rectangle" in four modes without understanding that ANY rectangle works the same way. The two-unit split (sq ft vs. sq cm) adds a secondary signal that prevents grouping by unit instead of by dimensional content.
---
### KDD-14: M8 Bridge Poses the Reverse Question Without Answering It
The Synthesis closure asks: "What if you know the area... and need to figure out what the dimensions could be?" This is the TVP's specified bridge to M8, and it's deliberately left as an open question. M7 ends with students confident in forward reasoning (dimensions → area); M8 begins with the reverse challenge (area → dimensions). The bridge creates a curiosity gap that M8's Warmup will pick up. The phrasing "could be" (not "are") subtly signals that multiple dimension pairs can produce the same area — M8's territory.
---
### KDD-15: Early Phase Factor Constraint (≤5) Protects Column Counting
In partial-grid mode, students count the first column to determine the number of rows. The ≤5 constraint on one factor ensures this column is short enough (5 or fewer) for reliable counting. If both factors could be 7-9, the first column would be long enough to introduce counting errors, shifting cognitive load from "partial grid → multiplication" to "accurate counting of a long column." The constraint frees attention for the conceptual work.
---
### KDD-16: All Four Units Required in Mid and Late — No Unit Introduction
M7 uses all four standard units (square feet, meters, inches, centimeters) but introduces none. All four were established in M5-M6. The four-unit requirement across Mid and Late phases serves two purposes: (1) it prevents students from associating a specific unit with a specific grid mode (e.g., "tick marks always mean square feet"), and (2) it reinforces M6's unit system within M7's grid-fading context, maintaining spiral continuity.
---
### KDD-17: Card Grouping Satisfies the Active Task Check — MC Fallback Documented
The Synthesis Phase Playbook requires "at least one task requires placement, matching, or creation (not all MC)." The original S.1 design used MC, which left all three student interactions (S.1, S.2, S.3) as selection-based — failing the Active Task Check. S.1 was redesigned as a Card Grouping interaction (drag-to-group: 8 cards, 2 groups of 4) which satisfies the check through drag-based classification. Card Grouping is pedagogically stronger than MC for this task because it forces engagement with ALL representations — the full grid can't carry a student to the correct answer when they must read and classify dims-only and tick-mark cards too. If Card Grouping is unavailable at engineering time (the Notion spec is "Initial Spec Draft" status), the MC fallback is documented in ISF-S1: display four modes of a single 6×7 rectangle, student selects area from MC options \{13, 24, 42, 67\}. The MC fallback degrades both the Active Task Check (all interactions become MC again) and the diagnostic richness (misplaced-card data is lost), so Card Grouping availability should be prioritized.
---
### KDD-18: Teaching Constraints and Practice Constraints Serve Different Purposes
Early's ≤5 factor constraint and ≤45 product limit exist to protect column counting during *teaching* — when the student is first learning to read partial grids, the column must be short enough for reliable counting so cognitive load stays on the conceptual insight, not the counting procedure. Practice opens to the full factor range (2-10) and ≤100 product limit because the student has already established the partial-grid reading skill in the Lesson; Practice tests whether that skill *transfers* to unfamiliar dimensions, which requires larger and more varied rectangles. If Practice inherited the teaching constraints, it would only confirm that students can do what they were just shown — not that they own the skill independently.
