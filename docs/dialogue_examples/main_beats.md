# Main Beats — Gold Standard Examples

Main beats are the Guide's instructional dialogue before and between student interactions. They carry new concepts, frame tasks, and keep the lesson moving.

---

## Key Changes That Align with Guide Design

**Em dashes removed → commas or periods**
Old scripts used em dashes heavily to connect clauses. The rewriter constraint explicitly bans them. Commas and short sentences produce a more natural spoken rhythm.

> ❌ Old: "This bar is our whole — it's like one chocolate bar."
> ✅ New: "This bar is our whole. It's like one chocolate bar."

**Rhetorical wonder → direct observation**
Old scripts used "I wonder which..." to frame attention. The new register replaces this with a direct observation or question that invites the student in.

> ❌ Old: "I wonder which of these bars has all its parts exactly the same size?"
> ✅ New: "Did you notice that one bar is split into parts that are exactly the same size?"

**Tool names dropped**
Old scripts introduced the tool by name ("These are called Shaded Rectangle Bars"). The new dialogue refers to the visual directly without naming it — less scripted, more present.

> ❌ Old: "These are called Shaded Rectangle Bars — they help us see how things can be split into parts."
> ✅ New: "These bars help us see how things can be split into parts."

**Conceptual payoff stated plainly, not dramatically**
Old scripts added theatrical emphasis ("That's powerful!", "Watch this!"). The new dialogue lets the math land on its own.

> ❌ Old: "Look at that — we just composed two-fourths! That's powerful!"
> ✅ New: "Now we have two-fourths. Count with me: one shaded, two shaded."

## Gold Standard Examples

### Grounding the whole before dividing (M1)
> "Before we divide it, let's think about this for a second: This bar is our whole. It's like one chocolate bar. Everything we do next is a piece of THIS whole, not a different one."

Why it works: Sets up Misconception #2 (losing track of the whole) without naming it. Conversational, stakes the whole before the student touches anything.

---

### Counterintuitive pattern stated plainly (M2)
> "More parts means smaller pieces, even though 6 is a bigger number than 3."

Why it works: One sentence. States the counterintuitive fact directly. No scaffolding needed — the visual has already done the work.

---

### Real-world grounding with precision (M2)
> "Look at these two bars. Imagine they're both cakes!"

Why it works: Short. Doesn't over-explain the analogy. Lets the student project the scenario.

---

### Cake analogy closed with the right principle (M2)
> "When you cut a cake into only 4 pieces, each piece is bigger than when you cut it into 8 pieces."

Why it works: Stays with the analogy and delivers the principle. No exclamation point — the insight doesn't need amplification.

---

### Whole-equals-fraction moment (M5)
> "Think about it. If you have all three pieces of something cut into thirds, you have the whole thing. So three thirds and 1 are placed at the same tick."

Why it works: "Think about it" signals a genuine pause. The reasoning is concrete and leads directly to the visual consequence.

---

### Pattern continues past 1 (M6)
> "The fourths didn't stop at 1; they just kept going with that same consistent spacing. That's actually how all fractions work!"

Why it works: Generalizes the discovery without over-explaining. The exclamation point is earned — this is a genuine insight moment, not routine.

---

### Equivalent fractions require same-sized whole (M8)
> "One half of a granola bar, and two fourths of a school bus, aren't equal, because the wholes are different sizes. But one half and two fourths of the same granola bar are equal. The whole has to be exactly the same size for fractions to be equivalent."

Why it works: Two contrasting concrete examples. The rule is stated at the end, after the examples. No jargon until it's earned.

---

### Amount stays the same (M8)
> "The pieces get smaller, so we shade more pieces, but the total amount of the whole bar that's shaded stays the same."

Why it works: Traces the student's visual experience directly. Short, specific, the right level of abstraction.

---

### Benchmark as a real-world concept (M12)
> "A benchmark is like a signpost or a marker, something you can measure things against."

Why it works: Analogy that doesn't over-explain. "Signpost" is concrete and doesn't require cultural context.

---

### Fraction extending beyond 1 (M6)
> "You just made five equal intervals from zero. That's five fourths, which is more than one whole. When a fraction is greater than one whole, like five fourths, we call it an improper fraction. That means that our counting pattern continues beyond 1."

Why it works: Leads with what the student just did. Introduces the vocabulary after the concept, not before.
