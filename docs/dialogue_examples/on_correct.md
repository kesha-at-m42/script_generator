# On-Correct Beats — Gold Standard Examples

On-correct beats fire after a student answers correctly. They confirm the answer and — when appropriate — extend the insight or name what the student just demonstrated.

---

## Key Changes That Align with Guide Design

**Generic confirmation → specific observation**
Old scripts often confirmed with "Yes!" or "Exactly!" with a restatement of the answer. The new register uses the Four Quality Tests: specific, observable, journey-aware.

> ❌ Old: "Yes! One-half is bigger than one-fourth. Even though the bottom number 4 is bigger than 2, the actual piece is smaller."
> ✅ New: "Yes. One-half is bigger than one-fourth or one-fourth. Even though 4 is a bigger number than 2, the actual piece is smaller. This pattern works for all unit fractions."

The new version adds the generalization ("This pattern works for all unit fractions") rather than just restating the local observation.

**Hollow exclamation removed**
Guide design flags more than 1 exclamation point per 3 interactions. Old scripts routinely had multiple per interaction. On-correct beats were the main offender.

> ❌ Old: "Perfect! You've discovered the pattern!"
> ✅ New: "You identified the pattern."

**Earned vs. unearned specificity**
When a student solves a routine task, a short confirmation is correct. When they make a genuine discovery, the on-correct beat should name the discovery specifically. The new scripts distinguish these better.

**Strategy named after the fact**
The new scripts sometimes name the strategy the student just used after they succeed, rather than before. This reinforces transfer without front-loading.

> ✅ Example: "You picked the Same denominator strategy and counted equal parts." (M11)

## Gold Standard Examples

### Routine success — short and clean (M1)
> "Yes. Two equal parts."

Why it works: Confirms the task. Nothing more. The lesson moves on. Appropriate for procedural confirmation.

---

### Insight named immediately (M5)
> "Right at 1! That's because three thirds equals one whole."

Why it works: The exclamation point is earned — the visual payoff of landing at 1 is a genuine discovery. Immediately names why.

---

### Pattern extended into the future (M2)
> "Yes. When you cut the same cake into only 4 pieces, each piece is bigger than when you cut it into 8 pieces."

Why it works: Confirms the specific action while restating the principle in concrete terms.

---

### Insight named + generalized (M6)
> "Eight fourths lands exactly at 2! Whole numbers are just collections of unit fractions, like how eight fourths equals 2 wholes. Every group of four fourths makes another whole number."

Why it works: The exclamation marks the visual payoff. The next two sentences add mathematical depth — this is a synthesis moment, not a routine confirmation.

---

### Pattern observed in the student's specific action (M7)
> "Three fourths away from 0, you're almost at 1. Notice the pattern? Each fraction is one more fourth."

Why it works: References the specific position on the number line, then names the pattern. "Notice the pattern?" is a genuine question, not rhetorical — the student can see it.

---

### Equivalent fraction named precisely (M9)
> "You recognized that six fourths lands at the same position as three halves. They're equivalent fractions: different names for the same position!"

Why it works: Names what the student did ("recognized"), then states the principle with a precise definition. The exclamation is earned — this is a conceptual breakthrough.

---

### Strategy named after success (M11)
> "Yes! Two sixths is less than four sixths. When intervals are the same size, counting the intervals tells us which is greater."

Why it works: Confirms, then names the principle the student just applied. Helps them recognize the strategy for future use.

---

### Comparison confirmed with specific reasoning (M10)
> "Nice. You followed our strategy for comparing fraction sizes. Four sixths is bigger than two sixths because the pieces are equal in size and 4 is more than 2. If the denominators were different, we'd need to use a different strategy."

Why it works: "Nice." is brief. The explanation that follows names the reasoning and flags the boundary condition — when this strategy applies and when it doesn't.

---

### Counterintuitive result restated clearly (M9)
> "Right! When the top number and bottom number match, the fraction equals 1. This rule is always true."

Why it works: "This rule is always true" is a strong, direct statement — appropriate for a rule the student has just discovered rather than been told.

---

## Short Confirmation Templates (Routine Success)

For procedural tasks where brevity is right:

- "Yes. Two equal parts."
- "Right. Four equal parts."
- "Good. One out of three equal parts."
- "Two equal parts, and they match perfectly."
- "Right. That's where three fourths goes."

These are NOT hollow — they confirm the specific thing the student just did. The math content of the confirmation is the specificity.
