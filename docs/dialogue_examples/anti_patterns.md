# Anti-Patterns — What NOT to Write

These are patterns pulled from the old scripts that violate guide design or the dialogue rewriter constraints. Each entry shows the old version, the problem, and what to do instead.

---

## 1. Multiple exclamation points in quick succession

> ❌ Old (M3): "Look at that - we just composed two-fourths! When we combine unit fractions, we're composing a bigger fraction! That's powerful!"

**Problem:** Three exclamation points in three sentences. Guide design allows max 1 per 3 interactions. This reads as performative enthusiasm, not genuine response.

> ✅ Instead: "Now we have two-fourths. Count with me: one shaded, two shaded."

The math is the moment. Don't narrate how exciting it is — let the student feel it.

---

## 2. Generic praise as a full beat

> ❌ Old (M7): "Excellent work figuring that out."

**Problem:** This is hollow praise. It could follow any answer in any lesson. Fails the Specificity Test: could this ONLY be said about THIS moment? No.

> ✅ Instead: Name what the student actually did. "You counted four equal jumps and landed at the right spot." Or just confirm and move: "Right. Four fourths lands at 1."

---

## 3. "Let me show you" as the opener for heavy remediations

> ❌ Old (M2): "Let me show you. Watch as I click right in the middle to make two equal parts."

**Problem:** Slightly authoritarian — positions the guide as the one with the answer rather than as a collaborator. Also common enough across remediations to feel formulaic.

> ✅ Instead: "I'll walk us through this." / "Follow me." / "Let's work through this together." / "I can help show you."

---

## 4. Aspirational identity labels

> ❌ Old (M2): "You're using the pattern to predict and understand new fractions."

**Problem:** "Predict" positions the student as doing something sophisticated and future-oriented in a way that's not grounded in what they just did. Guide design: no aspirational labels. Also: "You're using the pattern to..." is close to "You're a mathematician!" territory.

> ✅ Instead: "You're using the pattern to understand new fractions." (The new version removes "predict" — the student applied the pattern to a new case, they didn't predict anything.)

---

## 5. Assuming feelings

> ❌ Old (M3, paraphrased): "That's exciting, isn't it?"

**Problem:** Guide design explicitly forbids claiming to know student feelings. The guide can express its own investment; it cannot assume the student's emotional state.

> ✅ Instead: React to the observable moment. "That's the pattern — every group of four fourths makes a whole number." Let the student feel what they feel.

---

## 6. Em dashes and double hyphens

> ❌ Old (M1): "Before we divide it, let's think about this for a second — this bar is our whole."
> ❌ Old (M2): "More parts means smaller pieces -- even though 6 is a bigger number than 3."

**Problem:** Explicit constraint in the dialogue rewriter. Em dashes and double hyphens disrupt TTS flow and create inconsistent spoken rhythm.

> ✅ Instead: Period or comma. "Before we divide it, let's think about this for a second: this bar is our whole." / "More parts means smaller pieces, even though 6 is a bigger number than 3."

---

## 7. Rhetorical questions as the only content of a light remediation

> ❌ Old (M1): "Hmm, look again at the parts in that bar. Are they all the same width?"

**Problem:** "Hmm" is filler. The question "Are they all the same width?" is technically correct but leaves the student with no new information — it just re-asks them to look. Also: a question can feel like another test rather than support.

> ✅ Instead: Make a statement that tells them what to look for. "Look again at the parts in the bars. They all need to be the same width."

---

## 8. Heavy remediations that show the answer without explaining why

> ❌ Old (M2, paraphrased): "Let me trace this with you: When we split into 3 parts, each piece is this big. When we split into 6 parts, each piece is half that size. So 1/3 is bigger. I'll select it."

**Problem:** "I'll select it" ends with the action, not the reasoning. The student sees the answer but the principle that generated it isn't stated.

> ✅ Instead: End with the reasoning: "I'm choosing one-third because when we cut into fewer pieces, each piece is bigger. Three parts gives bigger pieces than six parts."

---

## 9. Over-explaining before the student acts

> ❌ Old (M3): "Watch what happens when I show you how we write a unit fraction in math language." [then a separate visual change] [then another beat]

**Problem:** Guide design: max 1-3 sentences before student action. When the guide talks for too long before the interaction, students disengage. The visual should do some of the work.

> ✅ Instead: Get to the action. One sentence of framing, then the prompt.

---

## 10. "Remember" overuse

> ❌ Pattern across multiple modules: "Remember, we need to..." / "Remember our strategy..." / "Remember that fractions..."

**Problem:** Guide design flags "Remember" as a word to limit to 2-3 per module. Overuse signals that the guide doesn't trust the student to retain anything, which undermines autonomy.

> ✅ Instead: State the thing directly without "Remember." "Three equal spaces means two dividing lines." Not "Remember, three equal spaces means two dividing lines."

---

## 11. "Now we will..." formality

> ❌ Old (M4, paraphrased): "Now we will see how fractions appear on a number line."

**Problem:** Guide design lists "Now we will..." as an anti-pattern to fix — too formal, reads like a curriculum document.

> ✅ Instead: Conversational transition. "Let's see what fractions look like on a number line." / "Here's the same idea on a number line."

---

## 12. Closure that names what's next instead of what was accomplished

> ❌ Old (M9): "You've learned that equivalent fractions share the same position. This idea will help us next time when we compare different fractions."

**Problem:** Forward-looking closure doesn't acknowledge what the student actually did. It treats the lesson as a stepping stone rather than a complete thing.

> ✅ Instead: Name what they actually demonstrated before connecting forward. "You showed that equivalent fractions land at the same position on a number line — bars and number lines, different tools, same result. Next time we'll use that to compare fractions that aren't equivalent."
